# David John Lewis — Sicily to the Po, and the U.S. Silver Star

Military narrative for [David John Lewis](../people/david-john-lewis.md) (**1918–2000**): **Major (temp.)**, **1st Battalion, The Welch Regiment**, **British Army**; army number **143587**. **U.S. Silver Star**; **Mentioned in Dispatches**. **Structured tree:** [family-tree.json](../family-tree.json). **Welsh line (parents):** [Lewis in Aberdare and Merthyr](lewis-aberdare-merthyr-coalfield.md).

---

## The university student

When the 1939 Register was taken on 29 September, David John Lewis — twenty-one years old, born in Merthyr Tydfil, raised in Aberdare — was listed as a **university student**. Within months Britain was at war, and Lewis was among the generation of undergraduates who went straight from lecture halls to officer cadet training. On **23 August 1940**, his emergency commission as **2nd Lieutenant** in the **Welch Regiment** was gazetted — army number **143587**, one of a batch of ten Welch officers (143584–143593) commissioned that day. By 1943 he was serving with the regiment's 1st Battalion in the Mediterranean.

---

## Sicily — July 1943

The 1st Battalion Welch Regiment joined the **168th (2nd London) Infantry Brigade** in May 1943, a London Territorial formation that had already seen action in North Africa. The 168th Brigade was at that point attached to the **50th (Northumbrian) Infantry Division**, which had lost its 150th Brigade at Gazala the previous summer. In July 1943, with the 50th Division, the brigade fought in the **Allied invasion of Sicily** — the first large-scale amphibious assault on occupied Europe.

After Sicily, the 50th Division was recalled to England to prepare for D-Day. In October 1943 the 168th Brigade was returned to its parent formation, the **56th (London) Infantry Division**, and went back to the Italian mainland. The 1st Welch went with it.

---

## The Garigliano and Cassino — January 1944

The 56th Division, part of **British X Corps**, fought through the autumn and winter of 1943–44 on the Italian mainland: actions at Maddaloni, Caserta, Capua, and the defended river crossings that made up the German Winter Line. In mid-January 1944, the 168th Brigade crossed the **Garigliano river** as part of the **First Battle of Monte Cassino**. During this operation, Private George Allan Mitchell of the 1st London Scottish — the 168th Brigade's sister battalion — won the **Victoria Cross**, the only one awarded to the brigade or the 56th Division during the war.

---

## Anzio — February 1944

On 30 January 1944, X Corps was ordered to send a brigade to reinforce the **Anzio** bridgehead, which was under severe German pressure. The 168th Brigade was detached from the 56th Division and placed under the **1st British Infantry Division**, itself operating within **U.S. VI Corps**. The brigade landed at Anzio on **3 February** and was almost immediately thrown into combat as the Germans launched a major counterattack.

What followed was six weeks of attritional fighting in the confined, flat, waterlogged beachhead. The 168th Brigade suffered **fifty per cent casualties** — the worst rate of the three brigades in the 56th Division. The London Irish Rifles lost 582 men (32 officers and 550 other ranks), embarking for Egypt with only 12 officers and 300 men, most of them wounded who had returned. The 10th Royal Berkshires were reduced to roughly forty men fit for duty.

The 1st Welch was in the middle of this. The brigade was eventually pulled out and sent to Egypt to rebuild, taking on replacements that included ex-anti-aircraft gunners of the Royal Artillery retrained as line infantry — men whose officers later rated them excellent.

---

## The Gothic Line — August–September 1944

The reorganised brigade landed at **Taranto** on 17 July 1944 and rejoined the 56th Division under Eighth Army command. Within weeks they were fighting through the **Gothic Line** — the last major German defensive position across Italy — in **Operation Olive**.

The specific engagement was the **Battle of Gemmano**, a hilltop village in the Apennine foothills that the Germans had turned into a fortress. The fighting was brutal. The 56th Division took nearly half of the Eighth Army's total Olive casualties — approximately 6,000 men. By 21 September the brigade was pulled out of the line. The 1st Welch left the 168th Brigade on **26 September 1944**, reduced once again to **cadre strength**. A generation of officers and men who had landed in Sicily barely a year earlier had been consumed.

---

## Rebuilding — winter 1944–45

The 1st Welch was rebuilt from cadre during the winter of 1944–45 and attached to the **61st Infantry Brigade**, **6th Armoured Division** — a formation that had been in Italy since 1943, fighting at Kasserine, up through the Liri Valley, Arezzo, Florence, and the Gothic Line under Major General Horatius Murray. By spring 1945 the rebuilt battalion was ready for the final offensive.

---

## The Argenta Gap and the Po — April 1945

**Operation Grapeshot**, the final Allied offensive in Italy, opened on 6 April 1945. The 6th Armoured Division was part of **V Corps**, British Eighth Army, tasked with breaking through the last German defences before the Po valley.

The key battle was the **Argenta Gap** — a narrow corridor between Lake Comacchio and the Reno river carrying Highway 16 to Ferrara. It was the last defensible bottleneck before open country. Between 13 and 19 April, V Corps forced the gap. The German LXXVI Panzer Corps was effectively destroyed, with only a few hundred men managing to cross the Po.

On **19 April**, the Argenta Gap secure, the 6th Armoured Division was released through the advancing 78th Infantry Division to swing northwest along the Reno toward **Bondeno**, aiming to link with the U.S. Fifth Army advancing north from Bologna and complete the encirclement of the German forces. Bondeno fell on **23 April**. The next day the 6th Armoured linked with the U.S. **10th Mountain Division** at Finale.

The Po crossings followed immediately. And on the morning of **25 April 1945**, Major David Lewis received his order.

---

## The action — 25 April 1945

Lewis was ordered to seize and hold **Faccenda** — a cluster of farm buildings in enclosed country on the right flank of the **Po bridgehead**. His company came under heavy machine gun and mortar fire on the approach. The advance stalled. Lewis took a platoon forward himself, led them into the German positions around the farm buildings, and overran them. The defenders fled, leaving seven dead and six prisoners.

Hours later, a German counterattack formed south of Faccenda. Lewis did not wait for it to develop. He placed himself at the head of his reserve platoon and attacked again. The counterattack collapsed.

For this action he was awarded the **United States Silver Star** — an Allied decoration for a British officer, promulgated by Headquarters, U.S. Army Forces, Mediterranean Theater of Operations, **General Orders No. 285 (10 November 1945)**. The full citation:

> The President of the United States of America, authorized by Act of Congress July 9, 1918, takes pleasure in presenting the Silver Star to **Major David J. Lewis**, **British Army**, for heroic achievement in connection with military operations while serving with the **1st Battalion, The Welch Regiment** in **Italy on 25 April 1945**. During the establishment of a **bridgehead over the Po River**, Major Lewis was ordered to seize and hold **Faccenda**, a small village in enclosed country, to protect the right flank of the bridgehead. On approaching the objective, the company was suddenly subjected to heavy machine gun and mortar fire, imposing a check on the whole advance. With speed and aggressive determination Major Lewis led a platoon to assault the strong German positions in and around farm buildings, and by his personal efforts and example successfully overran the enemy, who fled leaving seven dead and six prisoners. Later, an enemy counterattack formed south of Faccenda, with the evident intention of destroying Major Lewis' company. Again showing the highest qualities of leadership and initiative, Major Lewis gave the enemy no opportunity to launch his attack, but immediately placed himself at the head of the reserve platoon and assaulted the enemy who speedily withdrew. Throughout the day, Major Lewis' offensive spirit and total disregard for his own personal safety were an inspiration to his whole company, and a complete deterrent to the enemy's further efforts to threaten the bridgehead.

*Hall of Valor* lists **Service: Foreign** — an Allied (non-U.S.) servicemember. Published at [Hall of Valor — David Lewis](https://valor.militarytimes.com/hero/107821).

Lewis was also **mentioned in dispatches** — confirmed in the London Gazette, Supplement 37575, 23 May 1946, page 2466. He appears as **Maj. (temp.) D. J. LEWIS (143587)** under the Welch Regiment heading. The MiD and the Silver Star are separate recognitions: the American decoration was promulgated by U.S. Mediterranean Theater HQ, while the British mention came through the standard Commonwealth dispatches system. Between them they document both the specific Faccenda action and his broader service in the Italian campaign.

## Austria — May 1945

The war in Italy ended on **2 May 1945** with the surrender of all Axis forces. The 6th Armoured Division pushed north into **Austria** in the final days, and Lewis later recalled that he **ended his war in Austria**. The division subsequently moved to the **Trieste** area, where it remained on occupation duty — the contested border zone between Italy, Yugoslavia, and Austria that would become a Cold War flashpoint. Lewis had fought from Sicily to the Austrian Alps — the full length of the Italian peninsula.

### Rank progression

Lewis rose from 2nd Lieutenant in 1940 to **Major** commanding a company in combat by 1945 — a progression earned across Sicily, Cassino, Anzio, the Gothic Line, and the final offensive. The wartime British Army used layered rank designations: "war substantive" Captain, "temporary" Major. The 1953 gazette formalised his departure with the honorary rank of **Major**, the rank he held in action and the rank the family has always known.
