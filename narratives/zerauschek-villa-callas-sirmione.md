# Villa Ester — from the Zerauscheks to Maria Callas

On the tip of the Sirmione peninsula, where the road curves toward the Roman ruins improperly called the Grotte di Catullo, a villa rises at the edge of a park thick with conifers. The industrialist Giannantoni built it around 1928 — mixed styles, Swiss-inspired sloping roofs, four different window types on the front facade — and it passed through a chain of owners that connects Zadar's Italian exile community to the most famous soprano of the twentieth century.

## The pearl of Lake Garda

Sirmione juts into Lake Garda like a finger: a narrow peninsula, barely four kilometres long, guarded at its neck by the thirteenth-century Scaliger castle. Beyond the castle gate the old town thins to a single road, flanked by olive trees and thermal springs, until it reaches the archaeological park of the so-called Grotte di Catullo — the remains of the largest Roman domestic villa in northern Italy. It was here, on the most coveted stretch of waterfront in Lombardy, that the Giannantoni family chose to build.

## Antonio Zerauschek buys the villa

Antonio Zerauschek — trader, banker, factory owner in interwar Zara — acquired the Giannantoni villa at some point before the Second World War. He was at the peak of his commercial career: the Manifattura Zaratina Sigarette producing Calypso cigarettes, the Ausonia chocolate factory exporting to Western Europe, a hotel and café complex on Široka ulica designed by the Trieste architect Umberto Nordio. Sirmione was a natural choice for a man of his standing — Lake Garda had drawn Italian industrialists and professionals since the Belle Époque, and the peninsula's position between Lombardy, the Veneto, and the Trentino made it a social crossroads.

The family called it **Villa Ester**, after Antonio's wife [Ester Addobbati](../people/ester-addobbati.md). In the villa's generous rooms and its park of conifers the Zerauschek family spent their summers and holidays, the children growing up between Zara's Adriatic light and Garda's Alpine calm.

## Fulvia at Villa Ester

[Fulvia Ottilia Antonia Zerauschek](../people/fulvia-ottilia-antonia-zerauschek.md) was born in Zara in 1923, the daughter of Antonio and Ester. A photograph from the family archive — taken when Fulvia was a young woman, perhaps in the late 1930s or early 1940s — shows her sitting on the balustrade of the villa's loggia, framed by the open French doors of a room with polished marble floors. Behind her, the park is in full leaf. The wrought-iron scrollwork on the doors, the round stone medallions on the balustrade, the deep interior shadow and the bright garden beyond — this is Villa Ester as the Zerauscheks knew it.

The villa represented a world that would not survive the war. Within a few years of that photograph, Allied bombing would devastate Zara, the Yugoslav partisans would take the city, and the Italian-speaking families who had shaped its commercial life for generations would scatter into exile.

## War and exile

The Zerauscheks left Zara under the bombing of 1943–44. Everything Antonio had built in the city — the cigarette factory, the chocolate works, the hotel, the houses — was either destroyed or confiscated by the incoming Yugoslav authorities. The Manifattura Zaratina Sigarette attempted to reconstitute itself in Florence, but the Tribunale di Firenze annulled the shareholders' resolution and the company ceased to exist.

Antonio and Ester settled in Florence. In the upheaval of the immediate post-war years, they sold the Sirmione villa to **Angelo Marinoni**, a Veronese manufacturer of electric ovens. Villa Ester passed out of the family's hands, though the connection to Sirmione endured — Fulvia and her husband [David John Lewis](../people/david-john-lewis.md) would eventually be buried there.

## Meneghini buys for Callas — December 1957

In December 1957, the villa changed hands for the last time that matters to this story. **Giovanni Battista Meneghini** — Commendatore, Veronese brick-and-tile industrialist, and husband of **Maria Callas** — bought it from Marinoni for approximately sixty million lire. It was a gift for his wife, who wanted a house on Lake Garda.

Meneghini had married Callas in Verona in April 1949, in a church ceremony witnessed by six people. By 1957 she was the most celebrated soprano in the world, and their Milan apartment on Via Buonarroti 40 was already famous for its French eighteenth-century furniture and Venetian Settecento paintings. Sirmione would be the summer counterpart: a lakeside retreat between international engagements.

## Telefoni e rose

A journalist from the weekly *Incom*, Savino Mariani, visited the villa on 21 April 1958 and left a vivid record. Callas arrived in a green olive Giulietta, wearing a dark turban hat and a single strand of pearls. Her first order, given in February, had been: *"Telefoni, telefoni"* — telephones in every room. Her second: *"Rose, rose"* — the rarest varieties, climbers, hedgerow roses, dwarf and giant, to fill the park.

She spent three hours inspecting the restoration work, directing an architect from Verona, a building contractor, and a Padua nursery specialist. She approved the arched entrance, the guest apartment, the wall colours. In the garden she ordered hundreds of new plantings: maples, acacias, araucarias, gardenias, magnolias, camellias — and along the travertine-paved path leading to the Lombard-era church of **San Pietro in Mavino**, fragrant hedges. The dominant note would be roses.

Mariani noted that Callas moved through practical problems with the same confidence she brought to the stage. At lunch afterward — marking the Meneghinis' ninth wedding anniversary — she discussed lead-polishing the floors and adjusting the kitchen, switching between Italian, Veronese dialect, and Milanese.

## "Questa villa è una catapecchia"

For all the restoration work, Callas was not entirely satisfied. At the summer 1957 inauguration she invited all her Milan friends but banned photographers: *"Verranno quando potrò mostrar loro la villa che dico io, non questa catapecchia"* — "They can come when I can show them the villa I have in mind, not this hovel." Meneghini hid his embarrassment with a quip: *"Tu meriteresti la villa più bella del mondo"* — "You would deserve the most beautiful villa in the world." The *catapecchia* was, a magazine correspondent noted drily, in fact elegant and comfortable: three floors plus a solarium built as a turret on the terrace, a rectangular swimming pool in the park.

## Villa Callas

The villa that the Zerauscheks had called Villa Ester became, in the eyes of the world, **Villa Callas**. Maria Callas lived there from 1958 to 1959, when her marriage to Meneghini collapsed after she met Aristotle Onassis aboard his yacht *Christina* in the summer of 1959. By late that year "Titta" Meneghini was receiving divorce lawyers from Verona and Milan in the villa's comfortable rooms, while the press reported that Onassis planned to give Callas the artistic direction of the Opera of Monte Carlo.

After the separation Meneghini moved to nearby Colombare; Callas kept the Sirmione villa until 1970. The property later passed through inheritance and subdivision, and is today a private condominium at Via Caio Valerio Catullo 7.

A plaque on the exterior commemorates Callas and Meneghini. The villa's facade has not changed since the 1950s restoration — the same mixed styles, the same sloping roofs, the same loggia where a young woman from Zara once sat in a striped dress, watching the conifers.
