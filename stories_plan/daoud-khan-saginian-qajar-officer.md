# Daoud Khan Saginian — Georgian Officer in the Qajar Army

## Pitch

The oldest story on the direct Persia line. Daoud Khan Saginian fled Russian Georgia in 1811 with his brother Zaal and the Georgian nobleman Solayman Khan Saham al-Dowleh, entered Qajar military service, and rose to the rank of **Sartip** (brigadier). He commanded forces in Shiraz and Isfahan under Abbas Mirza and Fath Ali Shah, and in 1834 led one wing of the Isfahan army during a factional conflict between the princes Sayf ol-Dowleh and Shafti. After his patron's fall, he disappears from the sources. His daughter Anna Saginian married Edward Burgess; his other daughter Tamar married Dr. William Cormick. He is buried in the Armenian cemetery in Tabriz, near Edward Burgess.

A story about identity, patronage, and survival: a Georgian Christian who made a military career inside a Muslim monarchy, whose descendants married into Anglo-Irish and French families across three generations.

## Proposed sections (scrolly beats)

1. **Georgia, 1811** — flight from the Russian Empire with Zaal and Solayman Khan. The Caucasus route south to Persia.
2. **The Qajar army** — how Armenians and Georgians served in the Qajar military structure: *gholām* parallels, the role of Christian minorities in an Islamic state.
3. **Abbas Mirza and the crown** — Daoud Khan's service under the crown prince, Tabriz as the military frontier.
4. **Isfahan, 1834** — commanding one wing of the army in the factional conflict between Sayf ol-Dowleh and the Shafti governor. The devastation of the city.
5. **The patron falls** — after Mohammad Shah's succession, the political landscape shifts. Daoud Khan "disappears from the sources." The precarity of military patronage.
6. **The portrait** — the Sartip in Qajar uniform, Persian caption. The book-page scan in the vault.
7. **Two daughters** — Anna marries Burgess, Tamar marries Cormick. The father's military world produces a family embedded in Persian court life for three more generations.
