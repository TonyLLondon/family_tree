# The Cormick Doctors: Father and Son at the Qajar Court

## Pitch

**John Cormick** (d. 1833) came to Persia as physician to the Shah and married an Armenian woman. His son **William Cormick** (1822–1877), born in Tabriz, trained in London, returned as royal physician to Mohammad Shah and Crown Prince Naser al-Din Shah, and in 1848 became one of the few Western eyewitnesses to the **trial of the Báb** — the founding event of the Bahá'í Faith. William received the Lion and Sun decoration in 1875 and died in Tabriz in 1877. He married Tamar Saginian, connecting the Cormick line to the Saginian–Burgess–Bottin–Stump trunk.

The father-and-son arc spans Irish Catholic origins, Qajar court patronage, Armenian intermarriage, vaccine science, and a front-row seat to a world-historical religious event — material that the existing Persia stories only glance at.

## Proposed sections (scrolly beats)

1. **Ireland to Persia** — John Cormick arrives at the Qajar court, the IMS/AMD pathway for Irish Catholic physicians.
2. **Abbas Mirza's physician** — John treating the crown prince, the smallpox treatise (*Resāla-ye Taʿlīm*), vaccination in Persia.
3. **Tabriz and the Armenian marriage** — John marries an Armenian woman; William born in Tabriz c. 1822.
4. **London training** — William studies medicine in London, returns to Persia with European credentials.
5. **Royal physician** — William as physician to Mohammad Shah and then to crown prince Naser al-Din.
6. **The trial of the Báb, 1848** — Tabriz, the examination of the Báb before the religious authorities, William as eyewitness; his account preserved in Browne's *Materials*.
7. **Edward Burgess's deathbed** — William rides eight days from Tabriz to meet Anna and Edward, attends Edward through his final illness (1855).
8. **Lion and Sun** — the 1875 decoration, the *Medical Times and Gazette* obituary (1878), death in Tabriz 1877.
