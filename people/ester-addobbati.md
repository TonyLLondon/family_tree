# Ester Addobbati

Ester was born into one of the old **Addobbati** households of Italian Dalmatia—the pen-and-ink respectability of the Habsburg post, parish weddings at the **Basilica Metropolitana**, and a family habit of working for a living when the "better" families thought they should not. She married the younger **Antonio Zerauschek**, who would become the public face of interwar Zara: hotels at the centre of the city, cigarette papers and chocolate factories at its industrial edge, a life measured in employment and skylines. Her name is on **Villa Ester** at Sirmione, the refuge on Lake Garda that her husband bought and named for her—a small, stubborn poetry against exile.

She was mother to [Fulvia Ottilia Antonia Zerauschek](fulvia-ottilia-antonia-zerauschek.md) and the other children of that marriage. She died in **Florence** on **13 September 1971**, four days after being admitted to the **Arcispedale di Santa Maria Nuova**, in a private room under **Prof. Tonelli** (Ward 16). Her husband's obituary in *Difesa Adriatica* (1973), speaking from the Italian exile community, treats her loss as the wound that never closed: Antonio, it suggests, folded something essential when she left him.

The Zaratini community mourned her in their own way. In *ZARA* (A.N.D.A.Z., January 1972), former Zerauschek employees published a collective tribute: they remembered "questa figura di donna, madre e sposa esemplare, intelligente e buona, spoglia di qualsiasi frivolezza e superbia" — this figure of an exemplary woman, mother and wife, intelligent and good, free of any frivolity or arrogance — who with her husband had offered many Zaratini work and serenity, rising "al primo posto nell'attività industriale zaratina." Even as a shy woman, they wrote, her noble and generous heart showed through.

## Life

Ester spent her first years in **kingdom-era Zara** (now Zadar, Croatia), anchored to [Pietro Pio Addobbati](pietro-pio-addobbati.md) and [Ottilia Anna Vincenza Boara](ottilia-anna-vincenza-boara.md). On **30 October 1920** she married [Antonio Zerauschek](antonio-zerauschek.md) in the same civic world her parents had documented in ink and wax. The remainder of her life tracks the family's move from Dalmatian grandeur to Italian refuge.

In Zara she was the family's **anchor correspondent**: Antonio's letters to their daughters explain that he rarely wrote because Ester covered everything — she wrote in his presence, read the children's letters aloud at the dinner table, and relayed all news so comprehensively that "non mi resta nulla, sai è proprio un egoista" (Mar 1936). By **November 1940** she was active with the **San Vincenzo de' Paoli** charity in Zara. During the war she planned the logistics of collecting the children ("i merli e la merla") from their various boarding schools for the holidays.

In **December 1949** the **Ministero dell'Africa Italiana** issued her a *lasciapassare* (No. 20749, 22 December 1949) to travel to **Tripoli, Libya** — joining her daughter [Fulvia](fulvia-ottilia-antonia-zerauschek.md) and son-in-law [David John Lewis](david-john-lewis.md) during his service in the **British Administration of Tripolitania**. She held Italian passport No. **3137475/P** (issued 20 June 1964), in the name **Addobbati Ester in Zerauschek**.

Her last days were in **Florence**: admitted to the **Arcispedale di Santa Maria Nuova** on **9 September 1971**, she died on **13 September 1971**. Her husband survived her by eighteen months, dying on 28 February 1973. They lie together in the **Zerauschek tomb** at the **Cimitero delle Porte Sante** beside the basilica of **San Miniato al Monte**, overlooking Florence.
