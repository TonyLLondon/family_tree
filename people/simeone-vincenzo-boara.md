# Simeone Vincenzo Boara (Šimun)

Simeone Vincenzo Boara—known in Croatian as **Šimun**—was the husband of [Vittoria Elisa Carobbi](vittoria-elisa-carobbi.md) and father of [Ottilia Anna Vincenza Boara](ottilia-anna-vincenza-boara.md). They married on 8 August 1849 at Sv. Šime, Zara. Geni URLs use **Šimun Boara**. The structured tree lists him as *assessore comunale* (municipal councillor) in Zara in 1855 and 1891 (**@S12@**).

**Data hygiene:** The export’s **F11** note names Lucia Boara (born Bozich) while the wife pointer is [Vittoria Elisa Carobbi](vittoria-elisa-carobbi.md)—likely a first-wife remnant. **F204** repeats Simeone with spouse I558 and the same 1849 date; treat **F11** as the union that produces Ottilia (I21) and Artemisia (I62) in the graph.

## Context

The Boara were Italian-speaking **landowners and intellectuals** in Zara, part of the interlocking network of civic families — alongside [Carobbi](casimiro-carobbi.md), [Addobbati](pietro-pio-addobbati.md), Coltelli, Sandri — that formed the Italian urban establishment across centuries of Venetian, Napoleonic, and Austrian rule. Fulvia's memoir describes them as "very much Italians."

Simeone's role as **assessore comunale** (municipal councillor) placed him on Zara's city council alongside the podestà (mayor). In Habsburg Dalmatia, the assessore was an elected or appointed councillor who helped govern the municipality — comparable to an alderman or town councillor. Simeone held this position across a span of at least 36 years (documented in **1855** and **1891**), spanning the era when Italian was still the language of municipal administration in Zara. Nicolò Trigari, who served as assessore di Zara in the same period, later rose to podestà (1874–1899), illustrating the civic trajectory of Italian-speaking notables.

The 1890 Austrian census counted **7,672 Italians in Zadar** — 27% of the population — and Italian civic families dominated the municipal government until the Austrian authorities began suppressing Italian administrative language (Italian lost official status in Dalmatia in **1909**). Simeone lived through the entire arc: Venetian cultural inheritance, the post-1866 irredentist awakening triggered when Italy gained Veneto but not Dalmatia, and the rising Slavization pressure of the 1880s–90s.

The Boara family's pro-Italian convictions ran deep. Three of Simeone and Vittoria's unmarried sons left their entire inheritances to the **Lega Nazionale**, an Italian cultural-irredentist association founded in **1891** in Trieste by Carlo Seppenhofer. The Lega operated across Habsburg provinces with Italian populations — Dalmatia, Istria, Trieste, Friuli, Trentino — establishing **private Italian-language schools** where Austrian authorities had closed public ones, and running Italian cultural circles. In Zara the Lega founded the **Collegio-Convitto "Niccolò Tommaseo"**, a boarding school that became the central institution for Italian youth in Dalmatia. Luigi Ziliotto led the Zara section from 1896–1898.

The marriage church, **Sv. Šime** (Church of Saint Simeon), is one of Zadar's most prominent churches, famous for housing the silver sarcophagus of Saint Simeon the Prophet, commissioned by Queen Elizabeth of Hungary in 1380. Its parish registers are held at the **Zadar State Archives (DAZD)**, which has been digitizing registers covering 1564–1937.
