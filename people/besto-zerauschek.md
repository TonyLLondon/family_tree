# Roberto Zerauschek (Zio Besto)

His baptismal name was **Roberto**; the family knew him as **Zio Besto** or **Berto**. He went to sea in the **merchant navy** and washed up in **South Africa**, where he married **Lily** — an English-speaking Cape Town woman who did sewing and dress-making for customers. He named their first home **"Villa Zara"** on Royal Road, Maitland — the old city stamped on the new continent. He talked [Antonio](antonio-zerauschek.md) twice into considering **investment**; Antonio held back. Roberto built a **furniture workshop** in Cape Town and hated almost every hour of it: **homesick**, hemmed in, then caged as an **enemy alien** in the Second World War, "in agony… without news." The memoir says the couple was childless, though Lily's 1961 letter mentions "our Michael" attending St George's Grammar School in Cape Town — possibly a foster child, nephew, or child from a previous relationship.

By 1954 they had moved to **"Wynnton"**, Selous Road, Claremont, C.P. Roberto's health was never easy: blood poisoning at Christmas 1953, three fingers cut in his workshop on 5 January 1954, another operation in February 1960 that left him unable to work for months. In his letters to Fulvia he wrote in Italian — cramped, sloping, deeply emotional — about family news, money worries, and the ache of distance. Lily wrote in English, typed, warm and practical, always signing off as "Auntie Lily."

He was taken ill suddenly on **20 April 1964**, fitting and well until then, making plans to visit Italy. After a week at home in bed he suffered a **severe stroke** and was taken to hospital. He never regained consciousness and died in the early hours of **Tuesday 5 May 1964** at 1:15 a.m. Lily cabled Antonio in Italy. After almost 28 years of marriage, she wrote to Fulvia, she still didn't know much Italian.

His **will** favoured someone called **Tina** (perhaps **Jureme**) over Lily; Fulvia thought that illegal, and the families **split** (1996). The Tina letter annotation (28 Jul 1965) places Lily Zerauschek at **Fermo near Verona** at the time of the dispute. An extensive correspondence about the will survives in the R&L Zerauschek / Will folder, including letters from solicitor **H. Broadhead** and from Fulvia writing from Verona in August 1965.

After Roberto's death, Lily remained in Cape Town. By the mid-1970s she was living alone with her dogs at **Gordon Road, Belmont**. A Cape Town newspaper clipping — headline "High winds rip roofs in city" — interviewed **"Mrs Lily Zerauschek, 74, of Belmont, Gordon Road"** after a mini-tornado ripped tiles off her roof: she had "frantically phoned her sister." The photograph shows her on the wrecked veranda with her dogs.
