# Daoud Khan Saginian ("Mayor Khan")

Georgian-born military commander who fled Russian-controlled Georgia in **1811**, joined the Qajar army, integrated fully into the Armenian community in Iran, and rose to the rank of **Sartip (Brigadier General)**, commanding Qajar forces in the provinces of **Shiraz and Isfahan** under Fath Ali Shah and, from **April 1845**, placed in charge of the **Nestorian Christians of Urumia** at the request of the British and Russian consuls in Tabriz. Patrilineal anchor for both [Anna Saginian](anna-saginian.md) and [Tamar Saginian](tamar-saginian.md). The family sits in the **military-administrative elite**, not the commercial periphery of Qajar Iran.

## Early life and Russian service

David Saginean — known in Iran as Daoud Khan Saginian — was born c. **1790** in the Tbilisi area of Georgia (Yaghoubian 2014, n. 5; year only, no day or month is recorded in the vault). The Georgian form of the surname was **Saginskilli**, also rendered *Saginean* in Armenian sources and related to the Georgian noble family **Saginashvili**. After the dissolution of the Georgian kingdom in 1801 he entered the **Russian army** as an officer, the service Joseph Wolff still tied to him when Wolff met him in **Tabriz in 1845**: *"Daoud Khan, a Colonel in the Russian service... a genuine Georgian"* ([corpus extract](../sources/corpus/narrative-mission-bokhara/extracted.pdf.md), Ch. XXVI).

## Flight to Iran (1811)

In **early 1811** David and his brother **Zaal Saginean**, together with their companion **Solayman Khan Saham al-Dowleh** (an Armenian-Georgian nobleman), fled Russian-controlled Georgia. Maeda's 2019 Brill chapter, drawing on Galust Shermazanian's nineteenth-century Armenian history of distinguished Armenians in Persia, gives the immediate context: fear of forced relocation to Russia and forced religious conversion, set against the practical abolition of the Georgian Church and the exile of the Catholicos Anton II. Solomon and David obtained leave from their Russian commander on a pretext — the officer thought they wished to take part in **wrestling** — and travelled via **Yerevan, Akhalkalaki, and Gyumri** to **Tabriz**, where no one had been warned of their arrival. Maeda himself notes that the rumour and its influence on the brothers' decision is difficult to confirm independently of Shermazanian ([Maeda corpus reference](../sources/corpus/maeda-2019-enikolopians-saginian-flight/reference.md) · [snippets](../sources/corpus/maeda-2019-enikolopians-saginian-flight/transcription.snippets.md)).

In Tabriz the brothers entered the orbit of Crown Prince **Abbas Mirza**'s military reforms. Shermazanian, again via Maeda (note 71), supplies the religious pivot that defines the family thereafter: "they embraced the **Grigorian faith** after their arrival in Tabriz and their children became Armenians." This is the bridge from Georgian **Saginskilli/Saginean** to Armenian-Iranian **Saginian** — a change of communal allegiance rather than of bloodline, and the reason later observers could describe the same man as Georgian by origin and Armenian by community in the same breath.

## Qajar military career

Daoud Khan rose to the rank of **Sartip** (سرتیپ) — Brigadier General, the senior non-royal military grade in the Qajar army. He served under **Fath-Ali Shah Qajar** (r. 1797–1834) and commanded troops under **Sayf ol-Dowleh**, who in the **1834 Isfahan conflict** divided his army and placed one division under "David (Daoud) Saginian"; Armenian military literature names **Davit Khan Saginian** as head of the Isfahan army in the same episode. Yaghoubian (2014) records his formal titles as **farmandeh** (supreme military commander) of Shiraz and Isfahan and **sarperast-e Aramane** (guardian of the Armenians). The Persian caption beneath his portrait in Esmail Ra'in's *Iraniyan-i Armani* preserves the same memory: *"Sartip Daoud Khan Sakinian, who during the reign of Fath Ali Shah was commander of the Iranian forces in the provinces of Shiraz and Isfahan, and died at the age of 78"* ([Photograph source card](../sources/sartip-daoud-khan-sakinian-photograph.md)).

## Wolff's dual identification (1845)

Dr. Joseph Wolff's *Narrative of a Mission to Bokhara* (1845) places him in **Tabriz across 1845**, not only in an earlier year: Edward Burgess's letter to Wolff is dated **8 February 1845**; Mar Yohannan, Bishop of Oroomiah, wrote Wolff from **Oroomiah, 27 March 1845**; Wolff **left Tabriz on 9 December 1845** ([corpus extract](../sources/corpus/narrative-mission-bokhara/extracted.pdf.md), Ch. XXVI). In that chapter Wolff records two labels for the same man that look contradictory: *"Daoud Khan, a Colonel in the Russian service... a genuine Georgian"* (and *"not very fond of the Armenians"*); later, in the party seeing Wolff off, *"**Daoud Khan the Armenian**, all the Russian Attachés and the Greek merchants."* The tension resolves once the Maeda/Shermazanian account is admitted: Wolff is recording **origin** in the first line and **communal affiliation** in the second.

## In charge of the Nestorians of Urumia (April 1845)

**In the same calendar year** as Wolff's Tabriz sojourn (February–December 1845 in the printed narrative), Daoud Khan was given a brief that took him out of the Shiraz and Isfahan world entirely. Vicky Uffindell's *The Cormick family in Persia* (vol. 2) records that "**Tamar's father, Daoud Khan**… following a request by the **British and Russian consuls in Tabriz**, had been placed **in charge of the Nestorian Christians of Urumia in April 1845**" — the Assyrian/Church-of-the-East communities of the Urmia plateau in north-western Iran, geographically distinct from his earlier provincial commands further south. The American Board historian **Rufus Anderson** (1872), drawing on mission archives, records the same event one rung higher in the diplomatic chain — shah, **English and Russian Ambassadors**, appointment of **Dawood Khan of Tabriz** as **Governor of the Nestorians** in **May 1845**, with stated purpose to shield Christians from **Muslim nobles** in the district; verbatim extract and Gutenberg pointer in [corpus `reference.md`](../sources/corpus/history-of-the-mission-by-rufus-anderson-5264293694/reference.md). Anderson's phrase *"Armenian from Georgia"* is a contemporary, non-family confirmation of exactly the dual identity Wolff had recorded in 1843, and which Yaghoubian's family-line summary makes structural. In the same volume Anderson later calls Dawood Khan the **"Christian Governor and civil protector of the Nestorians of that province"** — caught between **patriarch and Jesuits** (who wished to remove him) and **Muslim nobles** sympathetic to that removal because he blocked **their exactions**; second verbatim block in the same [corpus reference](../sources/corpus/history-of-the-mission-by-rufus-anderson-5264293694/reference.md#transcription-christian-governor-and-civil-protector-later-1840s). The Urumia posting also explains Daoud Khan's later association with the Rev. **William Stocking**, the American Presbyterian missionary based at Urumia and serving the Nestorian villages: when Stocking officiated at the 1850 marriage of his daughter **Tamar Daoudian** to William Cormick at the British consulate in Tabriz, the two men had a five-year working acquaintance ([Uffindell vol. 2 / family-friends-1850s — corpus](../sources/corpus/uffindell-cormick-persia-v2-family-friends-1850s/extracted.pdf.md); [Anderson source card](../sources/rufus-anderson-1872-history-missions-oriental-churches.md)).

## Family

His brother **Zaal Saginskilli** (also *Zaal Saginean*) fled Georgia with him in 1811; nothing is recorded in the vault about Zaal's later career in Iran. All scholarly sources — Yaghoubian (2014), Maeda (2019), and the Wikipedia article on Solayman Khan Saham al-Dowleh — agree that David and Zal were **brothers**; Armen Saginian's 2022 family memoir incorrectly recasts them as father and sons.

Anna's 1880 interview, the only first-hand statement of the family structure, says she was the **eldest of four sisters** with **two brothers** who were merchants in Tabriz and predeceased her. Yaghoubian (2014), drawing on oral interviews with the descendant Sevak Saginian, adds **Goorgen Khan** as the **oldest son** — a *sarhang* (colonel) in the Qajar army who died in his forties (so probably in the 1860s, before his father). The two accounts together give at least seven children: Goorgen Khan; **[Anna](anna-saginian.md)**, who married [Edward Burgess](edward-burgess.md) in 1851; an unnamed sister who became the widow of the Tehran merchant Aga Pedros and was raising three sons there in 1880; a fourth, unnamed sister; **[Tamar](tamar-saginian.md)**, the youngest, who married [Dr. William Cormick](william-cormick.md); and the two unnamed merchant brothers. Anna does not mention Goorgen Khan by name — most likely because he had already died by 1880 and her narrative kept to the sisters and the two brothers she had known longest.

Anna's mother — Daoud Khan's wife — is not named in any source in the vault. Anna says only that her *"dear mother died some time before"* her father, which means he was **widowed** in his last years; no second marriage is recorded.

## Death and burial

Two evidential chains give different years and need to be held together. Uffindell's *Cormick family in Persia* (vol. 2), drawing on the **NYPL Burgess Family Papers** transcript of Anna Burgess's interview, recounts the actual circumstances: Daoud Khan "had been **dangerously ill** when Anna Burgess arrived in Tabriz with her daughter Fanny… on their way to live in Urumia where Fanny was to be taught English with the children of the American missionaries there. Anna nursed [her father] for several months, then as his health improved went on with Fanny to Urumia. However they had been there only a **few weeks** when Daoud Khan died and was buried in the Armenian cemetery." Uffindell sequences this account between **Shireen Cormick's death on 28 December 1864** and **the birth of James Ernest Cormick on 25 January 1865**, which places Daoud Khan's death in **late 1864 or very early 1865** ([Uffindell vol. 2 / epidemics-tabriz-eastwick — corpus](../sources/corpus/uffindell-cormick-persia-v2-epidemics-tabriz-eastwick/extracted.pdf.md), p. 3). The standing scholarly date is **1867** in **Tabriz**, from Yaghoubian (2014, note 10), working from later oral interviews with the great-great-great-grandson Sevak Saginian: *"David Khan Saginian died in 1867 and is buried in the Saginian mausoleum and church in Tabriz known as Surb Astvatsatsin."* The Persian portrait caption in Ra'in records his death age as **78**, which against a birth year of c. 1790 implies a death around **1868**. The two-to-three year gap between Uffindell (Anna's family chain, late 1864/early 1865) and Yaghoubian (Sevak's family chain, 1867) is unresolved on present evidence; Uffindell is closer to first-hand testimony for the death itself, Yaghoubian closer to the burial-and-mausoleum record. A primary dated record — burial register, consular despatch, or contemporary newspaper — would settle it.

Daoud Khan was buried in the **Saginian family mausoleum at Surb Astvatsatsin** (Holy Mother of God church) outside the walls of Tabriz, the principal Armenian cemetery serving the city. His grandson **Solayman Khan Saginian** (d. 1913) was buried in the same complex (Yaghoubian 2014, note 11), and Armen Saginian's autobiography refers to it under its English name "Mary Mother of God." Anna's 1880 interview describes the proximity in human terms: *"My dear father's grave is four or five English yards from [Edward's grave]; it is among the graves of all my relations. And old Mr. & Mrs. Cormick and the Europeans are all buried there. The trees are very beautiful."*

## Armen Saginian's account (2022)

Armen Saginian's autobiography *Thank You, America & Americans* (Xlibris, 2022) — written by a great-great-grandson via the Gorgin Khan line — contains chapters "Who Was Dawood Khan Saginashvilli," "Who Was Gorgin Khan," and "Who Was Colonel Solomon Khan?" with family photos. Armen describes Daoud Khan as a Brigadier General in Georgian Artillery and adjutant to King Heraclius II, from the noble Saginashvilli family. He incorrectly structures the 1811 emigration as a father (David Khan) with two sons (Gorgin Khan, Zali Khan); scholarly sources and Anna's interview establish David and Zal as **brothers**. Armen's account places Tamara Khanum (who married Dr. Cormick) as Gorgin Khan's daughter (Daoud Khan's granddaughter) — conflicting with Anna's direct testimony that Tamar was her **sister** (both daughters of Daoud Khan). Armen's lineage: David Khan → Gorgin Khan → Solomon Khan → Hovsep → Armen. Published by Xlibris, 2022.

## Yaghoubian (2014) — scholarly confirmation

David Yaghoubian's *Ethnicity, Identity, and the Development of Nationalism in Iran* (Syracuse University Press, 2014), Chapter 4, based on oral interviews with **Sevak Saginian** (Zohrab's son), confirms: born Tbilisi 1790; died Tabriz 1867; buried in Saginian family mausoleum at **Surb Astvatsatsin** (Holy Mother of God church); David and Zal were brothers. Gorgin Khan (sarhang/colonel) was David Khan's oldest son; died in his forties. David Khan held titles **farmandeh** (supreme military commander) of Shiraz and Isfahan and **sarperast-e Aramane** (guardian of the Armenians). Source card: [yaghoubian-2014-ethnicity-identity-nationalism-iran.md](../sources/yaghoubian-2014-ethnicity-identity-nationalism-iran.md).

## Open questions

- Fate of brother **Zaal Saginskilli** (Zaal Saginean) after 1811 arrival in Iran.
- Reconciling Anna's testimony (Tamar = sister, daughter of Daoud Khan) with Armen's account (Tamara = Gorgin Khan's daughter). Anna's first-hand 1880 testimony should take priority.
- Whether Anna is Daoud Khan's daughter directly (as she states) or possibly Gorgin Khan's daughter/sister with a generation collapsed in oral transmission.
- Resolving the **death-year discrepancy**: Uffindell (NYPL Burgess Papers, via Anna) places it in **late 1864 or very early 1865** between Shireen Cormick's death (28 Dec 1864) and James Ernest Cormick's birth (25 Jan 1865); Yaghoubian (Sevak Saginian oral testimony) gives **1867**; the portrait caption's **age 78** against birth **c. 1790** implies **~1868**. A primary dated record would settle it.
