# Fulvia Ottilia Antonia Zerauschek

Fulvia is the hinge where the Adriatic line meets the Welsh one: the only child of [Antonio Zerauschek](antonio-zerauschek.md) and [Ester Addobbati](ester-addobbati.md) who carries this vault forward into [David John Lewis](david-john-lewis.md) and their English descendants. She was born in **Zara** in **1923**, when the city still wore the Italian kingdom's flag and her father's name was on cigarette papers and café awnings. A young woman on the **loggia of Villa Ester** at Sirmione—marble, cypress, lake light—survives in the family photograph; the villa would pass out of the family and into the story of **Maria Callas**, but Fulvia's smile on that terrace is the human-sized memory.

She died at **Hove** in **Sussex** in **2000** and was laid to rest at **Sirmione**, beside the Garda shore, not far from the worlds she had left and kept returning.

## Life

Antonio's letters to her — fifty-one items spanning 1936 to 1972 — paint the sharpest portrait: he called her **"Fulle"**, scolded her constantly for terrible school marks ("note disastrose"), and threatened to pull her from college and put her to work in "one of my businesses." She wanted to study the **violin** (Dec 1936); her father thought she should pass her **ginnasio** exams first. By late 1940, in wartime **Genova**, the air-raid alarms amused her more than they frightened her. Antonio wrote: "attenta però che qualche allarme non ti faccia prendere lo spauracchio." By **February 1943**, with Italy disintegrating, she was at the **Albergo Palazzo** in **San Pellegrino Terme** (Bergamo province) — her father warned her not to risk traveling to **Milan**. After the **armistice** (8 Sep 1943) the family retreated from Zara to **Sirmione** (Villa Ester); Antonio wrote from there congratulating her on exam results (14 Oct 1943).

In **October 1947**, Fulvia married **[David John Lewis](david-john-lewis.md)** at Sirmione. A customs declaration for the *corredo di nozze* (wedding trousseau), stamped by the Banca d'Italia at Brescia on **15 October 1947**, names her as **"Sig. Fulvia Zerauschek sposata al Sig. David J. Lewis"** with a marriage certificate attached. The four parcels shipped to England encompassed everything a young Italian bride needed to set up house in Wales: a black fur coat, a silver fox jacket, three silk dresses, a tailleur, four pairs of shoes, **two rackets**, five books, eight pairs of gloves, Italian silver and tea service, a **Marelli Fido** radio, carpets, and woollen mattresses. David signed the English-language declaration at Sirmione on 16 October — giving his address as **32 Pendarren Street, Aberdare, South Wales** — and had it notarised at the **British Consulate General, Milan** the next day.

She and [Giuliana ("Liana")](giuliana-zerauschek-rivolta.md) were at **boarding school in Genova** together from at least 1936, when Fulvia was twelve. Her brothers **Mario** and **Guido** were at other boarding schools; Mario teased her about her grades, running to their mother and shouting "ecco i cinque della brava" (Jan 1939). A **Northern Rhodesia Telegraphs** form (16 Jul 1953) shows Antonio sending congratulations to Fulvia at **Jameson**, confirming she was with David in what is now Zambia.

She played **competitive tennis** — a newspaper clipping from the *Central African Post* (25 May 1963) records "Lewis" in the women's doubles results at the **Lusaka Club** tennis tournament. Antonio chided her for it, saying she should spend less time on the court and more attention on Valerie's homework. David called her **"Fa"** in his letters, while Carlo Enrico Rivolta addressed her as **"zilla Pù"** — the family had no shortage of pet names for her.

In the **1970s and 1980s**, Fulvia was deeply involved in David's work at the **Keep Britain Tidy Group**. Her contribution was recognised with a **trophy named in her honour**: the **"Fulvia Lewis Keep Britain Tidy Trophy"**, presented at the **Beautiful Britain in Bloom** awards at the **Café Royal** in October 1983 by **Princess Michael of Kent**, with both the *Daily Telegraph* and *The Times* reporting the event. In **January 1995** she was enrolled as a member of the **National Geographic Society** — "Mrs. F. Lewis."

The courtship between Fulvia and David can be traced through postcards preserved in the family archive. In **July 1945**, David — still on active service — sent a postcard of **Villa Donnagemma, Sirmione** to his brother Keith at 32 Pendarren Street, Aberdare, from a **Field Post Office**. By **July 1946**, David was writing postcards to Fulvia — addressing her at **"Sig. Fulvia Zerauschek, Presso Cazzaniga, Via Fieschi 3/6, Genova"** — expressing regret about leaving Sirmione. A September 1946 photograph shows Fulvia at **Villa Guarienti, San Vigilio, Lake Garda**, taken by **Count Belgioioso**. By **March 1947**, Fulvia was sending postcards to David at **32 Pendarren St, Aberdare, S. Wales**. A note on the back of a 1946 group photograph at **Villa Ester** mentions a "particular visa in Dover" — Fulvia exploring the possibility of travelling to England. By October 1947 they were married.

Fulvia was the family's memory-keeper. In **September 1996** she dictated a **seven-page family history** to her son [Peter Martin Lewis](peter-martin-lewis.md), covering Addobbati origins (Bergamo to Zara, 1493), Boara landowners, Zerauschek genealogy, her father Antonio's business empire, his siblings' scattered fates, the 1943 armistice, exile, and the failed INCAS chocolate venture in Florence. In **January 2000** she added a handwritten note on the reliability of Sabalich's *Zara Archeologica* (1897) as a source for Addobbati dates, observing that the dates in that work refer to nominations and honours rather than births. She also drew at least **two large handwritten family trees** (NTree01 covering the Boara–Addobbati side; NTree02 covering the Zerauschek–Herenda side) which remain the most complete visual record of both family branches with married surnames, children's names, and approximate dates.

## The lace collection

Fulvia owned one of the most important antique lace collections in Europe — over 600 pieces spanning the 16th to 20th centuries. The collection had been partly auctioned at Sotheby's in London. After her death in 2000, her sons **Owen Michael Lewis** (Michele, the lawyer in Twickenham) and **[Ivor Anthony Samuel Lewis](ivor-anthony-samuel-lewis.md)** (the professor in Bolsena) inherited the collection and in December 2006 sold it in two lots to the **Provincia autonoma di Trento** for €120,000 total, destined for the planned **Museo del Tessuto** (Textile Museum) at **Palazzo Taddei, Ala**. The Soprintendenza's director **Laura Dal Prà** described the pieces as "rare, of considerable size, and from Europe's finest manufacturers," with a market value far exceeding the purchase price. The appraisers were **Paolo Peri** and **Doretta Davanzo Poli**. Two articles in *L'Adige* (Trento, 28 Dec 2006) covered the purchase — see [scan 1 (delibere)](../media/docs/ladige-2006-lewis-lace-trento-delibere.png), [scan 2 (acquisto)](../media/docs/ladige-2006-lewis-lace-trento-acquisto.png), and [transcription + translation](../sources/corpus/ladige-2006-lewis-lace-collection-trento/translation-ladige-lewis-lace-2006.en.md).
