# Edward Burgess (Berjīs Ṣāḥeb · برجیس صاحب)

British merchant, translator, and government official who spent his entire adult life — twenty-five years — in Qajar Persia. Known in Persian as **Berjīs Ṣāḥeb** (برجیس صاحب), Edward is recognised in Iranian press history as a **founding editor of Iran's first newspaper**, the *Ruzname-ye Vaqaye-e Ettefaqiye* (روزنامه وقایع اتفاقیه, est. 1851), as **Iran's first press censor** (نخستین سانسورچی مطبوعات), and as the **first publisher of a confidential intelligence bulletin** (نخستین ناشر بولتن محرمانه) in the country. Beginning as his elder brother Charles's commercial partner, he was left stranded in Tabriz as a hostage for Charles's debts from 1836, and reinvented himself through linguistic talent and personal charm: village governor, consular agent, translator for Prince Bahman Mirza (including the *Joghrafiya-yi Alam* — "Geography of the World" — presented to Mohammad Shah in 1846), chief interpreter to Nasir-i-Din Shah, and finally **overseer (مباشر) and translator of the state gazette**. His widow [Anna](anna-saginian.md) could still say, twenty-five years after his death, that *"he was the most all-loved person"* she had ever known. He anchors the **Burgess → Fanny → Bottin → Stump** line that ties Tehran to the European engineer and dentist generations.

Some Iranian sources — notably Naser Najmi in *Vaziran-e Maqtul* ("Murdered Ministers") — also attribute to Edward the establishment of **Iran's first modern printing house** in Tabriz under Abbas Mirza's patronage. The SHARP journal (2023) treats this claim cautiously: *"some sources mention a European named Edward Burgess, said to have been hired by Abbas Mirza to establish a modern printing house and to teach printing. However, not much is known about Burgess' actual activities."* The chronology is plausible — Abbas Mirza died in 1833 and Edward arrived in Tabriz in 1831 — but the claim may conflate his later printing work for Bahman Mirza with the earlier Tabriz press enterprise, which is usually attributed to Mirza Zayn al-Abidin Tabrizi (sent to St Petersburg by Abbas Mirza c. 1818).

**No photograph or portrait of Edward is known to exist.** He died in June 1855, before cartes-de-visite became common. The [NYPL Burgess family papers](../sources/corpus/nypl-archives-mss-431-curl-mirror/) (Series VII, Box 9) hold ~70 unsigned watercolours and sketches by family members, plus "snapshots of unidentified individuals" — these have not been examined in person and represent the only plausible path to a visual likeness.

The principal source for Edward's life is the **Burgess Persian Letters** — a 130-page collection of family correspondence (1827–1855) edited by Benjamin Schwartz and published in the *NYPL Bulletin* (1940–42), culminating in an interview with Anna recorded in Leicester, March 1880. Edward's own letters, vivid and humane, form approximately two-thirds of the collection.

## Life

### Early years and arrival in Persia (1810–1831)

- **1810** — Born in England, son of [Henry Burgess](henry-burgess.md) and [Frances Ridsdale](frances-ridsdale.md). Second surviving son; elder brother **Charles Henry** had gone to Persia in 1827.
- **Late 1830** — Departed England with Charles. Travelled via Constantinople and the Black Sea to Trebizond, then overland to Erzeroum. His first surviving letter (postscript, **17 Jan 1831**) describes Trebizond's plague aftermath (3,000 dead, 400 in their own caravanserai), the brutal Russian displacement of 50,000 Armenians from Erzeroum, and the room he shared with Charles — three one-foot windows, oiled-paper substitutes for glass, pistols at hand on the carpet.
- **Jan 1831** — Arrived in **Tabriz**. Edward was roughly twenty years old and would never return to England.

### Merchant years and the trap of Charles's debts (1831–1840)

- **1831–35** — Worked as Charles's partner, selling English manufactured goods (chintzes, blankets, cloth) in Tabriz while Charles operated between Tehran, Ghilan, Constantinople, and London. Edward sold goods on commission, managed debtors, and dispatched gold and silk remittances to England via tartar couriers. He was critical of Charles's commercial judgement: *"Charles appears to me to have taken any goods he could lay his hands on without any regard as to whether these were suited to the Persian taste."*
- **1833 Feb** — Settled into Tabriz life. Dined with **Dr Cormick** three times a week, played flute duets in the evenings, went shooting with Russian mission officers. Spoke French and Turkish; learning Persian. Described the country between Tabriz and Tehran as "rocky mountains and barren plains."
- **1835** — Charles returned to England. Edward wrote of wanting to separate from the partnership since 1833 but Charles refused.
- **1836** — Charles's ventures collapsed. Edward was left in Persia as a de facto **hostage for Charles's debts** to Persian creditors and the government (principally for unpaid muskets). He described his position: *"I am made responsible for his debts in this country and am nailed down to Tabreez."* He could not leave without settling with creditors, and Charles sent neither money nor letters to the Shah to ameliorate Edward's position.
- **1837 Oct** — Sent two rare Persian partridges (*"vor keklik"*) to the London Zoological Society via brother George, with a detailed naturalist's letter describing their habitat and habits.
- **1838 Jan** — Described hawking with Prince Bahman Mirza's hawks and hog-hunting with Kurds. Wrote extensively about the Shah's failed siege of Herat and its commercial effects on the Tabriz market.
- **1839 Aug** — Witnessed disturbances in Tabriz; defended Armenian friends. Described a gruelling journey to Ahar — sleeping in stables with fleas, chickens, and a wounded sheep; riding through thunderstorms at night.
- **1840 Aug** — At Gulja near Isfahan. Faced the risk of imprisonment if the Shah grew angry about Charles's unpaid debts: *"one angry word from the Shah or his minister and I have the prospect of a prison before me."*

### Consular agent, village governor, translator (1841–1848)

- **Nov 1841** — The Anglo-Persian **Commercial Treaty** was signed. Edward noted he could have obtained the consul position at Tehran *"had it not been for my embarrassments on Charles' acct."* His knowledge of Persian and Turkish — particularly the latter, *"known to so few Europeans"* — was his chief asset.
- **Jun 1842** — **Bonham** (British consul at Tabriz) departed for England and left Edward **in charge of consular business** at a salary of **400 tomauns/year (~£180)**. Edward copied dispatches in the Foreign Office's required "large plain legible hand," packed missionary parcels into Her Majesty's dispatch bags, and managed government messengers.
- **1842–43** — Letters rich with geopolitical commentary: the Afghan massacres, Russian seizure of Caspian islands, slave trade on the eastern frontier, Turkish border disputes. Proposed Persian–American trade in nut galls, silk, and turquoises to George in New York.
- **Jan 1844** — Appointed **chief magistrate of two villages** (~1,000 inhabitants) belonging to the late Dr Cormick's children, under **Prince Bahman Mirza**. Dispensed justice, settled land disputes, punished highway robbery (100 blows on the soles of the feet), managed cotton cultivation. Described the villagers as *"much such fellows as we had on the borders of Scotland two hundred years ago, most of them armed, many of them robbers."* A village elder told him: *"you speak up, you speak manfully and you speak well, and you would fight with any nobleman in the land rather than not see your people get justice."*
- **May 1844** — Entered the **service of Prince Bahman Mirza** as newspaper translator at **400 tomauns/year**, though ordered to keep the appointment secret from the hostile Prime Minister. Nestorian Christian villages near Urumia petitioned to be placed under his governance, offering £1,000/year — urged by the American missionaries who said *"by doing so I shall deserve the blessings of all Christendom for protecting a number of suffering Christians."*
- **1844–45** — Wrote detailed letters about nightingales and roses, Persian alchemy, village elections, Kurdish frontier management, Dr Wolff's return from Bokhara (Edward slept in Wolff's room for two nights because the old man was too nervous to be alone). Managed Mrs Bonham's funeral in January 1845, reading the Church of England service over the grave.
- **1845–46** — Described cholera epidemics: *"in '35 we lost (deaths) for two days five hundred a day at Tabreez out of a population of certainly not then more than a hundred thousand."* Visited Tehran, presented a book on China to the Shah, dined with Colonel Sheil and foreign envoys.
- **1848** — The accession of **Nasir-i-Din Shah** transformed Edward's position. He *"sold everything he could lay his hands on and joined the triumphal march from Tabriz to Teheran."* Arrived with all his possessions on four mules, one lightly loaded because a servant rode atop it.

### Chief interpreter and gazette editor in Tehran (1848–1855)

- **1848–49** — Appointed **chief interpreter** to the new government. Later made **editor of the state gazette** (*ruznāme*). Pay: **400 tomauns/year**, chronically in arrears. Consulted the Prime Minister (Mirza Taki Khan, Amir-i-Nizam) and sometimes the Shah himself on articles. Managed the printer **Hadji Abdul Mahomed** with direct threats when deadlines slipped: *"if the paper is not out before 12 o'clock tomorrow I shall complain to the Govt and do my utmost to get a heavy fine inflicted upon you."*
- **Jun 1849** — Writing from **Gul Ahek** near Tehran (British summer retreat). Managed his mother Frances's estate via brother George. Described the Khorasan insurrection and government revenue problems.
- **1851** — Changed the gazette's publication day from Friday to Thursday. Continued translating foreign news with diplomatic caution: *"England, Russia or Turkey … might complain, give trouble to the government and get me into a mess."*

**Lady Sheil's corroboration.** Lady Mary Sheil, wife of the British Minister in Tehran (1849–53), independently describes Edward's role in *Glimpses of Life and Manners in Persia* (1856). She does not name him, but refers to "an English gentleman" whom the Prime Minister placed in charge of the gazette: *"whose duty it was to translate extracts from European newspapers suitable to Persian ideas. The 'leaders' were often the composition of the Prime Minister himself … Censure on any subject was rigidly excluded."* Crucially, she reveals that **Edward managed a second, confidential publication** for the Shah and Prime Minister alone: *"This Englishman enjoyed no sinecure; besides the above Gazette for the public, he had the superintendence of another newspaper, designed only for the eye of the Shah and his minister. The latter journal contained all the European political intelligence deemed unsuitable for the Persian public, besides details of gossip and scandal likely to give amusement to the Shah."* Iranian press historians recognise this as Iran's first confidential intelligence bulletin — making Edward both the country's first state newspaper editor and its first intelligence briefer.

### Marriage, children, and final years (1851–1855)

- **Autumn 1851** — Married [Anna Saginian](anna-saginian.md). Described her in a letter to George (22 Mar 1852) as *"a Georgian lady of good family … pretty and about thirty three years old."* She spoke Armenian and Turkish, wrote Armenian rapidly, kept the house in excellent order, and proved a good horsewoman — Edward took her cross-country to Cheshma Ali and *"she jumped beautifully."*
- **~1852** — **Son John** born in Tehran. Godfather: **Ronald Thompson** (British mission). Both children baptised at home according to Armenian rites.
- **~1853–54** — John died at **eighteen months**. Anna: *"My Edward mourned him as if he had been older, for he had hoped to send him to England, to give him to his dear father to put to school."* Buried in the Armenian graveyard in Tehran.
- **9 Jun 1854** — Daughter [Fanny Burgess](fanny-burgess.md) born. Godfather again Ronald Thompson.
- **~Late 1854** — Fanny had smallpox; recovered. Edward's health declining — disease settled in his spine and limbs, recurring fevers, too weak to ride.
- **Feb 1855** — Petitioned for **leave to return to England**. The Prime Minister agreed verbally; Edward awaited formal publication in the gazette. His letter to Henry: *"thankfulness to God for having given me so good and kind a father."* Planned route: Tehran → Tabriz → Trebizond → Constantinople → Trieste → Germany → England.
- **Mar 1855** — **Last surviving letter** (to George, 14 Mar). Still arranging departure; estimated two months to Trebizond. Mentioned he would need thirty to forty mules for household effects, compared to the four mules of 1848.

### Death and burial (1855)

Anna's testimony (interview, Leicester, March 1880) provides the only account of Edward's death:

- Edward grew weaker. *"Then it was said 'if he was to live at all, he must be taken to England.'"* They let their house, sold what they could, and used the money to provide for Charles's children. The government granted six months' leave.
- They travelled by **takhteravan** (mule-borne litter). Anna walked beside him or rode, with a servant carrying Fanny on another mule. The journey from Tehran to Tabriz took **nearly three months**. *"Often we were obliged to stay at those very dreadful Mahommedan villages, lest he should die with the hard travel: for the disease had settled in his spine and limbs, and often movement gave him such agony that he looked as dead."*
- **Eight days from Tabriz**, Anna's father **Daoud Khan**, her two uncles, and **Dr Cormick** came to meet them.
- They reached Tabriz and laid Edward in his own house. Dr Cormick attended him. **He lived for six weeks more.** He asked Anna to take Fanny to his father in England and *"be sure she is put to learn the English so soon as she can speak."* About a week before he died, he wrote a long last letter to Henry and told Mr Abbott: *"You will find in that large book a letter I have written; when I am dead, take it out, and send it straightway to my father."*
- *"When Fanny was three days past one year old, he died. We had been married just five years."* — approximately **12 June 1855**.
- Buried in the **Armenian cemetery just outside the walls of Tabriz**, close by the grave of Anna's uncle. A small stone with English letters was placed on the grave. Years later, Fanny returned to Tabriz and replaced it with a larger marble slab inscribed in both English and Armenian.
