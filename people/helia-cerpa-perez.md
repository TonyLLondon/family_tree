# Helia del Carmen Cerpa Pérez

**Helia del Carmen** was born **4 June 1943** in **Parral**, **Linares**, **Maule**—Neruda’s town, wine country, and later a nodal point of dictatorship and memory. Her parents were [Miguel Luis Cerpa González](miguel-cerpa-gonzalez-i346.md) and [Laurencia del Carmen Pérez Escobar](laurencia-perez-escobar-i347.md); her brother [Miguel Antonio Cerpa Pérez](miguel-cerpa-perez-i352.md) (1948–2015) shared that household. She married [Joseph Evans (1940)](joseph-evans-i18.md) in **1982** and was mother to [Jacqueline Caroline Evans](jacqueline-caroline-evans-i5.md). She died **2025** (year in tree; civil citation when filed). FamilySearch and the vault graph agree on **1943–2025** for her span.

## Parral — birthplace and political context

Parral is a city in Linares Province in Chile's Maule Region, founded in 1795 by Ambrosio O'Higgins. It is best known as the birthplace of the poet Pablo Neruda (born 1904), Nobel laureate in Literature (1971) and close ally of Salvador Allende. The town sits roughly 35 km from what later became **Colonia Dignidad**, the German colony that the Pinochet regime's secret police (DINA) used as a torture and extrajudicial-killing facility during the dictatorship.

After the **11 September 1973 military coup**, Parral itself housed the **Cuartel de la Brigada de Inteligencia Regional Sur (BIRS)** of the DINA, operating from a building on calle Ignacio Carrera Pinto between 1974 and 1977. The cuartel was the central hub for the DINA's counter-intelligence operations between Rancagua and Los Ángeles; political detainees were held there before being transferred either to Santiago or to Colonia Dignidad for interrogation, torture, and in many cases disappearance. The building was declared a National Historic Monument in September 2022 at the petition of families of the disappeared and executed from Talca, Parral, and Linares. In 2025, a court ordered the Chilean state to indemnify ten survivors of torture and political imprisonment in the Parral–Linares area, fifty-two years after the coup.

Helia grew up in this small agricultural city during the decades when the Chilean left was ascendant — the same political soil that produced Neruda's poetry and would culminate in Allende's election.

## Allende administration and the 1973 coup

Helia worked within the **Allende government** (Unidad Popular coalition, 1970–1973). The administration drew tens of thousands of functionaries — social workers, educators, local administrators, party organisers — into government service at every level. Women played expanding roles: Mireya Baltra became the first female Minister of Labour (1972); Allende's own daughter Beatriz was a key political adviser. The specific nature of Helia's position has not yet been documented in the family archive and warrants an oral-history capture.

When the military junta seized power on 11 September 1973, all parties and trade unions were made illegal by evening. Over the following days, embassies and safe houses filled alongside prisons, stadiums, and torture chambers. An estimated 40,000 people were murdered, tortured, or arbitrarily imprisoned during the seventeen-year dictatorship. Between 200,000 and 2,000,000 Chileans — party activists, trade unionists, intellectuals, government workers — were scattered into exile across the globe. For someone who had served the Allende government in the Parral–Linares area, where the DINA was about to establish its southern regional headquarters, the imperative to flee was immediate and existential.

## Exile — Rome

After the coup, Helia found initial refuge in **Italy**. The Italian embassy in Santiago sheltered approximately 700 people and became one of the most active diplomatic refuges in the capital. Chargé d'Affaires **Piero De Masi**, the senior Italian diplomat present, used personal funds to buy mattresses and managed conditions with over fifty people sometimes sharing a single room. Refugees inside the embassy organised themselves into commissions — cleaning, culture, education, security, cooking — coordinated by a political committee. Between 1973 and 1975, Italian diplomats facilitated safe-conduct passages for roughly 750 Chileans to resettle in Italy. Chile later honoured these diplomats for their role — compared in the press to "Schindler's List."

Rome became a significant hub for the Chilean exile community. The refugees were largely well-educated professionals who integrated successfully into Italian society. Rome was also important for the political renovation of the Chilean Socialist Party and for strategic planning toward Chile's eventual democratic transition. The Italian experience of accepting Chilean refugees was itself a landmark in Italian asylum policy, marking the country's first significant intake of non-European political refugees.

## Exile — London

Helia subsequently moved to **London**, joining the roughly 3,000 Chilean refugees who settled in Britain between 1974 and 1979. The pathway was shaped by British politics: the Conservative government under Edward Heath had quickly recognised Pinochet's regime and resisted Chilean asylum claims, but the **Labour government** that took power in March 1974 reversed course, with Foreign Secretary James Callaghan announcing that Chilean asylum applications would be considered "sympathetically." Margaret Thatcher's election in 1979 effectively ended further admissions.

The Chilean exile community in London was supported by a web of solidarity organisations:

- **Chile Solidarity Campaign (CSC)**, founded late 1973, with 60 local committees and 30 affiliated trade unions by 1976
- **Joint Working Group for Refugees from Chile (JWG)**, established 1974, financed by the Home Office until 1979
- **World University Service (WUS)**, administering scholarship grants for 900 exiles from offices on Seven Sisters Road, Tottenham
- **Chile Committee for Human Rights (CCHR)**, sponsoring political prisoners

Camden specifically had a visible Chilean presence. A solidarity mural painted alongside the railway line at King Henry's Road, Primrose Hill, read "Solidarity With the People of Chile" — it became a local landmark. Chilean refugees lived in squats on Chalk Farm Road during the 1970s. The broader Latin American community established centres like the Latin America Centre in Hoxton Square (1977) offering services and cultural activities. Political and cultural events — folk music by Inti-Illimani, empanada sales, fundraising for the resistance — were the connective tissue of a diaspora that, at first, expected to return home within a few years.

Most Chilean families in Britain were settled on council housing estates. The geographic distribution depended on sympathetic local councils, trade unions, and employers: by 1979, London and the South East had an estimated 1,190 Chileans, with the remainder dispersed across Scotland (400), Yorkshire (400), the North West (270), and elsewhere.

## Camden housing — decades of community service

Helia settled on the **Ossulston Estate** in Somers Town, Camden — a Grade II listed modernist council estate built 1927–1931 by the London County Council on Chalton Street, between Euston and King's Cross stations. The estate comprises Chamberlain House, Levita House, and Walker House (310 flats), designed by LCC chief architect G. Topham Forrest in a style influenced by Viennese social housing such as the Karl Marx-Hof.

She became a long-serving representative of the **Ossulston Tenants and Residents Association (TRA)** and an active participant in Camden's **District Management Committee (DMC)** system — the independent advisory forums of tenant representatives and councillors that advise the Cabinet Member for Housing on management, maintenance, expenditure, and regeneration across each housing district.

Her trajectory — from Allende government worker to political refugee to decades-long housing advocate on a London council estate — mirrors that of many Chilean exiles who, as the Tribune wrote in 2023, "contributed enormously to British society as teachers, engineers, human rights activists, local councillors, doctors, scientists and more."

## Pinochet protests — London, 1998–2000

General Pinochet was arrested in London on 16 October 1998 on a warrant from Spanish judge Baltasar Garzón. He was held under house arrest in Britain for 503 days. The Chilean exile community mounted a sustained picket — **el piquete de Londres** — outside the building where he was detained, drawing Chilean exiles from across Europe. The piquete remobilised solidarity networks that had been dormant since the end of the dictatorship in 1990 and was a formative moment for the second generation. Helia participated in these protests (family knowledge; no online record located naming her individually among the demonstrators).
