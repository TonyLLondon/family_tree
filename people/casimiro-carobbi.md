## Context

Casimiro was born in **Senj** (Italian: Segna), a strategically important Adriatic port on the Croatian Military Border (Vojna krajina). From the 15th century onward, numerous Italian families settled in Senj drawn by maritime commerce, military service, and administrative roles under Habsburg authority. Italian noble families in Senj — including Carina, Demelli, De Santi, Locatelli, Marocchi, Petazzi, Ponte, Rabatta, Raffaelis, Della Rovere, Rubelli, Sussani, and Zudenigo — were inscribed in the city's "Golden Book of the True Titled" and received coats of arms. Some obtained citizenship rights (Demelli in 1682, Carina in 1720); others achieved patriciate status or received baronies and knighthoods for military merit.

Senj's citizens played a crucial role in Habsburg naval strategy on the Adriatic during the 18th century, arming merchant vessels with military crews from the Military Border troops to ensure safe maritime trade. This military-commercial nexus, drawing on Senj's centuries-long experience fighting the Ottomans, contributed to the establishment of Austria's official Adriatic navy by the late 1780s.

The Carobbi trajectory — Senj (birth) → Kotor (marriage 1820) → Zara (settled by 1828, death 1866) — traces the path of an Italian family moving between the major Adriatic port cities of the Habsburg Empire, likely connected to trade, military administration, or professional service. Whether the Carobbi were among Senj's inscribed Italian noble families or part of a broader Italian-speaking professional class remains to be confirmed.
