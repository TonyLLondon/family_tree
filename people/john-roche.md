# John Roche

**John Roche** of **Castletown-Roche**, County Cork, was a member of the **Catholic Parliament** (the Confederate Council) held at **Kilkenny** during the 1641 rising. He is the earliest confirmed direct-line ancestor in the Roche of Limerick pedigree and the great-great-grandfather of [John Lysaght Roche](john-lysaght-roche.md), the merchant prince.

Burke's *History of Commoners* (1833) opens the Roche of Limerick entry with him:

> The family of Roche of Limerick has branched from that of Castletown-Roche, in the county of Cork. **John Roche, of Castletown-Roche, descended from the Viscounts Fermoy**, was a member of the Catholic Parliament or Council held at Kilkenny during the civil wars, and his name appears as such to the declaration of the Irish Roman Catholics in 1641.

## The 1641 Parliament

In October 1641 the Catholic gentry of Ireland rose against the Dublin government. The following year delegates assembled at Kilkenny as the **Confederation of Kilkenny** — a Catholic self-government that functioned until Cromwell's conquest in 1649–1653. The Confederation had its own Supreme Council, bishops' assembly, and a parliament modelled on Westminster, with Lords Spiritual and Temporal sitting alongside a Commons.

The Roche family sent multiple members: **Maurice Roche, Viscount Fermoy** sat among the Peers; **David Roche of Gleecure**, **John Roche of Castletown**, and **Redmond Roche of Cahirdowgan** sat in the Commons. John's presence in the Commons (not the Peers) confirms he was from a cadet branch of the Viscount Fermoy line — descended from them but not the title-holder himself.

## Castletown-Roche

The town and castle of **Castletown-Roche** (Baile an Chaisleáin) in the Barony of Fermoy, County Cork, take their name from the Roche family. Lewis's *Topographical Dictionary* (1837) records that the castle "sustained many sieges" during the parliamentary war, and "in 1649 was defended for several days by Lady Roche against a detachment of the parliamentarian army." When the owner refused to compound with Cromwell for its restoration, the castle was confiscated.

The Roches had held this castle and the surrounding lordship since the 13th century, when **Adam de Rupe** (later anglicised to Roche) came to Ireland from Wales with **Robert FitzStephen** during the Norman invasion (1169–1171). The family were created **Viscounts Fermoy** in the Peerage of Ireland and were among the most powerful Old English magnates of Munster.

## The Viscounts Fermoy

The Viscount Fermoy line, from whom John descended, ran:

| Viscount | Dates | Note |
|----------|-------|------|
| **Adam de Rupe** | fl. 1170s | Came from Wales with Robert FitzStephen; settled in Fermoy |
| ... | 13th–15th C | Lords of Fermoy; built Castletown-Roche castle |
| **Maurice Roche, 6th Viscount** | ~1550–1600 | Known as "the Mad"; m. Gráinne MacTeige of Muskerry |
| **David Roche, 7th Viscount** | 1573–1635 | Knighted by Sir Henry Sidney; loyal during the Desmond Rebellions; sat in Parliament 1613 |
| **Maurice Roche, 8th Viscount** | 1597–1670 | Sat among the Peers at Kilkenny 1641; outlawed under Cromwell |

John Roche of Castletown was likely a grandson or great-grandson of a younger son of the 5th or 6th Viscount — close enough to the main line to style himself "of Castletown-Roche" and to sit in the Confederation's Commons, but not the titleholder.
