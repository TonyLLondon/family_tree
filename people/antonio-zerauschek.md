# Antonio Zerauschek

Businessman and investor in **Zara** (Kingdom of Italy) in the **1930s** — **Hotel Zerauschek**, **Caffè Centrale**, **Hotel Bristol / Excelsior**. In the MyHeritage GEDCOM export he is **`@I14@`**, father of [Fulvia Ottilia Antonia Zerauschek](fulvia-ottilia-antonia-zerauschek.md). His parents in the GEDCOM chain are [Antonio Zerauschek (senior)](antonio-zerauschek-senior.md) and [Anna Herenda](anna-herenda.md) (**F13**). That earlier Antonio (`@I25@`) must not be confused with this file’s subject (`@I14@`).

## Life

The contemporary Italian obituary in *Difesa Adriatica* (issue **XXVII, no. 3**, **25 March–3 April 1973**) is the richest narrative source in this vault: signed **„g. z.“**, it places his death in **Florence** in the **last days of February 1973**, aged **84**, which matches the GEDCOM **28 February 1973** date. The piece stresses that he died **far from Zara**, roughly **eighteen months** after the death of his wife **[Ester Addobbati](ester-addobbati.md)**—a loss the author treats as having broken his health despite the care of children and grandchildren.

Socially, the obituary sketches a figure already visible before the First World War: tall, elegant, a strong dancer at balls run by the **„Società Ginnastica“** in Zara; after the war he was part of the circle around the **„Circolo Arturo Colautti“**. Politically he is called a **patriot** who followed the city’s fortunes. Economically it casts him as a **self-made** operator: starting from **import–export**, he rose to a leading place in Zara’s **commerce and industry**, helped by the **free port** regime, holding **agency** lines for foreign goods (flour, oils, petrol, cars, and similar). He was a **founder** of the **„Manifattura Tabacchi Orientali“** (later going out on his own), created the **„Calypso“** cigarette line, and founded Zara’s **„Ausonia“** chocolate factory—offering substantial employment and remembered with **gratitude** by workers in the article’s telling. Outside business he is portrayed as **family-centred**, modest, and widely **liked** across Zara society; the author recalls pre-war hospitality in a **house he had designed** shortly before the Second World War, then the **anguish** of leaving Zara under **Allied bombing** and abandoning industries, home, and city. **English translation** (use for wording; cite scan + Italian transcript for authority): [translation (EN)](../sources/corpus/difesa-adriatica-1973-antonio-zerauschek-obituary/translation-antonio-zerauschek-obituary-1973.en.md).

**Built works (interwar):** he demolished an older building on **Široka ulica** and commissioned a modern hotel/café complex (**1937–1938**), architect **Umberto Nordio** (Trieste) — [narratives/zerauschek-zadar.md](../narratives/zerauschek-zadar.md). **Hotel Bristol → Excelsior** (~1936): takeover and rename per Adriatic press/hospitality histories linked there.

**After WWII:** Zara passed to Yugoslavia; Italian-speaking business families often **emigrated**. The obituary’s emotional centre is exile and memory of Zara; **property and legal succession** in Yugoslavia/Italy still need document-by-document work (parish/civil scans under `media/collections/zerauschek/`).
