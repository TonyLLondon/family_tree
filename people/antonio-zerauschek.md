# Antonio Zerauschek

The family knew him as **Tonci** — the Dalmatian diminutive of Antonio — while Croatian headlines would later borrow the Habsburg vocabulary and call him **trgovac, bankar, veleposjednik i tvorničar**—merchant, banker, major landowner, factory man. In the interwar **Kingdom of Italy**, that meant **Zara** with its free port and its nerve: his name on **Hotel Zerauschek**, **Caffè Centrale**, **Hotel Bristol / Excelsior**; on the **Manifattura Zaratina** cigarette works (**Calypso**, **Samos**, the whole glossy portfolio of Dalmatian “foreign” tobacco); on **Ausonia**, the chocolate house at Voštarnica that sent bars to Italy and Western Europe while the new bridge was still drying its mortar. He was the son of [Antonio Zerauschek (senior)](antonio-zerauschek-senior.md) and [Anna Herenda](anna-herenda.md), the husband of [Ester Addobbati](ester-addobbati.md), and the father of [Fulvia Ottilia Antonia Zerauschek](fulvia-ottilia-antonia-zerauschek.md)—the line that reaches Wales and Sussex.

## Life

**Birth and baptism.** He was born at noon on **24 June 1889** in **Zara**, with the full baptismal name **Antonio Carlo Giovanni Battista**. The parish waited until **1 January 1890** to baptize him—an unusually long gap—and the ceremony was performed by **D. Marco Faccini**, the same canon who would soon delegate the **Addobbati–Boara** wedding. The household lived at **Via del Cristo, C.M. § 24**; his father signed the register as **falegname** (carpenter), his mother **Anna** as **cucitrice** (seamstress), married at Pag in 1878. The godparents were **Carlo Herenda** (merchant, almost certainly Anna’s brother) and **Catterina Zerauschek** (seamstress, likely paternal kin); witnesses included masons and carpenters; the midwife appears as **Maria Stein** in the hand of the scribe.

**Voice after exile.** The Italian obituary in *Difesa Adriatica* (issue **XXVII, no. 3**, **25 March–3 April 1973**) is the vault’s most humane portrait: signed **„g. z."**, it puts his death in **Florence** in the **last days of February 1973**, aged **84**, aligning with the civil **28 February 1973** date. It lingers on distance—he died **far from Zara**—and on timing: about **eighteen months** after **[Ester Addobbati](ester-addobbati.md)** died, as if grief had outpaced medicine even with children and grandchildren beside him.

The A.N.D.A.Z. obituary (*ZARA*, 1973) adds a detail the family memoir left unnamed: Antonio’s commercial education began as a **garzone di bottega** (shop boy) at the **Mazzocchi** trading house in Zara, under the supervision of partner **Tonti**. The same article ranks him alongside **Luxardo**, **Drioli**, and **Montecucchi** as the defining names of Zaratine industry, calling him “il vero d’Artagnan dell’industria zaratina.”

Socially, the obituary sketches a figure already visible before the First World War: tall, elegant, a strong dancer at balls run by the **„Società Ginnastica"** in Zara; after the war he was part of the circle around the **„Circolo Arturo Colautti"**. Politically he is called a **patriot** who followed the city's fortunes. Economically it casts him as a **self-made** operator: starting from **import–export**, he rose to a leading place in Zara's **commerce and industry**, helped by the **free port** regime, holding **agency** lines for foreign goods (flour, oils, petrol, cars, and similar). Outside business he is portrayed as **family-centred**, modest, and widely **liked** across Zara society; the author recalls pre-war hospitality in a **house he had designed** shortly before the Second World War, then the **anguish** of leaving Zara under **Allied bombing** and abandoning industries, home, and city. **English translation** (use for wording; cite scan + Italian transcript for authority): [translation (EN)](../sources/corpus/difesa-adriatica-1973-antonio-zerauschek-obituary/translation-antonio-zerauschek-obituary-1973.en.md).

### Manifattura Zaratina Sigarette

On **27 May 1928** Zerauschek acquired the **Manifattura Zaratina Sigarette** from **Niccolò Peristeridis**, a Greek-origin tobacco operator whose family also ran factories in Durazzo (Albania). The company originated as **Manifattura Zaratina Tabacchi e Sigarette "N. Peristeridis"** (Calle Giuseppe Sabalich 2), producing **Calypso** and **Samos**; renamed 1922 and moved to Calle Catena 6. Under Zerauschek it also absorbed the **Fabbrica Sigarette Diadora** and produced: **Calitea, Calypso, Diadora, Flavia, Lampo, Radio, Raminga, Samos, Samos Export, Si**. Zara sat outside the Italian customs line — these cigarettes were classified as "foreign" in mainland Italy. A surviving c. 1940 Calypso hinged-box pack is documented in the zigsam.at collector's database. A **Samos** pack is a plot-critical prop in Mario Camerini's *Il Signor Max* (1937, starring Vittorio De Sica): Lauretta (Assia Noris) uses the brand to test whether the kiosk vendor and the fake aristocrat are the same man — "Do you smoke this brand?" The family kept a credits page for the film in the Zara album ([SigMax.jpg](../media/docs/fulvia-lewis-photos/zara-album-01/miscellaneous/SigMax.jpg)). See [*Il Signor Max* — Samos on screen](../topics/il-signor-max-1937.md). **Sources:** [sigaretteitaliane.org](../sources/sigaretteitaliane-zara-1920-43.md) · [zigsam.at](../sources/zigsam-at-calypso-cigarette-packs.md).

The obituary separately names him a **founder** of the **Manifattura Tabacchi Orientali** (MTO) — a larger, state-majority manufacturer on Riva 4 Novembre. He later struck out independently with the Manifattura Zaratina; the two companies were distinct entities.

### Fabbrica Cioccolato e Cacao Ausonia

The **Ausonia** chocolate and cocoa factory stood in **Voštarnica** (Ceraria), built on the newly developed land that opened after the 1928 bridge over the Jazine inlet. By **1929** the factory was **exporting chocolate to Italy and Western European countries**. It won **numerous quality awards**. A 2011 Croatian business article speculates that **Luxardo** (est. 1821 in Zadar) probably used Ausonia products for its bonbon and chocolate lines. The Zerauschek family also erected modern residential and commercial buildings across Zadar in the 1930s. **Sources:** [Zadarski tjednik (2024)](../sources/zadarskitjednik-ausonia-tvornica-cokolade.md) · [Poslovni dnevnik (2011)](../sources/poslovni-hr-2011-ausonia-zadar-zerauschek.md).

### Built works (interwar)

He demolished an older building on **Široka ulica** and commissioned a modern hotel/café complex (**1937–1938**), architect **Umberto Nordio** (Trieste) — [stories/zerauschek-zadar.md](../stories/zerauschek-zadar.md). **Hotel Bristol → Excelsior** (~1936): takeover and rename per Adriatic press/hospitality histories linked there. After Yugoslav confiscation, the Hotel Bristol was renamed **Hotel Zagreb**; when Bruno Zerauschek and his wife visited Zadar in **1985**, the staff at the former family hotel read the Zerauschek surname in their passport and regarded them with open contempt.

### Villa Ester, Sirmione (Lake Garda)

Before the war, Antonio acquired the **Villa Giannantoni** on the tip of the Sirmione peninsula — Via Caio Valerio Catullo 7, overlooking the Grotte di Catullo — and renamed it **Villa Ester** after his wife. The family used it as their Lake Garda retreat; a [photograph of Fulvia on the loggia](../media/docs/Fulvia%20Zerauschek%20at%20Villa%20Ester%20Sirmione.png) survives from this period. In the immediate post-war years, after the family's exile from Zara, Antonio sold the villa to **Angelo Marinoni** (electric-oven manufacturer, Verona). Marinoni in turn sold it in **December 1957** to **Comm. Giovanni Battista Meneghini**, who gave it to his wife **Maria Callas** — it became the famous **Villa Callas**. The *Incom* article (10 May 1958) names Antonio directly as **"l'industriale zaratino Antonio Zerauschek, ora trasferitosi in quel di Firenze"**. **Source:** *Incom* reportage by Savino Mariani, 10 May 1958 · [corpus](../sources/corpus/gbopera-2009-telefoni-e-rose-sirmione-callas/) · original magazine scans: [INCOM100558-01.jpg](../media/docs/sirmione-villa-ester/INCOM100558-01.jpg)–[07](../media/docs/sirmione-villa-ester/INCOM100558-07.jpg). **Narrative:** [Villa Ester — from the Zerauscheks to Maria Callas](../stories/zerauschek-villa-callas-sirmione.md).

### Business life — letters 1936–1943

Antonio's own letters to Fulvia fill in the texture of his working life. He received **forty to fifty business letters a day** (Mar 1938), and owned **multiple businesses** ("qualche mia azienda," May 1940). In **February 1941** he traveled to **Belgrade and perhaps Sofia** to buy **tobacco** for his manufacturing operations — he went reluctantly ("non sono troppo entusiasta del viaggio") but the Manifattura Zaratina needed raw leaf. He was friends with **Pietro Luxardo** of the maraschino dynasty; after a convent lunch celebrating Father Poviè's 25th anniversary (Feb 1941), the two men went home together and fell asleep for three hours.

At the Zara property he was forever building: by late 1940 he had finished a new **chicken coop** and **dovecote** but still hadn't completed a **tennis court** and a small house (**casetta**). He kept animals — cats named **Venere** (Venus), **Picia**, and **Negus** — plus mice in a "topaia" that Venere systematically hunted. The family also had a property at **Lucarono** (Lukoran, island near Zara) reachable by motorboat, with **citrus trees** including a mandarin. He called his children at boarding school **"i merli e la merla"** (the blackbirds).

His business letterhead confirms the commercial registration: **A. ZERAUSCHEK / ZARA — C.P.E.C. N. 496, Telegrammi: ZERAUSCHEK, Telefono N. 48**. In Florence, after the war, he used personal stationery reading **ANTONIO ZERAUSCHEK — Firenze, Viale Mazzini, 60 — Tel. 63.311**. In his last years the ulcer that began after Ester's death tormented him: "Sempre di più mi pare impossibile che la buona Ester mi abbia lasciato" (6 Nov 1971). His final surviving letter (23 Oct 1972) reports that he gave **Carlo Enrico** (Giuliana's husband) **2,500,000 lire** and warns "queste sono le ultime"; he mentions **"zia Enrichetta moglie del zio Nini"** has just died, worries about **Marco's knee**, and signs himself **"Nonno Tonci."** He died four months later.

### After WWII

Zara passed to Yugoslavia; Italian-speaking business families **emigrated**. The cigarette factory — partly destroyed by Allied bombing — was **confiscated** by Yugoslav authorities. The Manifattura Zaratina Sigarette attempted to reconstitute in Italy, convening its shareholders' assembly in **Florence**; the assembly voted to convert into an *s.r.l.*, but the **Tribunale di Firenze annulled** the resolution and the company ceased to exist. The Ausonia chocolate factory was likewise **shut down by the incoming Yugoslav authorities**; no chocolate was produced in Zadar again for 82 years. The obituary's emotional centre is exile and memory of Zara; **property and legal succession** in Yugoslavia/Italy still need document-by-document work (parish/civil scans under `media/collections/zerauschek/`).
