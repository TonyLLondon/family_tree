# Cornelius O'Shea

Cornelius O'Shea was born in **1856** at **Inchigeelagh**, a remote parish in the Muskerry Gaeltacht west of Macroom, County Cork, and was baptised there on **19 September 1856** in the Roman Catholic church. He became a **National Teacher** at **Monkstown**, on the western shore of Cork Harbour — recorded under that title on the civil birth certificate of his eldest daughter [Mary](mary-oshea.md) on 19 February 1893, giving his address as **Castle Terrace, Monkstown**, in the Carrigaline No. 2 registration district.

## National Teacher

On Mary's 1893 birth certificate the column *"Rank or Profession of Father"* reads **"National Teacher"**. In Ireland this was not a generic description; it was a formal, state-regulated position within the **National School system** established by the Stanley Letter of 1831 — one of the first centrally funded primary-education systems in the English-speaking world.

National schools were administered by the **Commissioners of National Education** and managed locally — usually by the parish priest in Catholic areas. Teachers were appointed through this framework, classified into grades (third, second, and first class, each with subdivisions) under the **National School Teachers (Ireland) Act 1879**, and paid directly from the Board's funds. The post-nominal letters **N.T.** (National Teacher) were used by holders of the position.

By the 1890s the system enrolled roughly a million pupils across nearly 7,000 schools. Teachers were bound by strict codes of conduct: they were expected to avoid fairs, markets, and political meetings, and to promote cleanliness, neatness, and decency. The parish clergyman, acting as school manager, could dismiss a teacher at a quarter of an hour's notice. In return, national teachers occupied a respected, if closely scrutinised, place in their communities — described in contemporary accounts as "a conspicuous figure in society" and seen as guardians of literacy and order in rural Ireland.

Catholic teacher training had been a contentious issue. Archbishop Paul Cullen's 1863 boycott of the inter-denominational "model schools" left the majority of Catholic teachers without formal training for a generation; as late as 1874 only 27 per cent of Catholic teachers were trained (compared with 52 per cent of Protestants). Denominational training colleges were sanctioned from 1883 — at Drumcondra and Carysfort — so by the time Cornelius was teaching in the early 1890s, a new generation of Catholic N.T.s with training-college certification was entering the profession.

Cornelius was teaching during a period of rapid change in Irish elementary education: compulsory attendance for children aged 6–14 was introduced in **1892** (the year before Mary's birth), and the controversial **payment-by-results** system — in which a teacher's income partly depended on pupils' examination scores — was still in force (it ran 1872–1899). Irish could be taught only outside school hours until 1900, so Cornelius's classroom instruction would have been conducted entirely through English.

### 1905 Teachers' Census — absence

The **[List of Teachers Employed by the Commissioners of National Education on 31 March 1905](../sources/corpus/teachers-census-1905/)** (NAI/ED/PCO/10/1/29, covering O'Keane–O'Sullivan) lists every national teacher then serving in Ireland. The O'Shea entries span three pages and include dozens of teachers — Anne, Brigid, Daniel, Denis, Eliza, Elizabeth, Ellen, Helena, Honoria, Johanna, John, Joseph, Julia, Katie, Margaret, Mary, Michael, Mortimer, Patrick, Roger, Timothy, William — but **no Cornelius**. He had left the profession, retired, or died before March 1905, roughly twelve years after the 1893 birth certificate recorded him as a serving N.T.

## Life

Cornelius was the son of **Michael Shea** (1811–1881) and **Norry Buckley** (1821–1893), and grew up in Inchigeelagh among a large family: brothers Daniel (1842–1903), Patrick (b. 1843), Timothy (b. 1846), Thomas (b. 1849), and Jeremiah (b. 1863); sisters Mary (b. 1850), Joanna — known as Hannah — (1853–1930), and Catherine (b. 1859).

He married [Catherine Enright](catherine-enright.md) on **2 June 1892** at **Monkstown, Carrigaline**, County Cork. The witnesses were **Edmond Fitzpatrick** and **Ellie Enright**, Catherine's sister. Mary's birth certificate names the informant as **Catherine Enright** (her mark), described as "Grandmother, Present at Birth, Castle Terrace, Monkstown" — indicating that Catherine's mother was living with or very near the couple when the first child arrived.

The surviving portrait shows Cornelius in later life: an older man with a long grey beard, wearing a flat cap and a heavy double-breasted overcoat, standing on stone steps in front of a Gothic arched doorway — possibly a church.

By the **1911 census** Cornelius was living as a **lodger** at **4 James Street, Cork City** — separate from the family household in Monkstown — suggesting he may have taken work or lodging in the city after leaving teaching.

Cornelius died on **12 June 1929** at the **Little Sisters of the Poor**, Cork City — a home for the aged poor run by the order. His death record describes him as "Married, Teacher, aged 66 years — late of Inchigeela, Co. Cork." He was buried at **St. Joseph's Cemetery, Cork City**.
