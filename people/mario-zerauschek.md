# Mario Zerauschek

Mario was the youngest son of [Antonio Zerauschek](antonio-zerauschek.md) and [Ester Addobbati](ester-addobbati.md), brother of [Giuliana](giuliana-zerauschek-rivolta.md), [Fulvia](fulvia-ottilia-antonia-zerauschek.md), and [Guido](guido-zerauschek.md). Of the four children, Mario was the best student. In **January 1939**, when their father signed the report cards, little Mario ran to his mother shouting: **"ecco la tua Fulle, ecco i cinque della brava"** — here's your Fulle, here are the clever girl's fives — gleefully mocking his sister's bad marks. In **February 1941** Antonio reported that Mario said he was **"il secondo in camerata per lo studio"** (second in the dormitory for studies). His father noted in **May 1940** that Mario "studia bene." By **November 1942** his marks had dipped to "così-così" — middling — but still above his siblings' results. He was at **boarding school** during the war, one of the "merli" (blackbirds) Antonio tracked through Ester's letters.

Before October 1963 he **injured his leg** while travelling in **Austria** and then again in **Italy**; Lily Zerauschek, writing from Cape Town, commiserated: "Well, boys will be boys, as they say."

In **July 1962**, Mario flew from Florence to visit David and Fulvia in Africa. A **Rhodesia–Nyasaland Post Office telegram** (18 Jul 1962) sent from Florence to **"LT LEWIS 8 PROSPECT HILL LUSAKA"** reads: **"ASPETTAMI VENERDI VENI ORE 11.40 AEROPORTO SALISBURY DIRETTI BEIRA = MARIO"** — wait for me Friday, arriving 11:40 at Salisbury airport, bound for Beira (Mozambique). The close bond between the Zerauschek siblings and the Lewises stretched across continents.

When his cousin Bruno visited Florence in **1971**, Mario was **"di guardia all'ospedale"** (on duty at the hospital), confirming a medical or hospital career.

In **November 1972**, Mario's wife wrote a condolence letter to David and Fulvia on the sudden death of David's mother ("l'improvvisa scomparsa della Signora Lewis"), having heard the news from Giuliana. She wrote with affection and intelligence, noting that they themselves had recently experienced similar grief with the loss of "la nonna Ester" more than a year earlier. Mario — described by his wife as **"di poche parole"** (a man of few words) — asked her to convey his embrace. The letter confirms that by November 1972 Mario and his wife had **children**: "i nostri bambini stanno bene crescono e si fanno proprio carini" (our children are well, growing and getting quite cute). **Antonio** ("il Nonno") and **"la zia Mena"** also sent condolences.
