# Giovanni Zerauschek (Zio Nini)

**Zio Nini** — **Giovanni** in the registers — kept the family's hand on wood and water: a **small shipyard** in Zara along the *strada di Cereria*, sons enough to people a novella (**fourteen** born, **seven** lived). He married **Enrichetta Decovich** (born Zara 2 February 1894), a woman remembered by the exile community as always in motion, always with an open smile, the vital engine of a family where work never ran short.

After the **1945** transfer he missed the window for clean exit, stood a **Communist trial**, and served **eight months of hard labour** before Italy would take him; he finished in **Sardinia**, still building boats, settling near **Alghero**. Enrichetta died there on **14 October 1972**; an A.N.D.A.Z. obituary placed her alongside "le famiglie più salde e più quotate degli artigiani zaratini" — the sturdiest and most respected of Zaratine artisan families.

The family's escape from Zara was harrowing. Bruno, Giulietta (then pregnant), and two other families took partisan boats from **Ugljan** island across to **Vis (Lissa)**, where they waited for a transport ship. The partisans tried to separate the men from the women, insisting the Zerauscheks were Yugoslavs; after a fierce argument about being Italian, they managed to embark and reach **Bari** in southern Italy. A baby daughter, **Brunetta**, was born there but died of an intestinal infection — penicillin was not yet available. The family spent nearly seven years in the Bari area before **Bruno**, **Giulietta**, and their son **Valter** (b. 1947) emigrated to **Argentina** in **1950**, settling permanently in **Mar del Plata**.

**Ferruccio** went to **Australia** in **1949** with his wife and two children — Fulvia notes a grandson on the **national basketball team**. **Aldo** ended up in **Trieste**, where he died on **28 March 1992** aged 81, leaving a wife and two married children. **Emma** married **Gino Marsan** and settled in **Alghero**, Sardinia, near her mother Enrichetta. **Lina** settled in **Ancona**.

Bruno married **Giulietta Ceriani** on **28 February 1942** in Zara; the witnesses were his uncle **"Tonci"** ([Antonio Zerauschek](antonio-zerauschek.md)) and a friend of Giulietta's family who was **Secretary of the Government of Dalmatia**. Before the war Bruno had entered the **Italian Navy** in **1935** at **Pola**, serving on a cruiser as a **petty officer (sottufficiale) of carpenters** — eight months of deployment and seven years of military service. In Argentina he founded the **Circolo Giuliani** of Mar del Plata in **1984** and served as its president; he was also vice-president of the **Unione dei Circoli Giuliani in Argentina**. In **1985** he and Giulietta returned to **Zadar** for a bittersweet visit: at the former **Hotel Bristol** (renamed **Hotel Zagreb** under Yugoslavia), the staff read the Zerauschek name in their passport and regarded them with open contempt. Bruno died **22 May 1994** aged 79; the exile community honoured him with an impressive funeral, the *ex combattenti* draping the Italian flag over his coffin.
