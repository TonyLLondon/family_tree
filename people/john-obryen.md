# John O'Bryen

**John O'Bryen** (also O'Brien) of **Moyvanine and Clounties**, both in the County of Limerick, was the **chief of the O'Bryens of Arran** and a **lineal descendant of Brian Boru**, High King of Ireland (d. 1014). He was the father of [Sarah O'Bryen](sarah-obryen.md), who married [Stephen John Roche](stephen-john-roche.md) of Limerick.

Four independent sources — spanning sixty-three years — confirm his status:

- **Vallancey's *Collectanea de Rebus Hibernicis*** (vol. I, pp. 568–570, published 1770–1804) names him directly: "The chief and direct representative of this noble branch of the O Briens descended from Teige Gle, was **John O Brien**, lately living at his own estate of **Moyvainine and Cluanties** in the county of Limerick." Vallancey adds that John had a son, **Thady O Brien**, "an officer of known valour in the queen of Hungary's service" — i.e. in the army of Maria Theresa. On p. 570 Vallancey confirms John is "the sixth direct descendant from the said Morrogh… **as appears by his genealogy**" — he had seen the family genealogy document but did not reproduce the intermediate names.
- **Ferrar's *History of Limerick*** (1787, pp. 40–41) independently confirms the same facts: among the descendants of Brian Boru, "one of the most direct branches was **John O'Brien of Moyvanine** and Cluanties in the county of Limerick, who had a son called **Thady O'Brien, an officer of known valour in the Queen of Hungary's service**." Ferrar footnotes Vallancey's *Collectanea*, part 4, p. 568. The subscriber list includes a **"John O'Brien, esq"** — possibly our John, alive at or near the date of publication.
- **Hardiman's *History of the Town and County of the Town of Galway*** (1820, p. 207) names **"John O'Brien of Clonthis [Clounties], County Limerick"** as **"the direct chief of that princely family"** — i.e. the Mac Tiege O'Briens of the Aran Islands.
- **Burke's *History of Commoners*** (1833) describes Sarah as "daughter and co-heiress of **John O'Bryen, esq. of Moyvanine and Clounties**, both in the county of Limerick, **chief of the O'Bryens, of Arran, lineal descendants of Brien Borroimhe**."

## The Claim

The O'Bryens of Arran — properly the **Mac Tiege** or **Clan Tiege** — were descended from **Teige Ó Briain**, great-grandson of Brian Boru, from whom they took their name. They had been the temporal lords of the **Aran Islands** (Inishmore, Inishmaan, Inisheer) in Galway Bay "time out of man's memory." The Kings of Thomond exercised rule of the three islands, and in return for protecting shipping into Galway harbour against "pirates and coast plunderers," the Tribes of Galway paid them an annual tribute of wine.

The key document attesting this lineage is a **certificate dated 30 March 1588**, signed by **John Blake** (Mayor of Galway), **Walter Martin** and **Anthony Kirrivan** (bailiffs), and countersigned by Anthony Dermot (notary), addressed to **Queen Elizabeth**:

> They testify... in favour of **Murrogh Mac Turlogh O'Brien**, then living, that the Mac Tieges of Arran his ancestors, were, under her Majesty and her predecessors, the temporal captains or lords of the islands of Arran, and their territories and hereditaments elsewhere, under the name of Mac Tiege O'Brien of Arran, **time out of man's memory**; and that they had seen the said Murrogh Mac Turlogh, authorised by all his sept, as chief of that name, and in possession of the premises as his own lawful inheritance.

Burke's 1833 states that **Murrogh O'Bryen** was **"the sixth progenitor of the said John"** — meaning six generations separated them.

## The 1588 Certificate

Vallancey himself held a copy: "I have in my possession an authentic copy of a public instrument signed by the mayor and bailiffs of the city of Galway the 30th of March 1588." By 1833, the original had passed to **James Roche, Esq., of Cork** — Thomas Roche's brother, the scholar and banker who died in 1853. Burke's 1833 confirms: "the original of this document is in the possession of James Roche, esq. of Cork." Hardiman's *History of Galway* (1820) also records that a copy was held by John O'Brien of Clounties himself.

The certificate is referenced in:
- Vallancey, *Collectanea de Rebus Hibernicis*, vol. I, p. 569
- Hardiman, *History of Galway* (1820), p. 207
- O'Brien's *Irish Dictionary*
- Ferrar's *History of Limerick*

## Descent from Brian Boru

Vallancey (p. 570) confirms John O'Brien is "the sixth direct descendant from the said Morrogh son of Turlogh O Brien… **as appears by his genealogy**" — meaning Vallancey had access to a family genealogy document belonging to John O'Brien himself. He chose not to reproduce the five intermediate names. Those five generations between Murrough (1588) and John (~1700s) remain unrecovered from any published source; they were dispossessed Catholic gentry in Co. Limerick, too minor for the annals after losing Arran.

## The Loss of Arran

The Clan Tiege lost the Aran Islands to the **O'Flahertys** by 1581. By 1641 the islands had passed to Sir Roebuck Lynch of Galway. The Earl of Clanricarde warned the Earl of Thomond that "Boetius Clancy the younger and the Clan-Tiege of Thomond under pretence of some antiquated claim intend to invade" the islands — the last recorded attempt by the family to reclaim their ancient seat.

Dispossessed of the islands, the O'Bryen chiefs of Arran retired to **Moyvanine and Clounties** in County Limerick, where John O'Bryen held the estates when his daughter Sarah married Stephen John Roche in the 1760s. Through that marriage, the Clounties estate passed to the Roche family — Thomas Roche was managing it in 1808, and Burke's 1833 notes that the Mountcashell estate fee was also in Thomas's name.

## Family

- Son: **Thady O'Brien**, "an officer of known valour in the queen of Hungary's service" (Vallancey, p. 569) — serving in the army of **Maria Theresa** of Austria (r. 1740–1780), part of the tradition of Irish Catholic gentry serving in Continental armies after the Penal Laws excluded them from commissions at home.
- Daughter: [Sarah O'Bryen](sarah-obryen.md) (d. 1786), co-heiress; m. [Stephen John Roche](stephen-john-roche.md).
- Burke's 1833 describes Sarah as "daughter and **co-heiress**" — implying at least one other child, likely a second daughter.

## Open questions

- John O'Bryen's birth and death dates — Vallancey says "lately living," placing him in the mid-18th century; his son Thady served Maria Theresa (r. 1740–1780). "John O'Brien, esq" appears in Ferrar's 1787 subscriber list — if this is our John, he was alive at or near publication.
- Whether the original 1588 certificate survived James Roche's death in 1853, and where it might be today. Vallancey held a copy; the original was later with James Roche.
- The identity of the co-heiress (Sarah's sibling) mentioned in Burke's — possibly a sister, or Thady may have predeceased without issue.
- Thady O'Brien's military career — Austrian army records from the Maria Theresa period may survive in Vienna.
- Whether parish registers in Moyvanine or Clounties survive for the O'Bryen family.
