# Étienne Stump

In Persian letters and memoirs he is the court dentist with a surname that never quite settles—اشتومپ، اشتمپ، استامپ—while in English reference books he is often called Austrian or German. The record here is simpler: he was Swiss, born in Tallinn when the city was still the Russian Empire’s Reval, and he spent nearly half a century in Tehran shaping how the capital thought about modern teeth.

That reputation was earned in public rooms and private houses alike. Visitors remember a busy practice toward the end of Lalezar, porcelain sets priced in tomans, and a garden estate in Mahmoudieh that later fed into one of north Tehran’s most recognizable neighbourhoods. Simin Daneshvar’s novel *Savūshūn* even turns his name into a proverb: a proper smile meant a proper set of teeth from Dr Eshtamp—evidence, if any were needed, that he had crossed from foreign specialist into folk memory.

The [Europe-only line from Thurgau to Tallinn](../stories/stump-thurgau-tallinn-baltic-line.md) explains how a Swiss father and a Baltic German mother could raise a son beside the Gulf of Finland. What came next was stranger still: training in Chicago, a bruising brush with an Illinois licensing board, hire in New York as dentist to the grand vizier on an American tour, and then decades beside Ahmad Shah’s court and the classrooms of Dār al-Funūn, where chroniclers credit him among the first to teach dentistry in a systematic way.

He was arrested once in the Great War’s paranoia, released as a neutral national, and outlived his first marriage and the Qajar world he had first known. Second marriage brought Russian émigré society onto the Mahmoudieh estate. Through it all he accumulated land, beer-making interests north of the city, and the ambiguous blessing of wealth: one Majles debate even recycled the tale that only the Shah and Stump owned motor cars in 1912—likely embroidery, but telling.

Persian and Anglophone secondary works disagree on arrival year and on his age at death; parish Tallinn gives a firm March birth in 1880, while syndicated obituary arithmetic sometimes implies an earlier decade. The [synthetic Persian article on Beytoote](https://www.beytoote.com/art/negah-gozashte/dentist-atabak2-ahmadshah.html), widely mirrored, is a convenient hub for the Atabak–Ahmad Shah thread ([extract](../sources/corpus/beytoote-stump-dentist-atabak-ahmadshah/extracted.web.md); [annotated English](../sources/corpus/beytoote-stump-dentist-atabak-ahmadshah/translation.en.md)).

## Family

His parents were [Marc Francois Stump](marc-francois-stump.md) and [Olga Caroline Erbe](olga-caroline-erbe.md). He married [Henriette Bottin Burgess](henriette-bottin.md)—not [Fanny Burgess](fanny-burgess.md)—and later [Vera Obolensky](vera-obolensky.md), the Russian émigré who ran a large guest house on his Mahmoudieh land before their marriage. Children include [Robert Marc Murard Stump](robert-marc-murard-stump.md), [Theodore Stump](theodore-stump.md), [Irene Stump](irene-stump.md) (“Loulou”), [Jacques Stump](jacques-stump.md), and [Jean Stump](jean-stump.md).
