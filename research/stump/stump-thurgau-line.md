Assessment of the Stump/Stumpp line in Thurgau

At this stage, the safest conclusion is that Hans Jacob Stump of Erlen and his son Marc François Stump almost certainly belong to the same broader northeastern Swiss Reformed surname landscape as the documented Stump families of Zürich and Thurgau, but the exact patrilineal connection is still unproven. The key problem is that surname presence alone does not establish descent from a specific Bürgerort line. For Swiss research, the decisive evidence will come from family registers, parish books, and especially Heimatort/Bürgerregister records, not from the surname by itself.

Two things do support the hypothesis of a connection. First, the surname Stump is established in Swiss surname reference tools: the online Familiennamenbuch der Schweiz lets you search Swiss surnames and their places of citizenship/origin here: https://familiennamenbuch.ch/ and specifically for the surname here: https://familiennamen.ch/name/Stump . Second, the name is clearly present in the Zürich–Thurgau Reformed world. The Zürich State Archives search portal is here: https://suche.staatsarchiv.djiktzh.ch/ and is worth searching for Stump/Stumpp marriages, citizenship notes, and family files. I would treat the Erlen/Sulgen parish books as the first proof stage, and only after identifying Hans Jacob’s exact home-origin entry would I try to connect him to a Zürich Stump branch.

Erlen parish-register situation

For Marc François, born in Erlen in 1834, the parish situation is crucial. Erlen did not function as a fully separate Reformed parish in the way many researchers first assume. The Historischer Verein des Kantons Thurgau guide to parish books states that Erlen belonged to Sulgen until the eighteenth century, that a Reformed church was built at Erlen in 1764, and that Erlen became an independent Reformed Kirchgemeinde only in 1819, still in a dependent relationship to Sulgen for a long time afterward. The PDF is here:

https://hvtg.ch/skin/frontend/PDF/publikationen/kirchgemeinden_pfarrbuecher_thurgau.pdf

That matches the local historical summary for Erlen, which says that Erlen had always belonged to the parish of Sulgen before the church was built in 1764, and that in 1819 it received its own cemetery and church administration while Catholics remained with Sulgen:

https://hls-dhs-dss.ch/articles/001869
https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erlen_TG

For actual records, the most useful online entry is the FamilySearch catalog for Sulgen Reformed church books, which explicitly covers baptisms, marriages, deaths, confirmations, family books, and even temporary resident / citizen registration material:

https://www.familysearch.org/en/search/catalog/53343

That catalog is especially valuable because it states that the records were microfilmed from the original in the Bürgerarchiv Frauenfeld (“Mikrofilme aufgenommen vom Original im Bürgerarchiv Frauenfeld”). It also lists the relevant chronological spans, including books covering 1802–1865 and several Familienbuch volumes, including one for 1834. So for a birth in Erlen in 1834, I would begin with the Sulgen Reformed parish books, not with a separate Erlen series.

The cantonal archival side is also important. The Staatsarchiv Thurgau search portal is here:

https://query-staatsarchiv.tg.ch/

One directly relevant archival entry is the dossier “Sulgen-Erlen, 1846–1865”, which confirms that Sulgen/Erlen material is preserved in the cantonal archive:

https://query-staatsarchiv.tg.ch/detail.aspx?ID=140682

So the short answer is: yes, the relevant parish books were microfilmed by FamilySearch, but the controlling archival context is in Thurgau, and consultation should be checked both through FamilySearch and the Staatsarchiv Thurgau.

Migration context: why would a Thurgau Swiss Reformed man end up in Tallinn?

Marc François Stump’s move to Tallinn by 1868 makes historical sense. In the nineteenth century, Swiss migration east into the Russian Empire was often specialist migration, not mass peasant colonization. The broader Swiss emigration context is summarized here:

https://hls-dhs-dss.ch/articles/007988

For Baltic and Russian connections specifically, one useful example from the Historisches Lexikon der Schweiz is Leonhard Weber, a Swiss merchant active in St. Petersburg and Reval (Tallinn), dealing in linen, mousseline, silk ribbons, timber, and estate-based dairy production:

https://hls-dhs-dss.ch/de/articles/029309/

That example matters because it shows the kinds of Swiss commercial and technical niches already established in the eastern Baltic/Russian sphere: textile trade, merchant networks, processing industries, agricultural improvement, and applied technical skills.

For someone from Erlen/Thurgau, the strongest migration context is probably textile-related commerce or an industry adjacent to textiles, with engineering/mechanics as a secondary possibility. Erlen’s own economic history points strongly in that direction. According to the Historisches Lexikon der Schweiz, Erlen became significant in the 17th and 18th centuries through linen trade and dyeing, and in the 19th century mechanical embroidery was added as an industrial occupation:

https://hls-dhs-dss.ch/articles/001869

That makes a Thurgau-to-Baltic move in search of commercial or technical opportunity very plausible. I would rank the likely occupational contexts this way:
	1.	Textile / linen / embroidery / cloth trade
	2.	Commercial agency or merchant work
	3.	Mechanical / engineering / workshop skills tied to industrialization
	4.	Watchmaking, possible as a general Swiss migration pattern, but less directly rooted in Erlen’s local economy than textiles

So unless Tallinn records identify him explicitly as a watchmaker, I would start with the assumption that Marc François came out of the eastern Swiss textile-commercial world.
