Eylandt surname: origins, distribution and sources for Tallinn / Riga Lutheran registers (ca. 1800–1850)

Origin and distribution of the Eylandt surname
	•	Distribution and rarity. The surname Eylandt is extremely rare; the Forebears on‑line database lists it as the 2,689,253rd most common surname, borne by about 52 people worldwide, with c. 35 bearers in Estonia and small numbers in the United States and Germany ￼. A few modern bearers include the Estonian show‑jumping rider Lisette Eylandt (b. 2003) and business people in Tallinn; there are no well‑known historical figures carrying the name.
	•	Probable origin. The spelling “Eylandt” is an archaic form of Dutch/Low German eyland/eiland, meaning “island.” The Dutch explorer Abel Tasman named Groote Eylandt in northern Australia in 1644; the article notes that the name is Dutch for “large island” in archaic spelling and that the modern Dutch spelling is Groot Eiland ￼. Old Dutch place‑names such as Roodt Eylandt (“Red Island,” the origin of Rhode Island) and Conyne Eylandt (“Rabbit Island,” early Coney Island) use the same form, showing that eylandt was standard seventeenth‑century Dutch for “island.” Baltic Germans and Low‑German merchants were active in Tallinn and Riga during the Hanseatic period, and it is likely that Eylandt was originally a toponymic surname adopted by a family with Dutch or Low‑German roots; variants such as Eyland, Eiland, Heylandt, Elandt and Hyland are listed by on‑line surname dictionaries (e.g., Geneanet and Forebears).
	•	Earliest bearers. The earliest documented members of the family are found in Estonian parish registers. Genealogical websites list Carl Ludwig Eylandt (c. 1770–1843) as a resident of Kadrina Parish in Lääne‑Viru County and Julius Ellandi Eylandt (1898–1969) born in Tallinn, but these are user‑submitted trees and should be verified with primary records. The concentration of the surname in Estonia suggests that the family became established in the Baltic provinces by the late‑eighteenth century, probably as part of the Baltic‑German community.

Lutheran parish registers in Reval / Tallinn (ca. 1800–1850)

Estonian Lutheran churches began keeping systematic registers in the eighteenth century. An article by the Foundation for East‑European Family History Research explains that parish registers from 1834 onward have been systematically preserved and that original parish registers through 1940 are now housed in the State Historical Archive in Tartu (Estonian Historical Archives); some original books from Tallinn’s churches remain in local custody and are kept at the Tallinn City Archive ￼. The same article notes that registers before the 1830s are more fragmentary but many survive.
	•	Key parishes. In the early nineteenth century the Lutheran congregations in Reval/Tallinn were divided along ethnic lines. The German‑speaking Baltic‑German community worshipped at St Mary’s Cathedral (Toomkirik) and St Nicholas’ Church (Niguliste); the large merchant‑church of St Olaf (Oleviste) also had a German congregation, while St Michael’s Church served Swedish and Estonian parishioners. Researchers tracing the Eylandt family should start with the German church books for these congregations.
	•	Where to find the records. The Saaga digital portal of the Estonian National Archives contains digitised images of most Lutheran parish registers. The Estonian National Archives (VAU) notes that the church records of Tallinn and the family lists (c. 1926–1944) are preserved in the Tallinn City Archives, but many parish registers have been digitised in Saaga ￼. Saaga’s Church Records section lists each parish; researchers can browse birth (sündinud), marriage (laulatatud), death (maetud) and confirmation registers. Access requires free registration and the interface is available in English. If a register is not digitised, the Archival Information System (AIS) provides reference codes to request the original at the Tartu or Tallinn reading rooms. Microfilms of Tallinn registers (1834–1911) are also available through the Family History Library.
	•	Additional sources. Because Estonian peasants only adopted fixed surnames in the 1830s, researchers may need to consult the 1826 Livonian naming registers (EAA 1865) and the 1835 Estonian naming books (EAA 1864) for families without surnames ￼. Population revision lists (soul revisions), taxation lists (wacka books) and military conscription lists held in the Estonian archives can provide supplementary information ￼.

Lutheran parish registers in Riga (ca. 1800–1850)

In Riga the Lutheran church likewise served separate ethnic communities. The main parishes in the nineteenth century were St Peter’s Church, St John’s Church and St Gertrude’s Church. St Gertrude Old Church had a long association with Riga’s German ethnic community; the congregation worshipped in the German language ￼. By the early twentieth century the parish split into German and Latvian congregations ￼. St Peter’s and St John’s served both German and Latvian parishioners and held important civic functions. An Eylandt ancestor living in Riga around 1800–1850 would likely appear in the registers of the German congregation of St Gertrude or in St Peter’s.
	•	Archives and online access. Latvian Lutheran registers, revision lists and the 1897 Russian Empire census are preserved in the Latvian State Historical Archives in Riga. The archive’s Raduraksti portal provides free digital access to church books (birth, marriage and death), Rabbinate records, revision lists and the 1897 census. A genealogy guide notes that Raduraksti requires users to create an account and is available in English, Latvian, Russian and German ￼. Researchers should search by parish (draudze) and time period; the registers are organised alphabetically and often labelled in German or Russian. Not all Riga parish registers are digitised; for gaps, users must consult the reading room of the Latvian State Historical Archives or hire a local researcher.
	•	Using Riga records. Church books before 1850 are primarily in German, with occasional Latin or Russian notes. Entries typically record the child’s name, parents (including the father’s occupation), godparents and residence. After the Russian Empire standardised vital statistics, entries were made in Russian; German congregations sometimes kept parallel registers. Researchers should also consult household revision lists (usually 1811–1850) for Livonia, which list household members and can link an Eylandt to a specific parish. For the later period, Riga family books (Rīgas mājas grāmatas) compiled in the 19th–20th centuries provide residential data.

Summary

The Eylandt surname is a very rare Low‑German/Dutch‑derived name meaning “island.” Its concentration in Estonia suggests that the family belonged to the Baltic‑German community. To trace the Eylandt family in Tallinn (Reval) for 1800–1850, focus on the Lutheran parish registers of St Mary’s Cathedral, St Nicholas, St Olaf and St Michael’s—records preserved at the Estonian Historical Archives in Tartu and digitised through Saaga ￼ ￼. For Riga, concentrate on St Gertrude’s Old Church and other Lutheran parishes serving the German community; their registers are held by the Latvian State Historical Archives and available online via Raduraksti ￼ ￼. Supplementary sources such as naming registers, revision lists and naming books can help bridge gaps and identify Eylandt ancestors.
