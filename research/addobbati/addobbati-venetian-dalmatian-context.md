Addobbati in Venetian‐Dalmatian Context and the Habsburg Postal Service

Origins and status of the Addobbati family in Dalmatia

The surname Addobbati appears regularly in the genealogical records of  Zara from the late seventeenth century onward.  An inventory of the Obitelj Addobbati fonds in the Državni arhiv u Zadru (DAZD, State Archive of Zara) states that the family originated in Bergamo and settled in Dalmatia in the early 1700s.  The first entries in Zara parish registers record a marriage in 1730 and births in 1731.  The inventory notes that the family was considered part of the Zara bourgeoisie but not originally noble.  Luigi Addobbati (c. 1769–1824), a lawyer (causidico), and his brothers Salustio and Don Giovanni were admitted to the council of Nin (the small inland town of Nin) in 1793; his sons Pietro and Vincenzo were enrolled in 1804 ￼.  Although they petitioned the Austrian heraldic commission for confirmation of nobility in 1817, the commission refused, and the family remained classified as prominent citizens rather than nobility ￼.

A later study on the List of Nin Nobles explains that the Addobbati descendants were accepted as cives (citizens) of Zara in 1733 and as nobles of Nin in 1804; a 1745 document from Bergamo described them as cives originarios rather than patricians ￼.  Dr. Petar Addobbati received the papal title of Knight of the Golden Spur in 1786 ￼—a form of honour often granted to educated laymen, priests or lawyers.  Yet when the Austrian authorities reorganised the nobility after 1814, the Addobbati petitions were denied, reinforcing their status as elite citizens (Zara’s cittadini), not patricians.

Cittadini originari and their civic role

The Addobbati belonged to the category of cittadini originari—hereditary citizens of Venice and its maritime cities—rather than patricians.  A general description of this class notes that cittadini originari were the descendants of non‑noble but “original” inhabitants of Venice.  They were inscribed in the Libro d’Argento (Silver Book) since 1315 and had access to public offices below the patrician level.  The requirements for recognition included being born in Venice for at least three generations, living off “liberal arts” rather than manual labour and maintaining an honourable reputation ￼.  They could hold prestigious bureaucratic posts such as lawyers, notaries, secretaries to the Senate or the Cancelliere Grando ￼.  A recent study of cittadini originari emphasises that the Maggior Consiglio only codified these rules in 1569; male members of a family had to be born and reside in Venice for at least three generations and avoid arti meccaniche (manual trades) ￼.  Most cittadini served as notaries or chancellery clerks, forming a bureaucratic middle class and expressing values of mediocritas (moderation) and onorevolezza (respectability) ￼.

This context helps explain why the Addobbati, despite occasional honors, remained within Zara’s civic elite rather than the nobility.  Members of the family served as notaries and legal professionals.  The DAZD Notaries of Zara inventory lists Angelo Daniele Addobbati (1806–1831) among notaries, and the introduction notes that notaries were educated at Italian universities and held public trust; under Austrian rule the basic organisation of notarial practice changed little from the Venetian era ￼.  Other archival fonds show the family engaged in property administration and lawsuits.  The Petricioli family archive includes a land register from 1814 certified by a notary Addobbati, as well as a court case between the Addobbati brothers and Gaspar Caligarich in 1801 ￼ ￼.  The Filippi family inventory references a document concerning Pietro Addobbati ￼, while the Croatian Nobility Index lists the surname under Heyer von Rosenfeld’s Adel des Königreichs Dalmatien (1873), confirming that genealogists and armorials took note of the family’s claim to nobility ￼.  Overall, the Addobbati were part of a network of Venetian families present in Dalmatia—including Boara, Coltelli, Sandri and Carobbi—and intermarried with them in later generations.

Habsburg administration and the “Assistente alla Direzione delle Poste”

After the fall of the Venetian Republic in 1797, Dalmatia changed rulers multiple times before becoming a stable province of the Habsburg Empire in 1814.  The Austrian authorities reorganised the postal service, establishing a hierarchical structure with postmasters, controllers and assistants.  Contemporary philatelic research notes that Austria opened the first post office in Zara on 29 July 1797; mail was dispatched twice weekly and a second office opened in Obrovac in 1798 ￼.  Johann Cratey, the initial administrator, proposed creating a higher postal administration (Oberpostamt) in Zara staffed by a senior administrator (salary 1,200 gulden), a controller (500 gulden) and a clerk (400 gulden).  Although many of his proposals were deferred due to the French occupation, an imperial decree of 14 April 1805 created the Higher Postal Administration of Zara, appointed Cratey head (salary 800 + 200 gulden) and confirmed local postmaster Johann Ivanković as postal clerk ￼.  These figures provide insight into salaries and relative ranks.

The position of I.R. Assistente alla Direzione delle Poste (Imperial‑Royal postal assistant) occupied the lower tier of this hierarchy.  Surviving lists of Dalmatian public officials from the early 20th century show dozens of names labelled Assistente postale, Officiante postale and Ufficiale postale among other civil service titles ￼.  The assistant rank fell below the Ufficiale postale (postal officer) and above clerks or apprentices, suggesting a mid‑level bureaucratic status.  Salary data from the early 1800s indicates that a controller earned 500 gulden, a clerk 400 gulden and a carrier 300 gulden; thus a later 19th‑century assistant likely received a modest but respectable income.  In the C. Dalbello essay on the Italian consular presence in Dalmatia, Michele Dalla Costa—an Imperial‑Royal postal assistant at Sinj—is described as the “last male of the Dalla Costa family” ￼, implying that the post was notable enough to identify an individual’s status in biographical narratives.  Consequently, Pietro Pio Addobbati (1852–1919), who served as I.R. Assistente alla Direzione delle Poste in Zara, would have enjoyed a stable middle‑class position within the Habsburg administration.  His role involved assisting the director of the Zara postal office, overseeing correspondence, accounts and possibly supervising subordinate clerks.

Locating Addobbati records: archives and genealogical sources

The Državni arhiv u Zadru (DAZD) holds the key archival collections for Zara.  The Addobbati family fonds (HR‑DAZD‑342) spans 1732–1930 and includes genealogical notes, deeds, testaments and correspondence.  It shows the family preparing elaborate genealogies for their nobility petitions ￼.  Researchers can consult the inventory online via DAZD’s website; physical access requires an appointment and permission to view original documents.

DAZD also maintains the Notaries of Zara series (HR‑DAZD‑31), which preserves protocols of Zara notaries from 1288–1797 and some 19th‑century registries.  Since Angelo Daniele Addobbati served as a notary from 1806 to 1831 ￼, his acts may appear in these protocols.  Family connections appear in other archival fonds, such as the Petricioli and Filippi collections, which contain property registers and court files signed or involving Addobbati members ￼ ￼ ￼.

For civil and parish registers, DAZD has digitised over 2,400 books of baptisms, marriages and deaths covering Roman Catholic, Orthodox and other communities.  The digital archive allows online search by place; however, not all volumes are yet uploaded and researchers must sometimes consult the physical archive ￼.  Microfilms of Zara parish registers were created by the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter‑day Saints (FamilySearch) during the 1980s.  Because access to FamilySearch is restricted, it is advisable to consult DAZD or local genealogical societies for details on available microfilms and indices.  Some Croatian genealogists maintain surname databases for Dalmatia that may contain transcriptions of Addobbati baptisms and marriages.

Conclusion

The Addobbati family illustrates how Venetian civic families transplanted to Dalmatia navigated changes in sovereignty.  Originally Bergamasque immigrants, the Addobbati became cittadini originari in Zara, pursuing careers as lawyers, notaries and public administrators.  They were accepted into the council of Nin and petitioned for noble recognition but were ultimately classified as elite citizens rather than patricians ￼ ￼.  Under Habsburg rule, members of the family continued to serve the state; Pietro Pio Addobbati held the mid‑ranking post of I.R. Assistente alla Direzione delle Poste, a role that carried respectability and stable income within the postal hierarchy ￼ ￼.  The Addobbati’s story is preserved in the collections of the State Archive of Zara, the notarial records and other family fonds, and can be traced further through parish registers and microfilmed genealogical resources.  Researchers seeking to reconstruct the family’s genealogical tree or study the broader network of Venetian‑Dalmatian civic families will find a rich tapestry of documents, reflecting both the local administrative structures and the cultural aspirations of the cittadini originari.
