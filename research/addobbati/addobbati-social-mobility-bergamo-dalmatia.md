🏛️ 1. Did the Addobbati keep trying after 1817?

❗ Short answer:

There is no strong evidence they succeeded later, and likely they did not persist effectively after the failed Austrian petition.

What we do know:
	•	1817: They formally petitioned the Austrian (Habsburg) authorities for recognition of nobility
	•	Result: ❌ Rejected

This matters because after 1814:

Only Vienna (not Venice, not the Pope) controlled noble recognition in Dalmatia

⸻

What usually happened after rejection?

Families had 3 possible paths:
	1.	Try again with stronger proof
	•	genealogies
	•	heraldry
	•	service records
	2.	Seek ennoblement through state service
(military, bureaucracy, finance)
	3.	Stop trying and remain elite citizens

👉 The Addobbati seem to have followed option #3

Why?
	•	No later confirmed Austrian ennoblement
	•	They continue appearing as:
	•	notaries
	•	officials
	•	postal administrators

👉 That’s a stable bourgeois-administrative trajectory, not a noble one.

⸻

🏙️ 2. What is “Nin” (your “nim”)?

📍 Nin

Nin is a small historic town near Zara.

Historically:
	•	One of the oldest Croatian royal centers
	•	Had its own local council / noble body
	•	Under Venice → then Austria

⸻

🧠 Why Nin matters for your family

When the Addobbati were:

“admitted to the council of Nin”

This means:

✔️ They entered a local patriciate / noble-like body

But here’s the key:

⚠️ Nin nobility ≠ Venetian or Austrian nobility

It was:
	•	A municipal elite status
	•	Sometimes semi-hereditary
	•	Often easier to access than true state nobility

⸻

⚖️ 3. Did it ever “work”?

✅ Partially — locally

❌ Not at state level

✔️ What worked:
	•	They became:

	•	legal roles

👉 So socially:

They succeeded in becoming upper-tier urban elite

❌ What did NOT work:
	•	They were not recognized as nobles by Austria

	•	title

	•	imperial nobility diploma

Level	Status	Addobbati

Upper 10–15% of society — but not noble
