The Addobbati Family: Social Mobility Between Bergamo and Dalmatia

Origins in Bergamo and Move to Zara

The Addobbati surname originated in Bergamo (in the Venetian Terraferma). According to a report of the Nin nobility compiled in 1817, the family produced a chain of public documents issued by the Bergamo magistracy in 1745 that traced their lineage back to Pietro de Adobatis in 1495 ￼. The document, which included the family coat of arms, described them as cives originarios—hereditary citizens—rather than patricians and identified their descendants Daniele, Lorenzo and Giuseppe, sons of Giovanni (Ivan) Addobbati ￼. These brothers arrived in Zara (Dalmatia) as Venetian cavalry officers and, on 13 November 1733, were admitted to the Zara council of citizens, making them hereditary members of the cittadini originari class ￼. The cittadini originari were descendants of the non‑noble but long‑established inhabitants of Venice; they had to prove three generations of legitimate birth, absence of manual work and good conduct to be recorded in the Libro d’Argento (Silver Book) ￼. While below the patriciate, original citizens could hold high bureaucratic posts—lawyers, notaries, secretaries, or the Cancelliere Grando—and were socially respected ￼.

Zara’s ethnic composition changed markedly from the 16th century onwards as merchants, craftsmen and officials from the Terraferma settled in the city. The community’s governance was divided between the noble council and the citizen council, the latter being a hereditary body of wealthy citizens such as merchants, notaries and professionals. Admission to the council conferred civic privileges and social recognition; it also enabled the Addobbati to intermarry with families such as Pasini, Marchi and Canova, forging alliances within the urban elite ￼.

Entry into Nin’s Noble Council and Petition for Nobility

Zara’s council of citizens offered prestige but not nobility. For centuries the small town of Nin maintained its own communal nobility, whose members enjoyed privileges comparable to those of other Dalmatian noble bodies. During the Kandyan War, the Venetians allowed the Nin council to adopt new families to replenish its numbers ￼. As Zara’s original elites dwindled, families like the Addobbati sought further advancement. In 1793, Luigi (Lujo) Addobbati and his relatives petitioned the Venetian authorities for admission to the Nin noble council ￼. On 24 June 1804, brothers Dr. Petar (Pietro) and Ivan Vicko Addobbati, sons of Lujo, were formally inscribed as nobles of Nin ￼. This local ennoblement offered social capital within Dalmatia but lacked recognition from state authorities.

To secure formal nobility, the Addobbati compiled genealogies and evidences of their Bergamasque ancestry. The 1745 testimonial from Bergamo and the family’s later distinctions—especially Dr. Petar’s investiture as Knight of the Golden Spur (16 October 1786) and as lateran count by Bishop Ivan Dominik Stratico—were meant to demonstrate nobility ￼. The order of the Golden Spur was a papal honour bestowed on educated professionals; by the 18th century it was often granted to lawyers, notaries and physicians. It conferred personal prestige but no hereditary title, and many recipients were members of the upper bourgeoisie rather than patricians. Nonetheless, the Addobbati integrated it into their narrative to emphasise aristocratic connections.

Following the fall of the Republic (1797) and the brief French administration, Dalmatia came under the Habsburg monarchy (1814). The Austrian state insisted that noble status must be confirmed by imperial authorities. In 1817 the communal administration of Nin submitted a petition to the Heraldic Commission in Zara, requesting confirmation of nobility for 52 families, including the Addobbati. The commission dismissed the request, arguing that membership in the Nin council did not constitute nobility recognised by the Austrian Empire ￼. Consequently, the Nin nobility lost legal standing and faded into obscurity ￼. The Addobbati’s attempt illustrates how local elites sought to adapt to new political frameworks but encountered stricter criteria for nobility.

Professional and Family Networks

Despite the failed petition, the Addobbati remained influential. The 1817 list records four family members among the Nin nobles: Dr. Petar and Ivan Vicko, sons of Lujo; Petar and Josip, sons of Ivan Vicko ￼. Genealogical notes in the HAZU study further detail the family’s lineage: Danijel and Lorenzo, sons of Ivan, were admitted to Zara citizenship in 1733; their descendants intermarried with the Petricioli and Scarpi families. Lujo’s sons Petar and Ivan Vicko joined Nin’s nobility in 1804, while Petar was decorated as Knight of the Golden Spur and lateran count in 1786. By 1799 Petar was practicing as a lawyer in Zara. During the French period (1806–1813), members of the Zara citizen council included Anđelo, Ante, Dominik, Petar, Salustio and Vicko Addobbati, suggesting a broad family network active in municipal administration.

One branch produced Angelo Daniele Addobbati (1760s?–1831), a notary in Zara whose documents are preserved in the State Archive. Notaries were key figures in the urban economy; as public officials they recorded contracts, wills and property transactions. Angelo’s register (1806–1831) shows the family’s ongoing role in legal affairs and provides invaluable genealogical data. The notary’s presence underscores how the Addobbati built their status through professional service rather than inherited titles.

Habsburg Administration and Postal Service

After 1814, Dalmatia became part of the Austrian Kingdom of Dalmatia. The Habsburg bureaucracy valued education and loyalty; many Italian‑speaking Dalmatian elites entered imperial service. One such branch of the family produced Pietro Pio Addobbati (1852–1919). In the Società Dalmata di Storia Patria volume of 2017, a protest list from 1909 names “ADDOBBATI Marino – cancellista giudiziario,” “ADDOBBATI Pietro Pio – Ufficiale postale superiore,” and “ADDOBBATI Vincenzo – Ispettore del catasto” ￼. These Italian officials, along with over 500 others, signed a petition against the introduction of Croatian in the administration. The entry identifies Pietro Pio as a “superior postal officer” (Ufficiale postale superiore). Another biography describes him as “I.R. Assistente alla Direzione delle Poste”—an Imperial‑Royal assistant to the postal director—suggesting he held a mid‑ranking post within the provincial postal administration. In the Habsburg postal system, each crown land had a Direzione delle Poste headed by a director and assisted by one or more assistenti. These assistants managed staff and operations; the role required a secondary or university education and often led to career advancement. Serving as an assistant conferred respectable income and social status, aligning with the Addobbati tradition of bureaucratic service rather than landed wealth.

Later History and Diaspora

By the late 19th and early 20th centuries, many Dalmatian Italians emigrated due to national conflicts and economic pressures. The Addobbati appear among the Italian exiles recorded by the Società Dalmata di Storia Patria. Some family members moved to Trieste or Italy proper; others settled in Egypt, the Americas or Australia. The surname remains in use in Italy today but is rare in Dalmatia. Notable 20th‑century bearers include Vincenzo Addobbati, an inspector of cadastre mentioned in the 1909 petition, and Simeone Addobbati, author of a history of the Dalmatian Regiment published in the 1980s.

Archival and Genealogical Sources

State Archive of Zara

The Državni arhiv u Zadru (DAZd) holds the primary records on the Addobbati. The family fonds (Spisi obitelji Addobbati) contain notarial copies of the 1745 Bergamasque testimonial, the 1786 Golden Spur diploma and correspondence relating to the 1817 petition ￼ ￼. The fonds also include parish records of baptisms, marriages and deaths (1730s–1860s) and property registers. Access requires an archival request; many documents are in Italian or Latin script. Some registers have been microfilmed by FamilySearch and can be consulted online; others are available in the archive’s reading room.

Parish and Civil Records

Genealogists should consult the parish registers of St. Anastasia (Sveta Stošija) and other Zara churches, preserved in the Diocesan Archive, for births, marriages and deaths from 1740 onward ￼. Civil registration began in the 19th century under Austrian rule; copies are held in Zara’s civil registry office and the State Archive. The Nin municipal records (Libri Consiliorum) list the Addobbati among council members and contain minutes of their admission in 1804 ￼. Researchers should also consult the Petricioli and Filippi family fonds, which include marriage alliances and property transactions linking them to the Addobbati.

Conclusion

The Addobbati illustrate the pathways of social mobility available to educated Italians in Venetian and Habsburg Dalmatia. Originating as hereditary citizens of Bergamo, they relocated to Zara and secured a place in the Zara citizen council ￼. Through professional roles—lawyer, notary, and later imperial postal official—they rose within local society. Their admission to the Nin noble council in 1804 and the award of the Golden Spur show how honours and municipal nobility were used to bolster status, even though the 1817 Austrian petition for formal recognition failed ￼. The family’s later service in the Habsburg bureaucracy, exemplified by Pietro Pio Addobbati, continued this tradition, demonstrating that bureaucratic careers and civic service were key avenues for upward mobility in a multi‑ethnic empire. For genealogists, the Addobbati’s story underscores the value of notarial records, council minutes, and parish registers in tracing families that straddle regional and social boundaries.
