
Armenian Officers in the Qajar Military (c. 1800–1850)

Background – the Qajar military apparatus

The Qajar dynasty (1789–1925) inherited a military institution that was fragmented and largely irregular. The royal household maintained a small standing guard, but the regime relied mostly on semi‑regular provincial militias and tribal levies that were called up only in time of need ￼. Provincial governors and tribal khans provided troops in exchange for appointments and used the revenue‑farming system to pay them ￼. Units were organised in decimal divisions commanded by officers with titles such as min‑bāshi (commander of 1,000 men), pānṣad‑bāshi (500), yuz‑bāshi (100), panjāh‑bāshi (50) and dah‑bāshi (10). Pay was negotiated individually and there was no fixed pay scale ￼. According to Stephanie Cronin’s description of the early Qajar army, troops carried firearms and traditional weapons and fought largely for plunder; the only artillery comprised zanbūrak camel guns ￼. The army was assembled by royal order at the start of a campaign and disbanded after harvest; supply came from requisitioning from local populations, often causing famine ￼. These structural limitations explain why Fath‑Ali Shah (r. 1797–1834) and his son ʿAbbās Mirzā attempted to build a European‑style nezam army with foreign instructors. However, aside from small experimental units in Azerbaijan, the Qajar state never maintained more than a few thousand regular troops ￼.

Armenian participation and the gholām tradition

The Safavid practice of recruiting Christian gholāms (usually Georgian, Circassian and Armenian slaves) was revived under the early Qajars. Armenians living in Iranian territories enjoyed considerable autonomy—Fath‑Ali Shah and ʿAbbās Mirzā allowed them to build churches and schools, permitted their faith and even exempted them from compulsory military service ￼. This benevolence was pragmatic: Armenians constituted a significant commercial community and the shah wanted to secure their loyalty in the face of Russian expansion. Yet Armenians were not confined to mercantile roles. A recent study on Iranian Armenians in the Iran‑Russia wars notes that many Armenians served in the Qajar army, particularly in the artillery, and that some held important administrative or diplomatic posts ￼. Abbas Mirza sent Satut Khan (an Armenian) as the first Iranian envoy to London; other Armenians acted as interpreters and mediators with European states ￼. The same study emphasises that Armenian soldiers participated in both the Iranian and Russian armies during the wars, though their number was greater in the Iranian forces ￼.

High office was therefore not closed to Christians. Ḵosrow Khan Gorjī, an Armenian from Tbilisi captured by Qajar troops, was castrated and became a trusted eunuch in Fath‑Ali Shah’s court; he served as governor of Gīlān (1815–19) and later as vizier to the shah’s son Sayf ol‑Dowleh in Isfahan ￼. His competence led to further elevation: in 1834 he escorted the royal treasury to Tehran to ensure Mohammad Shah’s accession and was rewarded with the governorship of Isfahan ￼. Manūčehr Khan Moʿtamed‑al‑Dawla, another eunuch of Caucasian Christian origin, similarly served as vizier in Gīlān and then as governor ￼. These examples demonstrate that Qajar patronage networks could absorb Armenians (usually converts or eunuchs) and raise them to provincial command.

The case of David/Dāvūd Saginian and the Isfahan army

Among the best‑documented Armenian officers is Dāvūd (Davit) Khan Saginian, also called Mayor Khan. A contemporary Armenian source identifies him as head of the Isfahan army in the late Fath‑Ali Shah period ￼. During the 1834 Isfahan conflict, when Sayf ol‑Dowleh—Fath‑Ali Shah’s thirty‑ninth son and governor of Isfahan—faced a rebellion led by the powerful cleric Moḥammad Bāqer Shafti, Saginian commanded one wing of the provincial forces. According to the narrative preserved in the Sayf ol‑Dowleh article, the prince divided his army into two contingents: one under David Saginian, which attacked rebel positions outside the city, and the other under his own command that fought Shafti’s forces in the urban quarter of Bīdābād ￼. Saginian’s contingent eventually helped to suppress the rebellion, though the conflict left the city devastated.

The Isfahan episode illustrates the factional politics of the late Qajar court. Fath‑Ali Shah’s death in 1834 triggered a succession contest, with several princes vying for power. Sayf ol‑Dowleh supported his own claim and resisted the nomination of his nephew Moḥammad Shah. Shafti’s rebellion—fuelled by grievances against princely misrule—became a proxy for this wider struggle. That Sayf ol‑Dowleh relied on an Armenian commander underscores both the multi‑ethnic composition of provincial armies and the role of personal loyalty over ethnic or sectarian considerations. Nevertheless, once Moḥammad Shah secured the throne, he punished his rivals: Sayf ol‑Dowleh was recalled and replaced by Ḵosrow Khan Gorjī as governor ￼. Saginian disappears from the sources thereafter, suggesting that his fortunes were tied to his patron.

What did “head of the Isfahan army” mean?

In Qajar usage the sardār or provincial military commander oversaw local militia, garrisons and tribal contingents. As described above, the army’s structure was decentralised; provincial forces were often raised and financed by the governor through the toyūl system of land revenue assignments ￼. Holding the title of head of a provincial army thus gave its bearer considerable authority over recruitment, pay and logistics, as well as access to spoils and patronage networks. Because there was no fixed pay scale and commanders negotiated terms personally ￼, Saginian could ensure generous compensation for his troops and distribute offices to his clients. In addition, military commanders often served concurrently as tax farmers or viziers, blurring fiscal and military authority ￼. The position therefore conferred not only military prestige but also economic leverage.

For an Armenian family, such a post represented extraordinary social mobility. Qajar policy exempted Armenians from conscription and protected their communal autonomy ￼; to rise above these limits, one needed royal favour. Saginian, described as “Davit Khan,” received the honorific khan and commanded Muslim and Christian troops. Other Armenians who achieved similar rank—such as Ḵosrow Khan and Manūčehr Khan—were eunuchs, which reduced the political threat they posed to the Qajar lineage. Saginian’s appointment suggests that an Armenian who embraced the Qajar cause, perhaps by conversion or proven loyalty, could be admitted to the familial state of the dynasty and rewarded with titles, land grants and marital alliances. The research article notes that Fath‑Ali Shah issued decrees thanking Armenian individuals and granting them allowances and medals for wartime services ￼.

Conclusion

Between 1800 and 1850 the Qajar military apparatus remained irregular, composed of ad hoc provincial militias under princely commanders. Attempts at modernisation under ʿAbbās Mirzā were limited, and provincial armies continued to rely on traditional structures of min‑bāshi and yuz‑bāshi ￼. Within this system, Armenian officers carved out significant roles. Qajar rulers pursued pragmatic policies toward Armenians, granting them religious autonomy but also employing them as artillerymen, interpreters and, occasionally, high officials ￼ ￼. The careers of Dāvūd Saginian and Ḵosrow Khan Gorjī show that loyalty to the royal family could elevate Armenian individuals to governorships and provincial commands. The 1834 Isfahan conflict demonstrates how these officers were drawn into factional politics: Saginian’s command under Sayf ol‑Dowleh underscores the multi‑ethnic composition of Qajar forces, while his subsequent disappearance after the prince’s fall reveals the precariousness of such appointments. Ultimately, the title of head of the Isfahan army conferred on an Armenian both military authority and social advancement, illustrating the complex interplay between ethnicity, patronage and military service in early Qajar Iran.
