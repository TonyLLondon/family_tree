￼￼Modern Medicine and Dentistry in Iran during the Qajar–Pahlavi Transition (1900‑1940)

Early introduction of Western medicine

From the 1800s European physicians began to accompany diplomatic and military missions to Qajar Iran and gradually introduced modern medical concepts. British, French and Italian doctors such as Salvatori, Andrew Jukes and James Campbell vaccinated members of the court and wrote Persian‐language treatises on smallpox ￼. Crown Prince ʿAbbās Mirzā sent the first Iranian students to study medicine in England, signalling official support for Western medicine ￼. The French doctor Ernest Cloquet became Nāṣer‑al‑Dīn Shah’s physician; after his death, the Austrian Jakob Polak took the post and in 1851 became the first professor of medicine at the Dār al‑fonūn polytechnic ￼. Polak learned Persian to solve problems of translation and wrote several textbooks on anatomy and surgery ￼. He and the Dutch physician Johann Schlimmer created a Persian medical vocabulary and taught modern surgery and pharmacology ￼. Graduates of Dār al‑fonūn were sent to Europe for postgraduate training and later returned to teach, forming Iran’s first generation of Western‑trained physicians ￼.

Missionary hospitals also played a role. American missionaries opened the first American hospital and medical school in Tehran in 1893, and similar hospitals existed in Urmia, Tabriz, Mashhad and other cities ￼. British and Russian missions established hospitals that sometimes certified physicians ￼. These institutions supplemented the medical department of Dār al‑fonūn, which became an independent Medical School in 1918 and later formed the nucleus of Tehran University’s School of Medicine (1934) ￼. Because domestic resources were limited, the government sent hundreds of students abroad between 1928 and 1935 (including 125 medical students) to study medicine ￼.

Transition to dentistry

For centuries dentistry was part of general medicine. Medieval Persian physicians wrote about dental anatomy and toothache, but dentistry never became a separate specialty; barbers and masseurs performed extractions, and physicians treated dental problems ￼. Under Nāṣer‑al‑Dīn Shah (r. 1848‑96) the court physician Joseph Tolozan brought a Parisian dental technician known as “Sapivafor” to make false teeth and teach his craft ￼. Teachers at Dār al‑fonūn such as Mirzā ʿAlī Doktor and Mirzā Abu’l‑Ḥasan Khan incorporated dental anatomy and European techniques in their textbooks (1891) ￼. Even so, most people continued to visit barbers, goldsmiths or druggists for extractions and fillings until the Medical Practice Law of 1921 required dentists to hold licences ￼.

Polish and Swiss dentists and the birth of modern dental education

The turning point occurred around 1921 when two foreign dentists settled in Iran: Falak Kush Melczarski, a Polish dentist who later served Reżā Shah, and Étienne (Atkin) Stump, a **Swiss** dentist (memoirs and Iranica often mislabel him Austrian or German) ￼. The Ministry of Education licensed Dr Moḥsen Sayyāḥ—often regarded as the first fully qualified Iranian dentist—and Dr Harutiun Stepanian (who had studied in the United States) to train dental technicians ￼. These four men trained most of the first generation of Iranian dental technicians and laid the foundation for dental education ￼.

Under Reżā Shah the state formalised dental education. A Health Department report (1926) recommended training dentists, and regulations issued in 1928 required the government medical college to teach oral and dental diseases ￼. In 1930 a College of Dentistry (Madrasa‑ye ʿĀlī‑ye Dandān‑pezeškī) was attached to the medical college; it offered a five‑year diploma course and admitted fifteen students. Melczarski served as director and Sayyāḥ as deputy, assisted by Dr Ašot Haratūnīān and Dr Šahrīār Salāmat ￼. Pedal drills and vulcanizers replaced pincers and cauterising rods, symbolising the modernisation of dental equipment ￼. In 1934 the dental school became a department of the new Tehran University; Sayyāḥ taught dental anatomy, and other professors taught therapy, periodontics, technology and radiology ￼. By the 1950s the department became an independent Faculty of Dentistry with chairs in orthodontics, prosthodontics and oral surgery ￼. Iranian dentists were still rare; thus many students were sent abroad, but the local programmes gradually produced qualified dentists, and technicians trained by Melczarski and Stump continued to train apprentices ￼.

Étienne Stump and the Mahmoudieh estate

Dr Étienne (Atkin) Stump stands out among early dentists. This vault treats him as **Swiss**; older accounts often said Austrian origin with Swiss citizenship or called him German (Mustawfi). He allegedly came to Iran around 1904 as the private dentist of Mirzā ʿAlī‑Aṣghar Atabak and stayed on after his patron’s assassination ￼. Stump became dentist to Ahmad Shah Qajar and taught dental procedures at Dār al‑fonūn; he is described as the first dental instructor there ￼. Dr Moḥsen Sayyāḥ’s memoir recounts meeting Stump in the mid‑1920s: he was one of only three qualified dentists in Tehran, lived in a large garden north of Ferdowsī Square and operated a clinic on ʿAla‑al‑Dowla Street ￼. Sayyāḥ wrote that Stump was affable, skilled in riding and hunting, but secretive about his education ￼.

Stump accumulated significant wealth from his practice. Sayyāḥ recorded that he built a luxurious villa on the Mahmoudieh estate between Tajrish and Evin and filled it with antiques and carpets ￼. He even owned one of the earliest automobiles in Iran ￼. The memoir of parliamentarian Seyed Mohammad Kamra’ī describes Stump’s crowded clinic and mentions his French assistant, Monsieur Gol, who sold porcelain dentures for thirty‑five tomans ￼. A 1915 incident recorded in newspaper reports notes that Russian forces temporarily arrested Stump during World War I but soon released him ￼. In the late 1910s Stump and Dr Stepanian even proposed a dental school to the Ministry of Education, but their plan appears to have failed ￼.

Abdullah Mostowfi’s memoir connects Stump to the Mahmoudieh house. While recounting the history of the Mahmoudieh district of Tehran, Mostowfi notes that the land belonged to “Dr Stemp the dentist” ￼. In another passage he describes suffering from pyorrhea (periodontitis) three decades earlier, when few dentists practised and urban residents seldom visited barbers for tooth extractions. Mostowfi recalls that Dr Stump (whom he calls ‘German’) extracted his teeth “with European forceps and prior injection,” emphasising Stump’s modern methods ￼. By the early 1940s Stump had become wealthy, owned extensive lands in north Tehran, established a brewery in the Majidieh area and never returned to Europe. He died in Tehran in 1951 and his clinic was operated by Monsieur Gol ￼. Stump’s fame was such that his name appears in the novel Savushun, and the phrase “to get a set of teeth from Dr Stump” entered Iranian parlance ￼.

European influence and institutional legacy

European doctors shaped modern medicine and dentistry in Iran not merely by treating patients but by developing institutions. Austrian and Dutch professors at Dār al‑fonūn established curricula and terminology; French doctors such as Cloquet and Tolozan served as court physicians and oversaw public health reforms, including smallpox vaccination laws ￼. German, Polish and Armenian dentists introduced modern instruments, while American missionary hospitals brought Western nursing and medical education ￼. The Qajar–Pahlavi transition thus witnessed a layered exchange: state patronage, missionary initiatives and private practitioners all contributed to a gradual shift from traditional remedies to regulated medical and dental services. By the time of Tehran University’s establishment in 1934, Iran possessed a medical school, a dental department, a public health council and a cadre of professionals trained at home and abroad. The story of Étienne Stump, with his Swiss background, private practice and connection to the Mahmoudieh estate, illustrates how individual Europeans both profited from and contributed to this transformation.
