# Order of the Golden Spur (Milizia Aurata)

The **Ordine della Milizia Aurata**, commonly known as the **Order of the Golden Spur** (*Ordine dello Speron d'Oro*), is a papal chivalric honour whose origins reach to the first half of the fourteenth century. It is considered the second-highest order of the Holy See — below the Supreme Order of Christ and above the Pian Order. Membership is restricted to a single class of one hundred knights, conferred *motu proprio* by the Pope.

## Character and conferral

The Golden Spur began not as an order but as an **equestrian dignity** — a knighthood conferred through a solemn ceremony of investiture with arms, the military girdle (*cingolo militare*), and gilded spurs. Both Popes and Emperors conferred it. Candidates served a period as pages or in military service before the ceremony.

From the sixteenth century the dignity was linked to the title of **Count Palatine of the Lateran** (*Conte del Sacro Palazzo Lateranense*). The combination conferred personal nobility and, under certain pontificates (Paul III onward), generic hereditary nobility. The Sovereign Order of Malta recognised the Palatinate as nobility of lineage (*nobiltà generosa*) only when held together with the Milizia Aurata.

## Sub-collation

The Pope delegated conferral rights — *sub-collation* — to bishops, universities, and noble families. Paul III and Sixtus V granted the universities of **Macerata** and **Fermo** the power to bestow the Golden Spur and Palatinate in the Pope's name. The ducal house of **Sforza di Santa Fiora** (later Cesarini Sforza) held the privilege as a hereditary family right from 1539.

Sub-collation was the mechanism by which most provincial recipients received the honour. **Dr. Petar Addobbati** of Zara received his investiture on **16 October 1786** from **Bishop Ivan Dominik Stratico** — a classic example of episcopal sub-collation in a Venetian-Dalmatian diocese.

## Decline and reform

By the late medieval period the proliferation of sub-collation had diluted the distinction. Pius VII suppressed university and collegial sub-collation in **1815**. Gregory XVI persuaded the Cesarini Sforza to renounce their hereditary privilege and reconstituted the Milizia Aurata as a formal order under the patronage of **St. Sylvester I**, issuing the brief *Cum Hominum Mentes* on **31 October 1841**.

In **1905**, Pope Pius X separated the Golden Spur from the Order of St. Sylvester, restoring it as an independent order limited to **one hundred knights** — a restriction Fulvia Zerauschek's 1996 memoir cited as evidence of the family honour's rarity. The reform commemorated the golden jubilee of the dogmatic definition of the Immaculate Conception and placed the order under the patronage of the Blessed Virgin Mary. Its insignia became a golden eight-pointed cross with a small spur pendant, the monogram *Maria* on the obverse, and the date MDCCCCV with *PIUS X RESTITUIT* on the reverse, suspended from a red ribbon bordered in white.

## Famous recipients

Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart received the Golden Spur from Pope Clement XIV in **1770**, making him one of the order's most celebrated holders.

## Dr. Petar Addobbati — Knight of the Golden Spur, 1786

![Portrait of Dr. Pietro Addobbati with the Golden Spur cross on his lapel, annotated by Fulvia Zerauschek](../media/docs/fulvia-lewis-photos/zara-album-01/addobbati-portraits/PAddobbati01.jpg)
*Dr. Pietro Addobbati (1769–1832), Knight of the Golden Spur. Photograph of the family painting, annotated by Fulvia: "Holder of the Order of the Holy Spear / 100 holders at any one time."*

[Dr. Petar (Pietro) Addobbati](../people/dr-pietro-addobbati.md) (1769–1832), son of [Luigi Addobbati](../people/luigi-anzolo-alloisio-addobbati.md), received the Golden Spur and Lateran count dignity as a law student or young lawyer. The **1786 diploma** is preserved in the Addobbati family fonds at the [Državni arhiv u Zadru (HR-DAZD-342)](../sources/dazd-addobbati-family-fonds.md). The family used the honour as evidence in the **1817 Nin nobility petition** to the Habsburg Heraldic Commission. The miniature of the order was passed down through the family and given to **Peter Martin Lewis**.

Fulvia's 1996 memoir records the family tradition: Petar received the Golden Spur while still a student at the **University of Padova**, and only a hundred knights held the title at any time. She wrote to the Vatican seeking details but received no reply. ([Fulvia memoir](../sources/famhist-nonna-memoir-1996.md).)
