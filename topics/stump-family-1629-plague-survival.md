# The STUMP Family and the 1629 Swiss Plague Epidemic

**The Great Mortality of 1629** nearly wiped out the STUMP family in **Sulgen**, **Thurgau**, **Switzerland**. Five family members died in just four months during a devastating plague epidemic that swept across Europe as part of the **Thirty Years' War** (1618-1648). The survival of **Jacob Stump** and his young son **Augustin** through this crisis ensured the continuation of our documented STUMP lineage for over 400 years.

## The 1628-1630 European Plague Epidemic

### Origins and Spread

The bubonic plague outbreak began in **Northern France around 1623** and spread across Europe through troop movements during the Thirty Years' War. German and French imperial armies carried the disease along military routes, crossing the Alps **around 1629** and devastating Swiss communities.

This was part of the same epidemic that caused the catastrophic **Italian Plague of 1629-1631**, which killed an estimated **280,000 to one million people** in Northern Italy alone—approximately 35% of the affected population. Major cities suffered enormous losses: Milan lost 46% of its population, Venice 33%, and Verona 61%.

### Swiss Documentation

Academic research using parish registers from **Uri canton** provides detailed evidence of the 1629 plague's impact on Swiss Alpine communities. The epidemic allowed researchers to track deaths across thirteen parishes, examine mortality patterns, and analyze relationships between plague deaths and demographic recovery.

This pattern matches historical plague mortality, which often killed across age groups but particularly affected the very young, very old, and those weakened by malnutrition during wartime.

## The Survivors: Jacob and Augustin Stump

### Critical Survival

The survival of **Jacob Stump** (baptized 1606) and his infant son **Augustin Stump** (baptized 22 December 1628) through the 1629 epidemic was **crucial for genealogical continuity**. Augustin was barely ten months old when the plague struck Sulgen in August 1629.

**Jacob's survival factors:**
- **Age advantage:** At 23, Jacob was in the prime age group with better plague survival rates
- **Possible immunity:** May have survived earlier plague exposure or possessed natural resistance
- **Geographic isolation:** Possibly lived in a less affected part of Sulgen
- **Family care:** Wife **Barbel Cuntz** may have provided nursing care during illness

### Generational Bridge

Jacob and Augustin became the **essential bridge** connecting:
- **Pre-epidemic STUMP families** (1590s-1620s multiple lineages)
- **Post-epidemic continuation** (1630s-1800s single surviving line)

Without their survival, our documented STUMP lineage extending through **Hans Jacob Stump** (1800) to **Marc François Stump** (1834) would not exist.

## Historical Context and Significance

### Thirty Years' War Impact

The 1629 plague epidemic in Sulgen was directly connected to the **Thirty Years' War** (1618-1648), one of Europe's most devastating conflicts. Although Switzerland maintained neutrality, the country suffered from:
- **Military transit routes** carrying disease
- **Economic disruption** affecting food supplies and weakening populations
- **Refugee movements** spreading contagion across borders

### Demographic Recovery

The STUMP family's recovery from the 1629 crisis demonstrates remarkable resilience:
- **Augustin's longevity:** Lived 84 years (1628-1713), exceptional for the period
- **Family rebuilding:** Augustin married and had children, restoring family numbers
- **Geographic stability:** The family remained in Sulgen despite the traumatic losses
- **Generational continuity:** Seven more generations followed through to the 1800s

### Community Impact

The 1629 mortality crisis likely reshaped Sulgen's social structure:
- **Property consolidation** as families died out
- **Marriage patterns** as survivors sought new partnerships
- **Economic opportunities** for surviving families
- **Community memory** of the epidemic influencing later generations
