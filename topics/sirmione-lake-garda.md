# Sirmione on Lake Garda — The Family's Axis Mundi

Sirmione juts into Lake Garda like a finger: a narrow peninsula, barely four kilometres long, guarded at its neck by the thirteenth-century Scaliger castle. Beyond the castle gate the old town thins to a single road, flanked by olive trees and thermal springs, until it reaches the archaeological park of the so-called Grotte di Catullo — the remains of the largest Roman domestic villa in northern Italy. The peninsula sits at the meeting point of three regions — Lombardy, the Veneto, and the Trentino — and has drawn Italian industrialists, artists, and travellers since the Belle Époque. For the Lewis and Zerauschek families, Sirmione is the single place where every thread of the narrative crosses.

## Villa Ester (pre-war–c. 1950)

[Antonio Zerauschek](../people/antonio-zerauschek.md) — trader, banker, cigarette and chocolate manufacturer in interwar Zara — acquired the **Villa Giannantoni** on the tip of the peninsula at **Via Caio Valerio Catullo 7**, overlooking the Grotte di Catullo. The Mantuan industrialist Giannantoni had built it around 1928 with mixed styles and Swiss-inspired sloping roofs; Antonio renamed it **Villa Ester** after his wife [Ester Addobbati](../people/ester-addobbati.md). The family used it as their Lake Garda retreat — summers and holidays, the children growing up between Zara's Adriatic light and Garda's Alpine calm. A [photograph of Fulvia on the loggia](../media/docs/Fulvia%20Zerauschek%20at%20Villa%20Ester%20Sirmione.png) survives from this period: a young woman on the balustrade, framed by the open French doors and the park of conifers beyond.

The villa's ownership inscription on a 1940 book — *Al Congo con Brazzà*, "Fulvia Zerauschek, Villa Ester, Sirmione, Brescia" — captures the family's settled possession before the catastrophe.

In **April 1948**, [Carlo Enrico Rivolta](../people/giuliana-zerauschek-rivolta.md) wrote from Sirmione to Fulvia in a long, witty letter — he, [Giuliana](../people/giuliana-zerauschek-rivolta.md), and baby Daniela were staying in the house with Antonio and Ester ("i nonni"), who planned to wave handkerchiefs from the windows as the young family flew past in a tourist seaplane over the lake. By then the villa's days in the family were numbered.

## The 1st Welch at Sirmione (December 1945)

After the German surrender in Italy on 2 May 1945, the **1st Battalion, The Welch Regiment** — part of 61st Infantry Brigade, 6th Armoured Division — moved through Austria and the Trieste border zone before being stationed at Sirmione. **Battalion Orders No. 113** (4 Dec 1945, Lt-Col D.L.C. Reynolds OBE commanding) were issued from Sirmione, formally announcing [David John Lewis](../people/david-john-lewis.md)'s **U.S. Silver Star** under "Honours and Awards."

It was here, on the peninsula where the Zerauscheks spent their summers, that the Welsh major met the Italian industrialist's daughter. [Fulvia Ottilia Antonia Zerauschek](../people/fulvia-ottilia-antonia-zerauschek.md) was twenty-two, the family displaced from Zara by Allied bombing and Yugoslav confiscation; David was twenty-seven, a veteran of Sicily, Anzio, the Gothic Line, and the Po crossing.

Forty years later, Brigadier **O.G. Brooke CBE DSO**, David's former commanding officer, wrote from England: *"Wouldn't it be nice to escape from this filthy summer and escape to Sirmione?"* — the lakeside posting still resonated across the decades.

## Marriage and the corredo di nozze (October 1947)

David returned to Sirmione after demobilisation to marry Fulvia. By mid-October 1947 the couple were at Sirmione preparing the **corredo di nozze** — the traditional Italian wedding trousseau — for export to England. An Italian customs declaration, stamped by the **Banca d'Italia at Brescia** on **15 October 1947**, names her as **"Sig. Fulvia Zerauschek sposata al Sig. David J. Lewis"** with a marriage certificate attached. The authorisation came from the **Ministero del Commercio con l'Estero** (protocol 521538/189/Z/1467, dated 8 October 1947).

The four parcels shipped to England encompassed everything a young Italian bride needed to set up house in Wales: a black fur coat, a silver fox jacket, three silk dresses, a tailleur, four pairs of shoes, two tennis rackets, five books, eight pairs of gloves, Italian silver and tea service, a **Marelli Fido** radio, carpets, and woollen mattresses. David signed the English-language declaration at Sirmione on 16 October — giving his address as **32 Pendarren Street, Aberdare, South Wales** — and had it notarised at the **British Consulate General, Milan** the next day.

From Sirmione and the British Consulate in Milan, the couple headed to Wales, then Bristol (where David resumed his degree), then the Colonial Service: Tripolitania, Northern Rhodesia, Zambia, and finally Sussex. They would not live in Italy again.

## Villa Callas (December 1957)

In the immediate post-war years, Antonio sold the villa to **Angelo Marinoni**, a Veronese manufacturer of electric ovens. Marinoni in turn sold it in **December 1957** to **Comm. Giovanni Battista Meneghini**, who gave it to his wife **Maria Callas** — it became the famous **Villa Callas**.

A journalist from *La Settimana INCOM Illustrata*, **Savino Mariani**, visited on 21 April 1958 and left a vivid record. The article names Antonio directly as **"l'industriale zaratino Antonio Zerauschek, ora trasferitosi in quel di Firenze"** (the Zara industrialist, now relocated to the Florence area) — confirming the ownership chain: Giannantoni → Zerauschek → Marinoni → Meneghini/Callas. Callas arrived in a green olive Giulietta and spent three hours inspecting the restoration. Her first order had been *"Telefoni, telefoni"* — telephones in every room. Her second: *"Rose, rose."* The villa was registered in the cadastre as three floors including basement, twenty-one rooms, 780 square metres, with a park of 7,552 square metres. The family's physical magazine scans — seven pages including photographs of Callas on the villa steps and the restoration team — are the primary source for this account.

The full narrative: [Villa Ester — from the Zerauscheks to Maria Callas](../stories/zerauschek-villa-callas-sirmione.md).

## Burial (2000)

David died on **22 August 2000** at Brighton and Hove. Fulvia followed four months later on **20 December 2000**. They are buried together at **Sirmione** — the place where the war ended and their life together began. Not far from the villa loggia where Fulvia had sat as a girl, and from the terrace where David had received his Silver Star notice fifty-five years earlier.
