# The Lewis Lace Collection

Six hundred and seventy-four pieces of antique lace — Italian, French, Belgian, Flemish, English, Irish, Maltese, Bohemian, Hungarian, German, Spanish, Swedish — spanning five centuries of European needlework, from the sixteenth century to the twentieth. The collection was built over a lifetime by [Fulvia Zerauschek Lewis](../people/fulvia-ottilia-antonia-zerauschek.md), who knew lace the way her husband [David](../people/david-john-lewis.md) knew environmental policy: deeply, professionally, across the full range. After her death in 2000, her sons inherited it and sold it to the Province of Trento, where it now sits in a museum in the Trentino Alps. It is one of the few points in this family tree where a physical object — hundreds of physical objects — connects one woman's expertise to the institutional memory of the Italian state.

---

## Fulvia the collector

The collection was assembled by **[Fulvia Ottilia Antonia Zerauschek](../people/fulvia-ottilia-antonia-zerauschek.md)** (1923–2000) — the only daughter of [Antonio Zerauschek](../people/antonio-zerauschek.md) and [Ester Addobbati](../people/ester-addobbati.md), and the woman who married [David John Lewis](../people/david-john-lewis.md) in 1947 and carried the Dalmatian line into an English family. Fulvia was the lace expert: she knew the techniques, the periods, the provenances. Over decades — in England, in Italy, at auction and through dealers — she built a collection that spanned twelve countries and five centuries of European needlework.

She grew up in the world that produced such things. The [Addobbati](surname-addobbati.md) were *cittadini* of Zara from at least the seventeenth century — a civic family in the Venetian-Dalmatian milieu where lace was both a domestic art and a marker of standing. Venetian *punto in aria* and Dalmatian bobbin lace were household goods for families of their class. Her father [Antonio](../people/antonio-zerauschek.md) was an import-export trader with access to goods from across Europe through Zara's free port. Whether any pieces came out of Dalmatia in the family's 1943 flight is unknown, but Fulvia's eye and expertise were formed in that culture before she ever reached England.

By the time of her death at Hove in December 2000, the collection was large enough, old enough, and important enough to attract the attention of two of Italy's foremost textile scholars and to be described as among the most significant lace holdings in Europe.

---

## The sale (December 2006)

After Fulvia's death in December 2000, the collection passed to her sons — the "eredità familiare" (family inheritance) noted in the Province's official acquisition decision. They divided it and sold it to the **Provincia autonoma di Trento** in two simultaneous transactions, both decided on **21 December 2006**. The total acquisition comprised **674 laces** spanning the 16th to 20th centuries.

The two sellers were **Owen Michael Lewis** — described in the provincial records as "avvocato inglese, residente a Twickenham Middlesex" — and **[Ivor Antony Samuel Lewis](../people/ivor-anthony-samuel-lewis.md)** — "nato a Firenze e residente a Bolsena, di professione professore." Owen is Michele, Fulvia's son who lived at 44 Lebanon Park, Twickenham, with children Stephen and Alice; the "OML" prefix on correspondence files in the family archive stands for his legal name. Ivor is the analytical chemist and mass spectrometrist who had worked at King's College London and the Australian National University before retiring to Bolsena, on the volcanic lake north of Rome.

Vice-president **Margherita Cogo** (DS) told the *L'Adige* newspaper: "Le collezioni Lewis di merletti che abbiamo acquistato sono tra le più importanti d'Europa e una parte è stata battuta all'asta da Sotheby's a Londra" — the Lewis lace collections are among the most important in Europe, and part had been auctioned at Sotheby's in London.

---

## The appraisal

The Province commissioned two independent appraisals before purchasing. Both appraisers were scholars of acknowledged national standing:

**Doretta Davanzo Poli** (1945–2020) — conservator of the Museo del Merletto at Burano (1981–90), professor of the history of clothing and textile art at the University of Venice, curator of over seventy exhibitions worldwide, author of more than 160 publications on Venetian lace and textiles. Named "Venetian of the Year" in 2018. She died in December 2020.

**Paolo Peri** — textile expert and scholar.

Their conclusion: the collection's market value substantially exceeded the purchase price. Soprintendente **Laura Dal Prà** stated: "Sono pezzi rari di considerevoli dimensioni e delle manifatture maggiori d'Europa" — rare pieces of considerable size, from Europe's finest manufacturers.

On **17 May 2007**, Dal Prà and Davanzo Poli presented the collection publicly at a conference at **Palazzo Roccabruna** in Trento: *"Cinque secoli di merletti europei della Collezione Lewis: acquisizioni per il museo di Ala"* — Five centuries of European lace from the Lewis Collection.

## The laces

The collection encompasses both decorative lace (panels, borders, altar frontals, tablecloths) and clothing lace (collars, cuffs, flounces, veils), illustrating the technical and formal evolution of European lacemaking across five centuries. The geographic range — twelve countries — reflects the major centres of production:

- **Italy** — Venetian *punto in aria*, Burano needle lace, Milanese bobbin lace
- **France** — Alençon, Argentan, Valenciennes, Chantilly
- **Belgium and Flanders** — Brussels, Bruges, Mechelen (Malines)
- **England and Ireland** — Honiton, Carrickmacross, Irish crochet
- **Malta** — Maltese silk guipure
- **Central Europe** — Bohemian, Hungarian, and German bobbin laces
- **Spain** — blonde silk lace
- **Sweden** — Vadstena and other Scandinavian traditions

The chronological span — sixteenth to twentieth century — covers the entire arc of European lacemaking, from the earliest known needle laces of Renaissance Venice through the industrial machine laces of the nineteenth century.

---

## Destination

The laces are held by the Provincia autonoma di Trento as part of the collections destined for the **Museo provinciale delle Arti Tessili** (Provincial Museum of Textile Arts) at **Palazzo Taddei** in **Ala**, a town in the Vallagarina south of Trento. The museum, managed by the Castello del Buonconsiglio network since 2023, is scheduled to open in **2026**. Palazzo Taddei was built in the early 1500s by the Taddei family — Florentine in origin — who in 1657 launched Ala's celebrated velvet industry with the help of two Genoese weavers fleeing the plague. The building is tied to the textile story of the region.

The Lewis laces filled a gap in the Province's textile holdings. The foundational collection — the **Lorenzo Viesi collection from Cles**, acquired in 1992–93 — comprised some 2,300 textile specimens and 4,600 fringes and borders (fifteenth-century Venetian velvets, seventeenth-century brocades, eighteenth-century sacred vestments) but contained almost no lace. The Lewis acquisition completed the picture in a single stroke.

## Open questions

- **Sotheby's auction:** Cogo's comment that "part of them were auctioned at Sotheby's in London" implies the collection was once larger, or that separate pieces from the same provenance were sold commercially. The auction date and catalogue are not yet identified.
- **Museum display:** Whether the collection is currently on public display at Palazzo Taddei, or held in storage pending the 2026 opening, is not confirmed.
