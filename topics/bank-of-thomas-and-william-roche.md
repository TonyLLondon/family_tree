# The Bank of Thomas and William Roche, Limerick (1801–1825)

In 1801 two brothers from one of Limerick's oldest Catholic merchant families opened a bank on **Charlotte's Quay**. Thomas Roche was the elder, a quiet civic figure who would chair local meetings for half a century; William was the younger, flamboyant, politically fearless, and destined to become the first Catholic Member of Parliament for Limerick since the repeal of the penal laws. Between them they built one of the last successful private banks in Munster — and the only one in Limerick to survive the catastrophic banking crisis of 1820.

## Origins

Their father, [Stephen John Roche](../people/stephen-john-roche.md), had been "the oldest merchant in that city" when he died in 1804. The Roche merchant dynasty stretched back to John Lysaght Roche (1688–1760), oil-mill owner and trader, and before him to the Viscounts Fermoy through the Castletown-Roche line in Cork. By the time Thomas and William opened their bank, the family had been at the centre of Limerick's Catholic commercial elite for a century.

The Roche brothers were not alone in banking. Their brothers **Stephen and James** had opened a separate bank in Cork the previous year — **Roches' Cork Bank** (Stephen Roche & Co., 1800). The two operations were legally independent, a structural decision that would prove decisive twenty years later. The banks shared a common source of watermarked paper (both printed by Ashby of London) and used the family coat of arms — *Gules, three roaches naiant in pale* — on their early note issues.

## Charlotte's Quay to George's Street

The bank was initially registered at **Charlotte's Quay** in 1801. In **1804** the brothers purchased premises on **George's Street**, and in **1806** they announced the transfer of operations there. The George's Street building became the family's permanent base: Thomas lived above or beside the bank until his death in 1855, and it was the address given in his wife [Hellen Ankettle](../people/hellen-ankettle.md)'s death notice in 1849.

At the rear of the George's Street premises, William constructed his famous **Hanging Gardens** in 1808 — terraced gardens rising seventy feet above street level, covering approximately an acre, extending from George's Street to Henry Street. Built at a cost of **£15,000** (roughly €600,000 in today's money), the gardens were heated and cooled by a system of flues and vents, growing grapes, pineapples, peaches, and oranges in the upper hot-houses, pears and apples on the middle terraces, and flowers below. The gardens sat atop a series of vaulted stone stores. They became one of Limerick's landmarks, attracting visitors from across Ireland and mentioned in Fitzgerald's *History of Limerick*.

## Surviving the Crisis of 1820

On **25 May 1820**, Roches' Cork Bank — the brothers' separate operation run by Stephen and James — closed its doors. The collapse triggered a chain reaction across Munster: Leslie's Bank in Cork fell the same day; Maunsell's Bank in Limerick, which had been the city's first bank since 1789 and was considered unassailable, followed within days; and by 12 June, Alexander's Bank in Dublin had also failed. The crisis, later called the **Munster Banking Crisis**, was caused by a post-Napoleonic collapse in grain prices followed by a sharp fall in land values. Banks that had lent against land found their security evaporating.

**Thomas and William Roche's Limerick Bank was the only bank in Limerick to survive.**

The legal separation between the Cork and Limerick operations — maintained as distinct partnerships despite the family connection — shielded the Limerick bank from the contagion. While Maunsell's fell and the Cork Roches went under, Thomas held the Limerick bank steady. By 1824, Pigott's Trade Directory listed the Roche bank as the **only bank** recorded in Limerick — every competitor had been wiped out.

The crisis destroyed public confidence in private bank note issues. Examples of Roche's Limerick Bank notes from this period are now in the **British Museum**: a 1 Guinea & Half note from 1806 and an unissued £1 note from the 1820s, both printed on watermarked paper by Ashby of London.

## Takeover and Legacy

In **1825**, the bank was acquired by **The Provincial Bank**, one of the new joint-stock banks that were replacing the old private banking system across Ireland. Thomas Roche became a **Local Director** of the Provincial Bank's Limerick branch — a seat he held alongside William on the Board of Management through the 1840s. The Provincial Bank itself was eventually absorbed into **Allied Irish Banks (AIB)** in 1966.

After the bank's takeover, Thomas continued as a pillar of Limerick civic life. William entered politics, winning election as **MP for Limerick** in December 1832 — the first Catholic to represent the city since the penal laws. Daniel O'Connell said of him: "William Roche was the only man in Ireland from whom he would not demand a pledge." William served until 1841 and died unmarried and childless on **27 April 1850**.

Thomas outlived his brother by five years, dying on **15 December 1855** at George's Street, "in his eighty-seventh year." The *Limerick Chronicle* gave him a remarkable eulogy:

> Thomas Roche, Esq., elder brother of William Roche, Esq., M.P. for this city, deceased, and surviving partner of the late highly respectable and independent Bank of Thomas and William Roche, of Limerick, **the only one, probably, in the South of Ireland, that passed through the disastrous commercial crash of private banks 40 years ago, with perfect honour and integrity**. Mr Thomas Roche was like his brothers, a gentleman of highly cultivated mind and of strict moral rectitude in all his extensive business transactions. His domestic life was pure and irreproachable, his politics ever liberal and consistent.

## The Roche Banknotes

Surviving examples of Roche's Limerick Bank notes include:

- **6 Shillings "Silver Note"** (12 Nov 1802) — one of the earliest issues
- **9 Shillings "Silver Note"** (1804) — with prominent "Limerick" watermark
- **1 Guinea & Half** (20 Jun 1806) — attractively designed note, now in the British Museum (printed by Ashby, London)
- **5 Guineas Post Bill** (~1808) — denomination filled in by hand
- **4 Guineas Post Bill** — unissued example
- **£1 note** (~1820–1825) — unissued, in the British Museum
- **Blank denomination promissory note** (~1810–1825) — unusual format, denomination written by hand at issue

After both Roche banks ceased operations, their watermarked paper stock was reused to print banknotes in **Jersey** — notes of the Jersey Union Bank from 1858 have been found bearing Roche's "Limerick" watermarks.

Thomas's brother William Roche MP is **not** in the direct line (he died unmarried and childless). Thomas's son, confusingly also named William, became the Crown Solicitor — a different career entirely, but the professional thread runs from banking to law to Empire.
