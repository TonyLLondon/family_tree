# The Keep Britain Tidy Group — Environmental Campaigning and Royal Patronage (1969–c. 1987)

The Keep Britain Tidy Group was Britain's principal anti-litter charity, founded in 1955 and supported by royal patronage, government partnership, and international alliances. [David John Lewis](../people/david-john-lewis.md) served as **Director-General** from 1969 until the mid-1980s, bringing the administrative confidence of seventeen years in African colonial service to the very different challenge of changing British public behaviour. Under his leadership the organisation moved from Brighton to central London, hosted international conferences, partnered with Keep America Beautiful, and received correspondence from Buckingham Palace, Clarence House, and successive Secretaries of State.

## Origins

The campaign against litter in Britain began with **Lady Brunner** (née Elizabeth Irving), wife of the industrialist and philanthropist Sir Felix Brunner, 3rd Baronet. In **1954** she established the Women's Advisory Council on Solid Fuel, but her attention quickly turned to the growing problem of roadside litter in post-war Britain. A resolution at the 1954 WI AGM led to a conference of 26 organisations, and the **Keep Britain Tidy Group** was formally launched in **1955**, chaired by Lady Brunner. The NFWI initially provided office space at its own headquarters; the Group later moved to its own premises. KBT registered as a charity in **1960** (the original charity number predates the current registration; the modern entity, company 03496361, was incorporated in **1998** as "Environmental Campaigns" and re-registered as charity 1071737).

The organisation's early headquarters were at **Bostel House, 37 West Street, Brighton BN1 2RE**. By the time David Lewis became Director-General in 1969, KBT had established itself as the national voice on litter and environmental cleanliness, but it remained a relatively small charity with ambitions to grow its reach.

## David Lewis as Director-General

David took over as Director-General in **1969**, fresh from Zambia where he had served as Acting Permanent Secretary. The skills were transferable: managing a national organisation, coordinating government departments, working with voluntary groups, and dealing with the press. He brought the directness and organisational clarity of the colonial Secretariat to the environmental sector.

Under his tenure the London office moved from Brighton to **Cecil Chambers, 76/86 Strand, London WC2** (confirmed by David's own compliments slip, May 1972), and later to **27 Queen Anne's Gate, London SW1**. He held parallel roles as Member of the **Government Working Group on Waste Management**, Member of the **Packaging Council**, and Associate of the **Institute of Wastes Management**.

## Royal patronage

The **Queen Mother** was patron of the Keep Britain Tidy Group throughout David's tenure. The royal connection was personal and sustained:

- **4 August 1981** — **Queen Elizabeth II** sent a personal telegram: *"I am extremely touched by your telegram of good wishes on my birthday."*
- **29 December 1981** — The Queen Mother's private secretary thanked KBT for Christmas wishes.
- **4 November 1982** — Clarence House conveyed the Queen Mother's condolences on the death of co-founder **Sir Felix Brunner**, passed to Lady Brunner at Her Majesty's bidding.

These were not form letters. The correspondence between Clarence House and David's office reflects an active patron-charity relationship, with the Queen Mother's private secretary engaging directly on organisational matters.

## International partnerships

KBT was the UK partner of **Keep America Beautiful** (founded 1953), the American anti-litter organisation famous for the "Crying Indian" public service announcement. David cultivated the transatlantic relationship:

- **June 1981** — David hosted a reception at **Claridges** with **Sir Derek Ezra** (KBT Chairman) for **William A. Fickling Jr.**, Chairman of Keep America Beautiful, and the Charter Medical Corporation.
- **November 1975** — David led the British delegation to the **Clean World International Conference** in **Berlin**, held in the **Reichstag building**. The conference brought together anti-litter organisations from across the Western world, with **James C. Bowling** (Chairman, Keep America Beautiful) and **Gordon Oakes MP** alongside Lewis.

Photographs from KBT international events show David with Bowling and Oakes (KBT-042), with **Angela Rumbold MP** and TAFF Chairman David Hobbs (KBT-043), and at KFC Awards in the House of Commons (KBT-008).

## Published work

In **Spring 1978**, David authored a short communication in *Environmental Conservation* (Cambridge University Press), Vol. 5, No. 1, p. 56: **"The Keep Britain Tidy Group"**. The article — published in a journal edited by Nicholas Polunin, the foremost environmental conservationist of the era — placed KBT's work in the context of the emerging environmental movement. DOI: [10.1017/S037689290003811X](https://doi.org/10.1017/S037689290003811X).

The *Spectator* (9 October 1976) carried a contemporary assessment by Elisabeth Dunn — **"Consuming Interest: Litter Louts"** — discussing KBT's campaigns, enforcement philosophy, and the forthcoming Queen's Silver Jubilee clean-up (1977), all during Lewis's tenure.

## Government and political support

- **September 1984** — **Peter Walker**, Secretary of State for Energy, wrote praising the KBT annual report: *"Naturally I am delighted at the progress you continuously achieve and the impact you make in the country."*

KBT worked closely with local authorities on enforcement and education, and with the Department of the Environment on waste management policy. The Government Working Group on Waste Management, on which David sat, shaped the regulatory framework for litter and refuse disposal in the 1970s and 1980s.

## Legacy

The Keep Britain Tidy Group was renamed the **Tidy Britain Group** in 1987, then **ENCAMS** (Environmental Campaigns) in 2002, before reverting to **Keep Britain Tidy** in 2009. Today it operates as an independent charity from Wigan, running the Eco-Schools programme, the Blue Flag beach award, and the Green Flag Award for parks and green spaces. The Library of Congress authority record (LCCN n86809834) confirms the Bostel House, Brighton address from the organisation's early decades (a 1985 marine litter report still carried the Brighton address, though by then the main office had moved to London under David's leadership).

David's tenure as Director-General placed him at the head of Britain's anti-litter movement through its formative decades — from the first oil crisis and the rise of disposable packaging through the Thatcher government's early environmental legislation.
