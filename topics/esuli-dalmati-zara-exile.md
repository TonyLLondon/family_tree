# The Esuli Dalmati — Italian Dalmatian Exile after 1943

Between 1943 and 1954, an estimated 250,000 to 350,000 Italian-speaking inhabitants of Istria, Fiume, and Dalmatia left their homes in what became Yugoslavia. The exodus — the **esodo giuliano-dalmata** — was one of the largest forced population movements in post-war Europe and one of the least remembered outside Italy. The Zerauschek family of Zara were part of it. Everything [Antonio Zerauschek](../people/antonio-zerauschek.md) had built — the cigarette factory, the chocolate works, the hotels, the houses, the commercial agencies — was either destroyed by Allied bombing or confiscated by Yugoslav authorities. The family scattered to Florence, and their story of loss, attempted reconstitution, and enduring memory is a window into the broader Dalmatian experience.

## Zara under Italy (1920–1943)

The Treaty of Rapallo (1920) awarded **Zara** (Zadar) to Italy as an enclave surrounded by the Kingdom of Serbs, Croats, and Slovenes. It was the only Dalmatian city to remain Italian after the collapse of Austria-Hungary, and its economy depended on the **free port** regime that exempted it from Italian customs duties. This special status made Zara a natural base for manufacturing and import-export: tobacco, chocolate, and foreign goods could be processed and traded at prices mainland Italian firms could not match.

Antonio Zerauschek exploited this structure to the full. The **Manifattura Zaratina Sigarette** produced **Calypso**, **Samos**, and a dozen other brands classified as "foreign" cigarettes when shipped to mainland Italy. The **Fabbrica Cioccolato e Cacao Ausonia** exported chocolate to Italy and Western Europe. He held agencies for **Ford**, **Firestone**, and **Zenith**, and ran **Hotel Zerauschek**, **Caffè Centrale**, and the **Hotel Bristol / Excelsior** — the last rebuilt in 1937–38 by the Trieste architect **Umberto Nordio**. His business letterhead read: **A. ZERAUSCHEK / ZARA — C.P.E.C. N. 496, Telegrammi: ZERAUSCHEK, Telefono N. 48**.

The family lived well. Antonio received forty to fifty business letters a day. The children — [Fulvia](../people/fulvia-ottilia-antonia-zerauschek.md), [Giuliana](../people/giuliana-zerauschek-rivolta.md), [Guido](../people/guido-zerauschek.md), [Mario](../people/mario-zerauschek.md) — attended boarding schools in Genova. There was a property at **Lucarono** (Lukoran) reachable by motorboat, with citrus trees and a mandarin. There were cats named Venere, Picia, and Negus. There was a tennis court under construction that Antonio never quite finished.

## The bombing and the armistice (1943–1944)

The Italian armistice of **8 September 1943** exposed Zara to immediate danger. The city sat inside the new German-controlled zone, and as the war turned, Allied bombers targeted it repeatedly. The raids of **November 1943 to October 1944** devastated the old city — an estimated 60% of Zara's buildings were destroyed. The Manifattura Zaratina was partly wrecked. The civilian population fled.

Antonio and [Ester](../people/ester-addobbati.md) retreated to **Sirmione** (Villa Ester) — their Lake Garda summer retreat became a wartime refuge. Antonio wrote to Fulvia from there on **14 October 1943**, congratulating her on exam results; the family had left Zara behind. They would never return to live there.

## Yugoslav confiscation and the failed reconstitution

After the war, Zara was incorporated into the Federal People's Republic of Yugoslavia and renamed **Zadar**. The Italian-speaking population was given the choice of remaining under Yugoslav rule or emigrating. Most chose exile. Their properties were **confiscated** under Yugoslav nationalisation laws. The Zerauschek businesses were taken without compensation: the cigarette factory, the chocolate works, the hotels, the houses, the commercial buildings Antonio had built on Široka ulica.

The family attempted to save what they could. The **Manifattura Zaratina Sigarette** convened its shareholders' assembly in **Florence**, voting to reconstitute as an *s.r.l.* (limited liability company). But the **Tribunale di Firenze annulled** the resolution, and the company ceased to exist. The Ausonia chocolate factory was shut down by Yugoslav authorities; no chocolate was produced in Zadar again for eighty-two years (until 2011).

Antonio tried a new venture — **INCAS**, a chocolate factory in Florence — but it failed. Fulvia's 1996 memoir records the disappointment without elaboration. The man who had been, in the words of the A.N.D.A.Z. obituary, **"il vero d'Artagnan dell'industria zaratina"** — ranked alongside Luxardo, Drioli, and Montecucchi — spent his last decades in a Florence apartment at **Viale Mazzini 60**, writing letters to his scattered children and grandchildren, signing himself **"Nonno Tonci."**

## The exile community

The displaced Zaratini maintained a tight network in exile. The **A.N.D.A.Z.** (Associazione Nazionale Dalmati Zaratini) published the periodical *ZARA* from Ancona, carrying obituaries, memories, and community news. The newspaper *Difesa Adriatica* served the broader Dalmatian exile community. When Antonio died on **28 February 1973**, both publications carried obituaries — the *Difesa Adriatica* piece signed **"g. z."** (almost certainly Giuliana Zerauschek), the A.N.D.A.Z. piece calling him "a true d'Artagnan" and lingering on the pain of dying far from Zara. The A.N.D.A.Z. issue was mailed to **Rivolta Zerauschek Liana** — Giuliana — at Via Masaccio 112, 50132 Firenze.

Fulvia in England maintained the connection. In **December 1995**, writing to Carlo Enrico Rivolta, she enclosed her **Zara birth certificate** — stamped "3 Feb. 47 ZARA ITALIA" — but advised him not to show it to anyone because of the political sensitivity: the document bore the name of a city that was now Croatian Zadar, and the sovereignty change made Italian-era documents fraught. The exile was not just economic and physical but documentary: the paperwork of a vanished state.

## The Hotel Zagreb

In **1985**, Antonio's nephew **Bruno Zerauschek** and his wife visited Zadar from Argentina. They stayed at the former family hotel — the **Hotel Bristol**, now renamed **Hotel Zagreb**. When the reception staff read the Zerauschek surname in their passport, they regarded the couple with open contempt. Bruno wrote to Fulvia about the experience in January 1993, from Mar del Plata: the hostility was still raw, decades after confiscation.

Bruno's letter also recalled visiting Antonio in Florence in **1971** — the old man took them to Ester's grave at the **Cimitero delle Porte Sante** (San Miniato al Monte) and wept. He telephoned Lina in Ancona and Emma in Sardinia to coordinate the visit; the scattered family still orbited around the patriarch.

## Florence: the exile capital

Florence became the Zerauschek family's de facto capital after exile:

- **Antonio and Ester**: Viale Mazzini 60 (confirmed by Antonio's personal stationery; Tel. 63.311).
- **Giuliana and Carlo Enrico Rivolta**: Via Masaccio 112, 50132 Firenze (per the A.N.D.A.Z. mailing label, 1973).
- **Mario**: in Florence, working at a hospital ("di guardia all'ospedale," 1971), married with children by 1972.
- **Guido**: in Florence with children Nicola and Simone (per Antonio's 1971 letter).

Ester died in Florence around **mid-1971** (approximately eighteen months before Antonio's death in February 1973). Antonio's grief consumed his final years: "Sempre di più mi pare impossibile che la buona Ester mi abbia lasciato" (6 Nov 1971). Both are buried at the **Cimitero delle Porte Sante**, San Miniato al Monte — far from the Adriatic city where they had built their lives.

## The broader exodus

The Zerauschek experience was replicated across hundreds of thousands of families. The esodo giuliano-dalmata depopulated the Italian communities of **Pola** (Pula), **Fiume** (Rijeka), **Capodistria** (Koper), **Pirano** (Piran), **Rovigno** (Rovinj), and dozens of smaller towns. The refugees — **esuli** — settled across Italy, particularly in Trieste, Rome, and the special refugee settlements (the **Centro Raccolta Profughi** camps). Some emigrated further: Bruno Zerauschek ended up in Argentina. Antonio's brother [Riccardo ("Zio Ginco")](../people/riccardo-zerauschek.md) and his wife [Tatiana Machiedo](../people/tatiana-machiedo.md) passed through **IRO (International Refugee Organization)** processing in 1951 — their file describes Antonio as "his brother who had a representative of Ford, Firestone, Zenith etc."

The Italian state recognised the esuli with Law 137 of 1952 (indemnities for lost property) and subsequent legislation, though compensation was widely considered inadequate. The **Day of Remembrance** (*Giorno del ricordo*) was established on **10 February 2004** — the anniversary of the 1947 Treaty of Paris — as a national memorial day for the victims of the foibe massacres and the Istrian-Dalmatian exodus.
