# Freemasonry — Orient de Tehran

In the last years of the Qajar dynasty, Freemasonry became entangled with constitutionalism, French cultural diplomacy, and the modernizing ambitions of Tehran's European professional class. **Julien Bottin** — French arsenal engineer and member of the Burgess–Saginian family — was a founding organizer of the lodge that would become the **Réveil de l'Iran** (*Bidari-ye Iran*), the most significant masonic body in constitutional-era Persia.

---

## Before the lodge: the Alliance Française

The ground was laid by the **Alliance Française** committee in Tehran, which functioned as a hub for the city's French-speaking residents. The committee included:

- **Dr Feuvrier** (physician)
- **Paul Henri Morel**
- **Alphonse Nicolas**
- **Julien Bottin**, French engineer at the [Tehran arsenal](war-ministry-arsenal-tehran.md)
- **Jean-Baptiste Lemaire**

The Shah's court viewed the Alliance with suspicion. Rumours circulated equating French cultural activities with Freemasonry. **Montfort**, the Austrian police chief serving the Shah, was hostile. The British envoy saw the Alliance as a vehicle for French strategic penetration. In this atmosphere, the line between cultural association and secret society was deliberately blurred.

## The Spanish rite lodge (1898)

Bottin was a member of the **Orient de Tehran**, a lodge operating under **Spanish rite**, from at least **1898**. Fellow members included **Entezam al-Saltaneh**. Little survives about this early lodge's activities or membership beyond the names.

## Founding of Réveil de l'Iran (1906–1907)

On **29 November 1906**, Bottin, Lemaire, and Morel convened a founding meeting for a new lodge in Tehran. By December the group had **seventeen members**. On **23 December 1906** they formally constituted the **Réveil de l'Iran** (*Bidari-ye Iran*), operating under the **Scottish rite** and chartered by the **Grand Orient de France**. The lodge received official recognition in **November 1907**.

The timing is critical: the **Constitutional Revolution** was underway. The first Majles had convened in October 1906. Masonic networks in Tehran — overlapping with constitutionalist, francophone, and modernizing circles — became channels for political organization. Bayat characterizes Persian masonry in this period as a "fragmented modernising force," not the unified conspiracy imagined by the court.

## Masonry and tarikat

The Iranian scholar **Thierry Zarcone** draws a parallel between masonic lodges and Sufi **tarikat** (orders): both involved initiation, hierarchy, secrecy, and mutual aid. When the Grand Orient bylaws were translated into Persian in **1908** by the **Réveil de l'Iran**, the translators used Sufi vocabulary — the lodge was discussed as a *tarikat*. This framing made masonry legible to an Iranian audience familiar with esoteric brotherhoods, while also raising clerical suspicion.

## Family

**[Julien Bottin](../people/julien-bottin.md)** (b. Nice, 24 Mar 1853) married **[Fanny Burgess](../people/fanny-burgess.md)**, daughter of the translator **[Edward Burgess](../people/edward-burgess.md)** and **[Anna Saginian](../people/anna-saginian.md)**. His masonic and Alliance Française activities belong to the same milieu as his [arsenal contract](war-ministry-arsenal-tehran.md): the French technical community in late Qajar Tehran.
