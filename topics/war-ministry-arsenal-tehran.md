# Persian War Ministry — Arsenal and military manufactures

In the late Qajar period, the **Ministry of War** hired European technicians to run Tehran's arsenal, powder factory, and cartridge works. **Julien Bottin** — a French engineer who had married into the Burgess–Saginian line — held the post of *Ingénieur mécanicien de l'Arsenal et des manufactures militaires*, overseeing the machinery and training a generation of Persian technical staff.

---

## The contract

A surviving contract between the **Ministry of War of Persia** and **Julien Bottin** (preserved in the Henderson family papers) sets out the terms:

| Clause | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Title | *Ingénieur mécanicien de l'Arsenal et des manufactures militaires* |
| Term | 2 years from **1 Hamal 1334** (solar calendar) |
| Salary | **2,400 tomans** per year, paid quarterly in advance |
| Augmentation | **300 tomans** additional from an earlier contract |
| Duties | Oversee machinery at the **arsenal**, **poudrerie** (powder factory), and **cartoucherie** (cartridge works); teach **applied mechanics** to **30 students** |
| Attendance | Daily presence required at all three facilities; Fridays and holidays excepted |
| Outside work | Permitted with authorization |
| Travel | Government may send him to facilities outside Tehran for machine repairs |
| Termination | Loss of pay if contract broken |

The contract header describes Bottin as a **French subject** with the rank of **colonel** — a courtesy or equivalency title within the Persian military hierarchy.

## The facilities

Tehran's military-industrial complex in this period centred on the **arsenal** (established under Naser al-Din Shah with French and Austrian advisors), a **powder factory**, and a **cartridge works**. These facilities produced small arms, ammunition, and explosives for the Persian army. The technology was largely imported; the role of men like Bottin was to maintain it and transfer knowledge to Persian operators.

The **teaching obligation** — thirty students in applied mechanics — reflects the Qajar government's persistent goal of creating a domestic technical class. This parallels the broader pattern at the **Dar al-Fonun** polytechnic, where European instructors taught military engineering, medicine, and languages from 1851 onward.

## The French technical wave

Bottin was part of a broader stream of French technicians in Qajar service. The **Ambassade de France** in Tehran recorded a "French residence" built between **1894 and 1896**, likely connected to Bottin's presence as "engineer at Tehran arsenal." French influence in Persian military modernization peaked in the late nineteenth century, overlapping with Russian and British competition for strategic influence.

Bottin also appears in the **Alliance Française** committee in Tehran alongside **Paul Henri Morel**, **Alphonse Nicolas**, and **Jean-Baptiste Lemaire** — the same circle that would later organize the [masonic lodge](freemasonry-orient-de-tehran.md).

## Family

**[Julien Bottin](../people/julien-bottin.md)** was born in **Nice** on **24 March 1853**. He married **[Fanny Burgess](../people/fanny-burgess.md)**, daughter of **[Edward Burgess](../people/edward-burgess.md)** and **[Anna Saginian](../people/anna-saginian.md)**. Their daughter **[Henriette](../people/henriette-bottin.md)** married **[Dr Étienne Stump](../people/etienne-stump.md)**, the Swiss dentist. The arsenal engineer and the court dentist — two generations of European professionals embedded in the same state apparatus.
