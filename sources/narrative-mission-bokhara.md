# Narrative of a Mission to Bokhara (Wolff, 1845)

Dr. Joseph Wolff's account of his 1843–1844 journey from England through Persia to Bokhara, seeking news of the murdered British officers Stoddart and Conolly. Contains first-hand observations of Tabriz society, the British consulate, Armenian and Georgian notables, and American Presbyterian missionaries in the region.

## Key passages for this project

### Edward Burgess (Ch. XXVI, corpus lines ~4928–4948)

> "I must not forget also to name an interesting and unfortunate young gentleman at Tabreez, whose name is Edward Burgess, well acquainted with the Persian language. This excellent young gentleman is employed by Prince Bahman Mirza, as translator of the English Newspapers. His brother was employed by the Persian Government, and sent by them with several thousand tomauns to England to buy merchandize, and poor Edward Burgess remained guarantee for his brother's honour. His brother most shamefully neglected to redeem his honour, and abandoned Edward to the consequences of his generous devotion. He is therefore detained in Persia for his brother the defaulter. The poor man would be in the utmost distress if Mr. Bonham and the Russian Consul-General, and the Greek merchants, did not do all in their power to serve him."

### Daoud Khan (Ch. XXVI, corpus lines ~4916 and ~4948)

Two mentions in the same chapter:
1. *"I visited again Daoud Khan, a Colonel in the Russian service. He is a genuine Georgian."* — his origin and prior Russian military service.
2. *"Bonham, Osroff, Dr. Casolani, **Daoud Khan the Armenian**, all the Russian Attachés and the Greek merchants, accompanied me a long distance."* — his community identity in Tabriz by 1843.

Both refer to the same person: **Daoud Khan Saginian**, born in Georgia (surname Saginean), fled Russian service in 1811, integrated into the Armenian community in Iran.

### Mr. Burgess accompanying Wolff out of Tabriz (corpus line ~1983)

> "Mr. Bonham and Mr. Burgess accompanied me on my journey seven miles." (January 20, 1844 departure)

Confirms Edward's social standing — accompanying the British consul-general on an official departure.
