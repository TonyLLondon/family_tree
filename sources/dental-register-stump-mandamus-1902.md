# Chicago Tribune — Stump mandamus dismissed (10 Aug 1902)

**"Judge Scores Dental School. Refuses Mandamus for License to a German-American College Graduate."** Reprinted in *The Dental Register*, Vol. 56, No. 11, 15 November 1902, pp. 582–583.

Judge Chetlain dismissed Étienne Stump's petition for mandamus against the Illinois State Board of Dental Examiners after the Board refused (30 April 1902) to license him on the ground that the **German-American Dental College** — the same Chicago institution incorporated in 1891 as the "North American-German Dental College" at 569 Wells Street — was not of sufficient standing. The ruling detailed misconduct by Dr. Huxmann, the college's dean: no faculty meetings, no matriculation books, a course of only three semesters of six months, promises of speedy graduation, and furnishing exam answers to students in advance. Huxmann had also served on the Board itself for part of the preceding decade while simultaneously running the college — a conflict the judge called "at least illegal and unjust."

## Significance

- Places **Étienne Stump in Chicago in 1902** as a dental graduate, consistent with the Henderson family photo [Etienne — Chicago 1902](../media/albums/henderson/Etienne%20-%20Chicago%201902%20(1).jpg).
- The license refusal likely explains why Stump left the United States: within two years he was hired in **New York** (c. 1904) as personal dentist to **Atabak** during the latter's American tour and departed for Iran.
- Confirms Stump held a dental diploma — the dispute was about the college's reputation, not his competence.
