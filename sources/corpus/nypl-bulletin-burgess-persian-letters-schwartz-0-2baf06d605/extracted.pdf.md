# THE BURGESS PERSIAN LETTERS

#### Edited by BENJAMIN SCHWARTZ

#### PART IV

[Edward Burgess to George Burgess] Tdbreez, November 20, <sup>1841</sup>

Our Mission was exceedingly well received by the Shah and the Commercial Treaty signed <sup>a</sup> few days after their arrival, therefore I hope that nothing will occur to cause another quarrel.57 One of the articles in this treaty is that we are to have two Consuls the one at Tabreez and the other at Tehran ; Mr Bonham you know holds the one situation and Mr Abbott the other. If I had been free from my embarrassments on Charles' acct I might perhaps have obtained the latter place. I believe the pay is £350 or £400 <sup>a</sup> year with liberty to trade. But my present position prevents my trying to get any situation whatever. I still hope however that if I can get clear of Charles I may get some profitable employ ment in the East. My knowledge of the Persian and Turkish languages, par ticularly the latter which is known to so few Europeans may some day be of good service to me.

The road between this and Erzeroum is now much disturbed, two English gentlemen coming from Constantinople were robbed near this frontier a short time ago and narrowly escaped being murdered, the spears of the brigands having passed thro' their clothes and slightly wounded them. When the robbers (Koords) found they were English they gave them back the most part of their clothes but took all their money and valuables, and their servants were stripped to their shirts and drawers. Caravans now go a good many together so as to form a large party and there is no more danger to merchandize than formerly. But many single travellers and small parties have been robbed and some people murdered.

Mr Charles Scott (second son of Sir Walter Scott) who was attached to this mission died at Tehran a short time ago, of dysentery, a common complaint in this country although thank God one from which I have never suffered.

## [Edward Burgess to George Burgess] Tabrees, June 10, <sup>1842</sup>

Mr Bonham started for England on the 27th ult and has left me in charge of his business here, he gives me a salary of 400 Tomauns a year, about £180., this is very handsome and is quite enough to enable me to live very comfortably in this country. I believe I should have been appointed acting Consul during his absence had it not been for my embarrassments, as it is Mr Abbott, Consul at Tehran has come here to take charge of this Consulate. Sir John McNeill, who has gone home was very kind to me when he was here and I have no doubt, would do all in his power for me if I could once get free from my difficulties.

You have no doubt heard the disastrous intelligence of the massacre of our army at Canbool ; the Shah has behaved very well about the affairs of Afghan istan, he has had repeated messages from Herat offering to give up the place to him, to all of which he has refused to listen. If he were to make a movement to the Eastward it would materially increase our difficulties in Afghanistan, by giving encouragement to the Afghans who are opposing us.

The Russians have sent some ships and troops to occupy a small island or islands on the south eastern shore of the Caspian, near Astrabad. The way this has been effected is rather curious and characteristic of the Russian proceedings in the East. You perhaps have heard that for the last century the Turcomans or Tartar tribes who inhabit the great desert to the East of the Caspian have been in the habit of making incursions into Persia and carrying off the inhabitants into slavery and selling them in Khiva, Bokhara and other Tartar states. These inroads were at their height during the latter part of Fatteh Alee Shah's reign, but by this Shah's vigorous measures in Khorasan had been a good deal repressed. Still however they are always going on, chiefly over the frontier from the Cas pian Sea towards Herat ; and one of their roads was coasting in large boats by As trabad along the shores of the Caspian to plunder in the province of Mazenderan. Some time ago the Prime minister (the Hadji as he is called) was complaining of these inroads and piracies and said in the course of conversation with one of the attaches of the Russian Mission that he wished that there were some Russian forces there to stop them. No more was said upon the subject until some months afterwards when one of the Russian Mission came to the Hadji and said "I have brought you good news" "What is it?" said the Hadji. "Our army has arrived off Astrabad." The Hadji was quite taken aback and had very little to say for himself. The Russians now demand, permanently to occupy one of the islands in these seas, as they say to repress the piracies which have been committed by the Turcoman boats on their ships, which trade to the coasts of Mazenderan (pt of anct Hyrcania) . If the Russians succeed in forming <sup>a</sup> military settlement here, I shall think it most probable that we shall deem ourselves obliged to retake and maintain Herat. This will bring us, to within about thirty marches of one another. And a practicable road for armies between us.

[Edward Burgess to George Burgess] Tabreez, January 9, <sup>1844</sup>

These villages belong to the late Dr Cormicks children and as I was fre quently unable to get in their rents I asked the Prince to make me chief of them, which he did. I have to collect the taxes from the village and pay them into the treasury. This is all easy enough but what gives me work is this: according to the customs of the country all these villagers look to me for protection, if they are robbed they come to me to catch the thief, if they are in any way oppressed they come to me to plead their cause before the Prince and see that they have justice done them ; to such an extent is this carried that last autumn when I was at the village one day as I was walking along the street <sup>a</sup> man came to me and said "salaam aleikum" "Peace be unto you" making <sup>a</sup> low bow. ### [Edward Burgess to George Burgess] Tabreez, April 6, <sup>1844</sup>

[Edward Burgess to Mrs. Henry Burgess] Tabreez, June 6, 1844

On the 13th ulto I received yours of the 23 March. I am very sorry to hear of the death of Mr. and Mrs. Webbe. You my dear Mother must have felt the death of the latter acutely. She was almost the only person I recollect with whom you were on terms of intimacy and friendship. In mentioning your having heard Mozarts requiem you write "do not think I am grown superstitious." If you knew how superstitious your son was, you would not think it necessary to write that sentence. It is rather curious that your letter should have arrived a few days after I had written mine (9th May) about the jins.— I have just received a long letter from uncle and aunt E. Smith, the latter's letter is full of "Said" and unfortunately in answering it I quite forgot to mention any thing about him, which is an omission I am sorry for. I would have congratulated them upon their adopted son, when one can please people by paying them a few empty compliments I usually make it a rule to do so, particularly people who have been so kind to me as they have. Can you tell me what breed Mr Said is of. In Egypt Arabia and Persia there are three kinds of slaves well known to commerce; the first is the Abyssinian which are worth from all prices 25 to 500 £ or even more, the next the Nubian who are not so good, and the last the thorough bred negro who are dear at 6 or 7 £, their average price here. I am afraid they have been cheated so please tell me what breed Mr Said is.

On Monday the 13 th May we had a sharp shock of an earthquake and we have since learnt that this was felt more severely at Kashan and Meeanna. In the district of Meeanna a town about 100 Miles S. E. of this, many villages were completely thrown down and the town itself half ruined. Numbers of people have been killed and wounded. At Ispahan the great mosque is injured. It is very seldom that earthquakes spread over such an extent of country as this has done. Tabreez appears to have been the extremity on one side and Ispahan on the other. I believe this is the first time Ispahan has been visited by these calamities which may account for its being almost the only decent looking city in Persia. Who in his senses would build a house two or three stories high with the chance of having it come tumbling about his ears as soon as it was finnished.
