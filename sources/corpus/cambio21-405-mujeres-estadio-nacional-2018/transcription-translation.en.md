# Women victims of the dictatorship imprisoned at the Estadio Nacional: Learn the names of 405 women detained at the country's main sports arena in 1973

**By Alfredo Peña** | *Cambio21*, 31 December 2018

After last week's official opening of Escotilla 8 — the gate at the Estadio Nacional through which political prisoners were brought into the detention centre — details emerged about where the 1,200 women who arrived as prisoners were held, brought by the military to what was a true concentration camp. Just like the Nazis. Our own criollo Auschwitz, with Prussian-style helmets and uniforms guarding defenceless women.

Approximately twenty thousand people (unofficial figures) — men, women, boys, girls, and foreigners — passed through the facilities of the now decrepit Estadio Nacional, the country's main sports venue.

*[Photo: Artistic performance where the women prisoners were held]*

Escotilla 8 had remained closed since April of that year, due to restoration work and the recovery of the engravings and marks left on its walls — messages, calendars, dates, phrases in English, initials, etc. — made by the former prisoners with keys, wire, glass, and other objects found in the escotilla and its surroundings.

## The women prisoners

Wally Kunstmann, President of the Corporación Estadio Nacional Memoria Viva, states that "approximately 1,200 women were imprisoned here."

Almost no television footage of the women prisoners exists, and very few photographs of them.

Initially, after the coup, about 100 women were imprisoned in the coliseum itself, in its changing rooms or escotillas.

*[Photo: Soldier guarding at the Estadio Nacional]*

*[Photo: One of the changing rooms used as a concentration and detention camp]*

Later, all women were transferred to the changing room beside the swimming pool of the Estadio Nacional, near the corner of Pedro de Valdivia and Grecia, metres from the Metropolitan Railway station. There they were stripped of their belongings and many of them were assaulted by psychopaths in the Chilean army.

The changing room, which remains intact, measures approximately 30 metres long by 7 wide; the prisoners slept and performed their bodily needs there, several of them pregnant.

Many of them were subjected to sexual violence, something the former detainees do not speak about.

In this changing room, copper plaques bearing the names of some prisoners, both Chilean and foreign, who were detained there, were installed a few weeks ago.
