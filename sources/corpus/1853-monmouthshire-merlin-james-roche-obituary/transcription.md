## Transcription (from scan)

### JAMES ROCHE.

An extraordinary man recently died in Cork, at the ripe age of 86 years, who was called the **Roscoe** of his native city, and who had seen the "world at large" more intimately than many that had played the most prominent parts on its wide and extended stage. He was a literary character, too, and an able contributor, under the signature of J. B., to many a journal now living, and to many others, long since gone to the tomb of the Capulets. The going out of this "ancient light" we find thus mentioned in a principal newspaper of his native city.

"**James Roche**, merchant and justice of the peace, died at his house, by the Glanmire road, on Friday last, in the 86th year of his age. On the bleakest day of the bleakest month of the wintry spring past, the casual visitor to **Cork** might have encountered in any of our streets, an old man, of attenuated frame, with keen, intelligent aspect, and somewhat bent in figure, moving along with a quick pace, intent on the ordinary business of life. He wore a brown surtout, buttoned close up to the chin, dark gaiters, perhaps a coloured silk kerchief tied somewhat carelessly round his neck, by way of muffler, and if the weather was very wild, screened himself from the piercing blasts of wind by a large blue umbrella, which he bore bravely up against the storm. Most people would have passed him without observation. But one who had a quick perception of character, would, in all likelihood, be attracted by his appearance, and suspect that there was something remarkable about him.

He was indeed a remarkable man. He had witnessed the French Revolution from the States General to the Consulate.

"He had seen the whole era of Irish politics which is stamped with O'Connell's name. His life was the span of an age of historical prodigies. Living among great men and great events, he had known the close contact of both. Living another life among dead tongues and obsolete authors, his mind was a museum of rare and recondite lore. That brain, now a handful of inert ashes, was a hive in whose intricate cells the sap of whole libraries was stored... He was the friend of Vergniaud, and of O'Connell, of Arthur O'Connor, Lafayette, and Fontanes. He was suspect of Robespierre, imprisoned with Malesherbes, and released by Marshal Brune. One of his nieces nearly took the great Frederick prisoner at Kunnersdorf. His father's footman married the Theresa of Rosseau. He paid homage to the pudgy trunk and spindle shanks of Gibbon, in Switzerland; paid 14,000 francs in assignats for his dinners in a Paris restaurateurs; was intimate with the family of Montesquieu; and chatted on philology with Joseph Bonaparte. He dined with a full muster of the Girondists, only a few weeks before their heads rolled in the saw dust — on the very day before Robespierre struck the *coup d'état* of the reign of terror...

Two Royalist refugees, from the same braying of the Sections, J. R. sheltered in his house at Bourdeaux. They were both ex-officers of the Irish Brigade. One of them, an old general, was a relative. The other, a young aide-de-camp, subsequently became the General Count Bourke."

## Notes

This is the fullest obituary of James Roche (~1770–1853), Thomas Roche's brother. At 2,230 words, it provides an extraordinary portrait of a man who moved between the French Revolution, Irish politics, banking, and literature. The comparison to William Roscoe (1753–1831), the Liverpool banker, historian, and patron of the arts, was the highest literary compliment available.
