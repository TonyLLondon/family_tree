## THE BURGESS PERSIAN LETTERS

### Edited by BENJAMIN SCHWARTZ

#### PART V

[EDWARD BURGESS TO MRS. HENRY BURGESS] Tabreez, June 30, 1844

I am perhaps, and I thank God for it, the most contented European in this country; whenever I get discontented with my lot, I think how much better off I am than a clerk in Manchester, worked eight, nine, or ten hours a day in that filthy climate, and when he has done his work at night having no resource or amusement but to go to a tavern and drink brandy and water. Mr Zohrab who has just left this for England, to join his brother, who has a small commission business in Liverpool, thinks that in going to England in this way he will get into "such good society." Why the best society he can hope to get into will be something like that of Mr Perker's clerk (Pickwick) at the Magpie and Stump. I advised him, by no means to leave these countries if he could get any good place but so mad was he about England where he has never been, that he said if he was offered a place for a thousand a year under the British Govt to remain in Turkey or Persia he would not take it. And all because he thinks he is going to get into "good society." I did not like to vex him by undeceiving him, which would perhaps have been impossible, so I let him start expecting to find the streets of Liverpool paved with gold and silver.

It is about three p. m. Sunday the heat of the day is beginning to abate and I shall as soon as I have finished these few sentences, take a walk to a summer palace and pleasure grounds belonging to the Prince, which is about fifteen minutes walk from where I live; this is a very favorite resort of mine; the grounds are about three quarters of a mile long by nearly half a mile broad, and contain almost every sort of tree and shrub which will thrive in this climate. Some parts are laid out as vineyards others as almond and apple orchards and other fruit trees, and there are avenues of poplars and some forest trees which are not common here amongst which I may remark oaks, as they are rare. Here and there, where the paths cross, black bushy elm trees are planted (the witch elm I believe) which have a shade so thick that they form in this climate a more agreeable abode than any room. Lady McNeill once passed three months of the summer under the shade of trees like these near Tehran. The roses are now out and I often come back with large bouquets of them. Now could I enjoy this at Manchester?

1st July 7 a. m. I took my walk yesterday evening as I intended and took my dogs with me. Amongst them is a young pointer which one of my Greek friends

being little of a sportsman himself has sent to me to train, for you must know that amongst my numerous accomplishments, dog training forms one of the chief. This is not the sporting season so I can not finish his education ; but never did I see <sup>a</sup> dog of such promise. The great matter in this art is to teach them to obey, it does not much matter what they have to obey about, so long as they get the habit. I usually begin by placing their food before them and telling them not to eat it, the dog of course does not understand the meaning of the words, and rushes forward to gobble up his food. <sup>I</sup> then hit him <sup>a</sup> slap on his face and drive him back, constantly repeating the words that he may learn them. <sup>I</sup> then say "eat it" which he does not understand either, so <sup>I</sup> put the food before him and let him eat it caressing him at the same time. A sensible dog usually learns this lesson in <sup>a</sup> few days, this one learnt it in three. And when he is fed it is curious to see him crouch down beside his food and look me in the face for permission to eat it. In <sup>a</sup>week <sup>I</sup>had taught him to fetch and carry and now he stops, advances, walks behind me or goes before at the word of command. And he has learnt all this without <sup>a</sup> single blow of the whip ; the worst <sup>I</sup> have done is to slap his face and pull his ears. <sup>I</sup> believe it is great mistake to give dogs severe beatings: sometimes no doubt it isnecessary particularly with spaniels, but indiscriminate beating discourages them and makes them stupid. <sup>I</sup> believe that two severe beatings would be enough to spoil the dog <sup>I</sup> am training. Dogs differ in their dispositions just as much as children, and they ought (the dogs <sup>I</sup>mean) to like their work. As the quail and partridge season has not begun <sup>I</sup> can not go much further with Zunboor's education just now. Zunboor is the name of the dog and is Persian for Bee.

Ihave received yours of the 18th April. <sup>I</sup> like the Last of the Barons much, it iscertainly one of Bulwers best. Itwas new in Persia and <sup>I</sup> sent it to my friends at Tehran who had not read it. Indeed now we get very few new books in Persia. You seem to think Galignani72 <sup>a</sup>bad newspaper: it is so good <sup>a</sup>one that <sup>I</sup> think it not improbable that ifever <sup>I</sup> settle in England and can afford to take <sup>a</sup>daily paper <sup>I</sup> shall take it in ; itgives extracts from both sides and with great fairness and isthe only English paper we have in Tabreez except the "Illustrated London News" and "Punch." We have several French papers "Debats" "Siecle" "Na tional" etc. and Constantinople and Smyrna papers also in French, and seldom worth reading except for the Indian news. They are put under <sup>a</sup> strict "sur veillance" by the Turkish govt so that they can not publish any news about Turkey, that is to say the truth.

[Edward Burgess to Mrs. Henry Burgess] Tabreez, August 3, <sup>1844</sup>

I received yours of the 4th June last night, many thanks for it. It is <sup>a</sup> letter full of questions and therefore an easy one to answer. I am very glad indeed that the Persian dance amused you. First as to the word Moslem is I believe <sup>a</sup> corruption of "Muslim" which means the same as "Musulman," one who re ceives the true faith or "islam." Richardson also says that it means obedience to the true faith or written word of God. I do not think it can mean blind obedience, because the Mahomedans say that when a man comes to years of discretion it is his duty to learn, and enquire, whether his faith is the true one or not. And that if he receives his faith merely because his father and his mother received it before him, he is little better than an infidel.73 This is the opinion of many of the most learned Mahomedan divines, particularly amongst this sect, Sheeah. But whether the opinion is very general or not I do not know.

Now for the affairs of the villages, they thank God go on remarkably well. The cotton cultivation will be I believe nearly doubled and the grain is <sup>a</sup> very abundant crop, so I hope to make something this year and if you knew what work I have had you would say that I deserve any thing I can make. I believe that I told you that there were <sup>a</sup> number of soldiers of different sorts, cavalry, infantry and artillery, but I do not know whether I told you that there are two officers amongst them, a captain of cavalry and a lieutenant of infantry. The captain is an elderly, and as far as I have yet seen, <sup>a</sup> very respectable man, who thinks much more about farming than fighting. Last year I was of some little service to him in the matter of getting him a part of his pay which his colonel seemed incline to plunder him of. But the lieutenant who is a young fellow of two or three and twenty, is perhaps as great a scamp as ever took a moonlight leap over the border. Last winter I was obliged to get him rather severely pun ished, for turning out his soldiers and making a riot in the village, and disobeying a written order which he tore up before all the people and abused the writer. I was sorry for him because he is <sup>a</sup> very dashing fellow, and he was exceedingly civil when I was in the village last year: and the order altho' about my affairs was not written by me. However I gave him <sup>a</sup> few yards of blue cotton stuff to make a coat of, which he dignified with the name of a dress of honor, and we were better friends than ever.

Last winter a Captain who lives in a neighbouring village had come with <sup>a</sup> party of men, and driven off some sheep belonging to our village the Lieut heard of it mounted his horse armed with sword and spear, and accompanied by three or four horsemen chased the captain and overtook him, just as he and his party were driving the sheep into their own village. wounded him with his spear and unhorsed him and brought all the sheep back. No complaints were made on either side and so there the matter rested.

Now the poor lad is in trouble, and scamp as he is I am sorry for him. He and two or three more fellows of the same stamp, have been driving off cattle, buffaloes, from over the Koordish border. These he was too sharp to bring into the village, and I am much obliged to him for his consideration for had he brought them there and they had been traced they (the owners) would have come upon me to find them. He took them about twelve miles higher up, and hid them away amongst some plantations by the river side. The Koords, owners of the cattle, traced them by their hoof marks until near the town of Maranghd, when the tracks were obliterated by the great traffic. And altho these gentry are as clever at making out a track as Christie of Clinthill or any other borderer of the olden time, they were here at fault. By accident they met a boy from our village, who had been up the river to break down the dams and let the water down to irrigate our lands. He told them that he had seen amongst the trees four buffaloes, piquetted like cavalry horses with head and heel-ropes. The Koords went to the place and found their beasts. Khoda Kerreem Beg (the lieut.) who was near, made his escape and to use <sup>a</sup> Persian expression "he went and is still going" — that is nothing has been heard of him ; for the Koords made their complaint to his colonel, and altho these things are thought much less of in this country than in many others, still such affairs can not be entirely looked over: and at times very severe punishments are inflicted such as cutting off ears, noses, hands, or feet, and even death.

The oldest inhabitants of the village say they never recollect such quiet times and what is better the people from the neighbouring villages say the same, and would be delighted to be taken under my government. The cotton cultivated near the shores of the lake has turned out very well, this is new ground and as the produce was uncertain I agreed to take half rent and taxes on it. It has de pended upon rains for its moisture and has not been irrigated from wells as the other is. What induced the villagers to cultivate this ground was a water melon seed having dropped there by accident last year and the plant produced a fruit which weighed fifteen pounds. The villagers seeing this came to me and proposed to cultivate cotton if I would agree to take half rent and taxes, as if the season had been a very dry one, the crop might have failed and they would have lost their labour, and money laid out. This I agreed to do. But I do not think there is much chance of the crops failing for want of water, as they say that this is very near the surface, only about three feet I believe.

About half of the inhabitants of the village are tribes men or nomads be longing to the tribe of Mokuddum. These are bad cultivators but famous feeders

They come into town every now and then, and come to make their bows to me, and sometimes bring me presents of lambs, butter, and curds. They will be most of them down at the village in another month or so. I do not think I shall be able to go to the villages until the end of Sepr or beginning of October. I do not like to see poor fellows working twelve hours under a hot sun, and to know that they have tasted nothing, not even a drink of water, or a pipe, since an hour before sun rise and can taste nothing until a quarter of an hour after.

A French chargé d'affaires M. de Sartige<sup>74</sup> has arrived here he is going to Tehran to try to persuade the Shah to allow French Jesuit priests to come to Persia. They have been here several times before and have been turned out for the intrigues and the disturbances they made.<sup>75</sup> I do not wish him success, these gentry make quarrels amongst the Europeans and do mischief. — Otherwise they are many of them intelligent and well informed men. I never saw but one blockhead amongst them, and he was only a lay brother.

Poor old Wolf is I am afraid detained at Bokhara it is said that villain of an Ameer will not let him go.76

#### [EDWARD BURGESS TO MRS. HENRY BURGESS] Tabreez, October 7, 1844

This year I missed the speechifying [at my villages]. I did it on purpose, it appeared to me so utterly ridiculous to have to make a speech in a language which I talk by no means well. Altho I may say without boasting that I know of no European who either is, or has been in Persia who talks or talked it better except the late Dr Cormick. The people came to me by fours and fives and I spoke to them civilly and sent them off again. Flattering myself that by so doing I had escaped the bore of a public meeting and speech making. However I had not been back ten days before in came a villager with a letter to me signed by a great number of the villagers demanding to change their chief, in fact to have a fresh election.

I give the term election to this business because I have no other word for it but they do not meet and vote. The thing is arranged amongst themselves, they meet and talk the matter over and whenever a large majority is in favor of one man the govt people can not resist their wish, if they did the people would stand upon their rights, and would not pay taxes. If, as not infrequently happens, the govt man is a tyrant he might catch, fine, and punish, two or three of the leaders in such a movement, but he would get no good by this, and all men of sense find it best to let them have their own way. When the parties in <sup>a</sup> village are pretty equally divided the govt man may choose for himself whichever of the candidates he likes best. When there is a large majority in favor of one man he is obliged to yield or he will ruin his own village. I was once complaining to two or three of the priests of the village of the people being such drunken, fighting, riotous rascals, and asked whether they were always so. They laughed and said when the village was Houssein Khan's he took no pains about it and left it almost entirely to the management of his people, the consequence was that scarcely a week passed without fights and drunken squabbles, and some of the inhabitants who were joined by the soldiers and they fought with Houssein Khan's agent and his people, which ended in the defeat of the latter who was driven into a tower where they placed a guard over him and refused to let him come out, until he had paid a fine of twenty tomauns ! The story is almost too good to be true, particularly as they say the Khan took no notice of the matter. —

We have not yet seen the new comet77 which I see is mentioned in the news papers. — Did you see Capt Warner's experiment ? I translated <sup>a</sup> long account of it for the Prince. —

They keep these people in better order altho it is done in an infamous way ; they set one chief intriguing against another, perhaps even give orders appointing two rival chiefs governors of the same district, and when, after spending half their fortunes in presents and bribes, they turn out their followers to fight it out like good fellows, they fine both for not keeping the peace, and perhaps appoint <sup>a</sup> third man to the government. This is infamous enough no doubt, but then the govt keep them in subjection and are quite able to prevent their making incursions over the frontier.

Now to answer another question. There are I believe no nightingales at Tabreez, I never heard one north of Sultania. At Casveen, Tehran, and Ispahan during the rose season they make such a noise as to be almost unpleasant. After <sup>a</sup> day or two their constant singing is nearly annoying. I recollect one which appeared to sing four and twenty hours almost without stopping in a tree close to my window, most likely it was a succession of nightingales who kept up this singing, but the effect was as if one was constantly singing. The cats eat a great many of them. The Persians say the nightingales are in love with the roses, and that they sing to them until they lose their senses and being in a sort of singing rapture or stupor the cats catch them without much trouble. The love of the nightingale for the rose is the orthodox belief of the Persians, but I can bear witness to the fact, that their note is scarcely ever heard except in the rose season — let naturalists determine why.81

We have several sorts of roses here, some unknown in England until Sir Henry Willock took the plants there. Two or three sorts of double red roses the best is the "Gul i ateshi," the "fire rose" it is of a deep red colour without smell, another the same but smaller, called, "Gul i mishki," or "musk rose" with a fine perfume; this blooms for four months in the year. The "Gul Nestoran"82 or "Nestorian rose" is single and grows in a bush sometimes twenty or thirty feet high the branches weeping like those of a willow and covered with white blos soms like garlands, this also blooms for four or five months, say from May to September, the perfume is so strong that four or five trees planted in <sup>a</sup> garden of fifty or sixty yards square, make the place smell as if the borders had been watered with rose water. We have also single yellow roses; <sup>a</sup> sort of sweet briar and others of a dark reddish coffee colour inside and pale yellow out, single with a disagreeable smell. This is the first rose in blossom and whilst in blossom makes <sup>a</sup> very fine show, the tree being covered with flowers, which however only last about ten days. Indeed none of the roses except those above mentioned last more than ten or fourteen days, (each flower may perhaps last two days) the sun has such power that they blossom and fade with great rapidity. —

## Tabreez, December 2, 1844

I yesterday received yours of Octr 5th and I cannot tell you the pleasure the news of George's intended marriage gave me. I do not understand why you should take such a melancholy view of an event which ought to give you nothing but pleasure, I should certainly have been better pleased if the lady had possessed some fortune, but as it is I am truly glad to hear that he is about to be married, and as to connections, if she has good commercial connections they will, in all likelihood, be more useful to George than if she was first cousin to the Duke of Devonshire. It is true that I may look upon this, in an Asiatic way. There is <sup>a</sup> proverb in Persian that "God pays the debts of marriages" the meaning of which is this, that if a man without a sixpence married a lady equally rich, and runs five hundred pounds in debt to buy presents for his wife and expend in fire works and feasting, God is sure to place him in such a position that he soon gains five hundred pounds and pays his debts. And altho I have seen in this country some melancholy instances that this proverb does not always come true, yet I cannot but rejoice at my brother's marriage.

Dr Wolff arrived <sup>a</sup> week ago from Bokhara and for some days was unwell of a fever, he is now recovered and talks of proceeding on his journey in a few days. One of the most extraordinary things about this strange man is that altho he has passed a great portion of his life in travelling in countries where few dare venture he is one of the most helpless and timorous people I ever met with. He cannot mount a horse without assistance ; and when mounted he can not guide him out of a town or village ; he is obliged to have a man on foot holding his bridle until he gets in the open country and then the horse follows the others or is driven along by one of the party just as if he had an inanimate load on his back. He appears to be quite incapable of taking care of his money, clothes, or any thing else belonging to him, and is so short sighted that I do not believe he could recognise his own wife across a middle sized room. The other day at the Christening of Mr and Mrs Bonham's son he began making civil speeches to the servant, <sup>a</sup> woman of about fifty years old in mistake for Mrs Bonham, and it was not until he heard the woman speak that he found out his mistake ; you may imagine the laughter of a large party there assembled who saw his mis take from the beginning. He is still in fear that some of the people of the Ameer of Bokhara may way-lay him and murder him on the road; for two nights I slept in his room, chiefly because he was so nervous and melancholy when left alone. He has taken a great fancy to me and talks of trying to get me some place
