# Maldon County Court — A Strange Case about a Field of Greens

**Chelmsford Chronicle**, 19 June 1891

> **MALDON COUNTY COURT, JUNE 17.**
>
> *(Before His Honour Judge Abdy.)*
>
> **A STRANGE CASE ABOUT A FIELD OF GREENS.** — Daniel Mead, farmer, of Woodham Walter, sued Messrs White and Co., of Covent-garden, for £5, balance due on the price of a field of greens, sold by him in February to the defendants for £20. — Mr. Bright, who represented the plaintiff, said that Mr. Mead sold a field of greens at Woodham Walter, to Mr. Jeffreys, in the employment of the defendants, and Mr. **Coolbear**, a Maldon greengrocer, for £29. Mr. Mead was told by them that they were buying the greens for White and Co. The field was cleared, and the greens were sent to the defendants. Mr. Mead had received £15 of the £20, £5 by a cheque from White and Co. and £10 from **Coolbear**, and the remaining £5 was the sum for which he was suing. The defendants had written him asking why he had sent them the greens, as they had not ordered them. It was Mr. **Coolbear** who had bought them. — In reply, Mr. Jones, who represented the defendant, the plaintiff said he had received letters from Mr. **Coolbear** saying that he was the purchaser of the greens, and would pay for them. He (witness) had also called upon **Coolbear** and asked him when he would pay for them. **Coolbear** told him the company would send the remainder of the money. — George Bowen, innkeeper, of Maldon, corroborated Mr. Bright's statement. He heard the bargain struck, in which the two men bought the greens for White and Co., for he drove them over to plaintiff's farm. — Mr. Jones said that White and Co. were simply commission agents, selling greens for **Coolbear**. Mr. Mead had changed his front because he had found he could not get the money from **Coolbear**. He now turned round upon Messrs. White and Co. — Mr. Jones called Mr. Jeffreys, a salesman, in the employ of the defendants, who said his employers never bought goods. Their business was to sell fruit and vegetables on commission. He went down to Woodham to advise **Coolbear** whether the greens were marketable or no. — Under a sharp examination by his Honour and Mr. Bright, the witness said it was customary for White and Co. to send and advise any of their customers in the way he was sent to plaintiff's farm for **Coolbear**. — Mr. **Coolbear** was next called. He said the first £5 which was paid to Mr. Mead was a deposit, and was paid for him (witness) by White and Co. He bought the greens, and he owed the remaining £5, and had many times promised to pay it to Mr. Mead. — His Honour: I am going to give a verdict for the plaintiff in this case. The defendants have been acting wrongly the whole way through. — The expenses of plaintiff, solicitor, and one witness, with plaintiff's mileage were allowed.

By 1891 George Coolbear was trading as a "Maldon greengrocer" — the census that year lists him as "general dealer." This case shows him buying a field of greens for resale through London commission agents White and Co. of Covent Garden, but unable to pay the full £20. The judge found against White and Co. George died the following year (1892).*
