## **Executive Summary** 

John Cormick (d.1833) and his son William (1822–1877) were Irish-origin physicians in Qajar Iran. John, from  Tipperary,  served  Abbas  Mirza  for  two  decades,  introduced  smallpox  vaccination  and  even authored a Persian treatise on it 1 2 . William, born in Tabriz, trained in London and returned as a royal physician. He treated both Mohammad Shah and the young Naser-al-Din and is notably the only Westerner known to have treated the Báb in 1848 3 4 . Persian-language sources (e.g. ) **اقتصاد دنیای** corroborate John’s 22-year service to the crown prince 5 and William’s role in the Báb’s trial 6 . Encyclopaedia  Iranica  entries  (John  and  William)  provide  comprehensive  English  biographies  with citations. In William’s case, his account of the Báb’s bastinado is published in Browne’s _Materials for the Study of the Bábí Religion_ 3 . Key dates: John’s smallpox treatise (printed 1830) and death (1833)； William’s medical career (seconded 1846, Báb trial 1848, death 1877). ## **Medical Reports by William Cormick** 

William Cormick’s most famous medical note is his **letter to Dr. J. H. Shedd** describing the Báb’s examination and treatment (he notes he treated the Báb’s bastinado injuries). Browne’s _Materials_ cites it (pp.260–262) 3 . H. M. Balyuzi’s _The Báb (1997)_ quotes Cormick’s letter: e.g. “Our report … was of a nature to spare his [the Báb’s] life” 4 . These show Cormick advocated clemency by declaring the Báb insane. No separate full publication of Cormick’s report is known; it survives only via these secondary publications. (No complete Persian text of the letter is cited; presumably in mission archives or Browne’s notes.)
