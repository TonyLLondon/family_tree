# THE BURGESS PERSIAN LETTERS

#### Edited by BENJAMIN SCHWARTZ

## PART VII

Tabreeg, February 6, 1846

I don't know whether I mentioned in my last that we were expecting the cholera here. Last year it has been committing dreadful ravages in Afghanistan and in the autumn had come as far as Meshed and Nishahpoor on the way to Tehran. But, thank God, the last news we had of it was that it had turned off to the north among the Turcomanns of the great Khorasan (or Khiva as it is sometimes called) desert. And even had it come to Tehran I see no reason why it should come here, because it might have turned off either north or south from that place or continued its direction from west toeast. My friends the Greeks are terribly frightened or rather were. One day <sup>I</sup> happened tomention that there was <sup>a</sup>report that the pestilence had arrived at Resht. There were five or six of them inthe room and itwould have amused you tohave seen their blanc faces. Itwas <sup>a</sup>false report, but <sup>I</sup>believe if this disease was tocome asnear asTehran not one could be found to remain inthe town.

Talking of cholera have you read "the wandering Jew" by Eugene Sue, <sup>I</sup>have just finished it (the translation inthe "Family Herald")" and think it <sup>a</sup>most clever novel, ifyou have not read it, itwill please you much as itsaim is tobetter the condition of the working classes, and unmask the villainy of the Jesuits. It appeared inNos inthis paper from July '44 toOctober '45. <sup>I</sup>have seen something of the intrigues of the Lazarist monks inPersia, and these gentry consider them selves assuccessors tothe Jesuits inthis country. They have been twice ordered toquit the country with ignominy and disgrace but still hold on altho now their power ismuch diminished. They formerly had establishments at Ispahan, Ou roumia and here, now they have only <sup>a</sup> small establishment in the district of Salmas, tothe N: W: of this on the frontier. <sup>I</sup> have had to fight against their plots toinjure my friends the American missionaries." And what at first sight seems most strange isthat the agent ofFrance, at this court, liberal, revolutionary France, the France of July 1830, supports these black coated gentry with all his power. <sup>I</sup>believe the reason of this may bethat asthe French have nointerest in these countries and consequently no pretence for placing agents or consuls, and not liking from their vanity, tohave nothing to dowith the politics of these

#### [EDWARD BURGESS TO GEORGE BURGESS]

Tehran, March 27, 1846

How does trade go with you? Pray do not fail to let me know when you write. The trade here is a very different one from that at Tabreez. The latter is a great mart for the surrounding countries. This not so much so, altho it is situated in the line of the great route between Arabia and Tartary yet its transit trade is comparatively speaking trifling. The most lucrative trade I know of here, altho certainly not the most respectable, is usury: lending money chiefly on pledges chiefly jewels and shawls at 20, 30, 50, and sometimes to my knowledge 120 pr Cent pr annm! and this, that is, for the lower rates of interest 20 or 30% pr anm for no small sums. The governor of the province of Fars (Persia proper) when last here borrowed about thirty thousand pounds at this rate most of which I believe he has now paid. This money was I believe borrowed without pledges but the usual way is when the amount is only a few thousands or less to take pledges for about double the sum lent. Much money has been made in this during the last twelve years in Tehran, but the commerce is not thought respectable and I fancy (forgive my superstition) that there is a curse attending on it.

The trade of the Greeks at Tabreez is not nearly so good as it has been and for some time back the principals have not been getting much profit; of course the houses at Tabreez which are only agency do not loose [sic] but I suspect the owners of the goods have had some losses lately.

A few days ago I received news of the death of a friend of mine, Capn Todd, <sup>101</sup> poor fellow his head was carried of [f] by a cannon shot at the great battle fought on the banks of the Sutledge last December. He was an officer of the Bengal Horse Artillery and many years in Persia; well read, clever and accomplished, pity such men should be made food for gunpowder. — The last news we had of this victory was only a few days after the battle was fought, there appears to have been much mismanagement, and consequently an unnecessary loss of life but I suppose, that to take Gwalior, as it was a victory, no one will be blamed.

I have scarcely been out of the town since I arrived having no horse here, indeed outside there is little to tempt one in the immediate neighbourhood. A dry flat stony plain without herbage and only here and there patches of cultivation. Not like Tabreez where for miles on every side there is a continuation of orchards and vineyards. At Tabreez too we are on much better terms with one another than the Europeans are here. The two Missions English and Russian are at opposite ends of the town and the intercourse of the members composing them is confined to a formal visit every now and then or a dinner nearly as formal. Very different is our little society at Tabreez. Some of the Greek merchants there though odd looking fellows and very queerly dressed, are men of most general information, and besides the dinners at the two consuls and other houses, M' d'Ozeroff keeps open house every Saturday night; and at his parties one meets Persians Russians Greeks and a mixture of all the nations to be found at Tabreez.

Amongst other benefits the Shah has received from our doctor's (Dr Bell) treatment (I say our because I do not think the Russian is much of a doctor) is that he has cleared his nose in such a manner that those who were not well accustomed to speak with him could not understand him. I was most agreeably surprised on entering the room to find that I could understand all he said and talked away in a manner that appeared to surprise some of the courtiers who expect a man to be rather abashed when first presented.

I dine with Coll Sheil two or three times a week and have dined once with Prince Dolgorouki, 103 the Russian Envoy, and once with Count de Sartiges the French agent. The latter's dinners are by far the most agreeable. He is a man of about forty and has been travelling attached to different missions all his life. He gave me some curious facts about the slaves in north and south America, where he has travelled much.

#### Tehran, May 7, 1846

I wrote to you last month from this place and then hoped to have addressed my next from Tabreez. You will see by the date that I am still here, and as yet I have been able to get nothing done, however I have yet hopes, but this life of loitering about the court and attending the Prime ministers levies is to me a most miserable one. It is very true that in all countries a man who has business with great people must expect to kick his heels about lobbies and ante-rooms, but then in Europe there is this great difference that the man you are dancing attendance upon, generally speaking, is not one who excites nothing but contempt, I can not tell you the misery I sometimes feel whilst I am waiting for an opportunity to speak to the Hadji (Prime Minister) on my affairs, altho I see Princes of the blood no better off than I am; and therefore I sometimes think that this feeling of annoyance is one that I ought to get over. One reason why I have never been able to come to any arrangement with my creditors is that they are affraid to come to any settlement with me until the Govt have made some arrangement with Charles because they consider the government as first creditor. This is one amongst the difficulties with which I have to contend, but it would be neither entertaining nor useful to any one to trouble you with any more of these details, at present. If I can do any thing satisfactory you may be sure I shall not fail to let you know.

We have very little news in this dull place. The Shah whose death you saw announced last autumn is now pretty free from gout and in very good health, the French doctor who formerly treated him and left Persia with a fortune has

Tabreez, January 5, 1847

I trust this will find you in England and that you will finally find it to your advantage to settle there, perhaps I have adopted some of the Persian fear of water travelling, but I can not but think of the risks you run in going backwards and forward from England to America, particularly when I see accounts in the newspapers of the accidents which happen to the Great Britain<sup>106</sup> and the other large steamers. We have as yet no news as to whether they have succeeded in getting this unfortunate ship off or not, as the last news we have from Europe is the 10th November from Paris. We expect the monthly post in two or three days when we shall most likely have news up to the 10th Decr from Paris.

The Prince still keeps me translating from the natural history, which he is exceedingly I was going to say childishly fond of. The work I want to be about is a translation of a Gazeteer, which I could do now having found a Turkish Geography from which I could get the spelling of the names of the places in the Arabic letters, which would do for the Persian. I do not like these animals at all, translating natural history does me no good, and perhaps not much more to the Prince. But he who pays the piper has a right to choose the tune. He is remarkably fond of pictures and paints a little himself. He has a large collection of pictures chiefly copper plates, some of them pretty good ones, amongst these he has a large book full of the figures of animals; he looks this over and over and then tells me to write the history of any which take his fancy. Now the Gazeteer would be a respectable work, and besides if I finished it, and presented a copy to the King, it might do me good in many ways. You will perhaps be surprised to hear that we have now in Tabreez no less than sixteen printing establishments, and more in Tehran, in fact books have become so cheap in Persia that it has done much injury to the trade of the copyists; still very fine manuscripts hold their price and there are several copies of the Shah-Nāmeh (the Persian Homer) which could not be bought for less than a hundred pounds and you might perhaps buy a printed one for as many sixpences. 107

You will be glad to hear that the cholera has ceased here and that I and my friends (except poor Glen at Tehran) have been spared. It is supposed to have carried off from 15 to 20,000 of the inhabitants of this province, and perhaps nearly as many from some of the other provinces of Persia. #### Tabreez, January 6, 1847

To day is the Greek and Russian X'mas day, but unfortunately the present Russian Consul General is a miserable fellow who shuts himself up in his house and will see no one, very unlike that good hearted jovial fellow M' d'Ozeroff who is now on leave in Russia, indeed he was only acting here during this ones absence his place being secretary of legation at Tehran.
