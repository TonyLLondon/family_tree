## Relevance to Edward Burgess

Wright gives a compact secondary synthesis of the Burgess brothers' commercial story and Edward's later reinvention in Qajar service. The excerpt is especially useful because it places Edward inside the wider British trade route through Trebizond and Tabriz, then follows the consequences of Charles's failed business into Edward's detention, service to royal patrons, and newspaper work.

Key claims relevant to Edward:

- Charles Burgess persuaded Edward to join him in Tabriz two years after Charles first reached Persia.
- Edward's 1833 letter is quoted for Charles's poor commercial judgment: "Charles appears to have taken any goods he could lay his hands on without any regard as to whether these were suited to the Persian taste or not."
- Charles absconded with funds advanced for muskets and military equipment, leaving debts, two children by an Armenian mistress, and Edward "held hostage on Charles' account for the rest of his life."
- Wright characterises Edward as "a devoted son and saintly character" and quotes his refusal to leave Persia dishonourably: "I cannot leave Persia without coming to some arrangement with my creditors, that is without running away, which you know I would never do."
- Edward is described as supporting Charles's abandoned mistress and children.
- Edward's career path is summarised as work with Edward Bonham, translation for a royal prince, tutoring and stable management, translation for Crown Prince Nasir ed-Din Mirza, chief interpreter after the 1848 accession, and editor of Persia's first newspaper.
- Wright repeats Lady Sheil's evidence that Edward also translated press articles for the Shah and Prime Minister that were not suitable for the public newspaper.
- Wright states Edward finally received permission to visit England in 1855, left Tehran with his Armenian wife and young son, reached only Tabriz, died there, and was buried in the Armenian cemetery.

## Research Notes

- Wright states Charles first reached Tabriz with goods in **July 1828**, whereas Abrahamian's introductory note places Charles's initial Persian arrival in **1827** as a military adviser and the direct-trade return to Tabriz in **January 1831**. These are not necessarily conflicting: they describe different phases, but the exact sequence should be kept tied to the primary letters and Charles's own *Brief Notice*.
- Wright says Edward travelled in 1855 with his Armenian wife and **young son**. Anna's later testimony and the Edward Burgess page describe daughter [Fanny Burgess](../../../people/fanny-burgess.md) as the surviving child on the final journey, while son John had died in Tehran. Treat Wright's "young son" as a secondary-source error unless another primary source proves otherwise.
- Wright gives useful interpretive language ("devoted son and saintly character") but should not replace the Burgess letters for exact chronology.
