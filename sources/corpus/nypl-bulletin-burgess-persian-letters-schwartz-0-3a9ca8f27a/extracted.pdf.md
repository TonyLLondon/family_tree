# THE BURGESS PERSIAN LETTERS

## Edited by BENJAMIN SCHWARTZ

### PART II

[HENRY BURGESS TO CHARLES BURGESS] Hempstead, October 18, 1830

Mr. Hanson gave me yesterday <sup>a</sup> deplorable account of the condition of the East whither you and my dear Edward are bending your steps. He says that the Plague is raging at Trebizonde and other places : — that the Cholera morbus has not yet spent its ravages : — that trade is very bad in Persia, and that the Persians will buy nothing at Constantinople. But, he anticipated, that in a few months the diseases would disappear and trade revive. Not that the trade was affected by the diseases alone — there being <sup>a</sup> great glut of goods generally. But he said that nobody hardly is now shipping, instead of every-body, and that he had himself just now been buying Manchester goods, to send out, because he obtained them at a reduction of five per cent ; which is a plain proof that he calcu lated on a speedy revival of business at Constantinople.

I have paid the insurance on your goods amounting to £53.17.6. Messrs. Hanson will write out to Mr. C. Hanson tomorrow to advance, if necessary, 200f , I having engaged by <sup>a</sup> letter written to them, according to their desire, to honour any bill drawn by you in that gentleman's favour not exceeding that am\*. In drawing any bill, if you have <sup>a</sup> necessity for drawing one, you must be careful to draw it at three months after sight; — four months would be better than three.

I wrote to you from LPool in my way out to Ebro. The part of that letter written on the Rail-way9 is <sup>a</sup> great curiosity. Any distinguished Englishman would consider it such. You cannot imagine what interest another specimen which I wrote at the same time has excited.

[CHARLES BURGESS TO HENRY BURGESS] Erzeroum, January 17, 1831

At length I have the happiness to address you from this place, having arrived yesterday night, after a journey by sea from Constantinople to Trebizonde, and by caravan in 13 days from thence. The Black Sea treated us very civilly, our passage was eleven days during which we were more frightened than hurt, although from the state of ship and Captain we have every reason to be thankful the goods are perfectly safe, and I believe not at all damaged either by sea or land. We were unfortunate in not seeing Mr. Brant at Trebizonde, he was gone to Erzeroum and after waiting three days to see him I was obliged to depart. However we arrived in safety at Trebisond and found that the plague was over except in two houses but that it had been very bad and had carried off upward of 3000 people. In the Caravanserai where our goods were, we were told that four hundred men had died within the last three months. At Trebisond we were very kindly received by M. Fontanie the French Consul, Mr. Brant the English Consul being gone to Erzeroum ; and we remained in his house whilst at Trebisond. From Trebisond here our road has lain entirely among mountains until within twenty miles of Erzeroum when we came onto the plain (entirely covered with snow and surrounded on every side by snow mountains) at the end of which stands the town. Here we proceeded to the house of Mr. Zorab, a Levantine, who is employed by the British Consul at Trebisond and stationed here. I am now writing in <sup>a</sup> room of his house.

The house belonged to one of the unfortunate Armenians who went away with the Russians when they gave this place back to the Turks. These poor people, all of whom were living in comfort and many of them in affluence here, were some of them persuaded by the Russians' promises, and at last when they (the Russians) found that many would stay they drove them out of the town with whips and forced them to follow the army, since that time they have been living in tents and have been subject to the greatest privations. Some have returned but these are very few as the Russians will not allow them to do so if they can prevent it. Fifty thousand people have thus most cruelly been forced to leave house and home. The consequence is that this place is almost ruined and instead of being the capital of Asia Minor as it ought from its situation to be, and as it has in great measure formerly, it is <sup>a</sup> desolate place the houses in ruins and the remaining part of the people as poor as rats.

I happened to be in the street when he was entering the town and as all the people were bowing down almost to the ground I thought I must give him some sort of salute being remarkable from being the only Frank present and from my standing upright, so I touched my cap as he passed to which he bowed very civilly. We arrived here on the 6th ; when this letter will go I know not but of course by the first tartar going to Constantinople. I shall keep it open until we start for Tabriz. — The Thermometer this morning (the 19th) at <sup>7</sup> o'clock stood at -0- and it has never been above <sup>6</sup> degrees above it any morning since we came here. Looking from Erz Roum over the plain you see nothing but snow, not <sup>a</sup> tree, not <sup>a</sup> bush, to relieve the eye. You here and there see about three small villages but these have no trees about them and are only distinguished from the plain by the mud walls of the houses showing black, amid the waste of snow. What a contrast from Constantinople and Trebisond, the latter in particular is surrounded with mountains the tops covered with snow and the sides clothed with roderdendrums, arbutus myrtle and other shrubs mostly evergreen.

At Trebisond we had two days shooting in company with <sup>a</sup> Mr. Abbot <sup>a</sup> young man who came there with Mr. Brant: I am no shot (at least at birds flying, <sup>a</sup> bullet at <sup>a</sup> still mark I shoot well) and cannot boast of ever having struck a bird flying except ducks and these are in such numbers all along this coast and are always in flocks that a man might almost shoot them blindfold.

(21st) I have left Mr. Zorab and Charles in another room and am now (about <sup>8</sup> P. M.) squatting on my carpet with C's desk on my knees finishing this letter. Close to my hand are my pistols (this is <sup>a</sup> regular practice ; travellers in Turkey when on the road never sit down without their pistols close at hand) and sitting on the floor near me is an Armenian servant casting up his accounts. Behind me leaned against the wall are my double barrelled gun and Turkish sword and my cartridge box lies on the carpet close to them. All around lie cloaks, coats, trunks, blankets Turkish slippers and other travellers traps. At one end of the room is a wood fire on each side of which Charles' and my carpets are and fire wood but, at the other end of the room is the door, some cupboards and a sort of gallery over the cupboards. The windows are three in number one foot long by four inches in width of one pane of glass, quite at the top of the room. You now have a description of our room which is much like all the better sort of rooms that I have seen here except that glass is <sup>a</sup> great rarity and only to be found in the best description of houses ; oiled paper is substitution in all the inferior ones.

What I have said about the Russians you may rely upon as <sup>a</sup> fact and I think a more disgraceful fact never was recorded of any people. Wherever we have been mentioning that we are Englishmen puts us instantly on a friendly footing with the Turks; no man living in England can conceive the great name the English have all over the East — may we continue to deserve it — although of late years our political conduct towards the Turkish government has done a great deal towards lowering us in the eyes of that power.

## [CHARLES BURGESS TO HENRY BURGESS] Corfu,29 July 23, 1835

We arrived here all well to day, after <sup>a</sup> very pleasant but fatiguing journey during the whole of which we had travelled night and day. The only unfortunate part of it was our not being able to get through the Neapolitan territories, owing to the absurd quarantine regulations of that government.30 We had purposed going the most direct route to Corfu, on Rome, Naples, and across to Otranto on the opposite coast, there to embark for Corfu. When we arrived at Rome we found that the Neapolitans had made a new law that no persons coming from France either by Land or sea should enter their territories until they had been sixteen days in Italy, the cause of this was the Cholera having broken out with violence at Toulon and Nice. Of course we could not submit to this delay. We therefore went back to Ancona still in the Papal dominions and there embarked on board the Ionian steamer, which we were very lucky in catching. Had this "contreteins" not occurred we should have reached Corfu three days sooner, however we have much cause to be thankful that the Ancona route was open to us, for the sixteen days detention would have been bad indeed. On 25th, Captain Stewart, Johnstone31 and myself leave for Zanta, thence to Patras, Corinth and Athens and then up the Archipelago to the Dardanelles. According to our calculations we hope to reach Constple in seven days, but it may be <sup>a</sup> day or two more than that, at all events we are all three inclined to use every possible dispatch. I do not think the Emma32 will be gone as at this season ships usually make very long passages, in fact I would rather bett that I shall be at Constple before her than otherwise.

# [CHARLES BURGESS TO HENRY BURGESS] Constantinople, August 10, 1835

We arrived here all well on the 7th inst. and found the Schah already returned having discharged her cargo at Trebizond and the Emma half way up the Bosporus, so that as to time I have managed exactly to hit the mark. My goods per Schah are now on their way to Erzeroum and I go on with those of the Emma which will form the last detachment. The Persian trade is here dull owing to the want of respectability and credit of the Persian merchants, prices in Persia still up and goods of all descriptions are much wanted. Edwards letters are very encouraging on all these points. In politics also a move has taken place very favourable to me ; the late Prime minister has been completely disgraced and it is said put to death,33 and in the course of these changes my most intimate friend to whom I have formerly rendered signal services has become commander in chief of the whole army.34 Who is Prime minister is not yet decided35 but it is thought that a person very favourable to English interests will succeed, at all events these changes are very favourable for the Old minister had become a complete Russian and was deservedly hated on all sides. Edwards letters are very cheerful, and he has been going on very well in every respect. I have heard much about him from Kirwan and Moore the two Irish gentlemen of whom you have heard and who have been living some time with Edward. #### 39 Mr. G. William Bergquist is the author of the following note:

The "system" mentioned in this letter is what is known as a progression or "martingale." These systems are not concerned with the particular numbers or places on which one should place's one's bets, but rather with the procedure as to the amount of the bets, according to whether the bets are won or lost.

Most gamblers maintain that the past does not affect the future and the chances of winning a bet may not be affected by a previous loss, but if the following bet is won, the previous loss is wiped out. Progressions in betting are based on this theory and it is considered advisable to increase one's bet after a loss, in order that one's wins will balance or exceed the losses. The fallacy in this theory lies in the assumption that one can not always lose.

The simplest progression is the system of doubling the stake after each loss. The stakes in the event of a succession of losses would be: 1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32. Each win would give a net profit of one unit. It would seem that the system mentioned in the letter was either this one or the so-called Montante Stanislas. Such systems are usually used in conjunction with betting on the "simple chances" in Roulette, namely: odd or even, black or red, first <sup>18</sup> numbers or last <sup>18</sup> numbers. All these chances pay even money. That is to say you win an amount equal to the amount of your stake.

The systems used vary in their details as to the amount the bet is increased after each loss and the number of times a certain amount is wagered. They are all supposedly based on rough laws of mathematical probabilities, but all scientific investigators have insisted that they are based on fallacies and, if adhered to, will result eventually in ruin. What some of the more conservative ones often do is to prolong the play at the table with a minimum of loss.

Insufficient details are given in the letter as to how the bets are to be made in the system men tioned. Obviously, the writer was not going to disclose his secret in its entirety. It is difficult to translate accurately since words are used with very special meanings, intelligible only to a gambler. It is clear that he proposes to play the black or red, for he says, when he hits upon it on one color, he changes to the other. This probably means when he wins on red he switches to black. This is in accordance with the gambler's dictum, "A change to black is imminent."

Briefly, as near as I can make out, the writer's system consists of making his first stake three francs (the unit) or any multiple of that. If he wins, he will continue to play the same stake until he loses. After his first loss, he adds two units to his stake. If he loses again, he adds two more units and continues to do so until he wins. When he wins, he continues to play the same stake he won with until he loses again. His next stake will be increased by two units. This continues until he has increased his stakes in this manner 19 times which would bring it to 39. No one but the writer knows what the modifications are. No doubt the absolute certainty (?) of winning depends upon these modifications.
