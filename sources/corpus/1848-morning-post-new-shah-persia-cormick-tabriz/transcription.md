# The New Shah of Persia — Morning Post, 13 November 1848

**Source:** Morning Post, 13 November 1848, p. 6. Via British Newspaper Archive / FindMyPast. [Scan](../../media/docs/1848-morning-post-new-shah-persia-burgess-dragoman.png).

**FindMyPast search:** [search link](https://www.findmypast.co.uk/search-newspapers/results?names=cormick&keywords=persia%20shah%20tabriz&newspaper=morning%20post&startdate=1848-11-13&enddate=1848-11-13&exactdate=true)

## Transcription

> **THE NEW SHAH OF PERSIA.**
>
> Letters have been received in town from Teheran to the close of September, announcing the felicitous accession to the Persian throne, and safe arrival in that capital from Tabriz (where he heretofore held the government of the province of Azerbaijan), of his Majesty Nesser-ud-Deen Shah, the eldest son of the deceased Monarch, Mahommed Shah.
>
> As we have before stated, the Shah proceeded to his new government with a large escort of troops of all arms, raised chiefly through the influence of Mr. Stephens, her Britannic Majesty's Consul at Tabriz. This gentleman holds a very high position in the estimation of the Shah, who, by a royal firman issued prior to his Majesty's departure from Tabriz, committed to his care the entire Armenian population of the town, amounting, it is said, to no less than 80,000. His Majesty also engages to ratify all decisions and judgments, penalties, punishments, and awards, ordered by the Consul Stevens, as if emanating from the Sovereign in person.
>
> Mr. Stevens is the same functionary who, when Consul at Samsoon, was despatched some years ago by Sir Stratford Canning to Moussul, to negotiate the liberation of the Nestorian Christians captured by the Kurdish chief Bader Khan, which mission he executed with great tact, as we at the time announced in our columns.
>
> A correspondent at Erzeroum of the *Echo de l'Orient*, a Constantinople journal, in confirming the above intelligence, notices, as somewhat remarkable, that the few British subjects at Tabriz have been the marked objects of the new Shah's favour, inasmuch as his Majesty has been graciously pleased to nominate and appoint **Dr. Cormick**, of that place, to be **King's Physician at Teheran**; **Dr. Fortunatus Consolani**, **Surgeon General to the Military Forces of the Empire**; **Mr. Stagno**, **Apothecary to the Forces**; **Mr. Burgess**, **King's Dragoman and Interpreter of Oriental Languages**; and **Mr. John Casolani** (a Maltese artist) to be **Miniature Painter of the Royal Family**, besides several other appointments of minor note, most of them in replacement of Frenchmen who held the like offices under the late Shah.
>
> At the date of our last advices tranquillity prevailed, and the youthful Sovereign was already adopting, under an able Prime Minister, very wise and salutary measures for the welfare of his subjects, who during the latter part of the late Monarch's reign have long groaned under the tyranny and oppression of the Prime Minister, Haggi Mirza Agassi, and who, after an unsuccessful attempt to place a younger brother of the present Shah on the throne, has fled to and taken sanctuary in a mosque near Teheran, reluctantly abandoning his ill gotten treasure and wealth in jewels, money, and entire villages, to the mercy of the people from whom he originally extorted them.

## Notes

This is a **complete, named appointment notice for Edward Burgess in a London newspaper**, published on 13 November 1848. Key findings:

1. **"Mr. Burgess, King's Dragoman and Interpreter of Oriental Languages"** — This is a formal Persian royal title not previously attested in Edward's own letters or in the academic literature. A **dragoman** (from the Arabic *tarjumān*) was an official interpreter and diplomatic intermediary — a position of considerable standing at a Middle Eastern court. The title "Interpreter of Oriental Languages" suggests Edward was responsible for translating not only English but possibly Turkish, Arabic, and other regional languages for the Shah.

2. **"In replacement of Frenchmen who held the like offices under the late Shah"** — This confirms that Nasir-i-Din Shah deliberately replaced the French-speaking court establishment of Mohammad Shah with British subjects. Edward replaced a French dragoman — explaining why the new Shah specifically sought someone with Edward's command of Persian, Turkish, and European languages.

3. **The full list of British appointments** at the new court:
   - **Dr. Cormick** — King's Physician at Tehran (Edward's close friend; they dined together three times a week in Tabriz)
   - **Dr. Fortunatus Consolani** — Surgeon General to the Military Forces
   - **Mr. Stagno** — Apothecary to the Forces
   - **Mr. Burgess** — King's Dragoman and Interpreter of Oriental Languages
   - **Mr. John Casolani** (Maltese) — Miniature Painter of the Royal Family

4. **Consul Stevens** — committed by royal firman to protect 80,000 Armenians at Tabriz, with his decisions to be ratified by the Shah "as if emanating from the Sovereign in person." This is the Consul to whom Edward was close.

5. **The "able Prime Minister"** praised in the final paragraph is **Mirza Taki Khan, Amir-i-Nizam (Amir Kabir)** — the same man Edward would work with directly on the state gazette from 1849 until Amir Kabir's murder in 1852.

This article provides the **earliest known formal record of Edward's court appointment** — predating his own letters about the gazette editorship by three years. It establishes that Edward was not merely an informal translator but held a named royal office: **King's Dragoman**.

### Significance for the historical record

**This title is not in any existing source.** As of March 2026:
- Wikipedia's article on Edward Burgess says only "promoted to principal translator" — no formal title.
- The Encyclopaedia Iranica articles on William Cormick and Tabriz do not mention this appointment list.
- Abbas Amanat's *Pivot of the Universe* discusses Edward's court role but does not use the term "dragoman."
- The NYPL finding aid and Schwartz's *Bulletin* series describe him as "chief interpreter" and "editor."
- **Consolani, Stagno, and Casolani** — the three other European appointees named in this article — appear to be **completely undocumented online**. No search results exist for "Fortunatus Consolani" in connection with Persia, and "Stagno" and "Casolani" return nothing relevant.

This 1848 *Morning Post* article may be the **sole surviving British newspaper record of the formal appointments** made by Nasir-i-Din Shah at the start of his reign, and the only source to give Edward's specific court title. It was apparently not known to Schwartz (1940), Amanat (1997), Wright (1998), or Karimi-Hakkak (1990).

### Iranica on Cormick — a nuance

The Wikipedia article on William Cormick states that Amir Kabir later **replaced** Cormick with the French physician Ernest Cloquet — the opposite of the 1848 article's claim that Frenchmen were being replaced by British subjects. Both are likely true: the initial appointments (autumn 1848) favoured British subjects, but Amir Kabir subsequently reversed some of these in favour of his own choices. This suggests the 1848 appointments reflect the young Shah's personal preferences at Tabriz, which Amir Kabir later overrode in Tehran.
