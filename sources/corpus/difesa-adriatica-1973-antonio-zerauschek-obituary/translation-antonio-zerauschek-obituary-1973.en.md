# *Difesa Adriatica* — “Another friend has left us”

**Masthead:** DIFESA ADRIATICA  
**Issue line:** Year XXVII — No. 3 — 25 March–3 April 1973  
**Headline:** ANOTHER FRIEND HAS LEFT US  
**Name:** ANTONIO ZERAUSCHEK  

---

Far from his Zara, he died in Florence in the last days of this past February, almost eighteen months after the loss of his faithful and beloved wife, Ester Addobbati. His wife’s death had dealt a fatal blow to both his body and his spirit, and the affectionate care and attention with which children and grandchildren lovingly surrounded him were in vain. He was 84 years old.

There is no native of Zara—nor anyone who lived in Zara between the two wars—for whom the figure of Antonio Zerauschek could be unknown. His fine appearance—tall, elegant, always impeccable—comes back to me from well before the First World War, when he, a perfect and tireless dancer, attended the balls and entertainments put on by the “Società Ginnastica” in Zara; after the war he was among those who enlivened social life at the “Circolo Arturo Colautti.”

A staunch patriot, he always followed the fortunes of his city.

His rise began with the end of the Great War. He can be regarded as a typical “self-made man”—someone who built his entire fortune by himself. From nothing, or nearly nothing, Antonio Zerauschek managed to rise, thanks to his tireless work, his skill, his integrity in business, his instinct for it, to the highest and most respected positions in the commercial and industrial life of the city—and not only that one.

He began with import–export trade, which he continued until the course of the Second World War forced him to leave Zara.

He then turned to industry as well; he was in fact one of the founders of the “Manifattura Tabacchi Orientali” in Zara, from which he later broke away and on his own account founded a tobacco factory that produced the famous and highly prized “Calypso” cigarette. He was also responsible for creating the first and only chocolate factory in Zara: “Ausonia.” In his factories and his business activities he offered substantial employment to Zara’s workforce: those who worked for him always remember him with gratitude and respect. He was generous with support and help to all his relatives and kin.

The particularly favourable situation created by the establishment of Zara’s “free port” opened every opportunity for the growth of Antonio Zerauschek’s commercial and industrial enterprises; he was able to hold the representation, for Zara and the Province, of every kind of foreign product—from flour to mineral oils, from petrol to motor cars, and so on.

Despite the demands of his many activities, which absorbed most of his days, Antonio Zerauschek knew how to apportion his free time between his family and his numerous friends. The centre of his attention, outside business, was always his family; he was an affectionate husband and father, counsellor and friend to his children, who returned his affection and care in equal measure, especially in the last, most painful period of his life.

Antonio Zerauschek also had a wide circle of friends, bound to him not by economic interest but by genuine friendship—drawn by his warmth, his simplicity, the charm of his modesty and his innate gentlemanliness: qualities that won him acceptance in every sphere of Zara society.

One of his greatest pleasures was to gather around him, in his always welcoming home, a group of his dearest and most trusted friends. I had no chance to enjoy—because I was then away from Zara—the gatherings he organised in the fine new house he had built to his own design shortly before the outbreak of the Second World War.

It is easy to imagine with what anguish he had to abandon, under the looming threat of Allied bombing, his interests, his industries, his creations, his house and his city. Dear, excellent friend! I keep always the warmest and most affectionate memory of him—for his sincere friendship, his simplicity, his innate modesty. When the war and the post-war years led our families to different places, our cordial friendship not only did not weaken—it grew stronger; therefore the loss of his wife first, and his own now, has deeply moved and grieved us.

*Difesa Adriatica* joins in the mourning of the children, grandchildren and all the relatives, and in particular the family of the friends Ziliotto, who will keep a dear, imperishable memory of the friend who has passed.

*g. z.*
