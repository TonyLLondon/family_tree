## **William O’Byrne White: evidencing regimental assignment and India postings from primary sources** 

## **Executive summary** 

The  strongest _primary-source_ evidence  currently  located  for  William  O’Byrne  White’s  regimental attachment is a notice in the The London Gazette 1 dated 16 March 1870, which explicitly connects him to the **1st Foot** (“appointed to 1st Foot”) in the context of Medical Department postings. 2 A second Gazette notice dated 25 April 1885 records that, as a **Surgeon-Major** , he was “granted retired pay, with the honorary rank of Brigade Surgeon.” 3 These two documents are the only governmentpublished notices about him that were identified and directly readable during this research. 

Clear _primary-source_ confirmation  of  his  being  physically  stationed  at **Jubbulpore/Jabalpur** (and therefore of attachment to the **2nd Battalion, 1st Foot / Royal Scots** ) has **not** yet been obtained in the form ideally sought by historians (e.g., a service paper, station return listing his name at Jubbulpore, or a regimental or medical return explicitly naming him at that station). What _has_ been located are two converging leads: (a) contemporary station-list publications that place British units (including the 1st Foot’s India battalion) at Jubbulpore in the relevant period (1879–1880), and (b) an indexed birth notice for “White — son — Jubbulpore — 1878” in a dataset derived from _Allen’s Indian Mail_ (as published and indexed by Families in British India Society 4 ), which is highly suggestive but **not yet attributable** to William O’Byrne White without opening the record-level detail or consulting the underlying issue. 5 

Critical gaps remain: the Gazette notices (or equivalent official notices) for his initial commission and promotions (1865, 1873, 1877), and any service papers showing India postings, transfers, leave/sick leave, hospitals, or duty stations. The report therefore concludes with a targeted archival plan to obtain the missing _primary record_ layers most likely to settle the Jubbulpore question and to reconstruct his full service chronology. 

## **Research approach and evidentiary standards** 

This  research  prioritised:  government  notices  (Gazette),  contemporary  printed  Army  publications (station lists), and record-derived indexes pointing back to identifiable archival series (especially India Office ecclesiastical returns, and War Office series known to contain returns for British troops overseas). The goal was not just “mentions,” but _traceable citations_ —issue/page references, dataset provenance, and pointers to archival series suitable for ordering or onsite consultation. 6 

A methodological constraint is explicit: some high-value sources could be identified but could not be read in full in this environment (for example, some digitised Army List PDFs from certain hosts failed to load; and some journal-hosted PDFs returned access errors). In such cases, this report distinguishes between (i) **confirmed** facts supported by a readable primary document and (ii) **leads/inferences** that require follow-up with the underlying document.

## **Verified service facts from the Gazette** 

## **Medical Department notice connecting White to the 1st Foot** 

A Gazette notice dated **16 March 1870** (War Office; Medical Department postings) contains the line: 

“Assistant-Surgeon  John  Middleton,  M.D.,  from  1st  Foot,  vice  William  O’Byrne  White, appointed to 1st Foot. Dated 16th March, 1870.” 2 

Two points follow, cautiously but firmly: 

1. **White is unambiguously in scope of the Medical Department posting system** used in Gazette notices, i.e., he is a commissioned medical officer being managed through War Office appointment machinery. 2 

2. The notice **explicitly associates White with the 1st Foot** by stating (in the printed line as reproduced) that he was “appointed to 1st Foot.” This is the key documentary hook for investigating whether he served with the 1st Foot in India, including any particular battalion. 2 

What the notice does **not** provide is equally important for historical method: it does not name a battalion, does not name a station, and does not state whether this was an India posting (or a home/ colonial posting). Therefore, it is **necessary** to connect this “1st Foot” appointment to later station evidence  (and,  ideally,  to  his  service  papers)  before  concluding  “Royal  Scots  2nd  Battalion  at Jubbulpore.” 

## **Retirement on retired pay with honorary rank** 

A later Gazette notice dated **25 April 1885** states: 

“Surgeon-Major William O’Byrne White is granted retired pay, with the honorary rank of Brigade Surgeon. Dated 25th April, 1885.” 3 

This provides primary evidence that by 1885: 

- he held the substantive rank of **Surgeon-Major** , and 

- 

- he exited active service onto **retired pay** , receiving the **honorary rank of Brigade Surgeon** (a retirement dignity in the printed notice). 3 

This notice is also a useful anchor for “pension/half-pay” style research because it is the formal public statement tying him to retired pay status, which should correspond to entries in retired lists and pay registers. 

## **Why earlier commissions/promotions may be hard to locate via quick text search** 

The Gazette’s own guidance on searching its archive notes that for older material (especially pre-1998 and supplements), filtering and indexing can be limited because it relies on scan extraction and OCR; consequently, name searches can miss relevant notices and researchers often need to search by **date ranges** , **variant name forms** , and manual browsing of likely War Office sections. 7 

This matters because it plausibly explains why the 1865/1873/1877 notices were not retrieved quickly, even though the 1870 and 1885 notices were.

## **Evidence and hypotheses for India postings and Jubbulpore** 

## **Contemporary station lists placing the relevant regiment in Jubbulpore** 

Digitised station lists for British troops show Jubbulpore/Jubbulpore as a station in the late 1870s–1880 period and are the correct class of printed source to cross-check regimental location against a medical officer’s regimental attachment. Examples located include a _Stations of the British Army_ issue dated **5 July 1879** and another dated **4 September 1880** , preserved as scans by military-history repositories. 8 

These station lists are valuable because, if they show (as expected) that the **1st Foot’s India battalion** was at Jubbulpore in the late 1870s, then— _conditional upon proving White was the 1st Foot’s medical officer  at  that  time_ —they  provide  a  coherent  explanation  for  family  events  in  India  and  for  the Jubbulpore connection. But they are not, by themselves, proof that White personally was present at the station. 

## **An indexed birth notice: “White — son — Jubbulpore — 1878”** 

A particularly consequential lead appears in the FIBIS database’s _Allen’s Indian Mail_ “All India Births 1878” dataset. On the dataset browse page, one index entry reads: 

• **White — Son — Jubbulpore — 1878** 9 

Because William O’Byrne White is known (from other family research) to have had a son born at Jubbulpore in 1878, this index entry is highly suggestive. However, as presented in the browse list, the entry does **not** display (a) the father’s full name, (b) rank/regiment, (c) the mother’s name, or (d) the exact date—i.e., the fields one would need to attribute it to this specific White family _as a historical proof_ .

**Historical inference (explicitly labelled):** If the record-level detail for that entry (or the underlying _Allen’s Indian Mail_ issue) states that the father is “Surgeon-Major W. O’Byrne White” or similar, it would become _primary evidence_ that White was at Jubbulpore in 1878 (and would often include regiment/ department). At present, that crucial confirming text is not yet in hand, so the report treats the index entry as a lead rather than proof. 9 

## **Place-context pointer: Jubbulpore/Jabalpur as a record cluster** 

FIBIS also maintains a Jubbulpore/Jabalpur category for cemetery and memorial materials, confirming that Jubbulpore/Jabalpur appears as a structured place unit within Bengal-related archival and indexing practices used for British-period records. 10 This is useful chiefly for broadening the search net: if White or his dependants appear in memorial inscriptions, cemetery registers, or burial returns there, it could produce direct station evidence.

## **Assessment of “1st Foot / Royal Scots 2nd Battalion” as White’s India attachment** 

## **What can be said on present evidence** 

From the Gazette alone, the clean, defensible statement is: 

- By 16 March 1870, White is documented in a War Office Medical Department notice involving the **1st Foot** , with the printed text linking him to the regiment by “appointed to 1st Foot.” 2 

From the station-list leads, the defensible statement is: 

- Station lists exist for 1879–1880 placing units in India (including at Jubbulpore), and these are the correct printed sources to establish whether the 1st Foot’s India battalion was stationed at Jubbulpore in the relevant window. 8 

From the birth-index lead, the defensible statement is: 

- In a dataset derived from _Allen’s Indian Mail_ , there is an indexed entry for a **White** son born at **Jubbulpore** in 1878; this entry requires opening at record level or consulting the underlying issue to determine whether the father is William O’Byrne White. 9 

## **What cannot yet be claimed as proven** 

On the currently retrieved and readable primary material, it is **not yet proven** that: 

- White’s 1st Foot appointment was specifically to the **2nd Battalion** , as opposed to another battalion or a different phase of attachment. 

- White personally served at **Jubbulpore/Jabalpur** , as opposed to simply being on the rolls of the 1st Foot at a different station. 

• The 1878 “White son Jubbulpore” index entry refers to his family. 9 

These points are precisely what the recommended archival next steps are designed to resolve.

## **Targeted next steps to complete the full service reconstruction** 

## **Secure White’s officer service papers and extract postings/leave** 

The single most efficient way to obtain a _full postings chronology_ is to locate White’s **officer service file** (or compiled service record) in the War Office series at The National Archives 14 . For a 19th-century Army medical officer, this is typically where you will find: 

- commission and promotion dates (often with authority), 

- overseas postings (including India), 

- medical leave and sick leave, 

- transfers between staff/regimental posts, and 

- 

- retirement authority and retired pay details. 

- 

Even if not all of that survives in a single file, the attempt is high yield because it replaces inference with administrative fact.

## **Reconstruct battalion-level attachment via regimental records** 

To prove specifically **“2nd Battalion”** (not just “1st Foot”), you will usually need one of: 

- battalion-level officer lists, or 

- regimental muster/pay records that list “medical officer attached”. 

If the 1st Foot muster books or pay lists survive for the India years, they can sometimes show the medical officer borne on the battalion’s strength at a given station. That is the documentary bridge between the _regimental_ (Gazette) and the _geographical_ (Jubbulpore). 

## **Exploit India-based “returns” that name officers at station** 

A powerful but underused method is to search annual or periodic **returns** (sick returns, casualty returns, station returns). The FIBIS database home page itself demonstrates the existence and indexing of War Office India-related returns in series such as **WO 334** , including “Annual Sick Returns” and 15 “Annual Casualty Returns” for British officers and troops in India. 

If White appears in such a return (especially as “Surgeon” or “Surgeon-Major” at a station), that can yield: 

- station + date, 

- duty status (sick, on leave, invalided), and • sometimes unit association. 

## **Open and capture the full text of the 1878 Jubbulpore birth notice** 

The “White — son — Jubbulpore — 1878” entry in the _Allen’s Indian Mail_ births dataset is potentially _the_ fastest way to produce a direct, contemporary statement connecting White to Jubbulpore, because birth 9 notices often include rank and service affiliation. 

Recommended workflow (historian-grade): 

1. Open the **record-level detail** for that entry in the FIBIS dataset (or locate the underlying _Allen’s Indian Mail_ issue in a library scan). 

2. Capture the exact wording (keeping quotation length within copyright limits), plus date/issue. 

3. Use that wording to search again in the Gazette/Army Lists for corroborating postings around the same date window. 

Even a single line such as “at Jubbulpore, the wife of Surgeon-Major W. O’Byrne White, A.M.D., of a son” would be direct proof of his presence and rank at station in 1878. 

## **Systematically re-search the Gazette for 1865, 1873, 1877 notices** 

Given the Gazette’s own warning about OCR/search limitations for older material, the best practice is: 

- search by **date ranges** around 31 March 1865, 1 March 1873, 31 March 1877, not only by name; • search on variants: `OByrne White` , `Byrne White` , `W. O’Byrne White` , and even just `White` within a narrow date slice; 

- browse the **War Office / Medical Department** sections within those issues. 7

## **India Office Records strategy: ecclesiastical returns and military department material** 

Although this report did not retrieve the specific IOR military file for White’s postings, FIBIS’s datasets demonstrate that relevant India Office ecclesiastical returns exist and are cited using the **IOR/N** series (e.g., Bombay marriages: **IOR/N/3** ), which is exactly the pathway you flagged in your research design.

- **IOR/N/…** birth/baptism returns for 1878 (to locate the “White son Jubbulpore” entry at archival source level), and

## **What was found, and what remains insufficient** 

This research located two decisive government notices for William O’Byrne White: a 16 March 1870 Gazette item linking him to the **1st Foot** , and a 25 April 1885 Gazette item recording his retirement on retired pay as a **Surgeon-Major** with honorary **Brigade Surgeon** rank. 17 It also located high-value leads for proving his Jubbulpore presence (station lists; and an indexed 1878 Jubbulpore birth notice for a “White” son in _Allen’s Indian Mail_ ), but it did **not** obtain, in readable form, the record-level birth notice text or a service/station return naming him at Jubbulpore, nor the earlier Gazette notices for his 1865/1873/1877 commission and promotions. 18
