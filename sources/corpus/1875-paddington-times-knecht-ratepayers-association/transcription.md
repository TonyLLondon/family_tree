# Paddington Ratepayers' Protection Association — Paddington Times, 26 June 1875

**Source:** Paddington Times, 26 June 1875. Via British Newspaper Archive / FindMyPast. [Scan](../../media/docs/1875-paddington-times-knecht-ratepayers-association-letter.png).

## Transcription

> **PADDINGTON RATEPAYERS' PROTECTION ASSOCIATION.**
>
> TO THE EDITOR OF THE "PADDINGTON TIMES."
>
> Sir, — In your report of the election of Vestrymen for No. 1 Ward, Paddington, I find a mistake as regards Mr. Knecht being put forward by the Ratepayers' Association. Such was not the case. Had it been so a poll would have been demanded, and no doubt, he would have been successful. Mr. Knecht is at the present time auditor for No. 2 Ward, and to whom the ratepayers are greatly indebted for the manner he has gone through the parochial accounts, which can be seen by his letter upon the gas meter question which appeared in your paper of last week.
>
> By inserting the above, it will oblige yours,
>
> THOS. G. SPENCER,
> Hon. Secretary of the Ratepayers' Association.
>
> Warwick Hotel, Bridge-terrace, Paddington, June 25, 1875.

## Notes

This is George Knecht's **earliest known press appearance** — a letter from Thomas G. Spencer, Honorary Secretary of the Paddington Ratepayers' Protection Association, correcting the *Paddington Times*' report of the vestry election. Spencer clarifies that Knecht was **not** put forward by the association for the No. 1 Ward election; had he been, "a poll would have been demanded, and no doubt, he would have been successful."

Key details:

- **Auditor for No. 2 Ward** — George already held a formal parochial role as ward auditor by June 1875, responsible for reviewing the parish accounts.
- **The gas meter question** — George had written a letter to the *Paddington Times* the previous week about gas meters, which Spencer praises as evidence of his thoroughness. This letter (not yet located) would be George's earliest direct writing.
- **Spencer's endorsement** — The honorary secretary speaks of George in glowing terms: the ratepayers are "greatly indebted" to him.
- **Political trajectory** — By 1875 George was already a prominent ratepayer and auditor. He would go on to serve on the vestry itself, where the *Paddington Times* regularly reported his contributions from 1876 to 1898.

George had been in England at most twenty years by this point — a German immigrant cabinet maker who had risen to become ward auditor and a figure the Ratepayers' Association would have backed for the vestry.
