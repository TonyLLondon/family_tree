### PART VIII — Conclusion

[Edward BURGESs To GEORGE BURGEss] Gul Ahek near Tehran, June 29, 1849 I enclose you <sup>a</sup> document to enable you to act for me with regard to the property left by my poor mother;" I doubt whether this will do but we can not get any regular powers of attorney in Persia and if such <sup>a</sup> thing is necessary you had better send me out one for me to sign here. Messrs Scott Bell Co of New Broad Street London, who know me as one of Cormick's executors and who have been in the habit of sending powers of attorney to Persia about Cormick's estate business, will tell you how this ought to be done. If my uncle pays you the money you can pay it into either Scott Bell C" or F. Ede and Sons so that I may draw against it if I want it.

have come out of town for <sup>a</sup> day or two to this village which is situated towards the mountains and is the place of the British Mission and the other English pass the summer months [here] toavoid the great heats and bad climate ofTehran; <sup>I</sup> have not yet moved out of town because the King is still at his summer palace just outside the walls, and those in the service are obliged to be near, and it is uncertain whether hewill go up towards the hills during the summer or not. <sup>I</sup> shall try toget leave during the Ramazan (the Mahomedan fast month) and come and live with my friend Abbott the consul; this month falls this year, from the 20th July to 19th Augt and the King talks ofgoing up to the hills during this month, if so do not think <sup>I</sup> shall be obliged togo with him, as his will not be astanding camp. <sup>I</sup> hear there are some most beautiful places in the range of mountains to the north, with some fine trout streams and <sup>a</sup>very cool climate. <sup>I</sup>enjoy coming out here for <sup>a</sup>day or two very much and feel like <sup>a</sup>cockney come toRichmond for the Sunday.

The war inKhorassan still continues indeed <sup>I</sup>am sorry tosay that our troops do not appear to beany nearer putting down this insurrection than six months ago, except that the rebel chief the Salar is pretty much confined tothe capital of the province Meshed. Until this war is finished we shall not get onwell, the governors of the other provinces will not send in their revenues regularly, nor will the people pay them, until we are fairly masters of Khorassan. This makes the government very hard up for money and consequently people are not regu larly paid.

### Tehran, February 21, 1850 [EDWARD BURGESS TO GEORGE BURGESS]

How is the climate of New York during the summer, I suppose hot, and very cold in the winter. Are you much troubled with mosquitoes during the hot months as we are here. In fact on all the plains of Persia these unpleasant little animals abound, that is wherever the cultivation is carried on by turning a stream of water over the ground to irrigate it, as this forms an artificial marsh and produces mosquitoes fevers and agues and other disagreeables.

Has the gold from California made much difference in the trade of New York? The papers say not, at least as far as the money market is concerned. But by degrees the people collecting there must cause a brisk trade in manufactures.

Persia is still a good deal unsettled and Meshed the capital of Khorassan is yet in the hands of the rebels. 124 The government have sent an immense force against this city but as yet they have not been able to take it. Partly owing to its being considered a holy place and both soldiers and officers being unwilling to fight against it. Its sanctity however would not prevent the soldiers from plundering it if they could get into the town.

My friend Mr Abbott our consul here has gone on a journey to the south of Persia from whence he will return by way of Bushur, Busora, Baghdad to Kermanshah and here. The government pay his expenses and he is to make up a commercial report on the trade of the countries he passes through.

124 Salar withstood a siege of eighteen months in Meshed, at the end of which period he was handed over to the besiegers by the townspeople. He was barbarously tortured in a vain attempt to make him reveal the hiding place of his treasure, and then strangled.

me the editor altho without any increase of pay or allowances as yet. (Our first number appeared on the 5th Feby and I received your letter on the 18th.)

You ask about my income and manner of life. My pay is four hundred tomauns a year (say about a hundred and eighty pounds) and I have an allowance for forage for my horses and house-rent. Soon after we arrived I was long in arrears now those arrears are paid up, except what was due to me before this king came to the throne which however I hope to get shortly. My employment is making out translations of the European news for the king and prime minister — these are about forty pages of Persian a month which is about equal to forty pages of English print the same size as a leading article in the papers and the pages are small quarto. Besides this I have to attend levees and other work and now I have the whole management of this newspaper on my shoulders. 125 So you see I have work enough. My friends are chiefly the officers of the British and Russian missions with many of whom I am on intimate terms. For the first week or two after the paper was started I was so harrassed that I did not know which way to turn, and one evening after I had been dining at Col Shiel's (our minister here) I went to Mr R. Thomson's the third attaché, and there [were] three or four of us there, and I thought we were to have a pleasant hour or two over our grog and songs, all free and easy, when Meerza Hussein Kouli the Persian secretary to the mission began criticising a passage in one of the articles in the gazette, upon a question of style. (In this however, I afterwards proved him wrong.) This rather annoyed me and as I had been hard at work at the paper all day I wished to hear no more about it, one of my friends perceiving this said "Why Burgess is vexed." I said, "I was but it has passed off in proof of which I will sing you a song" and I sang "Gaffer Gray Gaffer's son and their little jackass" which I dare say you recollect for our poor Mother used to sing it to us when we were children. Perhaps you may not recollect it for when you had attained an age to remember such things, misfortunes had come upon our family and our poor mother had not much spirits for singing. It is an old and very rare song with perhaps no great merit in it, but there is a verse in it peculiarly applicable to my being bothered by people from morning 'til night about the newspaper. I never heard any one sing it except our dear Mother.

Col and Mrs Shiel are both exceedingly kind to me, the lady plays the piano and sings prettily and when the Russians dine there we have some good music. [EDWARD BURGESS TO HENRY BURGESS]

Tehran, February 19, 1852

I am very much gratified to perceive that you take much interest, in my paper or rather the government Gazette managed by me. I was afraid that my writing in every letter about the paper might become tiresome; you are quite right in observing that the carrying on such a publication requires much caution, particularly with regard to the foreign news which I publish, otherwise England Russia or Turkey, the three governments with which Persia has most to do, might complain, give trouble to the government and get me into a mess. I have however gone on very well as yet and the longer the thing goes on the better I, and those employed under me, carry on the routine of the business.

My illness which I perhaps foolishly mentioned to you last autumn was nothing at all serious and has now left me, but after trying almost all the European medecines such as rhubarb magnesia soda etc without much success I found much benefit from the Persian treatment viz astringent acids particularly pommegranites and I now continue taking this delicious fruit occasionally although I have little occasion for it medecinally.

We are expecting another attaché to the Mission which I shall be glad of as we are so few English we are always glad of an addition to our party. Col Farrant now in England on leave will also most likely be back during the summer.

I should like you to see Sheffea Khan, the Persian Chargé d'affaires in London. He is not here considered a clever man, and never before held a higher place than that of Lt Colonel (unattached) and every one was much surprised at his being named Chargé d'affaires and that at such a court as London. I believe I told you in a former letter that my friend in his mission is Meerza Ibrahim the interpreter. I think it is probable that Sheffea Khan may now be recalled, his great protector was the late Ameer i Nizaum (the Prime Minister) and it is not likely this government will go on paying between three and four thousand pounds a year for a Chargé d'affaires at London when they can very well do without one. The Persian Government have also sent an expensive mission to St Petersburgh, this they will probably continue at least for some time to come.

My wife is quite well and sends her duty. I have found out that she is a good horse-woman for the other day as we were riding out, I proposed going to a place called Cheshma Allee a pretty place where there is an abundant spring of pure, clear, water gushing out from under a rock, and abounding with fish. and forgetting that ladies were not in the habit of riding across country I took a straight line and we had some little ditches to get over which she jumped beautifully.

[EDWARD BURGESS TO GEORGE BURGESS]

Tehran, March 22, 1852

I do not know whether you have heard that I was married last autumn to a Georgian lady of good family and thank God she makes me a most kind agreeable and affectionate wife. She is pretty and about thirty three years old. She has no money in property but, as is often the case in Asiatic marriages she has brought with her a great deal of what is well worth having, such as her own rich dresses, carpets curtains, embroidered cushions, and other things which tend to make a house comfortable. She speaks Armenian and Turkish and writes the former so fast that to day she has written two letters for my one. I would make her write to your wife in that language but you would have little chance of getting the letter read in New York. One of her carpets just fits the centre of our large room which is about twenty four feet by fourteen, with the window, a recess eleven feet by six, this carpet however which is a very rich one, we only put down on high festivals; my old carpets do very well for common wear. Lately she has been busy turning out the wool of the sofa having it and the chintz well washed and restuffing it, so you see she tends to keep my house in order.

I am going on much as usual and altho' I have not gain[ed] much in money by the Gazette I have gained a great deal in consideration both amongst the Persians and Europeans. I do not see so much of the latter as formerly. I was always fond of home and now that I am married I am more so than formerly but I am still received most kindly at the Foreign Missions and thank God I am contented with my lot.

[EDWARD BURGESS TO GEORGE BURGESS]

Neeawaran near Tehran. June 22, 1852

You will see that my letter is dated from Neeawaran. This is a small town some ten miles north of Tehran, and from that city here there is a gentle ascent, the Elbourz mountains rising in precipitous peaks to the height of perpetual snow almost behind the town. The place itself is built in terraces so that going up some of the streets is almost like going up stairs. The King has a summer palace here and as I am obliged to consult the Prime Minister and also sometimes the King himself about articles to be put in the gazette, I am ordered to be here and carry on the work here, all except the actual printing which is done in town. The climate here is most cool and agreeable and I am much better off in this, as thank God in many other respects than I was last year for then I was ordered to carry on the newspaper in town and altho I lived at a village some seven miles out, going backwards and forwards in the great heats, four days in the week, and working hard all day in such a filthy climate as Tehran is during the greater part of three months, was by no means agreeable. #### [EDWARD BURGESS TO GEORGE BURGESS] Tehran, September 22, 1852

We returned into town from our summer residence on the 5th and the climate is agreeable enough, the days hot but the nights mornings and evenings most delightful. I did not enjoy my residence in the country so much as most years both I and my dear wife having been much out of health. Indeed my health has been so bad that it was with difficulty I could carry on my work, the duties of which are rather arduous I mean editor of the state gazette. Just before the downfall of the late Prime Minister<sup>128</sup> I had got things into such train, that I carried on the business without much difficulty, and from having carried it on well during the absence of the court at Ispahan that is during nearly five months, the Prime Minister and also the King had such confidence in me that all I had to do was to go every Monday morning to the minister and ask him if he had any orders with regard to the weeks Gazette and his invariable answer was no. "you carry on the paper exceedingly well act according to your own judgment," except they had some particular articles to publish and then they were always sent to me in due time for the press, and I went away and brought out the paper to the best of my ability. Now the matter is very different. I publish nothing except the foreign news, without the prime minister's 129 having previously revised it, this gives me a great deal of trouble. ## [EDward BURGESs to GEORGE BURGEss] Tehran, December 21, <sup>1854</sup>

Persia is still neutral in the war which rages between Russia and the European powers" and I hope she will continue so. Russia has bid fair, for the assistance of Persia, against the Turks, but did not bid quite enough; Persia had great grounds of complaint against Turkey, and an army of fifty or sixty thousand Persians on the Turkish frontier, would have been a powerful diversion in favor of Russia: however Persia has remained quiet; and I am very glad she has done so; as any other course must have been most ruinous to her, except she had joined the allies against Russia; now, much depends upon the fall of Se bastepol for should that place hold out much longer, it is possible, but still very improbable that Persia may still be drawn into a Russian alliance. ### [EDWARD BURGESS TO HENRY BURGESS] Tehran, February 26, 1855

Since writing to you last month your most kind affectionate and fatherly letter, in answer to mine of the 19th Octr praying you to take care of my dear wife and child in case of your surviving me has arrived. How shall I describe to you the emotions this letter inspired in me, thankfulness to God for having given me so good and kind a father and gratitude to you were my predominant feelings. You appear hurt that I should for a moment have doubted your being, in the event of my wife being left a widow, a kind hearted protector to her, the feeling I had in writing that letter arose from another cause, that is the dislike I had of burthening you with such a serious charge; it being the sacred duty of a son, after he has arrived at the age of manhood to aid assist and support his father. Instead of being burthensome to him, the son ought to labour and undergo privations that the father may live in ease and comfort. How different have been our positions, whilst you have been undergoing privations, I have been enjoying many comparative luxuries.

You will be very glad to learn that, thank God, my health has somewhat improved, I am now altho' still very weak, able to put my horse into a trot or canter without the fear of falling off, a thing I have not done for many months, the disease still continues but its bad symptoms are much mitigated.

I have given in my petition for leave to the Prime Minister, it is addressed direct to the king, and I was about to take it to him direct, but was advised to present it first to the minister. He has agreed to it verbally, but I shall not think myself secure until the King has agreed to my petition and my leave is published in the Gazette. Mr Thomson put in a kind word for me to day in asking the minister to allow me something for my expenses to England, the minister laughed and said "this is rather too bad not only are we going to pay a man for sitting in his house and doing nothing but you ask us to pay his carriage home into the bargain; however tell Burgess to follow up his petition himself, and

#### [EDWARD BURGESS TO GEORGE BURGESS] Tehran, March 14, 1855

You will perhaps have heard that I am endeavouring to obtain leave to go to England. I have already applied for it and it has been promised me but I shall not think myself safe until my leave is announced in the Gazette which is the custom. We hope to start in about two months from this time. Before that I am afraid I shall not be able to make my arrangements as to the carrying on the government Gazette etc. If I could get my arrears of pay I would engage to start in ten days but this is the great difficulty. Most Europeans on going to Europe to help them with their expenses for the journey and because they do not know well where to leave the things during their absence sell off everything and repurchase on returning. This I have found a very bad plan. They sell things for say £100 by auction and have to replace them when they return and spend £300 in doing so. We shall only sell a few things which are comparatively useless to us, and leave the rest under the care of some friend until our return; when I arrived here with the King in the autumn of '48 my whole property tents, carpets, clothes every thing included was carried on four mules, and one of them was a light load for I had a servant riding upon it. Now I suppose that thirty perhaps forty mules might be required to carry every thing we have, so much do things collect when one sets up housekeeping and remains in a place. We shall travel as lightly as possible but travelling stage by stage from here to Trebizonde, as my wife and child can not unfortunately travel post we must have several loads with us, such as bedding, carpets, tents cooking utensils etc. We shall travel as quickly as possible but I am afraid we shall not get to Trebizonde in less than two months, 134 The new British minister to Persia.

Our lovely spring weather has commenced but unfortunately I am not often in a condition to enjoy it, my weak health often prevents my moving about or even attending to my duty. The orchards are now most beautiful from the masses of fruit tree blossoms, and the air mild balmy and oderiferous with the spring flowers. I am quite sure the journey will do me good, exercise and freedom from anxiety are the things I want, and I shall try hard to get off from this by the 1st May so as to have the benefit of the cool weather. It is true that at that time here, the days are too hot for travelling, but the mornings and evenings are still delightful, and except for a few stages between this, and near, Tabreez the road from Tehran to Trebizonde passes thro' cool upland districts, the best season for passing which is that at which we hope to be travelling over them.
