# THE BURGESS PERSIAN LETTERS

### Edited by BENJAMIN SCHWARTZ

### PART III

[Edward Burgess to his Parents] Tabreez, February 3, <sup>1833</sup>

At the time of <sup>a</sup> tartars departure I have always letters to write and accounts to make up for Constantinople, Trebizonde, and Erzeroum. Having to do this has more than once prevented me from writing to you so fully and at such length as I could wish.

I arrived here Jany <sup>17</sup> having been <sup>a</sup> little more than <sup>a</sup> month away from Tabreez ; the road was in <sup>a</sup> terrible state from snow, and I was <sup>14</sup> days performing <sup>a</sup> journey (400 miles) which I had travelled with ease in the summer in <sup>4</sup> days ; I have now forsworn post travelling in the winter, in these countries, for during this trip, I was three times lost in the snow, and it was almost by <sup>a</sup> miracle that I ever got out again alive.

Captn and Mrs Campbell were very glad to see me in Teheran and I spent <sup>a</sup> fortnight there very pleasantly. Beek arrived there in <sup>a</sup> litter, <sup>a</sup> few days before I left it. The reason of his travelling in a litter is that he is in a most deplorable state from cronic reumatism which complaint has stuck by him for the last six months and he is now hardly able to walk alone. He is disgusted with the Prince, and Persia altogether and said if he was able to travel he would not stay in Persia another day: I think he is disgusted unreasonably, for what with his pay and presents, etc., he will leave Persia a far richer man than he came into it.

Feby 24th. I take the opportunity of Mr Newman's going to England to send this letter: —Mr Newman was bred for the church but upon his friends pressing him to take orders he declined, and preferred travelling in the missionary cause, he is a remarkably learned man ; he is now returning from a long journey thro' Mesopotamia to Bagdad he has been several times plundered by the Arabs and has gone thro' <sup>a</sup> variety of adventures. If he calls on you and I dare say he will, you will be much pleased with his conversation.

I have <sup>a</sup> letter from Mr Hanson saying that the Joanna finally left the Bosphorus Jany 17. — having some time been detained by contrary winds. Since then I have no news of Charles.

I spend my time here pretty pleasantly and am very well contented with my lot ; I generally dine with Dr Cormick40 three times <sup>a</sup> week and in the evening we amuse ourselves with playing duets on the flute: whenever I can spare <sup>a</sup> day from my business I go out shooting frequently with some of the Gentlemen attached to the Russian mission, many of whom I find very pleasant companions.

I dine with the Ambassador41 occasionally, and his parties are very entertaining from the mixture of people of different nations attached to the mission. There are but two Russians amongst them, but then there are Poles, Germans, Georgians, Circassians, Turks and Armenians. His Exclly himself is <sup>a</sup> Sclavonian formerly <sup>a</sup> soldier of fortune in Napoleons army invading Russia ; was taken prisoner by the Russians, and after the French had retired, he took service with his captors, receiving the same rank which he had held in the French army. Since then he has been serving almost entirely in Georgia and against the Turks and Persians ; it is curious to hear six languages talked in the same room by different people, but this is frequently the case here. I myself can only manage French and Turkish. I understand some Persian and can talk <sup>a</sup> little but as all the people of this place talk Turkish, I have not much occasion for it.

I have received your letter of Octr 19th and in it you say that you wish me to come to meet Charles at Trebizonde, Charles in his former letter expressed the same wish in these words, "if you have nothing to do you had better take <sup>a</sup> trip to meet me" — had he said that it was perfectly necessary I should un doubtedly have come, but he did not mention it as on business, at all, I therefore regarded it as so much time lost, because while I am remaining here I am always gaining information about business, and for the last six months have been earning money, by selling goods on commission.

### [Edward Burgess to his Parents] Tabreez, August 15th, <sup>1833</sup>

Most sincerely my Dear Father and Mother do I ask your pardon for the neglect with which I have treated you during the last four months, the only excuse I have is that whenever <sup>a</sup> tartar has been about to start I have been as busy as we used to be at Leeds on "packing days." I should think that before this time Mr. Beek will have arrived in England, and have called on you, should he do so I have no doubt that your penetration will soon discover that all he says with regard to Persia does not deserve implicit credence ; although Mr. B. and I were on very fair terms whilst he was in this country he has not left very favourable impressions either upon my mind or upon the minds of any body else here or at Constantinople, he is just the sort of man is every body's acquaintance and nobody's friend. I should say just the contrary of Bonham. Should you happen to meet <sup>a</sup> Mr. Paxton42 in England (Lieutenant 4th Lt. Dragoons) please remember me to him. He staid about 10 days at my house as he was passing thro' Tabreez ; he was going home on leave for three years from India, if you should like to see him you will hear of him at the cavalry depot Maidstone.

Business — <sup>I</sup>have hitherto written nothing upon this subject nor did <sup>I</sup> intend to write upon itwhen <sup>I</sup> began this letter having thought it better to leave it to Charles, but upon consideration <sup>I</sup> think, perhaps <sup>I</sup> have gained some valuable information whilst remaining here, which he wants. About remitting <sup>I</sup> shall say nothing seeing that Charles must know most about our affairs in England: In answer to this question which <sup>I</sup>have put to him more than once as to "whether you would be seriously inconvenienced by not having funds to meet our bills when due" he answered "not seriously" ;how far this is the case <sup>I</sup> cannot judge. Charles left this place for Teheran June 13th. #### [Edward Burgess to George Burgess] Tabreez, August 4, 1836

It is rather curious that our Father and you should have thought it better for me to seperate from Charles, because this is a point I have been urging with him ever since the year 1833 and I can not get him to agree to it, one reason for his refusal is that Father Mother and you would be so shocked at hearing of our seperation! One of my reasons for wishing to seperate was that altho a partner I have no more control over the management of the business than if I were merely a servant and I do not think it fair that I should be made responsible by

[Edward Burgess to the Secretary of the London Zoological Society] Tabreez, October 12, 1837

Having heard that some skins of birds sent to England from the neighbour hood of Erzeroum and Trebizonde have been thought interesting I have taken the liberty of sending to the Society two birds of the partridge kind, which, as they are rare in this country, I hope may not be found unworthy of the Societys acceptance.44

They are called in the language of the natives of this country, Turkish, "vor keklik" and in Persian, "cafik i ders," which may be translated "partridge of the wilderness."

These birds were sent to me in the month of July last and I put them into <sup>a</sup> small garden adjoining my house and fed them on wheat but they also eat a great quantity of the leaves and even stalks of kidney beans and other kitchen garden plants and upon this food they became fat and seemed healthy.

They inhabit the most inaccessible and highest places in the mountains of Persia, and do not descend towards the plains in winter, which in this country even in the plains, is exceedingly severe. They are said to feed at that season, when the whole country is covered with snow, on the sides of the steepest banks and rocky ravines facing the south, where the snow never remains long and is never deep, and there they find in plenty the seeds of the mountain plants which grow in such places, and which I have observed to form the chief food of the red legged partridge, which is numerous here.

During the summer they appeared to suffer much from the heat, but, a thermometer on a north wall in the enclosure where they were stood at 95 for some hours during that day.

[Edward Burgess to George Burgess] Tabreez, October 13, <sup>1837</sup>

I send with this <sup>a</sup> letter to the secy of the London Zoological Society (mind not the Surry) Bruton St., you will see that I have said towards the end that you will see the birds and the letter delivered to the secy.

Mr Abbott44 sent home some skins of birds from the neighbourhood of Erzeroum and Trebizonde and the society made him <sup>a</sup> corresponding member. I should be glad if they made me one too, but I do not expect that they will at least not until I have sent them some more things, however in future I shall send them merely the skins for stuffing sending the birds alive costs so much and is so uncertain. I hope these two partridges reach you safely. They were in good condition when they started and I think they are very curious as they are evi dently of the partridge kind altho nearly as large as a turkey, their flesh is said to be high flavoured and tender and to be <sup>a</sup> cure for the plague ! — mind I do not say so only the natives do. These birds do not run as the red legged partridges do but when flushed they rise at once as the grey (English) partridges, but they are very strong in the wing and take very long flights. The only way to nail them is to walk a long way round them and get above them then with good dogs you may get shots at them for they rise well and lie so close when once you are above them that, without dogs a man is very likely to walk past them and then as soon as ever they are uppermost away they go up the hill side again running much faster than a man can particularly on the rocky banks where they are found. The Persians say that the red legged partridge, (kuzil keklik) is finer eating than the grey partridge (chiel keklik) if this is the case it is just the con trary of what is the case in England for I have heard that in Suffolk where the red legs abound they do not consider them good. Hanson will let you know in what ship my two partridges are sent from Constantinople.

Tabreez is now very empty of English, Bonham and Major Cameron gone to Teheran, Col Macintosh to Tiflis and Odessa, Major Woodfall and Dr Riach to Ouroumia, leaving Mr Abbott, Mr Nisbit and me here. I expect Bonham

The weather here is now very pleasant, just cold enough to have the windows shut morning and evening and the sun warm in the middle of the day. This season is the grape harvest and I seldom go outside the walls of the town without seeing camels, mules and asses coming from all parts loaded with grapes. A great part of the grapes are consumed now, some are dried like the sultana raisins, and some are half dried by hanging the bunches on strings fastened across the ceiling from side to side of the rooms, (much as they hang oat cakes in Lanca shire). In poor peoples houses the ceiling is sometimes covered with these grapes and they keep pretty fresh all thro' the winter. And a great part is made into wine for amongst the upper and middling classes of Persians I should think two thirds drink wine and one third did not. They also make of the ripe grapes a sweet syrup very much like treacle and of unripe grapes they express the juice and use it for making shirbets and also in stews and made dishes, the Persians are as fond of made dishes as the French are.

You may recollect that Charles brought out with him in the year 1835 three grey-hounds. Two of these, the two brown ones, were spoilt by being coupled together on the road, the black one is <sup>a</sup> fine dog but he also was much injured by the journey. Their sons and daughters are now in Mr Bonham's possession and they say nothing escapes them. I intend to go out and see them run tomorrow if nothing prevents me, and if we have the luck to find <sup>a</sup> hare which at this season are not numerous in this neighbourhood.

Should the Zoological society refuse to accept the two birds I have sent home, you can give them to any one you please or keep them yourself. I do not see why these birds should not breed in England and become numerous. The pheasant is said not to be originally a native of England but to have been brought from the neighbourhood of the river Phasis in Georgia.

I believe that people run more risks of their lives from broken limbs in England than we do here, witness dear Fathers horrible accident last spring. The first news I had of this was in a letter from Bonham which I received in Teheran which said "your father has recovered from his accident and has gone into the country."

The winter has been rather severe the thermometer frequently standing at 6 and even 10 below zero and the snow which now lies on the ground and has been deep has brought down quantities of hares from the mountains. I have not been out shooting for the last fortnight because the last time I was out I sprained my knee and Dr Riach told me to walk as little as possible.

The last time I was out I shot two hares four ducks and <sup>a</sup> goose. This is about the average of a days shooting here at this season, it is not much, but it is enough to amuse one and after all the exercise is the great thing. The days after Xmas day I was out and shot three Woodcocks two ducks and two snipe. Woodcocks are rare here and I believe I am the only man who ever shot three in <sup>a</sup> day. Since I hurt my knee I have been out once with the Prince, to see his hawks fly as this sport is carried on on horseback it suited me very well. The Prince by way of being civil ordered that I was to have all the hares which might be caught that day, they brought me six all caught by the dogs or shot. The first was caught in a manner which would be thought little better than poaching in England. The dogs, some pointers some mongrels, put it up and drove it towards the horsemen, the horsemen immediately began galloping their horses towards the hare and shouting like so many mad men, the hare was soon turned and went off in another direction and left the dogs, but there were horsemen in all directions and she was turned again and again and at last caught by the dogs.

As you, I dare say do not know much about hawking I will endeavour to describe how they mannage it here. The hawks they generally use are gos-hawks because they are more easily reclaimed than any other kind, and do not require to be carried hooded as falcons do ; they have little bells on their legs and fre quently a string with some beads or other ornament on their necks. The horse man with the hawk on his wrist always endeavours to get above the partridges if as is generally the case they are on the side of a hill, in doing this he frequently has to dash his horse up and down precipitate banks until he attains the summit of some cliff or rocky bank on the side of which are the partridges ; he then begins to descend until he has flushed the birds, and he pitches his hawk giving it <sup>a</sup> swing off so as to set it in motion and it seldom fails to take one of the partridges which is taken from him, and the man mounts his horse and pursues the par tridges in the same way again. If this hawk is flown at <sup>a</sup> pheasant which you know does not rise like a partridge but goes up perpendicularly, and as is generally jthe case he cannot reach the pheasant before the pheasant is above him, he lets the pheasant mount up into the air and continues flying quietly under him being quite certain that the pheasant must come down some time or other, the pheasant after he finds he can not escape by towering in the air makes a sudden dart to regain the thicket, then away goes the hawk after him and if he can seize the pheasant before the bird gets down into the bushes he is all right, but if once the bird gets again into the bushes it is almost impossible to flush him again as he knows very well that the hawk can do nothing to him as long as he continues running in the cover.

We have no satisfactory news from Camp. The Shah with his army was before Herat and had been there some time without taking it and it appeared to be doubtful whether he would succeed or not.48 If he fails his army will be in a bad state for want of provisions and will have to retreat some twenty marches in the depth of winter, where there are no supplies, with an enemy harrassing their rear, as the nearest point where they could winter is Meshed, or even where they could remain in safety. Having no good news from the camp throws a gloom over our market; else prices are very good and stocks in hand light. Bonham is holding for <sup>a</sup> rise and I think if we had decent news from the camp prices would advance. Silk has been too dear for the Constantin'le or London market this year, not a lb has gone that way. What has been bought has been taken to Moskow or Baghdad or used in home consumption. But there is yet a good deal on hand and the speculators have been let in for it this year, as they bought judging from the prices of last.

I have two little spaniels who fight as game as any terriers could and the most curious part of it is that they are the best friends possible until I rub their noses together, then they begin, and would not leave off until one was beaten to a stand still. This I do not allow but when I think they have done enough to satisfy their honor I seperate them and give them each <sup>a</sup> good dinner.
