# Vallancey, *Collectanea de Rebus Hibernicis*, vol. I, pp. 568–571

## "The Law of Tanistry Illustrated"

### p. 568

> In the mean time as to what regards this Teige in the genealogical way, I shall observe that he is the ancestor of a noble family of the O Briens, who were the antient possessors of the isles of Arran near Galway, and of Tromraith with several other landed properties in the county of Clare. And we find in John Magrath's Irish History of the Revolutions of Thomond, called *Caithreim Thoirdhealbhaig*, written by the author, A.D. 1459, that Donal son of Teige, surnamed Aluinn, or Comely, otherwise called Sean Teige, was in actual possession of the estate of Tromraith and held his residence at that place in the year 1277. The same history mentions, that said Donal O Brien sent his two sons, by name Mahon and Donal Oge O Brien, to assist Turlogh O Brien son of Teige Dalouisge against the earl Thomas de Clare, who came that year to the assistance of Brien Ruadh, said Turlogh's uncle. The above Teige Aluin O Brien was the sixth direct descendant from this Teige Gle, or Neat, according to all the genealogies that I have seen of this family. **The chief and direct representative of this noble branch of the O Briens descended from Teige Gle, was John O Brien, lately living**

### p. 569

> **living at his own estate of Moyvainine and Cluanties in the county of Limerick**; who had a son called **Thady O Brien, an officer of known valour in the queen of Hungary's service**. I have in my possession an authentic copy of a public instrument signed by the mayor and bailiffs of the city of Galway the 30th of March 1588, (John Blake being then mayor and Walter Martin and Anthony Kirrivan bailiffs) and countersigned by Alexander Dermot notary public; whereby they testify to queen Elizabeth in favour of Morrogh Mac Turlogh O Brien then living, "That the Mac Teiges of Arran, his ancestors, were under her majesty and her predecessors, the temporal captains or lords of the islands of Arran and their territories and hereditaments elsewhere, under the names of Mac Teige O Brien of Arran, time out of man's memory; and that they had seen the said Morrogh Mac Turlogh O Brien authorised by all his sept, as chief of that name and in possession of the premisses as his own lawful inheritance, as more at large, say they, doth appear in our books of records, wherein he continued until of late he was by the usurping power of the O Flaherty's expulsed, from whom it is taken by some inquest found in her majesty's favour. We say, moreover, add they, that the sept of Mac Teige O Brien of Arran, since the foundation of this city and town, were aiding and assisting to ourselves and our predecessors against her majesty's and her predecessors enemies in all times and places, whereunto they were called as true, faithful,

### p. 570

> "faithful, and liege people to the crown of England, to maintain, succour, and assist this town." Thus that public instrument. **The above John O Brien is the sixth direct descendant from the said Morrogh son of Turlogh O Brien**, mentioned in the preceding abstract, **as appears by his genealogy**, wherein I find it mentioned by way of a marginal note, that Dermod Mor O Brien, grandson of the above Sean Teige or Teige-Aluinn, received twelve tuns of wine as a yearly tribute from the town of Galway, in consideration of protecting the harbour and trade of that city, from all pirates and privateers, by entertaining a suitable maritime force for that purpose.
>
> N.B. A modern compiler of the genealogies of the peers of Ireland calls Teige Gle by the name of Teige-a-line (aiming doubtless at his sixth descendant Teige Aluinn) and makes him the second son of Dermod O Brien, and only brother of Turlogh the ancestor of the Thomond branch, whom he stiles the heir of his said father Dermod, and reserves Connor na Catharach, who was his true heir and eldest son, to make a kind of Pythagorean appearance in the world as second brother of Donal Mor, and third son of his own younger brother the said Turlogh. But a foreigner and perfect stranger to Irish annals must be excused in these blunders, gross and inexcusable as they otherwise should be deemed.

**Key findings:**

1. Vallancey himself names **John O Brien** as "the chief and direct representative" of the Clan Tiege, living at **Moyvainine and Cluanties** in County Limerick — corroborating Burke's 1833 and Hardiman 1820.
2. John O Brien had a son, **Thady O Brien**, "an officer of known valour in the queen of Hungary's service" — i.e. in the army of **Maria Theresa** (r. 1740–1780), placing John O'Brien's active years in the mid-18th century.
3. Vallancey states **"I have in my possession an authentic copy"** of the 1588 certificate — the same document later held by James Roche of Cork (per Burke's 1833).
4. The full text of the 1588 certificate is given in Vallancey's own words, confirming the Mac Teiges as "temporal captains or lords of the islands of Arran… time out of man's memory."
5. **p. 570 is critical**: Vallancey confirms John O'Brien is "the sixth direct descendant from the said Morrogh son of Turlogh O Brien" and says this is established **"as appears by his genealogy"** — meaning Vallancey had access to a family genealogy document belonging to John O'Brien. However, Vallancey chose not to reproduce the five intermediate generation names. The genealogy document existed in the 1770s–1780s.
6. The marginal note in John O'Brien's genealogy recorded that **Dermod Mor O Brien**, grandson of Sean Teige/Teige Aluinn, received twelve tuns of wine annually from Galway for protecting the harbour — confirming the family's role as protectors of Galway harbour from pirates.
