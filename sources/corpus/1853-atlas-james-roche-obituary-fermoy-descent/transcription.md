# Atlas (London), 9 April 1853 — Obituary: "J. R. of Cork"

## OBITUARY

> **"J. R. of Cork."** — James Roche, full of years, and of the respect and honour which should accompany old age, died on the 1st inst., at his residence, Woburn-place, Glenmire-road, Cork. His initials, "J. R. of Cork," were well known in the literary world. **Mr. Roche was lineally descended from the Viscounts Fermoy**, on the paternal side, **and maternally from the O'Bryens of Arra, in the County of Limerick, who traced their pedigree to the Irish Monarch Brien Boroimhe**. He was the third son of Stephen Roche, of Limerick, and his second wife **Sarah O'Brien**, and was born on the 30th of December, 1770, so that his death took place in the 83d year of his age. His younger brother, William Roche, represented his native city, Limerick, for some years, having been the first Catholic member for that city after the repeal of the penal laws. In very early life Mr. Roche left this country for France, where he was educated with a view to practising at the French bar as an *avocat*. Events which changed other destinies than his, frustrated, however, his plans, and led him to a totally different career. The self-exiled young Irishman witnessed the grand spectacle of the meeting of the States General at Versailles, on the memorable 5th May, 1789. He had heard Mirabeau thundering in the Tribune; seen Danton and Marat rushing from the Jacobins or the Cordeliers to the scarce less fanatical Assembly. Vergniaud, Lafayette, Jeanne Marie Roland, Dumouriez, the Cazales, Maury, Robespierre — all the chiefs of all the parties, Royalist, Girondist, Jacobin — he had lived amongst them, watched their proceedings, seen their elevation, and survived their fall. The precise date of Mr. Roche's return to his native country we do not know. He, however, got away from France, where he had been for some time imprisoned, and jointly with his eldest brother, established a bank in Cork, under the firm of Stephen and James Roche. Both brothers married Cork ladies, sisters. For many years the success of their banking establishment was immense; but with the peace, in 1815, came a fearful series of commercial disasters; and after a vain struggle for some years, against daily increasing difficulties, the bank was forced to stop payment of its notes in 1820. Throughout all his life, Mr. Roche had given up a large portion of his time to literature. He now proceeded to England, and he soon by his literary acquirements began to realise an income of respectable amount. Subsequent accessions of property made his circumstances easy; and when the National Bank of Ireland was projected, about 18 years ago, he was named as one of the original directory. About this time he returned to Cork, where he since continually resided, and where he acted as the local director of the banking establishment just named. He was nominated a Justice of the Peace for the Borough immediately after the passing of the Municipal Reform Act. He was Chairman of the Bench of Magistrates, President of the Cork Literary Society, the School of Design and other similar institutions, Vice-President of the Royal Cork Institution, and a member of nearly every Board and public body in Cork. Mr. Roche was not a man of great original ability, but a perfect prodigy of learning. He had literally devoured libraries year after year, and we believe he never lost the recollection of a single fact once acquired by him. Hence his information was absolutely wonderful. It extended to every subject, and was vast on each. His last, and at his time of life marvellous effort was a work entitled *Essays of an Octogenarian*, and, as its title indicates, revised for the press at eighty years of age. It is a most curious and singular collection of lore of all kinds. Out of respect for Mr. Roche's memory, the various public scientific and literary Institutes of Cork were closed till after his funeral obsequies, which took place on Monday last in his family vault at St. Finbar's.
