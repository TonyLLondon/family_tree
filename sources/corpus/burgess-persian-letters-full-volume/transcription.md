# The Burgess Persian Letters

Edited by Benjamin Schwartz. Published in the *Bulletin of the New York Public Library*, 1940–1942.

Family correspondence (1827–1855) of Charles and Edward Burgess, sons of the English banker, economist, and editor Henry Burgess. Includes an appendix: interview with Anna Burgess (née Saginian), Leicester, March 1880.

THE BURGESS PERSIAN LETTERS

                         Edited by BENJAMIN SCHWARTZ

Among the papers, letters, etc.,* presented to the Library by Mrs. Thomas A. Foljambe Burgess of Scarsdale, New York, are two series of letters from the Near East, written during the years 1827–1855, by the brothers Charles and Edward Burgess, sons of the English banker, economist, and editor Henry Burgess. These letters afford as connected a history of the adventures and fortunes of Occidentals in the Orient as any "narrative of travel and description" that has been published, while the pen of the trained novelist could make of the story as rich and warm and fascinating a novel as has been written on such a theme. But aside from their interest as human documents and their possible inspiration for the novelist, the letters, written during that period when Turkey became the "sick man of Europe," when Russia was whittling Persia down to her present boundaries, when England was establishing and consolidating her whole position in India and Asia Minor, acquire added value by affording unexpected glimpses into the play and counter-play of the Great Powers, the initiation and prosecution of policies which, at this very moment, are coloring events in Egypt, the western Mediterranean, and the Middle East.

From 1826 to 1828 the closing acts of the drama of Greek independence from Turkey were being played to their stirring conclusion. The famous English diplomat, Stratford Canning, was minister to the Porte; those perennial foes, England, Russia, and France, were banded together in a crusade to free the Christian Greeks; an allied squadron had "blundered" into Navarino bay in 1827 and destroyed the Turkish fleet. In Persia the grandiose schemes of the crown prince, Abbas Mirza, to recover territories lost to Russia was ending in the utter rout of the Persians, the capture of Tabriz by the Russians, and the humiliating Treaty of Turkomanchai, concluded just in time to enable the Czar to declare formal war on Turkey. The British Minister to the Shah, Sir John Macdonald, was striving mightily to throw up any sort of bulwark against a Russian threat to India, and British money, munitions, and drill sergeants for the Persian army (and the concomitant of British manufactured products) were beginning to trickle into the country in ever-increasing volume.

Against this background, Charles Henry Burgess, eldest son of Henry and Frances (Ridsdale) Burgess, went out to Persia at the instance of a Persian merchant, Sadik Bey (later ennobled by the Shah as Syeed Khan) in 1827, at the age of eighteen, to join the British military mission under Major Hart, as a Lieutenant of infantry with the forces of H. R. H. Prince Abbas Mirza. Charles does not appear in the British Army List, but his letters, notes, and sketches indicate education and training in military tactics. During his brief career as a soldier he distinguished himself by winning the Persian order of the Rising Sun and Lion, second class, for gallantry in action, and comported himself in such fashion as to win the approbation and confidence not only of his military colleagues, but also of those highly placed in the Persian government.

As a result of connections thus established, in 1829, when Persian finances became so muddled that most of the British military mission had to be released, Charles found himself set up as a merchant, financed by his father, and working through Sadik Bey, and the firm of Hanson, of London and Constantinople. Brilliant and bold in his conceptions, he was successful beyond expectations at the outset, and was probably the first Englishman to ship goods from the mother country through the Black Sea directly to Trebizond in Turkey, thence by relatively easy caravan stages to the markets of Persia, instead of the previous prohibitively expensive route via the port of Bushire in the Persian Gulf. He traveled back and forth between Persia and England constantly during the years 1830–1836, executing private and public commissions, and on at least one occasion, acted as King's Messenger for the British Foreign Office. His restless imagination running riot at the enormous possibilities opened up before him, he kept pyramiding venture on venture until finally, in 1836, the bubble burst.

Thereafter, a combination of circumstances such as ill-health, "trouble in the Balkans," inability to satisfy the clamorings of creditors, and perhaps interest in newer and greener pastures served to prevent his return to Persia. He died at Aix-la-Chapelle during the winter of 1854–1855.

In the meantime, his younger brother Edward, whom he had brought out with him in 1830 and associated in the business, was left in Persia, practically a hostage in the hands of Charles's Persian creditors, who thus vainly hoped to compel the older brother's return. At first Edward's position was extremely precarious, for apart from the risk to his person he led a hand-to-mouth existence which was in sharp contrast to his previous estate as "a prosperous and thriving merchant." But his singularly winning disposition (his widow could still say, twenty-five years after his death, that "he was the most all-loved person" she had ever known) enabled him to retain former friends and make new ones among both Europeans and natives, even those who had suffered from the unhappy outcome of Charles's speculations. Prince Bahman Mirza secured his appointment as civil governor of two small villages, an appointment which, because the villages were in the neighborhood of a community of Nestorian

Christians, led to Edward's acting as financial agent for the American Mission at Tabriz and Urumia. He tutored the Prince's children and had charge of the royal stables. He did odd jobs of translating into Turkish and Persian, both languages of which he had acquired a fluent knowledge. Thus, while continuing to be held officially responsible with his brother, and confined to the general neighborhood of the town of Tabriz, he managed to spend his days not too unpleasantly. His letters abound in the names and events which have brought Asia Minor into the western limelight; many of his references are of prime historical importance not only in regard to the matter discussed, but also the actors involved, with most of whom he had personal acquaintance. Thus, revealing bits of information are afforded us by the retailing of incidents concerning such great names as Rawlinson and Layard, the fathers of Assyriology, or of the inner workings of the British and Russian embassies.

In 1848 Edward's link with the royal house was greatly strengthened by the accession to the throne of Nasir i Din, nephew of the now exiled Bahman Mirza. Edward sold everything he could lay his hands on and joined the triumphal march from Tabriz to Teheran, where he was immediately appointed chief interpreter and later made editor of the state gazette. His most pressing personal concerns were now relieved, for although still technically a debtor, the change in government and the lapse of time had so altered his status that he was practically free to do as he pleased. By this time, however, the East had entered into his soul and the thought of returning to a mercantile existence in England was so repugnant that he cemented his ties in Persia by refusing the offer of a position in America (secured for him by his younger brother George), and finally, in 1852, by his marriage to a beautiful Armenian girl. And so, although most of his career had been shadowed by the meteoric rise and fall of his brother Charles, and although that shadow was only dispelled at the very end of his life, Persia came to mean the summum bonum of his existence. One small cloud remained; for a long time he was unable to secure leave to return to England for a visit. Ironically, when he finally did wring official permission from the government to leave the country for that visit, he died en route, still in Persia.

[CHARLES BURGESS TO HENRY BURGESS.] Tiflis, July 3, 1828

You will be surprised at the date of this letter as my last was from Constantinople since that I did not give you any certain intelligence that I should proceed through Russia — however here I am and upon the whole feel glad that I have taken this course although much that has occurred since my departure from Constantinople have changed my vision and expectations in what way I cannot here explain to you as this letter is almost sure to be opened before it reaches your hands. On May 1st I set sail from Constantinople having with some difficulty succeeded in finding a ship going to Taganrog — after a prosperous voyage of 16 days including 6 quarantine I landed at the lazarretto in Taganrog where I had still 14 days to remain in confinement on shore. When I left Constantinople it was with the intention of landing at Kertch in the Crimea from thence embarking for Redout-kale and thence proceeding to Tiflis which is only 5 days journey, on arriving at Kertch I found there was little chance of my getting an opportunity to proceed by sea to Redoutkale and the governor told me I should run great risk of being made prisoner or killed by the Mahometan tribes who inhabit the country between the Black Sea and Tiflis and as the war was already commenced against the Turks they would be sure to plunder and carry off any Christian they might meet — he therefore advised my proceeding to Taganrog and from there going by land to Tiflis, this I did and after a journey of 20 days arrived here.

  The "system" mentioned  in this letter is what is known as a progression or "martingale." These systems  are not concerned  with the particular numbers  or places on which one should place's  one's bets,  but  rather with the procedure  as to the amount  of the  bets, according  to whether the bets are won or lost.
  Most  gamblers  maintain that  the past does  not  affect  the future and  the chances  of winning a bet may not be affected  by a previous  loss,  but  if the following  bet  is won, the previous loss  is wiped  out.  Progressions  in  betting  are  based  on  this theory and   it  is considered   advisable   to increase  one's  bet after a  loss,  in order that one's  wins will balance  or exceed  the  losses.  The fallacy in this theory lies in the assumption   that one can not always lose.
  The  simplest  progression  is the system  of doubling the stake  after each  loss. The  stakes  in the event of a succession  of losses would be:  1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32. Each win would give a net profit of one unit.  It would seem that  the system  mentioned   in the  letter was  either  this  one or the  so-called
Montante  Stanislas. Such systems  are  usually used  in conjunction  with  betting  on  the "simple chances"  in Roulette, namely: odd or even, black or red,  first 18 numbers  or last 18 numbers.  All these chances pay even money. That  is to say you win an amount  equal  to the amount  of your stake.
  The  systems  used vary  in their  details  as  to the amount  the  bet  is increased   after each  loss and the number   of times  a certain amount   is wagered.  They  are  all supposedly  based on rough laws of mathematical   probabilities, but  all scientific investigators have  insisted  that  they  are based on fallacies and,  if adhered  to, will result eventually in ruin. What some of the more  conservative ones often do is to prolong the play at the table with a minimum of loss.

The Persians say the nightingales  are in love with  the roses, and that they sing to them until they lose their  senses and being  in a sort of singing rapture or stupor the cats catch them without much trouble. [Edward Burgess to George Burgess] Tehran, March 27, 1846
 How does trade go with you? Pray do not fail to let me know when you write. The trade here is a very different one from that at Tabreez. The latter is a great mart for the surrounding countries. This not so much so, altho it is situated in the line of the great route between Arabia and Tartary yet its transit trade is comparatively speaking trifling. The most lucrative trade I know of here, altho certainly not the most respectable, is usury: lending money chiefly on pledges chiefly jewels and shawls at 20, 30, 50, and sometimes to my knowledge 120 pr Cent pr annm! and this, that is, for the lower rates of interest 20 or 30 % pr anm for no small sums. The governor of the province of Fars (Persia proper) when last here borrowed about thirty thousand pounds at this rate most of which I believe he has now paid. This money was I believe borrowed without pledges but the usual way is when the amount is only a few thousands or less to take pledges for about double the sum lent. Much money has been made in this during the last twelve years in Tehran, but the commerce is not thought respectable and I fancy (forgive my superstition) that there is a curse attending on it. The trade of the Greeks at Tabreez is not nearly so good as it has been and for some time back the principals have not been getting much profit; of course the houses at Tabreez which are only agency do not loose [sic] but I suspect the owners of the goods have had some losses lately. [Edward Burgess to George Burgess] Tehran, September 22, 1852
 We returned into town from our summer residence on the 5th and the climate is agreeable enough, the days hot but the nights, mornings and evenings most delightful. I did not enjoy my residence in the country so much as most years both I and my dear wife having been much out of health. Just before the downfall of the late Prime Minister    128 I had got things into such train, that I carried on the business without much difficulty, and from having carried it on well during the absence of the court at Ispahan that is during nearly five months, the Prime Minister and also the King had such confidence in me that all I had to do was to go every Monday morning to the minister and ask him if he had any orders with regard to the week's Gazette and his invariable answer was no, “you carry on the paper exceedingly well act according to your own judgment,” except they had some particular articles to publish and then they were always sent to me in due time for the press, and I went away and brought out the paper to the best of my ability. Now the matter is very different. I publish nothing except the foreign news, without the prime minister    129 having previously revised it, this gives me a great deal of trouble. I have frequently to spend hours sometimes nearly the whole of the day, knocking about the minister's ante-room he being engaged and not to be seen, in the meanwhile the printers are waiting and the Gazette must come out on Thursday morning. This gives me great vexation and trouble. One mistake, which has made the minister so cautious was the official announcement in the Gazette of the appointment of a general to the command of eight battalions of infantry before the appointment had taken place!
The fact was that the minister who was an intimate friend of the general's had asked the King for the place, the King had given an undecided answer in the mean time the commission was drawn out backed by the minister and only waiting the King's signature. One of the minister's secretaries thinking the thing already finished sent a copy of the commission to me for publication. I thinking all was regular published the thing, the King was out of town and did not get his paper until the evening. Looking it over at night he saw an appointment he had never appointed, got in a fury with his minister and sent him several very sharp notes, in the morning there was enquiry and it was near going hard with me, but I believe the King soon found out who was to blame, and I heard no more about the matter — the general never got his regiments.

[Edward Burgess to George Burgess] Tehran, December 21, 1854
   Persia is still neutral in the war which rages between Russia and the European powers    133 and I hope she will continue so. Russia has bid fair for the assistance of Persia against the Turks, but did not bid quite enough; Persia had great grounds of complaint against Turkey, and an army of fifty or sixty thousand Persians on the Turkish frontier would have been a powerful diversion in favor of Russia: however Persia has remained quiet; and I am very glad she has done so; as any other course must have been most ruinous to her, except she had joined the allies against Russia; now, much depends upon the fall of Sevastopol for should that place hold out much longer, it is possible, but still very improbable that Persia may still be drawn into a Russian alliance. [Edward Burgess to George Burgess] Tehran, March 14, 1855
 You will perhaps have heard that I am endeavouring to obtain leave to go to England. I have already applied for it and it has been promised me but I shall not think myself safe until my leave is announced in the Gazette which is the custom. We hope to start in about two months from this time. Before that I am afraid I shall not be able to make my arrangements as to the carrying on the government Gazette etc. If I could get my arrears of pay I would engage to start in ten days but this is the great difficulty. Most Europeans on going to Europe to help them with their expenses for the journey and because they do not know well where to leave the things during their absence sell off everything and repurchase on returning. This I have found a very bad plan. They sell things for say £100 by auction and have to replace them when they return and spend £300 in doing so. We shall only sell a few things which are comparatively useless to us, and leave the rest under the care of some friend until our return; when I arrived here with the King in the autumn of '48 my whole property—tents, carpets, clothes, every thing included was carried on four mules, and one of them was a light load for I had a servant riding upon it. Now I suppose that thirty perhaps forty mules might be required to carry every thing we have when one sets up housekeeping and remains in a place, so much do things collect. We shall travel as lightly as possible but travelling by stage from here to Trebizonde, my wife and child can not unfortunately travel post so we must have several loads with us such as bedding, carpets, tents cooking utensils etc. We shall travel as quickly as possible but I am afraid we shall not get to Trebizonde in less than two months.

INTERVIEW WITH Anna [Mrs. Edward] BURGESS

My ancestors, on the sides of both parents, were Armenians, and I think for some time have lived in Tabriz. My father, Daoud Khan Seguinoff, was always in the military service of the Shah of Persia. My dear mother died some time before him. My uncles were men in high position. We were six in family—two brothers and four sisters. Both of my brothers were merchants in Tabreez; both are now dead. I am the eldest of the sisters. My next sister is the widow of Aga Pedros an Armenian merchant. She lives in Tehran, and has three sons. My youngest sister, Tamar, is the widow of Dr. Cormick. Dr. Cormick's father came to Persia to be on the medical staff of the Shah. He married an Armenian lady, and Dr. C. was born in Tabriz ...

We had known Charles and Edward Burgess a long time. They were merchants of china, guns, and many other things, and went to and fro between Tabriz and Tehràn. Edward Burgess asked for me of my father. I loved him even before we were married. Then we were married at my father's house...

When my husband was appointed to superintend the printing of the Government newspaper in Tehran, we got a few things needful for our house and then our little son John was born. Mr. Ronald Thompson was his Godfather (as he was afterwards for our daughter Fanny). Both of our children were baptized in our house in Tehran, according to the Armenian rites. And the priests said they registered the times for those gentlemen but I am not very sure about them—they neglect to do it at the very same time, and it is said they often make mistakes.
