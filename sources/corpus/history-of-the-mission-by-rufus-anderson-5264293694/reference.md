# Rufus Anderson — *History of the Missions* (vol. I, 1872)

American Board foreign secretary Rufus Anderson compiled this institutional history from mission archives, the *Missionary Herald*, and correspondence. **Volume I** includes the long **Nestorian** (Oroomiah / Lake Urmia) narrative used here.

## Transcription: Dawood Khan governor of the Nestorians (1845)

**Context in Anderson:** Nestorian mission, chapter treating **1841–1848**; immediately before Anderson’s account of the **1846** revivals on the plain of Oroomiah.

**Source:** verbatim from the vault PDF extract (`extracted.pdf.md`, block following `<!-- page 118 -->` in the ingest) and cross-checked against [Gutenberg `pg18930.html`](https://www.gutenberg.org/cache/epub/18930/pg18930.html) (search `In May, 1845`).

```
In May, 1845, the Shah, at the instance of the English and Russian
Ambassadors, appointed Dawood Khan, of Tabriz, an Armenian from
Georgia and an officer of the army, Governor of the Nestorians. The
object was to protect the Christians from the oppressions they had
long suffered from the Moslem nobles residing in the district.
```

**Genealogical / political reading:** independent confirmation that **Dawood/Daoud Khan of Tabriz** was understood as **Armenian from Georgia** and an **army officer**, given a **Qajar civil title over Nestorians** after joint **British and Russian** diplomatic pressure, with stated purpose **protection of Christians** against **local Muslim notables** (not against the American mission).

## Transcription: Christian Governor and civil protector (later 1840s)

**Context:** Patriarch Mar Shimun, Jesuit activity, and conflict with the American mission; Anderson explains why Mar Shimun and the Jesuits wanted Dawood Khan removed.

**Source:** same volume in `extracted.pdf.md` (block after `<!-- page 123 -->` in the ingest); align wording in Gutenberg HTML with search `civil protector`.

```
For a time the Patriarch and the Jesuits, both aiming at the
overthrow of the mission, were in practical combination. As a
necessary means to this end, both wished to expel from office Dawood
Khan, the Christian Governor and civil protector of the Nestorians
of that province; and the Mohammedan nobles were in sympathy with
them in this, as that dignitary stood in the way of their exactions.
```

This ties **Daoud Khan’s** office to **mission–patriarch–Jesuit–consular** politics on the **Urmia plain** and to **Mr. Stevens**, English Consul at Tabriz, in the paragraphs that follow in Anderson.
