# Article 1 — "Lace and bobbin-lace for 120 thousand euros"

**Masthead:** L'ADIGE — Trento
**Section:** PROVINCIA / DELIBERE (Decisions)
**Date:** Thursday 28 December 2006, p. 24
**By:** L.P. [Luisa Patruno]

**Banner:** The Soprintendenza for historical-artistic heritage has thus sought to "increase the provincial heritage" with 600 antique pieces of various sizes

**Banner (right):** A further 62 thousand euros were spent on eight period vestments. The public body also secured a prisoner's ankle-stock with padlock

**Headline:** Lace and bobbin-lace for 120 thousand euros

**Sub-headline:** Purchased by the Province from two private collectors

---

It is clear that there was a serious gap to fill in the historical-artistic heritage of the Province of Trento: namely a wholly inadequate endowment of antique lace and bobbin-lace for public enjoyment.

For this reason, the Provincial Soprintendenza for historical-artistic heritage must have felt it could not let slip the offer of a collection of laces which it describes as "appropriate and economically advantageous for the provincial administration," for a total of 120 thousand euros. To this expenditure is added, by a further December decision of the same Soprintendenza, the purchase for 62,300 euros of "eight textile artefacts" of the 16th–20th centuries, principally sacred vestments.

There are two decisions by the director providing for the purchase of antique laces, both dated 21 December and both for the price of 60 thousand euros. The first provides for the purchase by private negotiation from the English lawyer **Owen Michael Lewis**, resident in **Twickenham, Middlesex**, of a collection of textile artefacts consisting of antique laces of the 16th–20th centuries, over 300 pieces of various sizes. Mr Lewis had sent his offer to the Province on 5 December.

The second offer of laces, judged by the provincial offices to be unmissable, is very similar to the first. The seller is again a Mr Lewis, this time named **Ivor Antony Samuel**, born in **Florence** and resident in **Bolsena**, profession professor. Here too the purchase concerns 300 laces of various sizes from the same period, at a total price of 60 thousand euros.

**Photo caption:** ATTRACTION. The Province of Trento discovers an irresistible passion for lace and bobbin-lace

The two Mr Lewises came into possession of the "lace collection by family inheritance," the decision specifies — but they have evidently not hesitated to part with it in order to allow the Trentino community to enjoy it.

In light of two appraisals rendered by scholars of "acknowledged eminence in the field," **Paolo Peri** and **Doretta Davanzo Poli**, the director of the Soprintendenza, **Laura Dal Prà**, decided on the purchase, specifying that: "The collection of artefacts, consisting of laces dating from the 16th century onwards, is of significant historical-artistic interest for the purpose of documenting textile art, including a more recent period which is at the same time the subject of scant systematic collection by institutions." And it adds: "The purchase appears appropriate to ensure public enjoyment."

The third decision concerns the purchase from the firm Antichità Mirella Piselli di Balzano Paolo, in Florence, of eight Italian and French textile artefacts of the 16th–20th centuries for 62,300 euros.

A final curiosity: the Soprintendenza also purchased for 6,400 euros 24 copper moulds and a prisoner's ankle-stock with padlock and key of the 17th century, of Trentino manufacture.

# Article 2 — "The 600 laces for the Textile Museum"

**Masthead:** L'ADIGE — Trento
**Section:** PROVINCIA / L'ACQUISTO (The Purchase)
**Date:** Thursday 28 December 2006, p. 23
**By:** Luisa Patruno

**Banner:** The culture councillor and soprintendente Laura Dal Prà explain the reasons for the expenditure of 120 thousand euros on two collections of antique lace

**Banner (right):** The prisoner's ankle-stock will furnish the dungeons of Castel Stenico, and the twenty-four copper moulds will be placed in the kitchen

**Headline:** "The 600 laces for the Textile Museum"

**Sub-headline:** Cogo: we haven't gone mad — there is a project in Ala

---

The 600 laces purchased in recent days by the Province, at a cost of 120 thousand euros, are destined for the **Museo del Tessuto** (Textile Museum) which the provincial administration intends to open in **Ala**, at **Palazzo Taddei**, as soon as the restoration — begun in the spring of this year — is completed. Expected timeframe: about four years.

Explaining the reasons for the purchase of the expensive antique laces are the vice-president **Margherita Cogo** (DS) and the director of the Soprintendenza for historical-artistic heritage, **Laura Dal Prà**, for whom the Province has struck a genuine bargain by securing "rare pieces."

"It's not that we have gone mad," the culture councillor Cogo immediately raises her hands, aware that for many people it is hard to understand the reasons for this expenditure, "and decided to buy lace just like that. There is a plan behind the purchase and it concerns the project of the Textile Museum in Ala, which was launched in agreement with the municipal administration about ten years ago, well before my time, and is proceeding slowly because the restoration of Palazzo Taddei is quite complex. **The Lewis lace collections that we have purchased are among the most important in Europe, and part of them were auctioned at Sotheby's in London.** For years we have been buying pieces from textile collections specifically to enrich the Museum at Ala. Therefore the Soprintendenza, faced with the offers we received and having the necessary funds available, proceeded with the purchase."

Soprintendente Laura Dal Prà adds: "We have estimated, through the appraisal of Italy's foremost experts in the field, that the value of the laces is far greater than what we paid. They are rare pieces of considerable size and from Europe's finest manufacturers. We can therefore say that, in the context of acquisitions for the Textile Museum, in one stroke we have filled an important gap in antique laces that we did not have."

The provincial heritage of textile artefacts, the director explains, dates from the purchase, between 1992 and 1993, of the **Lorenzo Viesi collection from Cles**, comprising some four thousand pieces — tapestries and sacred vestments — currently held in the **Museo del Castello del Buonconsiglio**. This gave rise to the need for a Trentino centre of conservation and research, building on a history of textile manufacture that existed in Rovereto, Ala and elsewhere, and to plan the Textile Museum. The Viesi collection lacked certain categories of textiles — precisely laces — and for this reason purchases have been made over time.

Director Dal Prà also explains the reasons for the recent and equally curious purchase of 24 copper moulds and a prisoner's ankle-stock with padlock and key from the 17th century, for 6,400 euros.

"These purchases," says the soprintendente, "were requested by the Museo del Buonconsiglio, which as part of a policy of differentiating museum choices has planned to turn **Castel Stenico** into a furnished castle. At Stenico there was also the seat of justice with prisons, and therefore the ankle-stock is destined for the satellite site of the Castello del Buonconsiglio. These stocks are fairly rare as artefacts, because they were melted down over the centuries and are therefore not easy to find. The copper moulds will furnish the kitchen, again at Castel Stenico."
