## Summary

Four newspaper articles documenting the outlawry proclamation of **Charles Henry Burgess, late of Hampstead**, at the suit of **Richard Hart**, December 1847 – January 1848. Outlawry was a legal process invoked when a defendant failed to appear in court to answer a suit; the Sheriff's Court would formally proclaim them "outlawed," meaning they were placed outside the protection of the law until they surrendered.

Charles Henry Burgess was called to surrender on 9 December 1847, then formally proclaimed outlawed in early January 1848 alongside several other debtors including Crewe Read, the Hon. Henry Stanley, and Rev. William Cuthbert. The notices describe him as "late of Hampstead" — meaning formerly resident there, not deceased.

**Legal context:** "Late of [place]" in outlawry notices indicates the person's last known address, not that they are dead. The proclamation lists were posted at parish church doors as public notice.

### 5. Fraud charge — 23 March 1849

**Morning Post, 23 March 1849, p. 8** — "Police Intelligence" section  
**FMP URL:** <https://www.findmypast.co.uk/image-viewer?issue=BL%2F0000174%2F18490323&page=8&article=044&stringtohighlight=burgess>

#### Transcription

> **UTTERING SHAM CHEQUES.**—**Charles Henry Burgess**, a respectable looking young man, charged a few days ago with uttering a sham cheque, was brought up for re-examination.
>
> The police said that although they had made diligent inquiries, there was no other charge against the prisoner, and the person who had given him in charge now refused to prosecute.
>
> It will be remembered that the prisoner left the cheque as a sort of security for payment of a shilling for a bed for the night, and promised to redeem it, which Mr. Wontner submitted, on the prisoner's part, that he was about to do when he was apprehended by the police, within three or four doors of the house where he had left the cheque.
>
> A witness was called, who stated that he lent the prisoner the shilling about an hour and a half before he was captured, for the purpose alleged.
>
> **Mr. Burrell pointed out to the young man (the prisoner) that he had committed an offence in law, but as it appeared clear that he had no intention to defraud, discharged him.**

## Relationship to family tree

Charles Henry Burgess (1806-1854) was **brother of Edward Burgess** (1810-1855, consul and translator at Tabriz). Both were sons of **Henry Burgess** (1781-1863, banker and editor of "Circular to Bankers"). 

**Confirmed family details:**
- **Henry Burgess** (father, 1781-1863): Founded and edited "Circular to Bankers" (1828-~1850), Secretary of Association of British Provincial Bankers, died June 6, 1863, aged 82, at St. Swithin's Lane, London
- **Charles Henry Burgess** (1806-1854): First British subject to export goods directly from England to Persia, abandoned Edward in 1836 with debts to Persian royal family, outlawed 1847-48, died 1854 at European health resort
- **Edward Burgess** (1810-1855): Merchant, translator, village magistrate in Persia, married Anna (Armenian) 1851, died June 18, 1855, buried Armenian cemetery, Tabriz

**The betrayal:** Charles Henry left Edward stranded in Persia in 1836 as "more or less a hostage" to Charles's Persian creditors after Charles failed to repay money belonging to Persian royal family members and government officials. Charles's outlawry in London (1847-48) occurred while Edward remained trapped in this situation. Charles spent his final years (1836-1854) traveling European health resorts for his broken health, borrowing heavily from his father Henry.
