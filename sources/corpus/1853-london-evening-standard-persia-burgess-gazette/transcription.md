## Notes

*Many words are uncertain due to the quality of the 1853 newsprint scan — uncertain readings are marked with [?] and illegible passages with [...]. A higher-resolution scan would enable a complete transcription.*

The critical passage is unambiguous:

**"an article was published by a Persian ignoramus while Mr. Burgess was confined to his bed by fever."**

This is a **direct contemporary British newspaper reference to Edward Burgess by name in his role as the English editor of the Persian state gazette**, published during his lifetime (December 1853, eighteen months before his death in June 1855).

Key points from the full article:
- The article consists of two dispatches forwarded from a Constantinople correspondent: one from **Tabriz (19 Nov 1853)** and one from **Tehran (30 Oct 1853)**.
- The Tabriz dispatch discusses cholera, Turkish troop movements on the frontier, Kurdish unrest, and British consular arrangements.
- The Tehran dispatch discusses government finances, Anglo-Persian diplomatic tensions, the gazette, and Mr. Thompson (Ronald Thompson of the British mission — the same man who was godfather to Edward's children).
- The gazette is described as producing "a good deal of ill humour" when articles appeared that upset diplomatic sensitivities — consistent with Edward's own letters about walking a tightrope between Persian, Russian, and British interests.
- The phrase "English editor of the government gazette" is a precise contemporary description of Edward's role.
- His replacement by "a Persian ignoramus" during his illness matches Edward's own complaint (in his letters) about the gazette's quality declining without his oversight.
- The reference to the *Morning Herald* and the Persian minister in London suggests the gazette's content was being read and contested at ambassadorial level — confirming the diplomatic significance of Edward's editorial decisions.
