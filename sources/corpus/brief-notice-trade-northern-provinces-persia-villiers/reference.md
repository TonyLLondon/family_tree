## What this document is

Charles Burgess's firsthand account of his inaugural Trebizond trade venture, written in **1832** for **T.H. Villiers** (a Commons contact), intended as a **Parliamentary Paper** for the **Board of Control**. Villiers died in 1832; the piece never became a Blue Book. A handful of copies were printed privately in 1833. The NYPL holds the manuscript, which Abrahamian reprinted in 1983.

It is the **primary documentary source** establishing Charles — not Edward — as the pioneer of direct England → Persia trade via the Black Sea and Trebizond.

## Key factual record

| Claim | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **First voyage** | Sailed from England **September 1830**; reached Tabriz a **second time** in **January 1831** (four months, London → Tabriz) |
| **Investment** | **£6,000** of English manufactures: guns, pistols, telescopes, gold and silver watches, fine cloths, knives, china |
| **Route** | London → Constantinople (3% duty *en transit*) → Trebizond (Black Sea, 4–10 days) → Erzeroum (10–11 caravan marches, winter difficulties) → Tabriz (16 caravan marches, ~300 miles) |
| **Gaushupongar Mountain** | Five horses lost in snow; caravan detained three days |
| **Syeed Khan / Sadik Bey** | Named as the original intermediary who brought Charles to Persia in 1827 and financed early operations |
| **Abbas Mirza** | Took all remaining goods from Charles in camp; issued *Rukhams* granting Charles customs parity with Persian merchants on up to 8,000 tomans annually |
| **Charles's appointments** | Command of two infantry battalions (Hamadani's and Rumsehs); second class **Order of the Lion and Sun**; pay raised to **800 tomans/year plus forage** |
| **Edward Burgess** | Named explicitly: "It is on this ground that Mr. B. [Brant] is particularly anxious… to see **my brother, Mr. E. Burgess, regularly established there as a commission merchant**" in Tabriz — with £6,000 investment at Trebizond |
| **John Brant** | £20,000 Trebizond investment; named first British consul at Trebizond; hoping to link his Trebizond line to an Edward Burgess-run Tabriz depot |
| **Trade volume** | Erzeroum Custom House: 5,000 horse loads/year (~300 lb each); Constantinople–Persia trade via Erzeroum estimated at **£750,000/year**, two-thirds British produce |
| **Silk** | Province of Gilan raw silk output ~1,300,000 lbs in 1829; ~600,000 lbs in 1831 (plague devastation); prices too high for profitable English import |
| **Competitors** | Russian-Georgian route (200 miles longer); Russian merchants had 5% *ad valorem* duties under Turkmanchai treaty; British had no equivalent protection |

## Why this resolves the 1857 press attribution

The 1857 *Morpeth Herald* / *Morning Post* piece credited an unnamed "English gentleman of the name of Burgess" with opening the Trebizond trade route. The *Brief Notice* names **Charles** as the first-person narrator of that same Gaushupongar snow story and the £6,000 Tabriz landing. The 1857 piece is almost certainly about **Charles**, with Edward as the Tabriz partner — not a separate "Burgess." See [edward-burgess.md](../../../../people/edward-burgess.md) evidence section.
