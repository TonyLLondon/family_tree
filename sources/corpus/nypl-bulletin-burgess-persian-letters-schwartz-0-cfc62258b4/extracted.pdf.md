# THE BURGESS PERSIAN LETTERS

### Edited by BENJAMIN SCHWARTZ

\ MONG the papers, letters, etc.,\* presented to the Library by Mrs. Thomas £\. Foljambe Burgess of Scarsdale, New York, are two series of letters from the Near East, written during the years 1827-1855, by the brothers Charles and Edward Burgess, sons of the English banker, economist, and editor, Henry Burgess. These letters afford as connected a history of the adventures and for tunes of Occidentals in the Orient as any "narrative of travel and description" that has been published, while the pen of the trained novelist could make of the story as rich and warm and fascinating <sup>a</sup> novel as has been written on such a theme. But aside from their interest as human documents and their possible inspiration for the novelist, the letters, written during that period when Turkey became the "sick man of Europe," when Russia was whittling Persia down to her present boundaries, when England was establishing and consolidating her whole position in India and Asia Minor, acquire added value by affording un expected glimpses into the play and counter-play of the Great Powers, the initi ation and prosecution of policies which, at this very moment, are coloring events in Egypt, the western Mediterranean, and the Middle East.

From 1826 to 1828 the closing acts of the drama of Greek independence from Turkey were being played to their stirring conclusion. The famous English diplomat, Stratford Canning, was minister to the Porte; those perennial foes, England, Russia, and France, were banded together in a crusade to free the Christian Greeks; an allied squadron had "blundered" into Navarino bay in 1827 and destroyed the Turkish fleet. In Persia the grandiose schemes of the crown prince, Abbas Mirza, to recover territories lost to Russia was ending in the utter rout of the Persians, the capture of Tabriz by the Russians, and the humili ating Treaty of Turkomanchai, concluded just in time to enable the Czar to declare formal war on Turkey. The British Minister to the Shah, Sir John Macdonald, was striving mightily to throw up any sort of bulwark against <sup>a</sup> Russian threat to India, and British money, munitions, and drill sergeants for the Persian army (and the concomitant of British manufactured products) were beginning to trickle into the country in ever-increasing volume.

Against this background, Charles Henry Burgess, eldest son of Henry and Frances (Ridsdale) Burgess, went out to Persia at the instance of <sup>a</sup> Persian merchant, Sadik Bey (later ennobled by the Shah as Syeed Khan) in 1827, at

Charles does not appear in the British Army List, but his letters, notes, and sketches indicate education and training in military tactics. During his brief career as a soldier he distinguished himself by winning the Persian order of the Rising Sun and Lion, second class, for gallantry in action, and comported himself in such fashion as to win the approbation and confidence not only of his military colleagues, but also of those highly placed in the Persian government.

As <sup>a</sup> result of connections thus established, in 1829, when Persian finances became so muddled that most of the British military mission had to be released, Charles found himself set up as a merchant, financed by his father, and working through Sadik Bey, and the firm of Hanson, of London and Constantinople. Brilliant and bold in his conceptions, he was successful beyond expectations at the outset, and was probably the first Englishman to ship goods from the mother country through the Black Sea directly to Trebizond in Turkey, thence by rela tively easy caravan stages to the markets of Persia, instead of the previous prohibitively expensive route via the port of Bushire in the Persian Gulf. He traveled back and forth between Persia and England constantly during the years 1830-1836, executing private and public commissions, and on at least one oc casion, acted as King's Messenger for the British Foreign Office. His restless imagination running riot at the enormous possibilities opened up before him, he kept pyramiding venture on venture until finally, in 1836, the bubble burst. Thereafter, a combination of circumstances such as ill-health, "trouble in the Balkans," inability to satisfy the clamorings of creditors, and perhaps, interest in newer and greener pastures served to prevent his return to Persia. He died at Aix-la-Chapelle during the winter of 1854—1855.

In the meantime, his younger brother Edward, whom he had brought out with him in 1830 and associated in the business, was left in Persia, practically a hostage in the hands of Charles's Persian creditors, who thus vainly hoped to compel the older brother's return. At first Edward's position was extremely precarious, for apart from the risk to his person he led a hand-to-mouth existence which was in sharp contrast to his previous estate as "a prosperous and thriving merchant." But his singularly winning disposition (his widow could still say, twenty-five years after his death, that "he was the most all-loved person" she had ever known) enabled him to retain former friends and make new ones among both Europeans and natives, even those who had suffered from the un happy outcome of Charles's speculations. Prince Bahman Mirza secured his appointment as civil governor of two small villages, an appointment which, because the villages were in the neighborhood of a community of Nestorian

Christians, led to Edward's acting as financial agent for the American Mission at Tabriz and Urumia. He tutored the Prince's children and had charge of the royal stables. He did odd jobs of translating into Turkish and Persian, both languages of which he had acquired a fluent knowledge. Thus, while continuing to be held officially responsible with his brother, and confined to the general neighborhood of the town of Tabriz, he managed to spend his days not too un pleasantly. His letters abound in the names and events which have brought Asia Minor into the western limelight; many of his references are of prime historical importance not only in regard to the matter discussed, but also the actors involved, with most of whom he had personal acquaintance. Thus, reveal ing bits of information are afforded us by the retailing of incidents concerning such great names as Rawlinson and Layard, the fathers of Assyriology, or of the inner workings of the British and Russian embassies.

In 1848 Edward's link with the royal house was greatly strengthened by the accession to the throne of Nasir i Din, nephew of the now exiled Bahman Mirza. Edward sold everything he could lay his hands on and joined the triumphal march from Tabriz to Teheran, where he was immediately appointed chief interpreter and later made editor of the state gazette. His most pressing personal concerns were now relieved, for although still technically a debtor, the change in government and the lapse of time had so altered his status that he was prac tically free to do as he pleased. By this time, however, the East had entered into his soul and the thought of returning to a mercantile existence in England was so repugnant that he cemented his ties in Persia by refusing the offer of a position in America (secured for him by his younger brother George), and finally, in 1852, by his marriage to <sup>a</sup> beautiful Armenian girl. And so, although most of his career had been shadowed by the meteoric rise and fall of his brother Charles, and although that shadow was only dispelled at the very end of his life, Persia came to mean the summum bonum of his existence. One small cloud remained ; for a long time he was unable to secure leave to return to England for a visit. Ironically, when he finally did wring official permission from the government to leave the country for that visit, he died en route, still in Persia.

Mr. Fowler who is with him will tell you the truth, every body here is disgusted with Sadik and I expect his character is already public in England but you need not take any conspicuous part in making him known unless asked, I have reasons which I will explain hereafter. I am afraid you will not be able to read this I am writing sitting crossed legged and in a tremendous storm of wind and rain which blows my paper in all directions. Col. Monteith who takes charge of this goes through Russia and I must finish as he leaves immediately.

(To be c,i,i tinned)
