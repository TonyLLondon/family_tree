## Subhead

> We have leafed through the identity and formation of an old neighbourhood.

## Body (translated)

You have surely, at least once, passed through Shemiran and Tajrish. The easiest access route to this historic quarter of the capital is from the south, at **Chaharrah Park Way** and the **Mahmoudieh** neighbourhood. Two accounts exist for the name of this neighbourhood.

### Theory 1 — a local named "Mahmoudieh"

Some old residents of the neighbourhood say it came into being during **Reza Shah**'s era, and that a person named **Mahmoudieh** was an old resident who put in great effort during its formation; for that reason the neighbourhood was named after him.

### Theory 2 — the Qajar palace *Mohammadieh*

The other account is that during the era of **Fath-Ali Shah**, the lands in the area of today's **Zaferaniyeh** and **Mahmoudieh** belonged to the **Mo'ayyer al-Mamalek** family.

In the southern part of these lands stood properties where, during the reign of **Mohammad Shah Qajar**, a palace was built at the centre, amid the surrounding green trees, and was named **Mohammadieh**. But the palace did not serve the shah well: as soon as the shah entered his new palace, he died of **gout** (نقرس), and the palace was left abandoned and gradually fell to ruin.

After Mohammad Shah's death, the lands in this area and the ill-omened palace among them were put up for sale. Years later they came into the possession of the family of **Mohammad Rahim Khan Ala-ud-Dowleh**, court minister (*vazir-e darbar*) of **Naser al-Din Shah**, and then his son **Mahmoud Alamir**, known as **Ehtesham al-Saltana**.

To change the ill-omened history of these lands, he built a new palace inside the garden and changed its name to **Mahmoudieh**. After the renaming of the lands to Mahmoudieh and the fading of the Mohammadieh palace story, Mahmoud Alamir sold the lands to **Mirza Fath-Ali Khan Sahebdivan**.

These lands gradually, around the **[13]20s** [i.e. 1940s CE in the SH calendar, but contextually the 1910s–1920s CE given the Stump timeline], after passing through the hands of various notables, became the share of **Dr. Ashtump** (اشتمپ), the prominent dentist of that era. **Dr. Ashtump, a Swiss man** who was **Ahmad Shah Qajar**'s special dentist and continued to treat the nobility and elite during the first and second decades of the Pahlavi era, **bought a large portion of today's Mahmoudieh lands from Mohammad Hossein Amin al-Zarb** (محمدحسین امین‌الضرب).

The Swiss dentist built a house for himself in Mahmoudieh and even farmed there. After Dr. Ashtump's death, his heirs divided the lands into smaller plots and sold them to various people, and gradually today's Mahmoudieh neighbourhood took shape.
