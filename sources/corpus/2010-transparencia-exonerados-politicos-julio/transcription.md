## Significance

**Helia del Carmen Cerpa Pérez** is confirmed as a recognised *exonerada política* — a person formally dismissed from public-sector employment for political reasons during the Pinochet dictatorship. She worked at the **Servicio de Registro Civil e Identificación** (SRCEI) — Chile's Civil Registry and Identification Service, the state agency responsible for recording births, marriages, deaths, and issuing identity cards and passports. The SRCEI was founded 17 July 1884 as part of Chile's secular laws, transferring vital records from the Catholic Church to the state; in 1943 it absorbed the Identification and Passport Service. Under Allende, it was headed by **Heriberto Benquis Camhi** (Partido Socialista, 1971–11 Sep 1973), who was replaced the day after the coup. The SRCEI operated regional offices in every commune, including Parral (Helia's birthplace).

Helia was **27 years old** when Allende took office (November 1970) and **30** at the time of the coup. She was formally dismissed on **16 April 1974**, seven months after the coup. As of July 2010, she received a monthly reparation pension of **140,868 Chilean pesos** (~US$270) under Ley 19.234 (1993).

**Cerpa González Marcial** — dismissed from SOCOAGRO on 30 November 1973 — shares the exact double surname of Helia's father [Miguel Cerpa González](../../people/miguel-cerpa-gonzalez-i346.md), making him almost certainly Miguel's brother and Helia's uncle. His higher pension (279,792 CLP) suggests either a more senior position or longer service. SOCOAGRO was an agricultural cooperative entity — its employees were among the first purged as the junta dismantled Allende's agrarian reform.

**Cerpa Orellana Luis** — dismissed from the Superintendencia de Aduanas (Customs) on 31 December 1973 — also appears in the [Valech I victim list](../valech-i-nomina-victimas/source.yaml) (p. 57) as a survivor of political imprisonment and torture.

## Context: Ley 19.234 and the exonerados programme

Ley 19.234 (12 August 1993) established reparation pensions (*pensiones de gracia*) for persons dismissed from public administration, semi-fiscal institutions, autonomous administration entities, and state-owned enterprises for political reasons between 11 September 1973 and 10 March 1990. The law granted additional service credits (6 months per year for those dismissed Sep–Dec 1973; 4 months for 1974–1976; 3 months for 1977–1990) and non-contributory pensions for those with at least 10 years of qualifying service. Amendments: Ley 19.582 (1998) expanded coverage to parliamentary and judicial employees and increased maximum service credits; Ley 20.134 (2006) granted a one-time extraordinary bonus.

By 2010, the programme had approximately 157,624 beneficiaries. It was subsequently embroiled in a fraud scandal: investigations revealed that an estimated 1,500 individuals had obtained exoneree status through falsified documents, leading to criminal complaints by the Interior Ministry in August 2010.
