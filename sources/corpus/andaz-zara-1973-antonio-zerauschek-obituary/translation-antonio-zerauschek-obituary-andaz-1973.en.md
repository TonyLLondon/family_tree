# ANTONIO ZERAUSCHEK

The news of the death of one of our fellow citizens almost always strikes us in a particular way: it is precisely the "blow" that arrives without warning and leaves you bewildered.

This "surprise" comes from the fact that for many — almost all — of our fellow citizens, we have no longer been able to keep a present memory of them, because we no longer live among them. Memory becomes only occasional, almost exceptional, for specific and rather rare circumstances. And when the circumstance is painful, as with a death, the sensation is truly one of dismay. One is truly stricken.

This applies to everyone.

For Antonio Zerauschek, this "blow" struck me with particularly painful effect. His name bounced back to me suddenly in memory and reflection. It re-emerged after so many years from a particular regard I had for the man and from a circumstance and situation of my own that was altogether… special.

In 1928, I arrived in Zara carrying my definitive death sentence as a carefree student, sealed in my degree certificate; forced to become something serious and to face professional problems head-on. From one day to the next, I recall that I began by… looking around and reflecting on my own affairs, regarding myself in Zara from a point of view I had never before adopted: namely, the serious one. I set about discovering a Zara I did not know at all and fellow citizens I knew even less. One of the first walls of Zara that drew me was the economic one, and searching for the men who most defined it, I came across the name of Antonio Zerauschek for the first time. I could only capture sensations: of incomplete perfection [?].

But drawn in, pushed by my nature, by my instinctive "hobby" and by that particular "antenna" I have always had for Zara, as I peered for the first time into the true life of the city, the man who most impressed me — among those I could consider "the greats" of Zara — was precisely Antonio Zerauschek.

This investigation of mine, this study of the city, lasted only a short time. It was cut short at the start of 1940, when I was called to arms. But I preserve a vivid memory of that time, of that man, even though I knew him only by sight and never had the chance to speak to him. Even though the questions I asked about him were only a few, too few. And all with positive results, to the highest degree.

Among these, one remains imprinted on me. When I asked whether the man truly deserved his reputation for economic prowess, I was told that more than him, it was the excellent collaborators he had known how to choose for the most delicate and important management of the various sectors of his enterprise that deserved credit. I remember perfectly that my reply was: "But isn't knowing how to choose excellent collaborators precisely the first and best quality of great Men?"

I had planned to study the personage more deeply, as he truly interested me (as both "authority and citizen"). But… "I repeat" — military service, the war, and then the bombs scattered all those "dreams and more"… and everything ended as it ended.

All that remained was the nostalgia for the lost city and for the empty life one had lived in Zara. For the life of the mule and the overcoat [i.e. ordinary street life].

The life of the serious citizen I "did not" have time to pursue. It was cut short at the start.

So what can I say about a man I don't know? Only a few impressions, only a few sensations.

As an impression, I recall that witty saying: **"quello ze l'omo più rico de Zara"** — "that's the richest man in Zara."

As a sensation, the sincerely admiring one of a man who struck you with the instinctive refinement of his entire bearing: from his choice of clothes, to his walk, to his gestures. If he was a gentleman, it was as much for his appearance as for his wealth.

Later I learned that he was a gentleman also and above all in his spirit.

Many, many years later I then had a direct impression, a direct sensation. When for the first and only time I came into contact with Him and we exchanged no more than two words. It was in Florence, the year of our Reunion [*Raduno*], when I attended a session of the organising committee chaired by Don Luigi Stefani. Among those invited by Don Luigi was also Antonio Zerauschek.

I can still see him. He arrived alone. He remained almost isolated. I don't recall if he exchanged a few words with anyone. He never intervened in the discussion. But I noticed he was paying attention. Engaged in the work of the meeting, I had no way to follow through on my longstanding wish to analyse the real worth of the "personage" he had been in Zara.

At the end of the meeting I saw him approach me. A serious but relaxed expression: I would say pleased, towards me.

He gave a slight smile and handed me a cheque. He said only that if it proved insufficient to cover the organisational expenses, I should let him know. The amount was in keeping with his customary style: in the right measure — neither too little nor too much.

A brief nod of farewell. That was all.

After so many years, the man had presented himself to me in the same measure in which I had appreciated him in Zara… without ever knowing him.

He was still that remarkable. And only through this lens can I unconsciously judge him even now. And yet I am sure I am not mistaken.

Many citizens of Zara, of his generation, were able to evaluate him better than I. I believe that none of them can say anything but good. Alongside the names of the "musketeers" — Luxardo, Vhero [?] or Drioli — it was he, Antonio Zerauschek, **the true d'Artagnan of Zaratine industry**, completing the liquors with cigarettes and chocolate.

How much the city must acknowledge him for the economic potential of his life, I am not in a position to assess.

But in the overall judgement of the man, recalling the happy years of Zara throughout the period of its redemption [*redenzione* — Italy's sovereignty over Zara, 1920–1943], if we wished to judge him as a citizen who had well served the city in his various fields of activity over those 25 years — if we wished to make a comparison in the style of those newspapers that elect "Man of the Year" — I am convinced that in the ranking of the most deserving man of Zara, the first place would belong to Antonio Zerauschek.

Giuseppe Zilotto has drawn a much more complete portrait of him in *Difesa Adriatica*, illustrating the man in his industrial worth. I can only emphasise certain aspects of his moral worth.

Born of a humble family (his father was a carpenter and of origins… no less than… [?]), one can well imagine what the life of this child must have been.

**He began! As a "Garzon de botega" [shop boy] in the commercial establishment of the Mazzocchi, under the direct supervision of the partner [?] Tonti, an excellent director of the firm.**

I [?] know the stages of his career. I know no more.

I can report only one episode and I cite it in the same words in which it was told to me by letter: "… I was at the Lucia [school?], the headmistress Dedaletti was [?] towards the Zerauschek firm: the number of pupils of each sex. On the fateful day, the little van of the TONCO' went to all the schools, including of course those of the rural hamlets, to unload its most welcome gifts. They were — I don't remember if it was two soldi, but Ausonia chocolates (… don't quibble [?], for this reason I think that the Good Lord must have reserved a place in Paradise for him)."

So my friend Biagio writes [?] to me.

And to me who "repeat once more" — of the man I perceived only a sensation and what the sight of his person and the relative knowledge of his work left me — I can only immensely [?] turn my thoughts to the place that the Good Lord will have reserved for him in Paradise: a truly well-earned [?] place. As well-earned is the sincere, heartfelt condolence of all Zara, which mourns him as one of its finest sons.
