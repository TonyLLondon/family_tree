# Dublin Evening Packet — Death of James Roche, Esq., J.P.

**Date:** 7 April 1853 (and numerous papers, April–July 1853)
**Newspaper:** Dublin Evening Packet and Correspondent
**FMP:** [article link](https://www.findmypast.co.uk/search-newspapers/article/BL/0000812/18530407/059)

## Transcription (combined from multiple papers)

**Dublin Evening Packet, 7 Apr 1853:**
> The Directorship of the National Bank, Cork, and Presidency of the Cork Library Society, are vacant by the lamented death of **James Roche, Esq., J.P., formerly an eminent banker, and brother of Thomas Roche**, Esq.

**Bell's Weekly Messenger, 11 Apr 1853:**
> **James Roche, Esq., J.P.**, the well-known "J.R." of the literary world, and the author of *Essays of an Octogenarian*, died on the 1st instant, his residence... in his 83rd year.

**Weekly Chronicle (London), 9 Apr 1853:**
> **JAMES ROCHE, ESQ.** — This distinguished antiquary and writer died on Friday morning, in Cork, at the age of 83.

**Monmouthshire Merlin, 8 Jul 1853 (2,230-word obituary):**
> **JAMES ROCHE.** An extraordinary man recently died in Cork, at the ripe age of 86 years, who was called the **Roscoe of his native city**, and who had seen the "world at large" more intimately than many thousands of travellers...

**Cork Examiner, 13 Apr 1853:**
> Mr. James Roche, well known as a most learned and copious contributor to the "Gentleman's Magazine," under the signature of "J.R.," died last week, at Cork, in his 83rd year.

## Notes

**James Roche** (~1770–1853) was Thomas Roche's brother. Born in Limerick, educated at the Catholic College of Saintes in France, he became a wine exporter in Bordeaux, was imprisoned during the French Revolution (released after the death of Robespierre), returned to Ireland, and set up a bank in Cork with brother Stephen (Roches' Cork Bank, which failed in the 1820 crisis). A prolific author and antiquary, he contributed to the *Gentleman's Magazine* under "J.R." and published *Essays of an Octogenarian*. He was a magistrate, director of the National Bank (Cork), and president of the Cork Library Society. Multiple papers called him "the Roscoe of Cork" — comparing him to William Roscoe, the Liverpool banker-scholar.

The Dublin Evening Packet notice explicitly names him as "**brother of Thomas Roche**," independently confirming the sibling relationship documented in Burke's 1833 and the Limerick Chronicle.
