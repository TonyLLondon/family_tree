Sadly up to the age of 21years one is not interested in the past. After that age I had already missed the chance of finding out any more.

I had asked Zia Irma (dead and buried with honours, Dalmatian Flag, etc. in Lonato (Brescia) a couple of years ago, to write down everything she remembered in a note book, and for years I believed she did. But it turned out that she had not. So, we do no know much, and what we had heard is not always supported by books. For instance, we were always told that the Addobbati had moved, from Bergamo to Zara in 1493 - four brothers - gentlemen with diamond studs (at that time and till Sorrell ex-mistress of X King Dared, men only wore diamonds).

So which is right? In Zara I was shown some paper belonging to the town archives, bills, etc. - cannot tell dates and the man who showed them to me is long dead. Whether the papers are still there God only knows! In 1493 Bergamo was under Venice. So were they travelling about?

The story of the Order of the Golden Spur is true (have I not given you [PML] the miniature long time ago?) Why he was given it while still at University in Padova? I wrote to the Vatican and asked, but got no reply. Given that there are 100 only at any time it shouldn't have been too difficult to find out (still possible I suppose).

I read somewhere that he went with the delegation to Milan to beg Napoleon not to give Zara to Austria (failed). His wife accompanied him and being beautiful, witty and intelligent she very much caught Napoleon's eye. Again the Zie Addobbati giggled no end about us being Napoleon's cousins. Never found out whether it was real or a joke about her success.

As for Nonno, once only in Florence, he talked to me about his business life. I took notes, unfortunately so badly (as usual) that I cannot make head or tail although I kept them.

I got the general picture (true to remembrance of times in Zara) that it all was great fun, the success and the failure e.g. the boat of soap he bought in Napoli at the end of the first war. Everybody was desperate for soap, but when it arrived in Zara it was full of sand And the vessel commanded by Capitano Veldrich in charge of transporting the oil for the electricity of Zara from Trieste or Pola or Fuime. God knows - the breathing the weather, the this, that or the other but not once. The town was left in the dark!!

Nonno certainly had lots of money but he was extremely generous with his family, with Nonna's family and with anybody else. For this reason, but for the few exceptions, his position with the rest of the town was such that, as you know, when the communists occupied the town and took him (in absence) to trial for exploitation of his workers, they didn't find one willing to testify against him. Some were beaten up and still refused. The more I think about it the more the thing becomes extraordinary. What a courage on their part and what a glory for Nonno! The accusation was changed to selling the produces of his factories to the enemy. Italian town - Italian Nonno, Italian cigarettes smoked by Italian soldiers. It is all a joke if it wasn't that……, it is the only one in the history of Yugoslav behaviour as that we know of, nobody got chopped to pieces - only beaten. We were certainly more close as children to Nonna's side of the family, but I must say that not once I heard Nonno or Nonna speak ill of anybody. It is only to say that, if in my memories I have a few snips of family history from the Addobbati, I have practically nothing from the Zerauschek - Nonno mentioned once that his mother (?)……., grandmother ……..came from a noble family, I understand - Croat. Now two things: His grandfather Z married Guerrino Italian, his father Z married Herenda Spanish - I cannot think of any Croat but possibly so.

Visiting him I looked at the book saw nothing as I never knew the Croat surname, etc. But I mentioning the Addobbati, he proposed to research (? as very experience) and to tempt me sent a letter saying that the family could go up to a Roman Marthyr. At this point, I next mention the fact that history must be learnt before one understand the succession of Roman - Venetian - Austrian - etc. in the Italian peninsula - in the Austrian and in the Mediterranean - otherwise nonsense.

Also I must mention the fact that still with the little I know of our past ( remote ) and my past I can write a book the size of Gone with the wind, and always thought that I should, and I save the writing for when I will be in a wheel chair – it might be too late.

Ah! Ah! ( I might be wrong. It could be sand!) and damn I have four of them –

Going back to history, the Addobbati of few generations ago: Simeone …..to follow the military career went to the Teresianum Military Academy in Vienna and ended up Field Marshall - died after the first war, while shaking hands with the Italian General commanding the Piazzaforte di Zara and welcoming his guests at a Ball. Ball was suspended. Funeral with military honours, cannon and gun carriage, etc.

Wife was Lisette of the Luxardo liquor family, who, old that she was, died during the islands hopping exodus in 1943 and the husband of one of the group of girlfriends who came here told me that he had to give up some planks he had secured to make a shelter; a coffin for her was made instead.

Pietro Pio Addobbati - my Nonno, was destined for the church and was in Seminary in Zara. Having put a RAT in the ….. dish of a visiting Bishop, he was considered unsuitable and luckily released (or we wouldn't be here). Little do I known about him as he was dead before I was born. But that little rather reminds me of Michael.

His family, by then, was without money and he was a civil servant of some sort with wife and 7 children. Money didn't last to the end of the month. One of the girls was sent to borrow from my nonna's side of family of the Boara.

The Boara were landowners and intellectuals and, it appears very much Italians. Simeone Boara marrieded Vittorie Carobbi (1820 - 1911). They had eight children (I am not sure of the order). Eveline married a Croat. Hell. It followed a will that left the family money to the men in the family and to none of the girls. None of the men married and 3 at their death left the money to the Lega (an Italian Associozione against Austria). Casimiro, famous for his lectures of the classics lived with sister Carmela. To them my Nonna Ottilia Boara turned for help borrowing money which she returned sending one of her daughters at the end of the month. Zia Carmela always pocketed it, but Zio Casimiro waved them off. .

The two, whom I believed for years to be husband and wife, lived in absolute comfort, only people I ever heard of that sat down to a menu lunch and dinner every day.

The last piece of land of the Boara was sold by Zio Casimiro to my father and when Zia Carmela died, last of the Boara, having been assisted by Zie Addobbati, she made an alteration to her will and left them something. Where the rest went I do not know.

The Addobbati anyway, marvellous that they have always been, contrary to the habits of the gentry of the period worked - Nonno was a secretary in a lawyers firm (a friend of Nonno's, maybe she met him there). Zia Noemi was an employee of the Poste & Telegraphs - Zia Else a piano teacher - Irma and Bill were teachers and Villosie, for 25 years Nonno's right arm secretary, administrator, etc. She became a nun at the age of fifty and didn't take her long to become Sister Superior at the Convent of the Dame Inglesi at Rovereto (Trento). The Dame Inglesi were the order of the Convent School in Zara where the Addobbati went (with a ? through Zio Simeone). Later the Convent changed hands and passed to the Dominican nuns. The elementary school was then a state school and I attended it for five years. But the Addobbati spent every year holiday in Rovereto and because of the connection Zia Villoria was accepted at the age of 50 and destitute refugee that she was, even without a dowry (how kind).

Of the other five only Zie Bice married and lives now in Rome and is the grand-mother of the five Marie, three of which came here.

The only brother the eldest of the family became a Doctor, qualified in Vienna, during the war in the Austrian army of course, in the Officers mess he slapped a superior officer in the face. Only Zio Simenoe position and influence saved from Court martial. He was practising later in Trieste when he died in 24 hours of blood poisoning aged about 30.

A cousin Addobbati also a doctor in Trieste and described to us by some visiting Jewish as an Apostle (they do not have saints) was the unfortunate father who ran to a hospital to help after some schooling in the streets during difficult political moments only to find that the decent one was his son, Pietro aged 14, a bystander. There is a monument to him in Trieste.

Not much else from this side of historical importance, only that it was not unusual in Zara to hear as a compliment, un vero Addobbati.
