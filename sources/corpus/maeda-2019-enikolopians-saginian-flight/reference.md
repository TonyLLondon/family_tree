# Maeda 2019 - David and Zaal Saginean Flight to Iran

## Source

- **Author:** Hirotake Maeda
- **Chapter:** "Lives of the Enikolopians: Multilingualism and the Religious-National Identity of a Caucasus Family in the Persianate World"
- **Book:** *The Persianate World: Rethinking a Shared Sphere*, eds. Abbas Amanat and Assef Ashraf
- **Publisher:** Brill, 2019
- **Pages:** 169-195; selected snippets from pp. 174, 189-190
- **Transcript:** [`transcription.snippets.md`](transcription.snippets.md)
- **Facsimiles:** [`pages-png/`](pages-png/)

## Relevance to the Saginian line

This chapter gives the strongest scholarly support currently in the vault for the origin story behind [Daoud Khan Saginian](../../../people/daoud-khan-saginian.md). Maeda is primarily writing about the Enikolopian family, but in the course of explaining Solomon/Solayman Khan's flight to Iran he names Solomon's companions as the Georgian brothers **David and Zaal Saginean**.

The key passage states that Solomon/Solayman Khan, a former Russian official, fled to Iran partly from fear of forced relocation to Russia and forced conversion. Maeda then says that after Gurgen returned from Tabriz and told Solomon of **'Abbas Mirza's military reforms**, Solomon fled with "the Georgian brothers **David and Zaal Saginean**," who were also afraid of forced conversion. This is the scholarly bridge between the Georgian **Saginean/Saginskilli/Saginashvili** identity and the later Armenian-Iranian **Saginian** family identity.

## What this adds

- It anchors the **1811 flight** narrative to a peer-reviewed Brill chapter rather than only a Wikipedia summary or later family memoir.
- It confirms that **David and Zaal** are presented as **brothers**, not father and sons.
- It gives the religious context: fear of Russian relocation and forced conversion, the exile of Georgian Catholicos Anton II, and the practical abolition of the Georgian Church.
- It preserves the striking **wrestling pretext**: Solomon and David obtained permission to visit Tbilisi because the officer thought they wanted to participate in wrestling.
- It records the route: **Yerevan via Akhalkalaki and Giumri**, then **Tabriz**.
- It explains why the brothers could later appear Armenian in Iranian sources: Shermazanian says they embraced the **Grigorian faith** after arriving in Tabriz and that their children became Armenians.

## Limits

Maeda explicitly cautions that the rumor of forced conversion and its influence are difficult to confirm. The religious context was fragile, and the narrative comes through Shermazanian's Armenian historical work. It is strong evidence for how the family remembered and explained the migration, but it should still be cited as Maeda's analysis of Shermazanian rather than as an independent Russian military record.
