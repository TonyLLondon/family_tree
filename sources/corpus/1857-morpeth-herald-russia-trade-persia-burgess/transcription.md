## Transcription

> **RUSSIA AND THE TRADE WITH PERSIA.**
>
> Thirty years ago (says the Morning Post) an English gentleman of the name of Burgess succeeded in opening a direct trade between England and Persia by the way of Trebizond, the traffic up to that time having passed over the circuitous route of Smyrna. His journey from Trebizond, to Erzeroum taking place in winter, was made under considerable difficulties arising from the snow which encumbered, and in some cases almost obliterated, the numerous mountain passes through which the road, if it may be so called, winds for ten or eleven caravan marches: and on one occasion passing the Gauschupongar Mountain, about thirty-five miles from Erzeroum, he was obliged to leave five of his horses in the snow with their loads, which he did not succeed in recovering until three or four days afterwards, the horses having of course perished. This, however, applies only to three months of the year; in summer the passes being open, though hardly practicable for carriages. From Erzeroum to Tabriz, a distance of sixteen caravan marches, or three hundred miles, he found some little danger for weak parties of being plundered, but otherwise no serious impediments, and, arrived in Tabriz, he found the higher class of people were anxious to procure the best English double-barrelled guns, fine pistols, telescopes, gold and silver hunting watches, linens, china of the most valuable kind, cutlery, and other articles of the finest description, for which a ready market is not generally readily found in Central countries. This trade, so founded by him between 1827 and 1830, has continued and increased up to the present time, until the imports of Trebizond alone amount to nearly one hundred lacs of piastres per annum; a large proportion of which finds its way into Persia and Central Asia, a large proportion of the whole being English. This route only traversed by the caravans. In part the traffic is conveyed perhaps by Bayazed, the frontier town of the pachalic of Erzeroum, which, at the time of Mr. Burgess's first visit, was close to the Russian frontier. The steady march of Russian aggression, however, a few years after, seized Erivan — a province of Persia, of which some of the highest value was in sight, almost within her grasp; she has ever since crept steadily onward and has never lost a province gained; and, by a stroke of unexpected, and the creation of the new consulate country between Bayazed and Khoi, the Russian frontier has been extended to the south-west of these routes, and now the whole of the traffic of Europe with Persia and most of Central Asia, will of necessity, have to pass through Russian territory, guarded by Russian custom houses and supervised by Russian police. In their intercourse with Europe now the Persians are at the mercy of the Russians. Not a bale of goods can pass from Trebizond to Tabriz or from Tabriz from Trebizond without the permission of the Czar. Nor is this all. The great overland route from England, which the Americans call the "highway to Indian prosperity" — Erzeroum, formerly its chief halting place and entrepôt being now entirely in the possession of the Russians. Here too Russia holds the keys. What the price paid for this was we do not expect to learn; but probably some Persian states — for by one of those ingenious strokes of policy, the Czar has made himself irremediably odious both to the European trade of Turkey and the independence of Mahomedan Asia. In Baghdad it is difficult to say what view the Turk takes of recent events so far as concerns the importance trading routes. We are no wiser as to the trade of Europe — but that there are always half a dozen routes, from any one of our own territories, from any one of them can be selected that is the line of our most direct communication of the communications; but in these cases, with mountains, rivers, &c., as in this case, and it is a remarkable fact that, at the present state of matters, the traffic of Europe may be monopolised and the importance of its course of transportation from Persia. ## Notes

This is a remarkable 962-word article from the *Morning Post* (reprinted in the *Morpeth Herald*), published just two years after Edward Burgess's death. Key points:

- **"Thirty years ago … an English gentleman of the name of Burgess succeeded in opening a direct trade between England and Persia by the way of Trebizond"** — dates the opening of the trade route to c. 1827, consistent with Charles Henry Burgess's first journey to Persia. The article conflates Charles and Edward, or refers to "Burgess" as a family enterprise.
- **The Trebizond–Erzeroum–Tabriz route** is described in vivid detail: winter snow burying mountain passes, five horses lost with their loads on the Gauschupongar Mountain, ten to eleven caravan marches from Trebizond to Erzeroum, sixteen marches (300 miles) from Erzeroum to Tabriz.
- **Tabriz market**: the Persian upper class demanded "the best English double-barrelled guns, fine pistols, telescopes, gold and silver hunting watches, linens, china of the most valuable kind, cutlery" — matching Edward's own letters about selling chintzes, blankets, and cloth.
- **"This trade, so founded by him between 1827 and 1830, has continued and increased up to the present time"** — dates the Burgess commercial presence precisely and confirms it was still thriving in 1857.
- The article frames the Burgess trade route as strategically significant in the context of Russian expansion toward Persia and Central Asia — the "Great Game" in its commercial dimension.

This is the **first known British newspaper feature about the Burgess family's Persian trade enterprise**, and one of the earliest contemporary press references to Edward's world.
