## Notes

### Catherine O'Shea (née Enright, I119) — now head of family

Catherine has replaced her mother as head of household. Age 40 (born ~1871) — consistent with the 1901 census (age 30) and the tree export birth year of 1871. The Ancestry profile's alternate birth year of 1865 does not match either census return.

The fertility return — **3 children born alive, 3 still living** — confirms that all three daughters survived to 1911. Married **19 years** (married 2 June 1892, so 18 years and 10 months — rounded to 19).

Catherine lists no occupation for herself, but she is effectively running a **boarding house**: five paying boarders alongside her family. This was likely the household's primary income source, given Cornelius O'Shea's absence and the lack of any other earner in the family.

### Teresa Rosa O'Shea — age 16, Scholar

**Teresa Rose O'Shea** (b. 1894 per family records), here recorded as **Teresa Rosa**, age 16. Still at school. She was absent from the 1901 household — the 1911 return is her first documented census appearance.

### Madeline O'Shea — age 14, Scholar

**Madeline "Maddy" O'Shea** (b. 1897), age 14. At school alongside her older sister. Consistent with her age of ~4 in the 1901 census.

### Mary O'Shea (I13) — absent

**Mary O'Shea** (I13), who would be 18, is absent from this household. She appeared as a child of 8 in the 1901 return but by 1911 she has left home. Possibilities: in domestic service, at school or training elsewhere in Ireland, or already in England. Her later marriage to [Gerald Sebastian White](../../../people/gerald-sebastian-white.md) suggests she eventually moved to India; the route from Monkstown to Madras likely passed through England. Finding her 1911 census entry — whether in Ireland or England — would fill this gap.

### Catherine Enright (grandmother) — age 79, "Rlt Mother"

The grandmother is now listed as **"Rlt Mother"** (relation: mother) rather than head. Age 79 (born ~1832), she can **read only** (not write) and speaks **Irish and English** — the only Irish speaker in the household. She was 69 and head of household in 1901; by 1911 she has ceded that role to her daughter but still lives with the family. She would survive until **1956** — another 45 years, reaching approximately 124 by that chronology, which suggests the census age is understated or the death year of 1956 on the Ancestry profile may need revisiting.

### Cornelius O'Shea (I118) — still absent

Cornelius does not appear in this household. His person page confirms he was lodging at **4 James Street, Cork City** by 1911 — separate from his wife and children. He is listed in Catherine's fertility return under the "Married" column (not widowed), so the marriage was still legally intact. The separation appears to be practical — possibly related to employment or the breakdown of the teaching career documented on his person page.

### The five boarders

The boarders transform this from a domestic household into a small commercial operation:

| Name | Age | Occupation | Origin |
|------|-----|-----------|--------|
| John Leahy | 42 | General Labourer | Co. Cork |
| William Leahy | 27 | Boiler Maker | Co. Cork |
| Frederick McCotter | 40 | Engine Fitter at Works | Co. Cavan |
| Peter O'Hare | 24 | Golf Professor | Co. Louth |
| Patrick O'Hare | 20 | Golf Professor | Co. Louth |

The **Leahys** are likely brothers (both Cork, Catholic, single) — a general labourer and a boiler maker, representing local working-class trades.

**Frederick McCotter** is the one **Nonconformist** (Protestant) in the household and the only boarder from outside Munster (Co. Cavan, Ulster). "Engine Fitter at Works" suggests employment at a local industrial works — Monkstown's harbour location supported engineering and shipyard trades.

The **O'Hare brothers** from Co. Louth are both listed as **"Golf Professor"** — the Edwardian term for a professional golfer employed at a club to teach and play. **Monkstown Golf Club** (later renamed to **Raffeen Creek Golf Club**) was nearby, and many early Irish golf professionals came from the established golfing counties of the east coast. Two brothers from Louth working as golf professionals in Cork is a remarkable detail of early 20th-century sporting labour migration.
