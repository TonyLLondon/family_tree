# NYPL Burgess Persian Letters — Appendix: Anna Burgess Interview (1880)

**Anna (née Saginian) Burgess**, widow of Edward Burgess, interviewed in **March 1880** at **C. Belmont Villas, Leicester**. The manuscript of the interview is held at the New York Public Library. A portion is printed as an appendix to *The Burgess Persian Letters*, ed. Benjamin Schwartz.

**Corpus bundle:** [`corpus/nypl-burgess-appendix-anna-interview/`](corpus/nypl-burgess-appendix-anna-interview/extracted.pdf.md)

## Key passages (Anna's own words)

### On her family and father

> "My ancestors, on the sides of both parents, were Armenians, and I think for some time have lived in Tabriz. My father, **Daoud Khan Seguinoff**, was always in the military service of the Shah of Persia. My dear mother died some time before him. My uncles were men in high position. We were **six in family, two brothers and four sisters**. Both of my brothers were merchants in Tabreez; both are now dead. I am the eldest of the sisters. My next sister is the widow of Aga Pedros, an Armenian merchant. She lives in Tehran, and has three sons. **My youngest sister, Tamar, is the widow of Dr. Cormick.** Dr. Cormick's father came to Persia to be on the medical staff of the Shah. He married an Armenian lady, and Dr. C. was born in Tabriz."

### On the Burgess brothers and her marriage

> "We had known Charles and Edward Burgess a long time. They were **merchants of china, guns, and many other things**, and went to and fro between Tabriz and Tehran. **Edward Burgess asked for me of my father**. I loved him even before we were married. Then we were married at my father's house."

### On life in Tehran

> "When my husband was appointed to superintend the **printing of the Government newspaper** in Tehran, we got a few things needful for our house, and then our little son **John** was born. **Mr. Ronald Thompson** was his Godfather (as he was afterwards for our daughter **Fanny**). Both of our children were baptized in our house in Tehran, according to the Armenian rites."

### On Edward's illness and death journey

> "We travelled as fast as we could… Often we were obliged to stay at those very dreadful Mahommedan villages, lest he should die with the hard travel: for the disease had settled in his spine and limbs… we were nearly three months on that journey from Tehran to Tabriz. When we were eight days from Tabriz, there came to meet us my father, my two uncles, and my kind brother, **Dr. Cormick**… We both had had the horrible knowledge, that, if he had had to be buried outside one of those villages, the dogs would have eaten him directly."

### On Edward's death and burial

> "He lived for six weeks longer… Fanny was three days past one year old, he died. We had been married just five years. He was buried in the **little lovely green Armenian cemetery just outside the walls of Tabriz**, close by the side of my uncle."

### On the graves

> "Many years later… my Fanny wished to put a larger and better stone on her father's grave… The inscription is now double, both in English and in Armenian. **My dear father's grave is four or five English yards from it; it is among the graves of all my relations. And old Mr. & Mrs. Cormick and the Europeans are all buried there.**"
