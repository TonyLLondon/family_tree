# Zara — Antonio Zerauschek and the Italian Adriatic enclave

[Antonio Zerauschek](../people/antonio-zerauschek.md): **"trgovac, bankar, veleposjednik i tvorničar"** — trader, banker, large landowner, and factory owner in interwar **Zara** (Kingdom of Italy). **Hub:** [topics/zara-italy-dalmatia.md](../topics/zara-italy-dalmatia.md). **Addobbati narrative:** [addobbati-dalmatian-habsburg.md](addobbati-dalmatian-habsburg.md).

## Interwar Zara — the Italian enclave on the Adriatic

After the First World War, the Treaty of Rapallo handed Zara to Italy — an enclave surrounded by the new Yugoslav state, a small Italian-speaking city cut off from its Dalmatian hinterland. Under the *Porto Franco* regime it became a free port, drawing traders, manufacturers, and speculators to a city that was both politically important and commercially peculiar. Italian architectural and cultural trends flowed in; the pre-war Austro-Hungarian fabric was steadily overlaid with Rationalist facades and Italian civic life.

Antonio Zerauschek was part of the Italian-speaking urban elite that ran this enclave. His surname suggests Central European roots — likely Slovene or Austrian, a Germanized Slavic form (Žeravšek / Žeraušek) — but his world was Italian Zara: import-export, real estate, hospitality, and manufacturing. He married [Ester Addobbati](../people/ester-addobbati.md), daughter of [Pietro Pio Addobbati](../people/pietro-pio-addobbati.md), connecting two of the city's established civic families. Together they became one of the main private investment partnerships in interwar Zara.

Zara's other great industrial dynasty was the **Luxardo** family, whose maraschino distillery (founded 1821) had by the interwar period become Italy's most important, its harbour-front factory a city landmark. The Addobbatis bridged both families: Ester's great-uncle [Simeone Gilberto Addobbati](../people/simeone-gilberto-addobbati.md) — a Habsburg *Feldmarschalleutnant* — had married **Elisabetta Luxardo** ("Lisette") in 1885. Zerauscheks, Addobbatis, and Luxardos were interlocking parts of the same Italian-Dalmatian civic world.

## Široka ulica — Hotel Zerauschek and the Caffè Centrale

Zerauschek's most visible project was on **Široka ulica** (Calle Larga), the main commercial street. Around **1936** he acquired the **Hotel Bristol** and renamed it **Hotel Excelsior**. Then he went further: he demolished an older building on the same street and commissioned the Trieste architect **Umberto Nordio** to design a new hotel and café complex from scratch. Construction ran from **1937 to 1938**.

The result — the **Hotel Zerauschek** — was modernist in form, with loggias and stone cladding that acknowledged the city's historic context without imitating it. Below the hotel, the **Caffè Centrale** became Zara's principal meeting place: social, cultural, and commercial life ran through its tables. It was the signature private development of 1930s Zara.

## Calypso cigarettes — the Manifattura Zaratina Sigarette

Zara's free-port status placed it outside the Italian customs line. Tobacco manufacture was formally free but supervised by the Italian state monopoly; cigarettes produced there were classified as "foreign" in mainland Italy. Several private manufacturers operated: Greek, Italian, and state-backed. Into this milieu stepped Zerauschek.

He was first a **founder** of the **Manifattura Tabacchi Orientali** (MTO) — a large state-majority manufacturer on Riva 4 Novembre, producing brands including Delphi, Faro, Lido, Serenissima, and Zara. But he struck out on his own.

On **27 May 1928** he acquired the **Manifattura Zaratina Sigarette** from **Niccolò Peristeridis**, a Greek-origin tobacco man whose company had started as the **Manifattura Zaratina Tabacchi e Sigarette "N. Peristeridis"** at Calle Giuseppe Sabalich 2, originally producing **Calypso** and **Samos** cigarettes. The Peristeridis family was likely connected to the Fabbrica Tabacchi Turchi "Mustapha" (operating in Zara in the 1920s) and to the Peristeridis & Raftopulos tobacco factory in Durazzo (Albania). The company was renamed Manifattura Zaratina Sigarette in 1922 and moved to Calle Catena 6.

Under Zerauschek's ownership the company also absorbed the **Fabbrica Sigarette Diadora** (which had produced the "Diadora" and "Radio" brands) and grew its portfolio: **Calitea, Calypso, Diadora, Flavia, Lampo, Radio, Raminga, Samos, Samos Export, Si**. The *Difesa Adriatica* obituary (1973) says the factories offered **substantial employment** and Zerauschek was remembered with **gratitude** by workers.

Fulvia's 1996 memoir recalls the brand list slightly differently — **Calypso, Samos, Samos Export, Si Flaire, Lampo, Rominga** — with the addition of "Si Flaire" (possibly a variant of "Si") and "Rominga" (likely the same brand as "Raminga" in the published sources). "Oriental Tabac came from Bulgaria, Turkey, etc." ([Fulvia memoir](../sources/famhist-nonna-memoir-1996.md).)

A surviving c. 1940 Calypso hinged-box pack — twenty cigarettes, produced by "Manifattura Zaratina Sigarette S.A." — is documented in the zigsam.at Austrian cigarette packet collector's database. Later Calypso packs by Pritchard & Burton (Switzerland, 1960s) and Philip Morris (USA/Germany, 1970s) are unrelated.

The Manifattura Zaratina brands reached Italian cinema — and not as background dressing. In Mario Camerini's *Il Signor Max* (1937), Vittorio De Sica plays Gianni, a poor Roman edicola vendor who impersonates a wealthy count. A **Samos** pack sits on his kiosk counter. When Lauretta (Assia Noris) suspects the count and the vendor are the same man, she picks up the pack and asks: *"Do you smoke this brand?"* Gianni deflects: *"Not normally, but I do for this special occasion..."* The cigarette brand is the **plot mechanism** that tests the dual identity — one of the earliest examples of narrative product placement in European cinema. The Zerauschek family kept a credits page for the film in their Zara album, filed alongside business documents, suggesting they understood the placement as a commercial achievement. See [*Il Signor Max* — Samos on screen](../topics/il-signor-max-1937.md).

## Ausonia — Zadar's "sweetest factory"

Beyond tobacco, Zerauschek founded the **Fabbrica Cioccolato e Cacao Ausonia** — a chocolate and cocoa factory in the **Voštarnica** (Ceraria) quarter of Zadar. The neighbourhood developed rapidly after the 1928 bridge over the Jazine inlet connected the old centre to the suburbs; alongside the Ausonia factory, the Zerauschek family erected modern residential and commercial buildings in the area during the 1930s.

By **1929** the factory was already **exporting chocolate to Italy and Western European countries**. It won **numerous quality awards** for its products. A 2024 Croatian photo-feuilleton in *Zadarski tjednik* calls Ausonia Zadar's "sweetest factory" — noting that while the city's famous Maraschino liqueur industry is well remembered, few modern residents know that Zadar also had a thriving chocolate manufacturer.

A **possible Luxardo connection**: beyond maraschino, the Luxardo company also produced bonbons and confections. A 2011 *Poslovni dnevnik* article about a later Zadar chocolatier suggests that Luxardo may have sourced chocolate from Ausonia — an entirely plausible relationship given the family ties and the small size of the enclave — though this remains unconfirmed.

## The agencies and the empire of small businesses

Beyond manufacturing, Zerauschek held commercial agencies for an extraordinary range of goods. Fulvia's 1996 memoir lists them from family memory:

- **Ford motors** — cars arrived from America in enormous wooden packing cases. Ford invited Antonio to the United States twice, but the **Titanic** disaster put him off accepting.
- **Firestone** (tiles), **Frigidaire** (refrigerators), **Telefunken** (radios), **Zenith** (confirmed independently in [Riccardo's 1951 IRO interview](../sources/its-iro-riccardo-zerauschek-1951.md): "his brother who had a representative of Ford, Firestone, Zenith etc.").
- Later: **Lancia** motors and **DKW** (German cars and motorbikes), **Esso** (petrol).
- **Assicurazioni Generali di Venezia** — the insurance firm sent an employee to Zara with orders to stay until Antonio agreed to become their agent. It took a week.
- He held the **monopoly on oil supply** for the town's electricity, which competitors tried and failed to break.
- A **money exchange office**.
- An **ice factory** (acquired 1942) — ice delivered in 25-kilo bars by horse-drawn cart, later by truck, all summer long.
- A **fish cannery** and a **liqueur factory** (name not remembered).
- He imported **coffee, sugar, flour** and other food staples for local consumption and for provisioning vessels in port.

"He was keen on new things and no sooner was invented, than he had to have it." The *Difesa Adriatica* obituary corroborates the picture of a man who held agency lines for "foreign goods (flour, oils, petrol, cars, and similar)."

His relationship with his workers was paternalistic but genuine. A favourite anecdote: a dock porter called **Vize** insisted on carrying two sacks instead of one — Antonio was always yelling at him to stop. In one of his warehouses near his office ("where he never sat, as he constantly went from one business to the other in his small car"), Antonio kept a female cat called **la Tigre**, a legendary rat hunter. When the cat was pensioned off, Vize was given custody and a monthly allowance. Years later the exchange became a running joke: "Vize, but is it possible?" — "Yes, yes, Paron, it's alive, alive! Shall I bring it here for you to see?" When the family returned to Zara in **1960** and dined at the hotel that had been Antonio's, Vize was among the former employees invited. "I cannot believe it, I cannot believe it, I am dining with you."

([Fulvia memoir, 1996](../sources/famhist-nonna-memoir-1996.md).)

## The communist trial — and the workers who refused

When the Yugoslav communists occupied Zara, Antonio was taken to trial *in absentia* for "exploitation of his workers." They could not find a single worker willing to testify against him. Some were beaten — and still refused. The charge was changed to selling factory products to the enemy: "Italian town — Italian Nonno, Italian cigarettes smoked by Italian soldiers. It is all a joke if it wasn't that…" Fulvia described it as the only instance she knew in the history of Yugoslav occupation "where nobody got chopped to pieces — only beaten." ([Fulvia memoir, 1996](../sources/famhist-nonna-memoir-1996.md).)

## War, exile, and memory

Allied bombing devastated Zara in 1943–44. The Zerauschek factories, the Luxardo distillery, and much of the city were left in ruins. At the end of 1944 German troops withdrew and Yugoslav forces occupied Dalmatia. The Italian population fled into exile — to Trieste, Rome, Australia, the Americas — but too many were killed. Among the dead were **Nicolò II Luxardo** and his wife **Bianca**, wantonly drowned by the occupiers. The city passed to Yugoslavia; Italian families who had lived there for generations dispersed across Italy and beyond.

### Riccardo's escape: Lošinj, Silba, and the Opera

Antonio's youngest brother [Riccardo](../people/riccardo-zerauschek.md) ("Zio Ginco") had worked as his **procurator** — legal agent for the Ford, Firestone, and Zenith agencies — but was not a business partner. In **January 1944**, to avoid being mobilized by the Germans, Riccardo escaped with his family to the island of **Lošinj Grande** (Lussingrande). When the danger increased, they moved to **Silba** (February 1945). After returning to Zara in April 1945, Riccardo found work as a **singer with the Opera at Split and Zagreb**. In **April 1947** he obtained a permit from the Yugoslav municipal authority on the pretext of continuing his singing studies in Italy. He left for **Trieste** and never returned, opting to retain Italian citizenship.

His IRO displaced persons file (ITS Nr. S-1920, Trieste, 1951) records his political statement: "He hated the Faschism and Nacism as well as communism. He has been born as free man and wants to be and to live in freedom." At the time of filing he was jobless and seeking emigration to the USA or Canada. His wife was **Tatiana née Machiedo** — a Dalmatian Italian family, not Serbian as Fulvia later recalled — and they had three children born in Zara: Nora (1933), Renato (1934), and Luciano (1939).

"As a non-partner he escaped the fate of the Luxardo brothers, who were murdered" — among them Nicolò II and his wife Bianca, drowned by the Yugoslav occupiers. ([Fulvia memoir, 1996](../sources/famhist-nonna-memoir-1996.md); [IRO file](../sources/its-iro-riccardo-zerauschek-1951.md); [Luxardo company history](../sources/luxardo-history.md).)

The Manifattura Zaratina Sigarette — partly destroyed by the bombing — was **confiscated** by the Yugoslav authorities. The company attempted to reconstitute itself in Italian territory, convening its shareholders' assembly in **Florence**. The assembly voted to transform into a *società a responsabilità limitata* (s.r.l.), but the **Tribunale di Firenze annulled** the resolution and the company ceased to exist. The Ausonia chocolate factory was likewise shut down by the incoming Yugoslav authorities; no chocolate was produced in Zadar again for **82 years**, until a new artisan enterprise in 2011.

### The September 1943 armistice

Antonio survived because he happened to be in Italy. He had bought a house on the mainland "just in case things went badly," and was returning from a two-week holiday. He was in **Trieste**, at the hotel, waiting for his boat back to Zara, when the radio announced that Italy had changed sides. In the same hotel was the **General commanding the Trieste army** — Antonio knew him. The two spent three days wondering what would happen. The General waited for orders that never came. Eventually Antonio telephoned his brother **Ginco** (Riccardo) in Zara, who told him the Germans had taken over and suggested he stay in Italy. "This probably saved his life — in fact, saved his life, full stop." ([Fulvia memoir, 1996](../sources/famhist-nonna-memoir-1996.md).)

### Florence and the failed restart

Antonio and Ester Zerauschek settled in **Florence**. He sold a **210-hectare (420-acre) farm** in the Apennines near **Faenza** and bought into **INCAS**, a chocolate factory in Florence — an attempt to continue "as his from father for his father." But the venture failed. Ferrero's cheaper products were shaking the Italian chocolate market. Fulvia believed he made a strategic mistake: "He settled in Florence, a foreign town, instead of somewhere in Veneto, more like home, more in the middle of people he knew and who knew him. He said once, if I tell somebody here what I did, what I owned, what happened, they do not believe me. In short, fish out of water."

The Italian government's compensation scheme for the **350,000 refugees** (20,000 from Zara) was proportional and punitive: "You lost ten, have five; lost a hundred, have ten; lost two billion, have a hundred." It came in drops — "never enough at once to start a business." The scheme was eventually declared unconstitutional and scrapped, but by then Antonio was already dead. ([Fulvia memoir, 1996](../sources/famhist-nonna-memoir-1996.md).)

Ester died first. Antonio followed in **February 1973**, aged 84. The *Difesa Adriatica* obituary — published in the community's exile newspaper, issue dated 25 March–3 April 1973 — presented him as a central figure in Zara's commercial and industrial life between the wars, and recorded the forced abandonment of the city under Allied bombing.

They are buried together. The [grave slab](../media/docs/Grave%20slab%20Florence%20Ester%20Addobbati%20Zerauschek%20Antonio%20Giuliana%20Zerauschek%20Rivolta.png) in Florence reads Ester Addobbati Zerauschek, Antonio Zerauschek, and Giuliana Zerauschek Rivolta.
