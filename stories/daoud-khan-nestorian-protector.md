# Daoud Khan — Governor of the Nestorians

This is a visual life of [Daoud Khan Saginian](../people/daoud-khan-saginian.md): Georgian-born officer who entered the Qajar army, took root in **Armenian Tabriz**, commanded troops in **Shiraz and Isfahan**, and in **1845** was given a civil-military title over the **Nestorian** (Church of the East) communities of the **Urumia** region — not at the whim of a single vizier, but after approaches by **British and Russian diplomats** and an act of the **Shah**. Mission archives describe the posting as meant to shield villagers from **local exactions**; the same sources show how quickly his office became contested by **Muslim notables**, by the **patriarch and Jesuit** interests aligned against the American mission, and by the ordinary friction of a border province where every protection is also a patronage.

The plates pair **19th-century views of Tabriz** (Flandin lithographs, now in `media/context/tabriz-qajar/`) with **family and Armenian cemetery** photographs, the **Sartip portrait** reproduced in Iranian publications, **Mohammad Shah Qajar** as the sovereign on the throne in **May 1845**, and portraits of his daughters **[Tamar](../people/tamar-saginian.md)** and **[Anna](../people/anna-saginian.md)** with Anna’s child **Fanny**. For the full vitals, children, burial debate, and bibliography, see the [person page](../people/daoud-khan-saginian.md); for the wider Saginian–Burgess line, [topics/persia.md](../topics/persia.md) and [saginian-burgess-bottin-stump.md](saginian-burgess-bottin-stump.md).

## Tabriz above the plain

**Tabriz** in the early nineteenth century was a natural hinge: trade routes from the Caucasus and Anatolia met the roads south to **Isfahan** and the Gulf, and the court’s attention swung between Russian pressure on the north-western marches and the internal business of tax, troops, and tribe. For a Georgian officer who would spend most of his adult life there, the city was not a posting on the edge of “Persia” but the centre of gravity — the place where he would bury his wife, raise daughters who married into British and court medical circles, and end his days in the **Armenian cemetery** beside **Surb Astvatsatsin**, a few yards from his son-in-law [Edward Burgess](../people/edward-burgess.md).

---

## The face the books kept

The photograph that circulates in family and reference books is a **book-page reproduction** of an older portrait: **Sartip** uniform, Qajar braid, the steady gaze of a man who had already lived half a century under two shahs. The Persian caption preserved in Esmail Ra’in’s *Iraniyan-i Armani* summarises the career his children remembered: commander of Iranian forces in **Shiraz** and **Isfahan** under **Fath-Ali Shah**, death at **seventy-eight** ([source card](../sources/sartip-daoud-khan-sakinian-photograph.md)). That southern command is the layer most biographies emphasise; the **Urumia** governorship of **1845** sits on top of it like a second skin — same man, different theatre, and a title that tied him to villagers whose language and liturgy were not his own.

---

## Through the gate in 1811

In **1811** David **Saginean** — **Saginskilli** in Georgian — left Russian service with his brother **Zaal** and their companion **Solayman Khan Saham al-Dowleh**. Galust Shermazanian’s nineteenth-century Armenian history of distinguished Armenians in Persia, read critically through Hirotake Maeda’s **2019** Brill chapter, gives the texture of the decision: fear of forced relocation and religious pressure after the absorption of Georgia, a leave-taking obtained on a **wrestling** pretext, then the long walk south through **Yerevan**, **Akhalkalaki**, and **Gyumri** into **Tabriz**, where no warning had gone ahead of them ([Maeda corpus reference](../sources/corpus/maeda-2019-enikolopians-saginian-flight/reference.md)). In Tabriz they entered **Abbas Mirza’s** reforming army; Shermazanian’s line that they “embraced the **Grigorian** faith” after arrival is the reason later travellers could call the same officer Georgian in one breath and Armenian in the next.

---

## Armenian Tabriz

The Saginian household became **Armenian Iranian** in the full social sense: marriage within the community, daughters raised as Armenians, a military title (**Sartip**, brigadier general) earned in Qajar service rather than in the Russian regiments they had left behind. Yaghoubian’s **2014** work on ethnicity and nationalism in Iran, drawing on later oral history with the Saginian descendant line, records the formal titles **farmandeh** of Shiraz and Isfahan and **sarperast-e Aramane** — guardian of the Armenians — alongside the family mausoleum at **Surb Astvatsatsin** outside the walls ([Yaghoubian source card](../sources/yaghoubian-2014-ethnicity-identity-nationalism-iran.md)). It is that double footing — **imperial officer** and **communal elder** — that made him plausible to London and St Petersburg as a **Christian** governor over a **Christian** rural population on the **Urumia** plain.

---

## Shiraz, Isfahan, and the rank of the grandees

Before **1845**, his reputation rested on **soldiers and campaigns**: service under **Fath-Ali Shah** and his sons, command under **Sayf ol-Dowleh**, a division of his own in the **1834 Isfahan** fighting. Armenian military literature names **Davit Khan Saginian** as head of the Isfahan army in that episode. That is the world of **provincial governors**, **tents**, and **litters** — the machinery of Qajar rule in the south, far from the Nestorian villages beside **Lake Urmia**, where American Presbyterians were building schools and where the **Mar Shimun** patriarchate watched every foreign footprint.

---

## Wolff writes him twice

In **1843** the Jewish-born Anglican traveller **Joseph Wolff** reached Tabriz on his way toward Bukhara. In *Narrative of a Mission to Bokhara* he first names **“Daoud Khan, a Colonel in the Russian service… a genuine Georgian”** — origin and old commission. A few pages later, listing the party that rode out with him, Wolff writes **“Daoud Khan the Armenian”** with the Russian attachés and Greek merchants — community and present loyalty ([corpus extract](../sources/corpus/narrative-mission-bokhara/extracted.pdf.md), Ch. XXVI; [source card](../sources/narrative-mission-bokhara.md)). The two lines are not a slip; they are what happens when a man’s **passport story** and his **parish story** diverge and then reunite in a single career.

---

## The plain of Oroomiah

“Nestorian” in nineteenth-century English usage meant the **Syriac-speaking Church of the East** villages west and south of **Lake Urmia** — farmers and clergy in a landscape of mud brick, orchards, and winter cold, caught between Kurdish tribes, Turkish-speaking neighbours, and the tax logic of distant Tehran. American Board missionaries had made **Oroomiah** a station; English and Russian consuls watched the border from **Tabriz**. When family historians say Daoud Khan was “placed in charge of the Nestorian Christians of **Urumia** in **April 1845** at the request of the **British and Russian consuls** in Tabriz,” they are following Vicky Uffindell’s reconstruction from the Cormick–Saginian archive ([Uffindell vol. 2 — family & friends, corpus](../sources/corpus/uffindell-cormick-persia-v2-family-friends-1850s/extracted.pdf.md)). Rufus Anderson’s **1872** institutional history, compiled from mission papers, lifts the same event to **May 1845** and names the **English and Russian Ambassadors** and the **Shah** — a one-month gap between “consuls” and “ambassadors” is exactly what one expects when consular telegrams become an audience at court.

---

## At the instance of the ambassadors

**Mohammad Shah Qajar** had ruled since **1834**; in **May 1845** Anderson records the royal act in plain missionary prose:

> *In May, 1845, the Shah, at the instance of the English and Russian Ambassadors, appointed Dawood Khan, of Tabriz, an Armenian from Georgia and an officer of the army, Governor of the Nestorians. The object was to protect the Christians from the oppressions they had long suffered from the Moslem nobles residing in the district.*

The verbatim extract lives in the vault’s [Anderson corpus reference](../sources/corpus/history-of-the-mission-by-rufus-anderson-5264293694/reference.md); the [source card](../sources/rufus-anderson-1872-history-missions-oriental-churches.md) ties the volume to the scan. Anderson was writing **for** the American Board, not for the Foreign Office; his “object was to protect” is their reading of policy, not a Persian farmān translated in full. Even so, the sentence is precious: it confirms the **dual identity** Wolff had already noticed, it names the **coercion** the appointment was meant to check, and it shows **London and St Petersburg** acting in parallel at a moment when Anglo-Russian rivalry in Persia more often made headlines than partnership.

---

## Christian governor and civil protector

A few years later the same volume turns to the **mission–patriarch–Jesuit** struggle. Anderson writes:

> *For a time the Patriarch and the Jesuits, both aiming at the overthrow of the mission, were in practical combination. As a necessary means to this end, both wished to expel from office Dawood Khan, the Christian Governor and civil protector of the Nestorians of that province; and the Mohammedan nobles were in sympathy with them in this, as that dignitary stood in the way of their exactions.*

The second block is in the same [corpus reference](../sources/corpus/history-of-the-mission-by-rufus-anderson-5264293694/reference.md#transcription-christian-governor-and-civil-protector-later-1840s). Read soberly, it is not a certificate of sainthood; it is evidence that **protection** and **extraction** were competing uses of the same countryside, and that Daoud Khan’s office blocked one stream of revenue and leverage while the American mission blocked another. The **Muslim nobles** Anderson names were not abstract villains — they were landlords and notables with claims the Shah’s appointee was interfering with. The **patriarch and Jesuits** wanted a different religious geography. In the middle stood a **Tabriz Armenian** general who had accepted a brief written in the language of **imperial diplomacy** and **rural justice** at once.

---

## Ruins and alliances

Anderson’s following pages tie the same politics to **Mr. Stevens** at the English consulate and to the wider **Russian** presence — the same consular world into which Daoud Khan’s daughter **Tamar** would later marry [Dr. William Cormick](../people/william-cormick.md), court physician, with the Rev. **William Stocking** of **Urumia** officiating at the **British consulate** wedding in **1850** ([Uffindell vol. 2 — family & friends, corpus](../sources/corpus/uffindell-cormick-persia-v2-family-friends-1850s/extracted.pdf.md)). The mission archive is partisan; the family tree is not. What both agree on is that the **Urumia** posting was not a silent sinceure. It bound the Saginians to **Stocking’s** congregation, to **Nestorian** kin networks through work rather than blood, and to the **Anglo-Russian** monitoring of Persia’s north-west for the rest of Mohammad Shah’s reign and into **Naser al-Din’s**.

---

## Tamar’s line — medicine and the court

**Tamar Saginian’s** painted portrait belongs to the **Hovnatanian** school tradition: direct gaze, lace fichu, the modest finery of a merchant-officer’s daughter who had stepped from **Tabriz Armenian** society into the **Qajar** medical household. Her marriage to **William Cormick** linked the Saginian name to **Mohammad Shah** and then to **Naser al-Din** as crown prince — the inner court medicine that Edward Burgess’s world of translation and commerce touched only at an angle. The two sisters, **Anna** and **Tamar**, thus sat on different rungs of the same ladder: one facing **England** through Burgess, the other facing **the throne** through Cormick.

---

## Anna, Fanny, and the long shadow

**Anna Saginian** and her daughter **Fanny Burgess** appear here in a later photograph — mother and child in European dress, the line that would carry **Fanny** through **Urumia** schooldays with the American mission, through widowhood after Edward’s **1855** death on the **Tehran–Tabriz** road, and on to the **1880** interview preserved in the **NYPL Burgess papers** ([NYPL appendix corpus](../sources/corpus/nypl-burgess-appendix-anna-interview/extracted.pdf.md)). Anna’s transcript names her father as **“Daoud Khan Seguinoff”** and places his grave **yards from Edward’s** in the **Tabriz Armenian cemetery** — the same ground shown in the church photographs, tree-shaded in her remembered words. Uffindell’s reading of the papers places his **last illness** and **death** in the window after Anna nursed him in Tabriz and before she had long settled Fanny at **Urumia** — a chronology that sits a few years earlier than Yaghoubian’s **1867** date from later oral memory ([epidemics / Tabriz corpus](../sources/corpus/uffindell-cormick-persia-v2-epidemics-tabriz-eastwick/extracted.pdf.md)). The discrepancy is left open on the [person page](../people/daoud-khan-saginian.md); this scrapbook does not need to resolve it. It is enough to say that the **Governor of the Nestorians** ended as a **father** whose daughters carried his name into **British**, **Persian**, and **American** archives alike — each strand truthful about a different side of the same life.
