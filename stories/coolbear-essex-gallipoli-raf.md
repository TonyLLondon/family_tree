# Chimney Sweep to the Founding of the RAF

## The chimney sweep from Maldon

The High Street in Maldon, Essex, runs down a steep hill to the Hythe quay where Thames barges once unloaded coal. It was this small market town on the Blackwater estuary — not London, not an industrial city — that produced Archibald Percy Coolbear in March 1892. His father, [George Gridley Coolbear](../people/george-gridley-coolbear-i105.md), came from a tight Essex cluster whose surname had drifted through four spellings in two generations: the parish registers in neighbouring Mundon recorded **Colbia** in the 1830s, then Coolbar, Colbear, and finally Coolbear by the 1851 census. The family had been in this stretch of flat marshland and tidal creeks since at least John Colbia's marriage to Mary Barnard at Tollesbury in 1832.

Archibald grew up to be a chimney sweep. His RAF service form records the occupation matter-of-factly in field 5a: he was a working man who climbed inside flues for a living. He stood five feet seven inches tall with a thirty-nine-inch chest — unremarkable numbers on paper, but the frame of someone accustomed to physical labour. On 28 October 1911, at nineteen, he married [Rosina A Martin](../people/rosina-a-martin-i104.md), an Islington girl. The GEDCOM family file records the wedding in December 1911, but the date written in his own hand on the service form — "28.10.11" — is primary evidence and takes precedence. Their daughter [May](../people/may-coolbear-i100.md) arrived in March 1916, by which time her father had already been overseas for nearly a year.

## Gallipoli

On 29 December 1914, three months into the war, Archibald Coolbear enlisted in the **Essex Regiment** as a Private. He was twenty-two. The Army gave him regimental number **4677** — later renumbered to **16694** when the regiment standardised its rolls in 1917. The 1st Battalion Essex Regiment had been part of the 29th Division since the outbreak, and it was with the division that Coolbear deployed to the **Mediterranean Expeditionary Force**. His character page records the posting in five words: "Gallipoli, 20.5.15."

By the time he arrived, the beaches at Cape Helles had already become a killing ground. The 1st Essex had landed on 25 April — the first day of the campaign — under Lt Col Godfrey Faussett, storming Hill 138 under fire from the cliffs above. The cost on that first day alone was three officers and fifteen other ranks killed, eight officers and eighty-seven wounded. Faussett himself was killed on 2 May leading an assault on a defensive redoubt. By mid-campaign only four officers who had embarked from England with the 1st Essex were still on active duty. On 6 June, C Company lost sixty-five of its seventy men in a single counter-attack from the rear. The trenches at Helles were close-set, sometimes only thirty yards apart, and snipers made movement in daylight suicidal. Soldiers used periscopes to observe the Turkish lines while their comrades slept in the dust. The last major offensive on 5 August cost the battalion fifty killed, two hundred and two wounded, and a hundred and eighty missing.

Coolbear's official date of entry into the MEF was 7 June 1915, recorded on his medal index card (WO 372, roll B76/2C, page 109). He earned the **1914–15 Star** — the campaign medal awarded only to those who served in a theatre of war before 31 December 1915. But the peninsula did not break him with a bullet. It broke him with disease. On 4 October 1915 he was admitted to the General Hospital at Zagazig — in Egypt, where the MEF's medical infrastructure was based. Sick and wounded from Gallipoli were shipped across the eastern Mediterranean to Egyptian hospitals; Alexandria was the main receiving port. Coolbear was diagnosed with **rheumatism**. By December he had contracted **scarlatina** and was hospitalised again, this time at hospital 1784 in Alexandria. The medical board declared him fit to duty on 1 January 1916 — but by then Gallipoli had been evacuated. The Essex Regiment regrouped in Egypt before the next deployment, and the peninsula was left to the dead.

## France

In April 1916 the Essex Regiment crossed from Egypt to the Western Front. Coolbear's character page marks the transfer: "France, 21.4.16." A puzzling secondary number — **S. 8855C** — appears alongside his main regimental number, possibly an attached-unit or Machine Gun Corps designation that his surviving papers do not explain.

The 1st Essex, still part of the **88th Brigade, 29th Division**, arrived in France just over two months before the opening of the Battle of the Somme. On **1 July 1916** the 29th Division attacked the fortified village of **Beaumont-Hamel** — one of the most catastrophic failures of the entire war. The 86th Brigade's Newfoundland Regiment was virtually wiped out in the assault, advancing into uncut wire and concentrated machine-gun fire. The 88th Brigade, Coolbear's brigade, was the follow-up force tasked with exploiting a breakthrough that never came. Coolbear's service record does not itemise individual engagements, but it places him with the unit, in the sector, at the time. Twenty-two days later, on 23 July 1916, he was admitted to the **98th Hospital, Rouen**, with **dysentery** — his body breaking down in the weeks after the division's worst day.

By 3 November he had been evacuated back to England, to the **Mont Dore Military Hospital** in Bournemouth — a requisitioned Edwardian hotel turned convalescent ward.

He returned to France, but his body never fully recovered. On 20 August 1917 he was admitted to the **4th General Hospital, Camiers**, with severe diarrhoea. The same day, the 24th General Hospital at Étaples recorded him under observation for mild dysentery — a pair of entries suggesting he was shuttled between facilities as doctors tried to decide what was wrong. By 31 August he was back in England again, at a hospital in Fordham. The pattern had become circular: France, disease, evacuation, recovery, France, disease again.

## Into the air

On 26 January 1918, the loop broke. Coolbear transferred to the **Royal Flying Corps**. His new service number — **125257** — came from a block specifically allocated in February 1918 for Army transfers into the RFC (numbers 125001–128000, per the [RAFCommands reference table](https://www.rafcommands.com/reference/raf-service-numbers/)). After three years of infantry service, trench disease, and repeated hospitalisation, the Air Service took him in as an **Instructor** — a role that kept him at training airfields in England rather than returning him to the mud.

Then, on **1 April 1918**, the RFC and the Royal Naval Air Service merged to form the **Royal Air Force** — the world's first independent air force. Coolbear's service record documents the transition with characteristic military brevity: "R.A.F. 1.4.18." He was one of the tens of thousands who crossed that threshold automatically on the day the new service came into being. He belongs to the RAF's **founding generation** — not the aces who made headlines, but the ground instructors and technicians without whom no squadron could train or fly.

His postings show **18 Y.S.** (likely the 18th Training Squadron) and **Spittalgate**, an RFC training aerodrome on the outskirts of Grantham, Lincolnshire, that had opened in 1916 specifically as a training station. Spittalgate hosted successive reserve and training squadrons flying **Avro 504** trainers — the standard aircraft for instructing new pilots — alongside several United States Army Air Service squadrons that passed through in 1918. For a man whose war had been defined by disease and trenches, it was a different world: open Lincolnshire farmland, canvas hangars, the sound of rotary engines overhead instead of artillery. He drew corporal's pay from about February 1918. The war ended in November, and on **3 March 1919** he was transferred to the RAF Reserve. His discharge address was 69 Milton Street, Southend-on-Sea, Essex. His next of kin was listed as wife Rosina Coolbear, 33 Army Road, Southend.

## Silence at thirty-six

After the war, the Coolbears settled first in Southend-on-Sea, then moved to Hendon in Middlesex. There is no London Gazette notice of a commission or mention in dispatches — Coolbear served as an enlisted man throughout — and no Commonwealth War Graves Commission memorial, because he survived the war. But the repeated bouts of dysentery, diarrhoea, and rheumatism across Gallipoli, France, and English hospitals suggest a body worn down by four years of wartime service.

In **1928**, Archibald Percy Coolbear died at Hendon. He was roughly thirty-six years old.

The following year, the **Ministry of Pensions** processed a card (P.F. 23 M.) for his widow, **Mrs Coolbear**, at 10 Queensland Road, Holloway, N.4. The card cross-references his Essex Regiment number (16694, crossed out) and his RAF number (125257), and carries the awards reference **11/W/42429**. Copies were stamped 3 August 1929, with entries dated 3 May and 7 August 1929. A note on his RAF service file reads: "Last Card Destroyed 13.1.21" — an administrative purge of a prior record, not the destruction of the file itself.

He left behind Rosina and twelve-year-old May. In 1940, May married into the Holborn [Evans](../people/joseph-evans-i18.md) family — connecting the Coolbear line to the broader [Evans · Cerpa Pérez](../lines/evans-cerpa-perez-london-chile.md) tree that this project traces.

## Evidence and records

The primary record is the **AIR 79/1122** service file (TNA, Fold3 job 17-039), captured as images: [service record form](../media/docs/Archibald%20Colbear%20RAF%20service%20record%20form%20official%20number%20125257.jpg), [character / trade / medals page](../media/docs/Archibald%20Coolbear%20RAF%20record%20character%20trade%20medals%20Gallipoli%20France%20125257%20(3).jpg), and [Ministry of Pensions card](../media/docs/Cool%20Bear,%20Archibald%20Percy%20(125257).jpg). Medal index cards (WO 372), award rolls (WO 329), and the RAF Nominal Index (AIR 78) are documented in the full [person page](../people/archibald-percy-coolbear.md).

Three campaign medals are confirmed: the **1914–15 Star** (MEF entry 7 June 1915), the **British War Medal**, and the **Victory Medal**. His service numbers evolved from 4677 (original Essex battalion) to 16694 (regiment-wide renumbering) to 125257 (RFC/RAF).
