# Helia — Estadio, prohibition

This is a short visual life of [Helia del Carmen Cerpa Pérez](../people/helia-cerpa-perez.md). It begins in the civil service under **Salvador Allende**, passes through her imprisonment at the **Estadio Nacional** in **September 1973**, follows her name onto the prohibition lists that kept her out of Chile in the **1980s**, and ends with a photograph of Helia in old age, smiling beside her family. On the Corporación’s register of women held at the national stadium she is recorded as **CERPA PEREZ HELIA DEL C**.

Most of the documentary stills come from family scans kept in [`manual/`](../manual/). The opening portrait of Allende is from the **Library of Congress**, via Wikimedia Commons.

The plates run as a single arc. **Allende** and the **Registro Civil** open the story; the bombing of **La Moneda** and the street arrests follow; two photographs from inside the Estadio — the **bowl from the field**, then the **perimeter fence** — stand in for the imprisonment itself. After that the scrapbook moves through four later acts of remembrance: the **inscription** preserved inside the working stadium, an indoor installation of **suspended portraits**, an outdoor wall of **name plaques**, and a candle-lit **vigil for murdered children**. The booklet ***Cartilla del Retornado*** and the page that carries Helia’s entry close the political section. The last image is **Helia herself**.

---

## Allende — and Helia at the Registro Civil

**Salvador Allende** was President of Chile from **November 1970** until the morning of **11 September 1973**. Through those three years Helia worked for the **Servicio de Registro Civil e Identificación**, the civil registry that records births, marriages, deaths, and identity papers under the **Ministry of Justice**. She was **twenty-seven** when Allende took office and **thirty** when the armed forces moved against him. The registry under Allende was led by **Heriberto Benquis Camhi**, a Socialist; the junta replaced him on **12 September**, the day after the coup. Public servants like Helia were among the first categories of person the new regime purged, detained, and pushed into exile.

The 2010 Ministerio del Interior transparency release of *exonerados políticos* reparation payments names her workplace and the date she was forced out. Her line reads:

> *CERPA PEREZ HELIA DEL CARMEN — SERV.DE REGISTRO CIVIL E IDENTIF. — 16/04/1974* — **Helia Cerpa Pérez, Civil Registry and Identification Service, dismissed 16 April 1974.**

**Source:** [corpus](../sources/corpus/2010-transparencia-exonerados-politicos-julio/source.yaml); [citation card](../sources/exonerados-politicos-transparencia-2010.md).

## 11 September 1973 — La Moneda

On the morning of **11 September 1973** the Chilean armed forces surrounded the **Palacio de la Moneda** in Santiago. **Hawker Hunter** jets bombed the presidential palace, and Allende died inside it as the building was stormed. By that evening a junta led by General **Augusto Pinochet** had dissolved Congress, banned the parties of the left, and declared a state of siege. **Seventeen years** of dictatorship began that night.

---

## Roundups in the streets

In the days that followed, soldiers and police arrested **tens of thousands** of people across the country — party members, trade unionists, teachers, civil servants, and anyone they suspected of sympathy with the **Unidad Popular**. Many of those detained in Santiago were taken to the **Estadio Nacional**. Press photographs from the period show what the round-ups looked like in the open air: civilians lined up with their **hands on their heads** beside soldiers and waiting buses, the ordinary apparatus of mass arrest played out on a city pavement.

---

## The Estadio Nacional — the bowl from the field

The junta turned Chile’s national sports stadium into its largest holding camp. Between **12 September and 9 November 1973** some **twenty thousand** people passed through it; the **Red Cross** counted **seven thousand** on a single day. The first photograph here is taken from the playing field. **Soldiers** stand on the track in the foreground; the lower concrete tiers behind them are filled with **detainees**, hundreds of figures pressed together along the curve of the stand. It is an image about **scale** — the bowl of a stadium repurposed as a prison.

The list of women who passed through that bowl was kept by the **Corporación Estadio Nacional Memoria Nacional Ex Prisioneros Políticos** and published in *Cambio21* on **31 December 2018**. Helia is on it:

> *CERPA PEREZ HELIA DEL C* — between *CEBALLOS AREVENA ALEJANDR* and *CESPEDES LAVERGUE SILVIA X.* — **Helia del Carmen Cerpa Pérez, one of 405 women named.**

**Source:** [corpus](../sources/corpus/cambio21-405-mujeres-estadio-nacional-2018/source.yaml); [citation card](../sources/estadio-nacional-mujeres-detenidas-2018.md).

## At the wire

The second photograph is from the same weeks but a different distance. A **chain-link fence** cuts the frame in half: civilians in everyday clothes are gathered against it from the side of the stands, while soldiers in helmets stand on the field in front, **rifles in their hands**. Pieces of laundry hang on the wire. Where the first picture shows the **size** of the enclosure, this one shows the **perimeter** — the fence and the armed guard that fixed people inside it.

---

## *Un pueblo sin memoria es un pueblo sin futuro*

A short section of the original wooden bleachers has been kept inside the running track and screened off from the modern seating. A concrete beam above it carries a single line in **copper letters**:

> *Un pueblo sin memoria es un pueblo sin futuro* — **a people without memory is a people without a future.**

On match days, supporters fill the rows above in club colours. The same bowl that held prisoners in **1973** now carries a sentence that refuses to forget them, in plain view of anyone who comes to watch football.

---

## Suspended portraits

Indoors, a memorial installation hangs **dozens of translucent panels** from the ceiling, each one printed with a face in muted, **coppery tones**. The panels float at different depths, so that one portrait shows through another wherever you stand. The work is from long after the dictatorship, and it names no single week of **1973**; what it insists on is **plurality** — on the simple, exhausting fact that the regime broke many lives, not one.

---

## *El olvido está lleno de memoria*

In the open air, a row of white plaques stands on grass. Across the four largest boards, in cursive script, runs Mario **Benedetti**’s line:

> *El olvido está lleno de memoria* — **oblivion is full of memory.**

Below the words, the boards carry dense columns of names with markings beside each one — **D.D.** for *detenido desaparecido*, **E.P.** for *ejecutado político* — and a date, mostly in the **1970s**. The roll calls do the same work as the inscription in the stadium, by quieter means.

---

## *Niños asesinados*

At night, against a graffitied concrete wall, the words ***Niños asesinados*** stand in heavy black letters, with the **names and ages of children** written below. Candles run along the base of the wall. In the photograph a child kneels to tend one of the flames. The picture is recent, but it belongs to the same long act of remembrance.

---

## Banned from Chile — *Cartilla del Retornado*

For most of the **1980s** the dictatorship kept formal lists of **Chileans forbidden to re-enter** the country. The legal basis lay in **Decreto Ley 81** (1973) and **Decreto Ley 604** (1974), and in the **24th transitory provision** of the **1980 Constitution**. In **November 1985** the **Comité Pro-Retorno de Exiliados**, supported by the **Vicaría de la Solidaridad**, distributed ***Cartilla del Retornado* Nº 3** — a slim booklet that reproduced the relevant decrees alongside the current rolls of names so that exiles could read for themselves what was being done to them.

---

## Her name on the police list

Inside the booklet, on **page 12** of the ***Sexto listado*** dated **15 July 1985**, among **4,360 names**, sits her entry:

> *Cerpa Pérez, Helia del Carmen* — **named on the sixth official list of Chileans forbidden to enter their own country.**

Under international pressure the lists shrank year by year; on **1 September 1988**, **Decreto Nº 203** lifted the entry ban for the last **512** people on them. The timing was deliberate — registration for the plebiscite of **5 October** had already closed, so the people who had been kept out of Chile for years could finally come home but could not vote on whether the general who had exiled them would stay in power.

**Sources:** [corpus](../sources/corpus/1985-cartilla-retornado-3-prohibicion-ingreso/source.yaml); [citation card](../sources/cartilla-retornado-prohibicion-ingreso-1985.md).

## Helia

**Helia del Carmen Cerpa Pérez** was born in **Parral** in **1943** and died in **2025**. Between those dates she lived through **Allende**’s government, **detention** at the **Estadio Nacional**, **exile** through the Italian embassy in Santiago, resettlement in **Rome** and then in **London**, and many years of work for the tenants of the **Ossulston Estate** in **Camden**. The earlier plates in this scrapbook record the coup, the stadium, and the prohibition lists — they are one part of who she was. The last picture is the other part: **Helia outdoors with her family, a tree in white blossom behind her, smiling at the camera.**

For the longer biography see [Parral to Camden](helia-cerpa-perez-chile-exile-camden.md).
