# Parral to Camden: The Life of Helia Cerpa-Pérez

## Parral

Helia del Carmen Cerpa Pérez was born on 4 June 1943 in Parral, a small agricultural city in Chile's Maule region, founded in 1795 by Ambrosio O'Higgins. Parral's most famous son was Pablo Neruda, born there in 1904, whose poetry and politics would shape the landscape Helia grew up in. The Nobel laureate and the girl from the same provincial town breathed the same air of the Chilean left — a tradition rooted in rural poverty, copper and agriculture, and the conviction that the state existed to serve the poor.

Helia was the daughter of [Miguel Luis Cerpa González](../people/miguel-cerpa-gonzalez-i346.md) and [Laurencia del Carmen Pérez Escobar](../people/laurencia-perez-escobar-i347.md). Her brother [Miguel Antonio](../people/miguel-cerpa-perez-i352.md) was born five years later, in 1948. The family belonged to the social stratum — rural, educated enough to enter professional life, politically conscious — that Salvador Allende would draw into government a generation later.

Parral itself would become a place of darker significance. After the 1973 coup, the Dirección de Inteligencia Nacional (DINA) established its southern regional intelligence headquarters in the town, on calle Ignacio Carrera Pinto. The building operated as an interrogation and transfer hub between 1974 and 1977, feeding detainees to Santiago or to Colonia Dignidad, the German colony thirty-five kilometres away that the regime used as a torture and extrajudicial-killing facility. In September 2022, the Parral cuartel was declared a National Historic Monument at the petition of families of the disappeared.

## Allende's Chile

When Salvador Allende won the presidency on 4 September 1970 at the head of the Unidad Popular coalition, it was the first time a Marxist had been elected head of state in the Western Hemisphere. The administration drew tens of thousands of functionaries — social workers, educators, local administrators, party organisers — into government service at every level. Women played expanding roles: Mireya Baltra became the first female Minister of Labour in 1972; Allende's own daughter Beatriz was a key political adviser.

Helia worked within the Allende government. The specific nature of her position — her department, her level of government, whether she was based in Parral or elsewhere — has not yet been documented in the family archive. What is clear is that she belonged to the generation of Chileans who staked their careers and their safety on the Unidad Popular project: a democratic road to socialism, carried out through nationalisations, land reform, literacy campaigns, and the redistribution of wealth in one of Latin America's most unequal societies.

For three years, they believed it was working.

## The eleventh of September

On 11 September 1973, the Chilean armed forces surrounded the Palacio de la Moneda. By mid-morning, Hawker Hunter jets were bombing the presidential palace. Allende died inside — by his own hand or at the hands of soldiers, the debate would last decades. By evening, General Augusto Pinochet's military junta had dissolved Congress, banned all political parties and trade unions, and declared a state of siege.

Over the following days, stadiums, prisons, and torture chambers filled with tens of thousands of detainees. An estimated 40,000 people were murdered, tortured, or arbitrarily imprisoned during the seventeen-year dictatorship. Between 200,000 and two million Chileans were scattered into exile across the globe.

Helia was among the detained.

## The Estadio Nacional

The Estadio Nacional — Chile's national sports stadium in Ñuñoa, Santiago — was converted into the regime's largest mass detention centre. It operated from 12 September to 9 November 1973. An estimated 20,000 people passed through it; the International Red Cross counted 7,000 on a single day. Hooded informants circulated among prisoners to identify party members, who were taken away and often never seen again.

Helia appears as **CERPA PEREZ HELIA DEL C** on the official list of 405 women prisoners held at the stadium, maintained by the Corporación Estadio Nacional Memoria Nacional Ex Prisioneros Políticos and [published by *Cambio21*](https://cambio21.cl/cambio21/las-mujeres-vctimas-de-la-dictadura-prisioneras-en-el-estadio-nacional-conzca-en-exclusiva-los-nombres-de-405-detenidas-en-el-principal-coliseo-deportivo-en-1973-5c26692bcd49b0733e070387) on 31 December 2018.

Women were initially held in changing rooms within the coliseum. On 26 September 1973, all women were transferred to two changing rooms beside the swimming pool, at the corner of Pedro de Valdivia and Avenida Grecia. The camarín norte held Chilean women; the camarín sur held foreigners. Each room measured roughly 30 metres by 7. Women slept on mattresses pushed together in rows. Many were stripped of their belongings on arrival; many were subjected to sexual violence. The former DINA director Manuel Contreras cited 509 women detained; testimonies from survivors estimate approximately 1,200.

On 24 October 1973, a FIFA delegation led by secretary-general Helmut Kaser arrived to inspect the stadium for an upcoming World Cup qualifier. All prisoners were hidden from the inspectors. The stadium was cleared of detainees on 9 November — some released, others transferred to the Campo de Prisioneros Chacabuco, a converted saltpeter mine in the Atacama Desert that began receiving transfers the following day. Twelve days later, Chile lined up for the qualifier against the Soviet Union. The Soviets refused to play in a venue that had been used as a concentration camp. Chile won the tie by walkover.

Helia's own uncles were also caught in the repression. Three men sharing her parents' exact double surnames — Patricio Arnaldo Pérez Escobar, Luis Omar Pérez Escobar, and Hugo Amado Cerpa González — later appeared on the Valech Commission's official lists of victims of political imprisonment and torture. The camarín norte where Helia was held is now one of nine designated memory sites at the Estadio Nacional. Copper plaques bearing the names of detainees are installed on its walls.

## Embassy and exile

After the coup, Helia found initial refuge through the Italian diplomatic mission in Santiago. The Italian embassy sheltered approximately 700 people and became one of the most active diplomatic refuges in the capital. Chargé d'Affaires Piero De Masi, the senior Italian diplomat present, used personal funds to buy mattresses and managed conditions with over fifty people sometimes sharing a single room. Refugees inside the embassy organised themselves into commissions — cleaning, culture, education, security, cooking — coordinated by a political committee. Between 1973 and 1975, Italian diplomats facilitated safe-conduct passages for roughly 750 Chileans to resettle in Italy. Chile later honoured these diplomats for their role — compared in the press to "Schindler's List."

Rome became a significant hub for the Chilean exile community. The refugees were largely well-educated professionals who integrated successfully into Italian society. But Rome was also a base for the political renovation of the Chilean Socialist Party and for strategic planning toward Chile's eventual democratic transition. Italy's acceptance of Chilean refugees was itself a landmark — the country's first significant intake of non-European political refugees, a precedent that shaped Italian asylum policy for decades.

By mid-July 1974, Chile had issued 2,945 safe-conduct passes for asylum-seekers in embassies, plus 547 for family members. By August 1974, only nine people remained in any embassy in Santiago — all in the Italian one. If Helia passed through De Masi's embassy — and the Italian route is established family knowledge — she was almost certainly among the roughly 600 Chileans evacuated to Italy between late 1973 and mid-1974. The story of the embassy was told in Nanni Moretti's 2018 documentary *Santiago, Italia*, which interviews refugees now living in Italy and the diplomats who saved them. De Masi himself published a memoir — *Santiago. 1 febbraio 1973 – 27 gennaio 1974* (Bonanno Editore, 2013) — which documents his year at the embassy in personal detail.

Helia's exact route — whether she entered the embassy compound directly after release from the Estadio Nacional, or reached Italy via a third country — and the duration of her stay in Rome have not yet been documented.

## London

Helia subsequently moved to London, joining the roughly 3,000 Chilean refugees who settled in Britain between 1974 and 1979. The pathway was shaped by British party politics: the Conservative government under Edward Heath had quickly recognised Pinochet's regime and resisted Chilean asylum claims, but the Labour government that took power in March 1974 reversed course, with Foreign Secretary James Callaghan announcing that Chilean applications would be considered "sympathetically." Margaret Thatcher's election in 1979 effectively ended further admissions.

The Chilean exile community in London was supported by a web of solidarity organisations. The Chile Solidarity Campaign, founded in late 1973, had sixty local committees and thirty affiliated trade unions by 1976. The Joint Working Group for Refugees from Chile, established in 1974 and financed by the Home Office, operated until 1979. The World University Service administered scholarship grants for 900 exiles from offices on Seven Sisters Road, Tottenham.

Camden specifically had a visible Chilean presence. A solidarity mural painted alongside the railway line at King Henry's Road, Primrose Hill, read "Solidarity With the People of Chile" and became a local landmark. Chilean refugees lived in squats on Chalk Farm Road during the 1970s. Political and cultural events — folk music by Inti-Illimani, empanada sales, fundraising for the resistance — were the connective tissue of a diaspora that, at first, expected to return home within a few years. Most never did.

## Prohibición de ingreso

Twelve years after the coup, Helia was still formally barred from her own country. Her name — **Cerpa Pérez, Helia del Carmen** — appeared on page 12 of the official *Sexto listado* (6th edition, 15 July 1985) of Chileans prohibited from entering the national territory, issued by the Policía de Investigaciones and containing 4,360 names. The list was published by the Comité Pro-Retorno de Exiliados in their *Cartilla del Retornado* Nº 3 (November 1985), backed by the Vicaría de la Solidaridad — the Catholic Church's human-rights body. Immediately above Helia's entry on the page: **Cerpa Miranda, Carlos Alberto** — almost certainly a relative, given the surname's rarity.

The banned list had been compiled under three instruments of dictatorship law: Decreto Ley 81 (1973), Decreto Ley 604 (1974), and the 24th Transitory Provision of the 1980 Constitution. The OAS Inter-American Commission on Human Rights repeatedly declared the system a violation of Article VIII of the American Declaration — the right to residence and movement. The exiles on the list did not know what action, event, or hidden enemy had caused their inclusion.

Under international pressure, the list shrank: 3,878 by September 1985; 3,717 in 1986; 623 by June 1987. On 1 September 1988, Decree Nº 203 lifted the ban for the last 512 persons, officially ending the exile. The timing was deliberate: voter registration for the 5 October plebiscite had already closed. The exiles who had been barred for fifteen years could finally come home — but could not vote on whether the general who exiled them would stay in power. The "No" won anyway, 56 to 44.

## Ossulston

Helia settled on the Ossulston Estate in Somers Town, Camden — a Grade II listed modernist council estate built between 1927 and 1931 by the London County Council on Chalton Street, between Euston and King's Cross stations. The estate comprises Chamberlain House, Levita House, and Walker House — 310 flats designed by LCC chief architect G. Topham Forrest in a style influenced by Viennese social housing such as the Karl Marx-Hof.

She became a long-serving representative of the Ossulston Tenants and Residents Association and an active participant in Camden's District Management Committee system. Council records span at least twelve years: CamdenCEN Executive Board member from 2010 to 2012, representing front-line community organisations in the borough's partnership with the public sector; DMC representative to the Joint Monitoring Group; and in February 2014, she stood before the Camden Cabinet itself, delivering a deputation on the Better Homes Programme Consultancy Framework — speaking on behalf of tenants about a multi-million-pound investment programme that would shape the physical conditions for hundreds of households.

When she was nominated for the Tenants' Scrutiny Group in June 2013 and lost in a secret ballot, the DMC formally thanked "Helia Evans for her long-standing work as the DMC's representative to the JMG." It was the kind of quiet tribute that no newspaper notices but that measures a life in public service.

Her trajectory — from Allende government worker to political refugee to decades-long housing advocate on a London council estate — mirrors that of many Chilean exiles who, as the *Tribune* wrote in 2023, "contributed enormously to British society as teachers, engineers, human rights activists, local councillors, doctors, scientists and more."

## El piquete — Wentworth Estate, Surrey

On 16 October 1998, General Pinochet was arrested at a private clinic in London on a warrant from Spanish judge Baltasar Garzón, charged with responsibility for the deaths and torture of Spanish citizens during his regime. After back surgery, he was moved in December 1998 to **28 Lindale Close** — a house called "Twin Pines" on the Wentworth Estate, a gated luxury enclave at Virginia Water in Surrey, rented by the Chilean government for £10,000 a month. Armed police officers and dogs guarded the property. Inside, Pinochet read newspapers, searched for his own name on the internet, and organised his legal defence. His wife Lucía stood at the doorway encouraging counter-demonstrators through a megaphone.

Outside the gates of the Wentworth Estate, the Chilean exile community mounted *el piquete de Londres* — a sustained picket that lasted 503 days, from his arrest to his release in March 2000. Protesters displayed crosses bearing the names and photographs of the disappeared. They drummed and played tambourines. Chilean exiles travelled from across Britain and across Europe to stand at the gates of a Surrey golf-course estate and face the man who had destroyed their country and scattered their families.

The piquete remobilised solidarity networks that had been dormant since the end of the dictatorship in 1990. It was a formative moment for the second generation — the children of exiles, born in London or Rome or Stockholm, who had grown up hearing their parents' stories and now stood alongside them. Helia was among the protesters at Wentworth. Twenty-five years after fleeing Parral, she stood outside the gates of a mansion in the Surrey stockbroker belt, facing the general who had made her an exile.

## The Valech lists

In 2004, the Comisión Nacional sobre Prisión Política y Tortura — the Valech Commission — published the names of 28,459 recognised survivors of political imprisonment and torture during the dictatorship. A second commission in 2011 added thousands more.

Helia does not appear on either list, despite her documented detention at the Estadio Nacional. She never testified to either commission — a pattern common among women survivors, particularly those who experienced sexual violence at the stadium. But her family was also directly affected.

The Valech I list contains **Patricio Arnaldo Pérez Escobar** (entry 18,738). The Valech II list contains **Luis Omar Pérez Escobar** (RUN 6.495.935-2). Sharing both paternal and maternal surnames with Helia's mother [Laurencia Pérez Escobar](../people/laurencia-perez-escobar-i347.md) means they share both parents — Patricio Arnaldo and Luis Omar are almost certainly Laurencia's brothers, Helia's uncles. Both were victims of political imprisonment and torture.

The Valech II list also contains **Hugo Amado Cerpa González** (RUN 5.080.925-0). Same double surname as Helia's father [Miguel Cerpa González](../people/miguel-cerpa-gonzalez-i346.md) — making Hugo Amado almost certainly Miguel's brother, another uncle. A third victim.

The broader Cerpa and Pérez Escobar entries in the Valech lists run to more than a dozen names. The full case files are held by Chile's Instituto Nacional de Derechos Humanos, which has released over 15,982 individual files to victims and their families as of February 2026.

Helia died in 2025. She married [Joseph Evans](../people/joseph-evans-i18.md) in 1982 and was the mother of [Jacqueline Caroline Evans](../people/jacqueline-caroline-evans-i5.md). From Parral to Camden, from Allende to Ossulston, her life carried the full arc of the twentieth century's political displacements — and the stubborn civic impulse that made exile a beginning rather than an ending.
