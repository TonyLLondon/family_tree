# Addobbati: Venetian-Dalmatian civic family and the Habsburg postal service

Connected to the Zerauschek line via [Ester Addobbati](../people/ester-addobbati.md) × [Antonio Zerauschek](../people/antonio-zerauschek.md). **Archive:** Državni arhiv u Zadru (DAZD) — HR-DAZD-342 Obitelj Addobbati (1732–1930). **Narrative companion:** [Zara — Antonio Zerauschek](zerauschek-zadar.md). **Line hub:** [lines/zara-italy-dalmatia.md](../lines/zara-italy-dalmatia.md).

## Origins: Bergamo, 1495 to Dalmatia, 1733

The earliest trace is a name in a register: **Pietro de Adobatis**, Bergamo, **1495**. Two and a half centuries later the family still held their place — hereditary citizens of one of the great cities of the Venetian Terraferma, with a formal testimonial from the Bergamo magistracy documenting their lineage, their coat of arms, and their unbroken status as *cives originarios*. That document, compiled in 1745, would underwrite the family's standing for the next century.

Then three brothers crossed the Adriatic.

**Daniele, Lorenzo, and Giuseppe** — sons of Giovanni (Ivan) Addobbati — arrived in Zara as **Venetian cavalry officers**. On **13 November 1733** the Zara council of citizens admitted them to the hereditary *cittadini originari* class. They carried the Bergamo testimonial, military commissions, and legal credentials. They were exactly the kind of family the city's institutions were built to absorb — and they moved immediately into its networks, forming alliances with families like the Pasini, Marchi, Canova, Petricioli, and Scarpi.

---

## The *cittadini originari*: the professional backbone of the Republic

Venetian Dalmatia ran on two parallel systems. The patriciate — the dynasties of the *Libro d'Oro* — held legislative power. Everyone else who mattered belonged to the *cittadini originari*, inscribed in the *Libro d'Argento* since 1315: a hereditary class of lawyers, notaries, merchants, and administrators who wrote the laws, certified the contracts, kept the records, and ran the institutions.

The entry requirements were steep: three generations of legitimate birth, a livelihood from the liberal arts or commerce, and unblemished civic reputation. In return, *cittadini* held the most powerful posts below the patrician level — secretaries to the Senate, the *Cancelliere Grando*, provincial officers across the empire. Their values were *mediocritas* (careful judgment) and *onorevolezza* (public respectability). Trust accumulated across generations, expressed through institutional competence.

Zara's citizen council was the gate to this world — a hereditary body of wealthy merchants, notaries, and professionals. Admission meant civic privileges, social recognition, and access to the marriage market of the urban elite.

---

## Three generations of advancement: cavalry to the council of Nin

Each generation pushed further.

**Luigi (Lujo) Addobbati** (c. 1769–1824), a trained lawyer (*causidico*), petitioned the Venetian authorities in **1793** for his family's admission to the **council of Nin** — a small municipality in the Dalmatian hinterland whose noble council carried ancient standing. On **24 June 1804** his sons **Dr. Petar (Pietro)** and **Ivan Vicko** were formally inscribed as nobles of Nin, alongside Lujo's brothers Salustio and Don Giovanni, who had entered the council in 1793.

Petar had already collected a more unusual distinction. On **16 October 1786**, Bishop Ivan Dominik Stratico bestowed on him the papal title of **[Knight of the Golden Spur](../topics/order-golden-spur.md)** and the dignity of **lateran count** — the second-highest chivalric order of the Holy See, conferred through episcopal sub-collation on lawyers, physicians, and men of letters. By 1799 Petar was practising law in Zara. Papal knight, Nin noble, Zara lawyer — the family had never stood higher.

The honour was still remembered within the family two centuries later. Fulvia's 1996 memoir records that Petar received the Golden Spur while still a student at the University of Padova, and that only a hundred knights held the title at any one time. She had written to the Vatican seeking details but received no reply. The miniature of the order itself was passed down through the family and eventually given to Peter Martin Lewis. ([Fulvia memoir](../sources/famhist-nonna-memoir-1996.md).)

Then the ground shifted. The **1817 Granić list** records four Addobbati among the Nin nobility: Dr. Petar and Ivan Vicko (sons of Lujo), and Petar and Josip (sons of Ivan Vicko). The list was part of a petition from 52 Nin families to the Habsburg Heraldic Commission, marshalling genealogies, the 1745 Bergamo testimonial, papal honours, and council records into a dossier for Austrian recognition. The commission ruled that Nin council membership did not constitute nobility under Austrian law — a ruling applied uniformly across Dalmatian communal nobility, not specifically aimed at the Addobbati. Their civic standing within Zara continued undisturbed.

## The French period and continuing civic role

A family tradition, recorded in Fulvia's 1996 memoir, holds that an Addobbati ancestor travelled with a **delegation to Milan to beg Napoleon not to give Zara to Austria** — and failed. His wife went with him and, by Fulvia's account, was beautiful, witty, and intelligent enough to catch Napoleon's eye. The older Addobbati aunts used to giggle about the family being "Napoleon's cousins." Fulvia never established whether this meant a real liaison or was simply a joke about the wife's social triumph at court. ([Fulvia memoir](../sources/famhist-nonna-memoir-1996.md).)

Napoleon abolished the communal noble councils in 1806. The Addobbati barely noticed.

The census that year shows **six** family members simultaneously active in the Zara citizen council: Anđelo, Ante, Dominik, Petar, Salustio, and Vicko. The French Illyrian administration (**1806–1813**) reorganized everything above them. Beneath the surface, the infrastructure of city life — the notaries, the lawyers, the men who certified contracts and recorded wills — remained the same people.

**Angelo Daniele Addobbati** (c. 1760s–1831) practised as a **public notary** in Zara for thirty years, his register running from **1806 to 1831** and preserved in the DAZD Notaries series (HR-DAZD-31). A 1814 land register in the Petricioli archive carries his certification. The Filippi fonds reference documents involving Pietro Addobbati. A court case with the Caligarich family in 1801 shows the family embedded in property law years before the French even arrived. Regimes changed; the Addobbati kept notarizing.

---

## Habsburg administration: the Addobbati in imperial service

Dalmatia passed to the Habsburg Empire in 1814. The new administration needed precisely what the Addobbati had always been: educated, locally rooted, fluent in the city's legal and commercial traditions. Several branches entered imperial service.

By **1909**, three Addobbati held Habsburg posts simultaneously:

| Name | Post |
|------|------|
| **Marino Addobbati** | *Cancellista giudiziario* — judicial clerk |
| **[Pietro Pio Addobbati](../people/pietro-pio-addobbati.md)** | *Ufficiale postale superiore* — senior postal officer |
| **Vincenzo Addobbati** | *Ispettore del catasto* — cadastre inspector |

All three appeared on the **1909 Italian protest list** — over 500 Italian-speaking Dalmatian officials who signed a public objection to the introduction of Croatian in the provincial administration. The list, published in the Società Dalmata di Storia Patria volume, is a snapshot of a community fighting for its bureaucratic language — and the Addobbati were among its signatories.

[Pietro Pio](../people/pietro-pio-addobbati.md) (1852–1919) had built his career inside the Habsburg postal system, reaching the senior officer grade — a position requiring university or secondary education, carrying real administrative responsibility: staff, operations, and mail coordination across the province. His relatives covered the judiciary and land registry. Law, communications, and property — three pillars of provincial government, staffed by one family.

## Marriage at the centre of Dalmatian civic life

The Addobbati married the way they governed: strategically and within the network.

Pietro Pio married [Ottilia Anna Vincenza Boara](../people/ottilia-anna-vincenza-boara.md) in **1890**, joining two of Zara's most established civic families. The broader web — [Boara](../people/giovanni-battista-boara.md), [Carobbi](../people/casimiro-carobbi.md), Coltelli, Sandri — formed the interlocking core of Venetian-Dalmatian urban life across generations.

Their daughter [Ester Addobbati](../people/ester-addobbati.md) married [Antonio Zerauschek](../people/antonio-zerauschek.md), the interwar entrepreneur who built the Hotel Zerauschek and Caffè Centrale on Široka ulica — the signature private development of 1930s Zara.

### "Un vero Addobbati"

[Pietro Pio](../people/pietro-pio-addobbati.md) had originally been **destined for the church** and was placed in the Seminary in Zara. He was expelled after placing a rat in the dish of a visiting Bishop — "considered unsuitable and luckily released," as Fulvia put it. He became a civil servant, but the family was without money and his salary did not stretch. His wife Ottilia borrowed monthly from her Boara relations — Zio Casimiro and Zia Carmela — returning the money via one of the daughters. Casimiro always waved it off.

Despite their financial difficulties, the Addobbati worked in ways that defied the gentry conventions of the period. Pietro Pio was a secretary in a lawyer's firm. His children continued the pattern: the eldest son became a doctor (qualified in Vienna, died in Trieste aged ~30); Noemi was an employee of the Poste & Telegraphs; Else a piano teacher; Irma and "Bill" were teachers; Villosie served 25 years as Antonio Zerauschek's secretary before becoming a nun at 50, rising to Sister Superior at the Convent of the Dame Inglesi in Rovereto; Bice married and settled in Rome. The expression *un vero Addobbati* was used as a compliment in Zara. ([Fulvia memoir, 1996](../sources/famhist-nonna-memoir-1996.md).)

### The Boara inheritance

Ottilia's family, the **Boara**, were landowners and intellectuals. Her parents [Simeone Boara](../people/simeone-vincenzo-boara.md) and [Vittoria Carobbi](../people/vittoria-elisa-carobbi.md) had eight children. When one daughter, Eveline, married a Croat, the resulting family scandal led to a will that left all money to the men of the family and none to the women. None of the Boara men married; three left their inheritances to the **Lega**, an Italian association opposed to Austria. Casimiro, famous for his lectures on the classics, lived with his sister Carmela in what Fulvia remembered as absolute comfort — "the only people I ever heard of that sat down to a menu lunch and dinner every day." The last piece of Boara land was sold by Casimiro to Fulvia's father (Antonio Zerauschek). When Carmela died — the last of the Boara — she altered her will to leave something to the Addobbati who had assisted her. ([Fulvia memoir, 1996](../sources/famhist-nonna-memoir-1996.md).)

Two hundred years after three cavalry officers crossed the Adriatic, the Addobbati name was still shaping the city.

## Archive and records

The Državni arhiv u Zadru (DAZD) holds remarkable documentation for this family:

- **Addobbati family fonds (HR-DAZD-342)** — 1732–1930; includes the 1745 Bergamo testimonial, the 1786 Golden Spur diploma, genealogical notes, deeds, testaments, and the correspondence behind the 1817 Nin petition. Inventory: [corpus/dazd-addobbati-family-fonds/](../sources/corpus/dazd-addobbati-family-fonds/)
- **Notaries of Zara (HR-DAZD-31)** — Angelo Daniele Addobbati's notarial acts 1806–1831. Inventory: [corpus/dazd-notaries-zadar/](../sources/corpus/dazd-notaries-zadar/)
- **Petricioli (HR-DAZD-497) and Filippi (HR-DAZD-355) fonds** — property registers and legal instruments bearing Addobbati involvement. Analytical inventories: [dazd-hr-dazd-497-petricioli-family](../sources/corpus/dazd-hr-dazd-497-petricioli-family/) · [dazd-hr-dazd-355-filippi-family](../sources/corpus/dazd-hr-dazd-355-filippi-family/)
- **Parish registers** — St. Anastasia (Sveta Stošija) and other Zara churches, baptisms/marriages/deaths from 1730s; Diocesan Archive and DAZD; 2,400+ volumes digitised. FamilySearch microfilms from the 1980s.
- **Nin Libri Consiliorum** — minutes including the 1804 Addobbati admission
