## 1. A Baltic childhood (1880)

Étienne Marc Theodore Stump was born on 7 March 1880 in Reval — the old Hanseatic port that the Russians called Tallinn and the Germans called Reval, capital of the Governorate of Estonia in the Russian Empire. His father, [Marc François Stump](../people/marc-francois-stump.md), was a Swiss-born French teacher at the Tallinn government gymnasium who had settled in the Baltics in the 1860s. His mother, [Olga Caroline Erbe](../people/olga-caroline-erbe.md), came from a prominent Baltic German family with deep roots in the region's professional class. He was baptised on 6 April 1880 at the Tallinn Dome Church; his godparents included members of the von Landesen family and his grandmother Marie Erbe née von Landesen.

Étienne grew up between two worlds. The Stump line traced back through generations of Reformed Protestants in the Swiss canton of Thurgau — [a story told separately](stump-thurgau-tallinn-baltic-line.md). The Erbe line was Baltic German, Lutheran, rooted in Tallinn's merchant and professional networks. Marc taught French; Olga kept house on Tatari and later Mere streets, where the family rented rooms to a baker and a pharmacist. It was a comfortable, multilingual, middle-class life in a cosmopolitan corner of the Russian Empire — French at home, German at school, Russian on the street.

Marc died on 3 April 1903 in Yverdon, Switzerland, far from the Baltic. By then Étienne was already gone — across the Atlantic.

## 2. The German-American Dental College (1900–1902)

How Étienne ended up studying dentistry in Chicago is not recorded. What survives is the wreckage. He enrolled at the **German-American Dental College**, a small institution founded in 1891 as the "North American-German Dental College" at 569 Wells Street, near Lincoln Park. The school was run almost single-handedly by its dean, **Dr. Huxmann**, and catered to the city's large German-speaking population.

Étienne graduated and, on 30 April 1902, presented his diploma to the **Illinois State Board of Dental Examiners**. The Board refused to issue him a license. The German-American Dental College, they ruled, was not an institution of sufficient standing and repute.

Stump fought back. He petitioned the Cook County courts for a writ of mandamus to compel the Board to license him. On 9 August 1902 **Judge Chetlain** dismissed the petition and delivered a withering judgment. Huxmann, the judge found, had kept no matriculation books, held no faculty meetings, promised students speedy graduation, and furnished examination answers in advance. Worse, Huxmann had sat on the Board itself for part of the preceding decade — translating the very exam papers his own students sat — a conflict the judge called "at least illegal and unjust."

The *Chicago Tribune* printed the ruling on 10 August 1902. The *Dental Register* reprinted it that November. A [photograph from the Henderson family album](../media/albums/henderson/Etienne%20-%20Chicago%201902%20(1).jpg) shows Étienne in Chicago that same year — a young man with a diploma nobody would honour.

## 3. Hired in New York (c. 1903–1904)

The license refusal closed America to Étienne. What happened next comes from a single chain of Persian-language sources — Dr. Najm-Abadi's *Tarikh-e tebb dar Iran* (1974), cited by Dr. Mohsen Sayyah and republished by [Beytoote](../sources/corpus/beytoote-stump-dentist-atabak-ahmadshah/translation.en.md).

In 1904, **Mirza Ali-Asghar Khan**, the powerful Qajar grand vizier known as **Atabak** or **Amin al-Sultan**, was dismissed from the premiership and embarked on a long journey around the world — through Russia, China, Japan, Mecca, and Europe. According to Najm-Abadi, the tour also passed through the United States, and Atabak was accompanied by **Mehdi-Qoli Hedayat** (Mokhber al-Saltaneh). In **New York**, they hired a young Swiss dentist as Atabak's personal practitioner. That dentist was Étienne Stump.

It was a remarkable stroke of fortune. A man who could not get licensed in Illinois found, instead, the personal patronage of one of the most powerful figures in the Persian Empire. By late 1904, Étienne had left the New World for the old one — and arrived in Tehran.

## 4. Dentist to the Shah (1904–1951)

Étienne set up practice in Tehran at the corner of **Manuchehri and Ferdowsi streets**. Atabak was assassinated in 1907, but Stump stayed. He became **Ahmad Shah's court dentist** — the young king's personal practitioner — and was appointed the **first teacher of dentistry** at the **Dar al-Funun**, Iran's first modern university. The British physician [Cyril Elgood](../sources/wishlist/stump.md) recorded both facts in his *Medical History of Persia* (1951).

By the 1920s, according to Dr. Sayyah, Stump was one of only **three qualified dentists in all of Tehran**. His clinic near the end of Lalezar Street was always packed. His European assistant, **Monsieur Gol**, sold imported porcelain denture sets at thirty-five tomans apiece — a significant sum. After Stump's death, Gol ran the practice.

Stump published an article on dental hygiene in *Nasvan* magazine in 1922 and was elected a **Fellow of the International College of Dentists** in 1929 — among the earliest Fellows, just two years after the ICD's founding in Chicago. Persian-language sources describe him among the **second generation of foreign-trained dentists** in Iran, alongside **Hartioun Stepanian** (Armenian, American-trained) and **Falak Kush Melczarski** (Polish, Reza Shah's personal dentist). The rivalry with Melczarski was bitter: it kept Stump off examining boards and blocked his joint dental-school proposal with Stepanian — submitted to the Ministry of Education in 1916, years before Melczarski's school opened.

In August 1915, during the First World War, Russian soldiers arrested Stump alongside the Ottoman ambassador and the Austrian military attaché at Vardavard on the Karaj–Tehran road. He was sent to Qazvin but quickly released — his Swiss nationality, not Austrian, secured his freedom.

Simin Daneshvar's novel *Savushun* — the best-selling Persian novel of the twentieth century — uses the idiom of getting "a proper set of teeth from Dr. Eshtamp" as a byword for quality. Stump had become a household name.

## 5. Henriette and the family (1909–c. 1940s)

In **1909**, Étienne married **[Henriette Louise Fernande Bottin](../people/henriette-bottin.md)** in Tehran. Henriette was the daughter of **[Julien Bottin](../people/julien-bottin.md)** and **[Fanny Burgess](../people/fanny-burgess.md)** — herself part of the extraordinary [Burgess family of Tehran](stories/saginian-burgess-bottin-stump.md), English residents of Persia since the mid-nineteenth century. Through Henriette, Étienne married into one of Tehran's oldest European families.

They had five children: **[Robert Marc Murard](../people/robert-marc-murard-stump.md)** (1910), **Theo** (1911), **Irene** ("Loulou", 1917), **Jacques**, and **Jean** (1929). The [Henderson family tree](../sources/henderson-burgess-stump-family-tree.md) records the full genealogy.

Photographs from the period show a prosperous European family in Tehran — Étienne in a dark suit and moustache, Henriette in Edwardian white, children lined up in a garden. One 1909 album page preserves five portraits from their early married life.

## 6. The Mahmoudieh estate

By the 1930s and 1940s, Stump was very wealthy. He owned a large garden between **Fisherabad and Iranshahr streets** known by his name, a **brewery at Majidieh** in Shemiran, and reportedly one of the first automobiles in Iran — a claim repeated in a Majles debate, where an MP stated that in 1912 only two cars existed in Iran: the Shah's and Stump's.

His most famous acquisition was the **[Mahmoudieh estate](../topics/mahmoudieh-house.md)** — a grand property between Tajrish and Evin in north Tehran. The land had passed through a chain of Qajar grandees: from Muhammad Shah's *Muhammadieh* through Ala-ud-Dowleh, Mahmoud Alamir (Ehtesham al-Saltana), and the financier Amin al-Zarb before reaching Stump. [Abdullah Mustawfi's memoirs](../sources/mustawfi-mahmoudieh-stump.md) record both the dental practice and the estate, calling Stump simply "the German dentist" — one of the many misattributions of his nationality.

Dr. Sayyah described the villa as magnificent, filled with antiques and precious carpets. It was a European enclave in the foothills north of Tehran, surrounded by gardens and approached by tree-lined avenues.

## 7. The guesthouse and Vera (1920s–1951)

In the 1920s, a Russian émigré named **[Vera Obolensky](../people/vera-obolensky.md)** arrived in Tehran. Born Nemtchinova, previously married to Prince Yuri Obolensky, Vera was part of the wave of White Russian refugees who washed through Persia after the Revolution. Stump hired her to manage a large **European-style guesthouse** on the Mahmoudieh estate — forty or more rooms, catering to European business travellers and diplomats passing through.

Georges Obolensky, Vera's son, recalled the arrangement in his memoir [*Forever Russian*](../sources/forever-russian-stump-extract.md) (2015). He grew up in the guesthouse, attending a French school run by Mademoiselle Tomassin. His mother was always busy with her duties. "My mother was always busy with her duties at the bed and breakfast, which didn't leave much time for me," he wrote. "She usually spent it with her friend, Dr Stump, a Swiss dentist practising in Teheran. He was the only dentist in Teheran at that time. He was also the owner of the B and B my mother managed and expanded."

The relationship was "more than just a friendship." After **Henriette's death** (date unknown), Vera became Étienne's partner and eventually his wife. The guesthouse itself was effectively one of Tehran's first foreign-run hotels — a meeting point for Europeans in the capital.

## 8. Death and legacy (1951)

Étienne Stump died on **1 November 1951** in Tehran. He was buried in the **[Tehran Protestant Cemetery](../topics/tehran-protestant-cemetery.md)**, under a stone that reads "ICI REPOSE" — here rests. The grave marker gives his age as 71, consistent with the 1880 birth year from the Tallinn parish records, though the Beytoote article, citing Najm-Abadi, claims he died aged 83 — implying a birth year of c. 1868. The discrepancy remains unresolved.

After his death, Vera left Iran for Frankfurt and then New Jersey, where she died on 6 November 1974. The *New York Times* ran a death notice for "Princess Obolensky."

The Mahmoudieh estate was subdivided by Stump's heirs into residential lots, creating the modern **Mahmoudieh neighbourhood** — one of Tehran's wealthiest districts. The southwestern corner of the old estate became the site of the **Royal Tehran Hilton** in 1962, now the **Hotel Esteghlal**.

Dr. Gol continued the dental practice. The name "Eshtamp" lived on in Iranian popular memory, fixed there by Daneshvar's novel.

The arc of Étienne Stump's life — from a French teacher's son in Russian Tallinn, through a diploma-mill scandal in Chicago, to the court of the Shah of Persia — is one of improbable reinvention. He arrived in Tehran with a diploma no American state would recognise, and left it as a household name, a Fellow of an international college, and the owner of an estate that became a neighbourhood.
