# The Most All-Loved Person

## Into the East

In the autumn of 1830, a twenty-year-old Englishman named Edward Burgess left home forever. He travelled with his elder brother Charles — merchant, King's Messenger, master of eastern trade routes — through Constantinople and the Black Sea to Trebizond, where plague had killed three thousand that year, and then overland through the freezing mountains to Erzeroum, where the Russian army had just displaced fifty thousand Armenians. In their lodgings, three one-foot windows let in the cold through oiled paper instead of glass, and they kept pistols at hand on the carpet.

By January 1831 Edward arrived in Tabriz, the commercial capital of Persian Azerbaijan. He was to help manage Charles's trading enterprise. He would never see England again.

## The Merchant's Life

Edward settled into the rhythms of a young European in Qajar Persia. He dined three times a week with Dr Cormick, the physician attached to the British Embassy, and in the evenings they played flute duets. He went shooting with officers from the Russian mission, dined with the ambassador — a former Napoleonic soldier, now a Russian count — and taught himself Turkish and Persian. He described the country between Tabriz and Tehran to his parents as *"rocky mountains and barren plains"*, but found beauty in mountain valleys where trout streams ran through meadows of buttercups reaching to his knees.

He was sharp-eyed about the business. Cotton prints that matched the Persian taste could yield sixty to a hundred per cent profit, but Charles had shipped goods chosen *"without any regard as to whether these were suited to the Persian taste or not, when he ought to recollect that when you give even a Persian muleteer a coat of cloth, if it does not happen to be of the right colour you can hardly hire him to wear it."*

Edward sent rare Persian partridges to the London Zoological Society with a naturalist's letter, went hog-hunting with the Kurds, watched hawking with Prince Bahman Mirza's goshawks, and kept tumbler pigeons so tame they could be carried about in his pocket.

## Betrayed

Then the trap closed. Charles's commercial ventures failed, and he returned to England in 1836 — leaving Edward behind as a de facto hostage for debts owed to Persian creditors and the government. The sum owed for a consignment of muskets alone hung over Edward for years.

*"I am made responsible for his debts in this country and am nailed down to Tabreez,"* Edward wrote to his brother George. He could not leave without settling with creditors, and Charles sent neither money nor letters to the Shah that might ease his situation. From Isfahan in 1840, Edward's frustration boiled over: *"One angry word from the Shah or his minister and I have the prospect of a prison before me. A few lines from Charles to either H.M. or his minister would save me from this and this small favor he denies me."*

Charles spent the next fifteen years wandering from one European watering-place to another, trying to recover his health. He tried his hand at various ventures, all apparently brought to an end by illness. Edward — trapped, abandoned, indebted — had to build a new life from the ruins of his brother's reputation.

## The Village Governor

Edward reinvented himself through linguistic talent, personal integrity, and a gift for command. In 1842, when consul Bonham left for England, Edward was entrusted with the British consular business in Tabriz. By 1844, Prince Bahman Mirza had made him chief magistrate of two villages with about a thousand inhabitants — and his newspaper translator at four hundred tomauns a year.

He governed with a mixture of firmness and wit that the Persians respected. When villagers were robbed on the highway, he tracked down the thieves and had them given a hundred blows on the soles of the feet. When a villager came to demand he find a stolen kettle, Edward did his best. When the local lieutenant went cattle-rustling across the Kurdish border, Edward punished the offence but privately admired the man's dash.

A village elder told him: *"You speak up, you speak manfully and you speak well, and you would fight with any nobleman in the land rather than not see your people get justice."*

Nestorian Christian villages, oppressed by their Afshar chiefs, sent deputies begging to be placed under his governance. The American missionaries urged him to accept, saying *"by doing so I shall deserve the blessings of all Christendom for protecting a number of suffering Christians."*

## A New Shah

In 1848, Mohammed Shah died and the young Nasir-i-Din ascended the throne. Edward seized the moment. He *"sold everything he could lay his hands on and joined the triumphal march from Tabriz to Tehran"* — arriving with all his possessions on four mules, one lightly loaded because a servant rode atop it.

The new government recognised what the old had not: Edward's knowledge of Persian, Turkish, the country's customs, its revenue systems, and European affairs was unmatched among Europeans in Persia. He was appointed chief interpreter. Then, in 1851, Amir Kabir made him editor of the state gazette — the *Ruzname-ye Vaqaye-e Ettefaqiye* — Iran's first newspaper, at a salary of four hundred tomauns a year.

The role was larger than it sounds. Edward translated extracts from European newspapers arriving via Istanbul, India, and the European post. He decided what could be published and what could not — Iranian press historians recognise him as the country's **first press censor**. Lady Mary Sheil, wife of the British Minister, watched it all from Tehran and wrote that Edward *"enjoyed no sinecure; besides the above Gazette for the public, he had the superintendence of another newspaper, designed only for the eye of the Shah and his minister."* This **confidential intelligence bulletin** contained all the European political news deemed too sensitive for the Persian public, laced with gossip and scandal to keep the Shah entertained. Edward was, in effect, Iran's first intelligence briefer — selecting, translating, and filtering the world's news into two channels: one for the nation, one for the throne.

He managed the printer Hadji Abdul Mahomed with direct threats when deadlines slipped and diplomatic caution when translating foreign news, knowing that England, Russia, or Turkey *"might complain, give trouble to the government and get me into a mess."*

## The Georgian Lady

In the autumn of 1851, after twenty years alone in Persia, Edward married. He described his wife to George with characteristic directness: *"a Georgian lady of good family … pretty and about thirty three years old."* She was Anna Saginian, daughter of Daoud Khan, a Georgian-born commander in the Qajar army.

Anna spoke Armenian and Turkish, wrote Armenian rapidly, kept the house in excellent order, and proved a fine horsewoman — Edward took her cross-country to Cheshma Ali and *"she jumped beautifully."* A son, John, was born around 1852. His godfather was Ronald Thompson of the British mission. John died at eighteen months. *"My Edward mourned him as if he had been older,"* Anna said, *"for he had hoped to send him to England, to give him to his dear father to put to school."*

On 9 June 1854, their daughter Fanny was born. Edward's health was already failing — disease had settled in his spine and limbs.

## The Last Journey

In February 1855, Edward petitioned for leave to return to England. The Prime Minister agreed verbally. Edward wrote what would be his last letter to his father Henry — a letter so emotional it still cuts through a century and a half: *"How shall I describe to you the emotions this letter inspired in me, thankfulness to God for having given me so good and kind a father and gratitude to you were my predominant feelings."*

Henry had promised to care for Anna and infant Fanny should Edward die. Edward called this *"most kind, affectionate and fatherly."*

They let their house, sold what they could, and used some of the money to provide for Charles's children. Then Anna, Edward, and baby Fanny set out from Tehran by takhteravan — a mule-borne litter.

The journey took nearly three months. Anna walked beside him or rode, with a servant carrying Fanny on another mule. *"Often we were obliged to stay at those very dreadful Mahommedan villages, lest he should die with the hard travel: for the disease had settled in his spine and limbs, and often movement gave him such agony that he looked as dead."*

Eight days from Tabriz, Anna's father Daoud Khan, her two uncles, and Dr Cormick came out to meet them. They reached Tabriz and laid Edward in his own house. He lived for six weeks more.

About a week before he died, Edward wrote a long last letter to Henry and told Mr Abbott: *"You will find in that large book a letter I have written; when I am dead, take it out, and send it straightway to my father."* He asked Anna to take Fanny to England and *"be sure she is put to learn the English so soon as she can speak."*

*"When Fanny was three days past one year old, he died. We had been married just five years."*

## A Stone in Tabriz

Edward was buried in the little green Armenian cemetery just outside the walls of Tabriz, close by the grave of Anna's uncle.

Many years later, Fanny returned to Tabriz. She wished to put a larger and better stone on her father's grave. The inscription was made double — both in English and in Armenian. Anna's father lay only four or five yards away, among the graves of all her relations. And old Mr and Mrs Cormick and the other Europeans were all buried there.

Anna, interviewed in Leicester in March 1880, twenty-five years after Edward's death, said simply: *"He was the most all-loved person."*
