## 1. Qajar military origins (early 19th century)

In early 1811, two brothers deserted the Russian army.

[Daoud Khan Saginian](../people/daoud-khan-saginian.md) and his brother **Zaal** — Georgian officers born under the surname **Saginskilli** (also transliterated *Saginean*; related to the noble Saginashvili clan) — faced a choice: forced relocation and religious conversion under the empire that had absorbed their homeland, or flight. They chose flight. With their companion Solayman Khan Saham al-Dowleh they crossed south through Yerevan, Akhalkalaki, and Gyumri, reaching **Tabriz** and the army of Crown Prince **Abbas Mirza**. They never went back.

Daoud Khan rose fast. He served under Fath-Ali Shah Qajar and his sons, commanded troops under **Sayf ol-Dowleh**, and led a division during the **1834 Isfahan conflict**. Armenian military literature calls him "head of the Isfahan army." Over the following decades he integrated fully into the Armenian community in Iran. The surname shifted to the Armenian form **Saginian**. He married an Armenian woman. His daughters, Anna and Tamar, were raised Armenian. Georgia became an origin story, not an identity.

A British missionary, Dr. Joseph Wolff, met him in Tabriz in 1843 and called him "a genuine Georgian… Colonel in the Russian service." Four pages later, at departure, Wolff wrote "Daoud Khan the Armenian" — both entries in [*Narrative of a Mission to Bokhara*](../sources/narrative-mission-bokhara.md) (1845), Ch. XXVI. The scholarly account of the 1811 flight appears in Maeda (2019), *The Persianate World*, Brill, pp. 189–195.

## 2. The two sisters: court and diplomatic circles (1830s–1850s)

Daoud Khan's two daughters married into the foreign circles that orbited the Qajar court — and in doing so anchored the family at the intersection of Persian power and European influence for the next century.

[Anna Saginian](../people/anna-saginian.md) married [Edward Burgess](../people/edward-burgess.md) (1810–1855), a British merchant and translator who had arrived in Persia in 1831 and never left. By 1844 Burgess was magistrate near Tabriz and translator for Prince Bahman Mirza, moving in royal and intellectual circles. They married in **1851**. He died in **1855**, on the road between Tehran and Tabriz, leaving behind his wife and their infant daughter Fanny.

Anna's sister [Tamar Saginian](../people/tamar-saginian.md) married [Dr. William Cormick](../people/william-cormick.md) (1822–1877), an Irish-Armenian physician born in Tabriz. Cormick served as personal physician first to Mohammad Shah Qajar and then to Crown Prince Naser al-Din Shah — direct access to the innermost circle of the monarchy. He was part of the generation that began modernizing Persian medicine from inside the court itself.

Between them, the two Saginian sisters connected the family to British commercial networks, the diplomatic community, and the royal medical establishment — all within a single generation.

## 3. Second generation: Fanny Burgess → Bottin (late Qajar)

[Fanny Burgess](../people/fanny-burgess.md) was born in Tehran on 9 June **1854**. Her father died the following year. Anna took the infant to London to seek support from Edward's father, Henry Burgess — but he could not provide it, and mother and daughter returned to Persia. Fanny spent part of her childhood in Urmia, learning English from American missionaries (1862–65), and part in Tabriz, living with her aunt Tamar and uncle Dr. Cormick. Tehran was home.

In 1880 Fanny and Anna travelled to England, visiting Burgess relatives in London and Bedford. Anna's interview was recorded in Leicester in March. The following year Fanny met cousins in Dundee, and on 4 July 1881 left Bedford for Persia. She arrived back in Tehran on 29 June 1882.

On **19 April 1884** she married [Julien Bottin](../people/julien-bottin.md), a French mechanical engineer who had come to work for the Persian state. Bottin's [government contract](../sources/bottin-government-contract.md) — preserved in the original French, with the ministry seal — appointed him *Ingénieur mécanicien de l'Arsenal et des manufactures militaires*: chief engineer of the arsenal and military factories. Two-year term, 2,400 tomans salary, daily attendance at the arsenal, powder factory, and cartridge works, with responsibility for training thirty students in applied mechanics. ([Contract scan](../media/albums/henderson/Bottin%20Contract.pdf) · [transcription](../sources/corpus/bottin-contract/transcription-translation.en.md))

Bottin was deeply wired into Tehran's French community. He sat on the **Alliance Française** committee alongside Dr. Feuvrier and Jean-Baptiste Lemaire. In **1898** he joined the Lodge **Orient de Tehran** with Entezam al-Saltaneh. On **29 November 1906** he attended the founding meeting of what became the **Reveil de l'Iran** (Bidari-ye Iran), the first Masonic lodge formally affiliated with the Grand Orient de France — [recognized in November 1907](../sources/onnerfors-freemasonry-fraternalism-middle-east.md) (pp. 130–135 · [corpus extract](../sources/corpus/onnerfors-freemasonry-fraternalism-middle-east/)). A French embassy brochure records that Ambassador de Balloy was assisted by "un ingénieur français, en poste à la direction de l'arsenal de Téhéran" when building the French Residence in 1894–1896 — [almost certainly Bottin](../sources/ambassade-france-iran-residence.md).

Five children followed, all born in Tehran: Henriette (1888), Alice (1889), Edward (1891), René (1892), George (1895). The family spent 1901–1903 in England, then moved to France — first Melun, then Paris — for the children's education. Julien found the house but returned to Tehran; the marriage eventually ended in divorce. By 1919 Fanny was living alone in Tehran. Her sons had served in the First World War and stayed in France. Her last known letter was sent from Tehran in 1929.

She died on **6 November 1938**, aged 84, and was buried in the Tehran Protestant Cemetery. She had lived in the city for most of her eighty-four years.

## 4. Third generation: Henriette Bottin → Stump (Pahlavi transition)

[Henriette Bottin](../people/henriette-bottin.md), the eldest daughter, married [Étienne Stump](../people/etienne-stump.md) — a Swiss dentist whose own background was as improbable as the family he married into.

Étienne was born in **Tallinn** on **7 March 1880**, the son of [Marc François Stump](../people/marc-francois-stump.md), a Swiss-born French teacher who had spent his career as *Oberlehrer* at the Tallinn government gymnasium, and [Olga Caroline Erbe](../people/olga-caroline-erbe.md), a Baltic German woman born in St. Petersburg in 1844. Marc had arrived from Thurgau, sat his Tartu University exams in 1865, married Olga on 22 June 1868, and built a life in the Russian Baltic provinces. Olga died in Tallinn in 1894. ([Stump report](../sources/estonian-biographical-center-stump-report-2005.md) · [extract](../sources/corpus/estonian-biographical-center-stump-family-resear-856c90185e/extracted.pdf.md) · [Baltic line narrative](stump-thurgau-tallinn-baltic-line.md))

Étienne left the Baltic for Iran around 1904 and stayed for the rest of his life. By 1910, a Tallinn obituary for his mother Louise already noted "Dr Etienne Stump and wife in Teheran." He was among the first modern dentists in Iran, training Persian practitioners alongside a Polish colleague named Melczarski. The [*Encyclopaedia Iranica*](../sources/iranica-dentistry.md) calls him Austrian — he was Swiss — but gets the significance right: Stump helped build modern dental practice in Iran from scratch.

He died in **1951** and was buried in the Tehran Protestant Cemetery, a few rows from Fanny. The family's presence in Tehran had by then spanned nearly a century.

## Big picture

Four generations, each at a different pressure point of Iranian modernization:

| Generation | Role | Figures |
|------------|------|---------|
| 1 — Qajar military | Army command | Daoud Khan |
| 2 — Court & diplomacy | Translation, administration, royal medicine | Anna + Edward; Tamar + Cormick |
| 3 — Industry | State engineering, military manufactures | Fanny + Julien |
| 4 — Science / medicine | Modern dentistry | Henriette + Étienne |

## Closing note

Across roughly 120 years, one family moved from the Georgian-Armenian officer class of the Qajar army into royal administration and medicine, then into state engineering and military manufacturing, then into scientific practice under the Pahlavis — an unbroken chain at the centre of Iran's modernization.
