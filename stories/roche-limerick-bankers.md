# The Bankers of George's Street

## From Castletown-Roche to Limerick

The Roches arrived in Ireland with the Normans in 1169 — one of the first families ashore. They became lords of Fermoy in Cork, adopted Irish customs, married into Gaelic dynasties, and were Catholic when that cost them everything. After the Williamite confiscations stripped the senior branches of their estates, Stephen "Dov" Roche — known as "the Black" for his complexion — was forced to leave County Cork entirely. He retired first to Kilrush in Clare, then to Pallas in County Limerick, near his brother-in-law. He married Anastasia Lysaght, co-heir of the Lysaght estates, and from this union came a son who would rebuild the family's fortunes from nothing.

## The Merchant Prince

John Lysaght Roche, born in 1688, transformed a dispossessed family into one of the wealthiest Catholic trading dynasties in Munster. By the 1740s he was operating oil mills near Sixmilebridge in Clare and trading provisions across Ireland and Europe. In 1755 he fitted out "a huge ship, the largest of its kind ever seen in Limerick," and dispatched it to the West Indies, mounted with fourteen sixteen-pounder guns to fend off pirates on the hazardous crossing. The Limerick Chronicle would call him a "merchant prince." His sons fanned out across Europe: Stephen inherited the Limerick empire, Philip stayed to build, John took the business to Dublin, and Thomas went to Rotterdam. Every one of them became independently wealthy.

## Charlotte's Quay, 1801

When Stephen's sons Thomas and William Roche opened their bank on Charlotte's Quay in 1801, they were entering a business that was already in their blood. Their uncle Philip had built the Granary warehouse on Michael Street in 1787 — a massive limestone building that still stands today, now the Limerick City Library. Their father Stephen had been "the oldest merchant in that city" when he died in 1804. The Roche bank issued its own banknotes — promissory notes printed by Ashby of London, bearing the family coat of arms: three roach fish swimming on a red field, and the motto *Mon Dieu est ma roche*.

## The Hanging Gardens

In 1806 the bank moved to George's Street — the grand Georgian thoroughfare that was the spine of Newtown Pery. Behind the bank, William constructed something extraordinary: terraced gardens rising seventy feet above street level, covering an acre of land from George's Street to Henry Street, at a cost of £15,000. The gardens sat on vaulted stone stores. The soil on some terraces was five feet deep. A system of flues and vents heated and cooled the beds. At the top: hothouses growing grapes, pineapples, peaches, and oranges. In the middle: pears, apples, plums, melons, and cucumbers. At the bottom: flowers. The Duchess of Richmond visited in 1809. Every notable visitor to Limerick came to see the Hanging Gardens of the Roches.

## The Crisis of 1820

On 25 May 1820, Roches' Cork Bank — run by Thomas and William's brothers Stephen and James — closed its doors. The collapse triggered a chain reaction that would destroy the Irish private banking system. Leslie's Bank in Cork fell the same day. Maunsell's Bank in Limerick, which had been the city's first bank since 1789 and was considered unassailable, followed within days. By 12 June, Alexander's Bank in Dublin had gone under. The crisis, caused by a post-Napoleonic collapse in grain prices and land values, was later called the Munster Banking Crisis. Every private bank in Limerick failed — except one. Thomas Roche held the Limerick bank steady. The legal separation between the Cork and Limerick operations, maintained as distinct partnerships despite the family ties, proved decisive. By 1824, Thomas Roche & Co. was the only bank recorded in Limerick.

## "The Only Man in Ireland"

William Roche entered Parliament in December 1832 as the first Catholic M.P. for Limerick since the repeal of the penal laws. He was close friends with Daniel O'Connell — they were kin, through the marriage of William's aunt Christiana Roche into the O'Connell family of Iveragh. O'Connell said of him: "William Roche was the only man in Ireland from whom he would not demand a pledge." William was vocal on Catholic emancipation, Jewish emancipation, and the abolition of slavery. He was the first Roman Catholic in Ireland to become a Commissioner of the Peace. He died unmarried and childless at Lower Glentworth Street on 27 April 1850, and his gardens — with nobody to maintain them — were dismantled soon after.

## The Obituary

Thomas outlived his brother by five years, dying on 15 December 1855 at his house in George's Street, "after a short illness, in his 87th year." The Limerick Chronicle gave him a remarkable eulogy: "surviving partner of the late highly respectable and independent Bank of Thomas and William Roche, of Limerick, the only one, probably, in the South of Ireland, that passed through the disastrous commercial crash of private banks 40 years ago, with perfect honour and integrity. Mr Thomas Roche was like his brothers, a gentleman of highly cultivated mind and of strict moral rectitude in all his extensive business transactions. His domestic life was pure and irreproachable, his politics ever liberal and consistent."

His brother James, who had been imprisoned during the French Revolution and later became "the Roscoe of Cork" — a banker-scholar who contributed to the *Gentleman's Magazine* and authored *Essays of an Octogenarian* — had died two years earlier, in 1853. The Dublin Evening Packet named him "formerly an eminent banker, and brother of Thomas Roche, Esq." James is the only member of this generation whose face survives: Daniel Maclise drew him in pencil and wash in July 1829, seated at a desk, reading — the picture of the banker-intellectual.
